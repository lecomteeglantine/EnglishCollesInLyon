Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse British identity: Britishness, national symbols, class, accents, the four nations, devolution, multiculturalism, race, religion, empire, Brexit, regional divides and the tension between unity and diversity.

A. Core concepts: Britishness, symbols and national myths

British identity

IPA: /ˌbrɪtɪʃ aɪˈdentəti/
Pronunciation: BRIT-ish eye-DEN-tuh-tee
French: identité britannique

Definition: A sense of belonging to Britain or the United Kingdom, shaped by history, culture, institutions and values.

Example: British identity has become more contested since Brexit.

Britishness

IPA: /ˈbrɪtɪʃnəs/
Pronunciation: BRIT-ish-nuhs
French: britannicité ; identité britannique

Definition: The quality, feeling or idea of being British.

Example: Britishness can mean different things in England, Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland.

national identity

IPA: /ˌnæʃənəl aɪˈdentəti/
Pronunciation: NASH-uh-nuhl eye-DEN-tuh-tee
French: identité nationale

Definition: A shared sense of belonging to a nation.

Example: National identity can be inclusive, exclusive or politically contested.

shared identity

IPA: /ˌʃeəd aɪˈdentəti/
Pronunciation: shaird eye-DEN-tuh-tee
French: identité partagée

Definition: A sense of belonging based on common values, symbols or history.

Example: The monarchy is often presented as part of a shared identity.

collective memory

IPA: /kəˌlektɪv ˈmeməri/
Pronunciation: kuh-LEK-tiv MEM-uh-ree
French: mémoire collective

Definition: The way a society remembers and interprets its past.

Example: Collective memory shapes debates about empire and war.

national narrative

IPA: /ˌnæʃənəl ˈnærətɪv/
Pronunciation: NASH-uh-nuhl NAR-uh-tiv
French: récit national

Definition: The story a nation tells about itself.

Example: The national narrative often celebrates resilience and liberty.

imagined community

IPA: /ɪˌmædʒɪnd kəˈmjuːnəti/
Pronunciation: i-MAJ-ind kuh-MYOO-nuh-tee
French: communauté imaginée

Definition: A nation understood as a community whose members feel connected despite not knowing each other personally.

Example: British identity can be analysed as an imagined community.

sense of belonging

IPA: /ˌsens əv bɪˈlɒŋɪŋ/
Pronunciation: sens ov bi-LONG-ing
French: sentiment d’appartenance

Definition: The feeling of being accepted as part of a group or place.

Example: Citizenship can strengthen a sense of belonging.

patriotism

IPA: /ˈpætriətɪzəm/
Pronunciation: PAT-ree-uh-tiz-uhm
French: patriotisme

Definition: Love, pride or loyalty towards one’s country.

Example: Patriotism can be inclusive or used to exclude critics.

national pride

IPA: /ˌnæʃənəl ˈpraɪd/
Pronunciation: NASH-uh-nuhl pryde
French: fierté nationale

Definition: A feeling of satisfaction or pride in one’s country.

Example: Royal ceremonies often produce displays of national pride.

national symbols

IPA: /ˌnæʃənəl ˈsɪmbəlz/
Pronunciation: NASH-uh-nuhl SIM-buhlz
French: symboles nationaux

Definition: Objects, flags, songs or institutions representing a nation.

Example: Flags, anthems and ceremonies are powerful national symbols.

Union Jack

IPA: /ˌjuːniən ˈdʒæk/
Pronunciation: YOO-nee-uhn jak
French: Union Jack ; drapeau britannique

Definition: The national flag of the United Kingdom.

Example: The Union Jack symbolises the union of several nations.

Union Flag

IPA: /ˈjuːniən flæɡ/
Pronunciation: YOO-nee-uhn flag
French: Union Flag ; drapeau de l’Union

Definition: Another name for the Union Jack, especially in formal contexts.

Example: The Union Flag appears at state ceremonies.

national anthem

IPA: /ˌnæʃənəl ˈænθəm/
Pronunciation: NASH-uh-nuhl AN-thuhm
French: hymne national

Definition: A song officially representing a country.

Example: The national anthem is sung at royal and sporting events.

God Save the King

IPA: /ˌɡɒd seɪv ðə ˈkɪŋ/
Pronunciation: god sayv thuh king
French: God Save the King

Definition: The UK national anthem when the monarch is a king.

Example: God Save the King links national identity to the monarchy.

Remembrance Day

IPA: /rɪˈmembrəns deɪ/
Pronunciation: ri-MEM-bruhns day
French: jour du Souvenir

Definition: A day commemorating those who died in war.

Example: Remembrance Day remains central to British collective memory.

poppy

IPA: /ˈpɒpi/
Pronunciation: POP-ee
French: coquelicot du souvenir

Definition: A red flower worn as a symbol of remembrance for war dead.

Example: The poppy is a symbol of remembrance in Britain.

Blitz spirit

IPA: /ˈblɪts ˌspɪrɪt/
Pronunciation: blits SPIR-it
French: esprit du Blitz ; résilience britannique

Definition: The idea of British resilience and unity during the Second World War bombing of cities.

Example: Politicians often invoke the Blitz spirit during crises.

Keep Calm and Carry On

IPA: /ˌkiːp kɑːm ənd ˌkæri ˈɒn/
Pronunciation: keep karm and KARR-ee on
French: Keep Calm and Carry On ; garder son calme

Definition: A wartime slogan now associated with British stoicism.

Example: Keep Calm and Carry On became a popular symbol of British stoicism.

stoicism

IPA: /ˈstəʊɪsɪzəm/
Pronunciation: STOH-i-siz-uhm
French: stoïcisme

Definition: The ability to endure difficulty without emotional display.

Example: British stoicism is often presented as a national trait.

stiff upper lip

IPA: /ˌstɪf ˌʌpə ˈlɪp/
Pronunciation: stif UP-uh lip
French: flegme britannique ; retenue émotionnelle

Definition: A stereotypically British ability to remain calm and unemotional under pressure.

Example: The stiff upper lip is both admired and mocked.

politeness

IPA: /pəˈlaɪtnəs/
Pronunciation: puh-LYTE-nuhs
French: politesse

Definition: Respectful and socially acceptable behaviour.

Example: Politeness is a common stereotype of British behaviour.

queueing

IPA: /ˈkjuːɪŋ/
Pronunciation: KYOO-ing
French: faire la queue

Definition: Waiting in line in an orderly way, often seen as stereotypically British.

Example: Queueing is sometimes jokingly described as a British national habit.

understatement

IPA: /ˌʌndəˈsteɪtmənt/
Pronunciation: UN-duh-STAYT-muhnt
French: litote ; euphémisme ; retenue

Definition: Expressing something in a restrained or less dramatic way than expected.

Example: Understatement is a classic feature of British humour.

self-deprecation

IPA: /ˌself deprəˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: self dep-ruh-KAY-shuhn
French: autodérision

Definition: Making modest or humorous criticism of oneself.

Example: Self-deprecation is often associated with British humour.

dry humour

IPA: /ˌdraɪ ˈhjuːmə/
Pronunciation: dry HYOO-muh
French: humour pince-sans-rire

Definition: Humour delivered in a calm, understated or deadpan way.

Example: Dry humour is a common British stereotype.

class system

IPA: /ˈklɑːs ˌsɪstəm/
Pronunciation: klass SIS-tuhm
French: système de classes

Definition: A social hierarchy based on wealth, education, accent, occupation and background.

Example: The class system still shapes British identity.

B. Class, accents and elite institutions

social class

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl ˈklɑːs/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl klass
French: classe sociale

Definition: A group defined by economic position, education, occupation and cultural habits.

Example: Social class remains important in Britain.

working class

IPA: /ˌwɜːkɪŋ ˈklɑːs/
Pronunciation: WUR-king klass
French: classe ouvrière ; classes populaires

Definition: People associated with manual, service or lower-paid work.

Example: Working-class identity has changed with deindustrialisation.

middle class

IPA: /ˌmɪdəl ˈklɑːs/
Pronunciation: MID-uhl klass
French: classe moyenne

Definition: People associated with professional work, education and relative economic comfort.

Example: Middle-class norms often dominate public institutions.

upper class

IPA: /ˌʌpə ˈklɑːs/
Pronunciation: UP-uh klass
French: classe supérieure

Definition: The wealthiest and most socially privileged class.

Example: The upper class is linked to private schools and inherited wealth.

class consciousness

IPA: /ˈklɑːs ˌkɒnʃəsnəs/
Pronunciation: klass KON-shuhs-nuhs
French: conscience de classe

Definition: Awareness of class identity and class divisions.

Example: Class consciousness remains visible in British politics.

class divide

IPA: /ˈklɑːs dɪˌvaɪd/
Pronunciation: klass di-VYDE
French: fracture de classe

Definition: A social gap between classes.

Example: The class divide affects education, health and housing.

accent

IPA: /ˈæksent/
Pronunciation: AK-sent
French: accent

Definition: A way of pronouncing language associated with region, class or background.

Example: Accent can strongly shape perceptions of class in Britain.

regional accent

IPA: /ˌriːdʒənəl ˈæksent/
Pronunciation: REE-juh-nuhl AK-sent
French: accent régional

Definition: An accent associated with a particular region.

Example: Regional accents can be a source of pride or discrimination.

Received Pronunciation

IPA: /rɪˌsiːvd prəˌnʌnsiˈeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: ri-SEEVD pruh-nun-see-AY-shuhn
French: Received Pronunciation ; accent britannique standard prestigieux

Definition: A prestigious British accent historically associated with the upper classes and the BBC.

Example: Received Pronunciation once dominated broadcasting.

RP

IPA: /ˌɑː ˈpiː/
Pronunciation: ar pee
French: RP

Definition: Abbreviation for Received Pronunciation.

Example: RP is often associated with elite education.

posh

IPA: /pɒʃ/
Pronunciation: posh
French: chic ; huppé ; bourgeois

Definition: Informal word for upper-class, expensive or socially privileged.

Example: A posh accent can affect how speakers are judged.

elite education

IPA: /eɪˌliːt ˌedjʊˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: ay-LEET ej-yoo-KAY-shuhn
French: éducation d’élite

Definition: Education in prestigious schools or universities.

Example: Elite education is often criticised for reproducing privilege.

public school

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk ˈskuːl/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik school
French: école privée prestigieuse britannique

Definition: In Britain, an expensive private school, often historically prestigious.

Example: Public schools have produced many British political leaders.

private school

IPA: /ˌpraɪvət ˈskuːl/
Pronunciation: PRY-vuht school
French: école privée

Definition: A fee-paying school not funded by the state.

Example: Private schools play a major role in elite reproduction.

Oxbridge

IPA: /ˈɒksbrɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: OKS-brij
French: Oxbridge ; Oxford et Cambridge

Definition: Oxford and Cambridge universities considered together.

Example: Oxbridge remains influential in British public life.

establishment

IPA: /ɪˈstæblɪʃmənt/
Pronunciation: i-STAB-lish-muhnt
French: establishment ; élite dirigeante

Definition: The network of powerful people and institutions controlling society.

Example: The British establishment is often criticised as closed and privileged.

old boys’ network

IPA: /ˌəʊld ˈbɔɪz ˌnetwɜːk/
Pronunciation: ohld boyz NET-wurk
French: réseau des anciens ; entre-soi masculin élitiste

Definition: Informal connections among privileged men educated at elite institutions.

Example: The old boys’ network can limit social mobility.

social mobility

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl məʊˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl moh-BIL-uh-tee
French: mobilité sociale

Definition: Movement between social classes.

Example: Low social mobility challenges Britain’s meritocratic image.

meritocracy

IPA: /ˌmerɪˈtɒkrəsi/
Pronunciation: mer-i-TOK-ruh-see
French: méritocratie

Definition: A system where success is based on talent and effort.

Example: Critics argue that Britain is not a true meritocracy.

inherited privilege

IPA: /ɪnˌherɪtɪd ˈprɪvəlɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: in-HER-i-tid PRIV-uh-lij
French: privilège hérité

Definition: Advantages passed down through family background.

Example: Inherited privilege remains visible in education and politics.

four nations

IPA: /ˌfɔː ˈneɪʃənz/
Pronunciation: four NAY-shuhnz
French: quatre nations

Definition: England, Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland within the UK.

Example: The UK is often described as a union of four nations.

England

IPA: /ˈɪŋɡlənd/
Pronunciation: ING-gluhnd
French: Angleterre

Definition: The largest nation of the United Kingdom.

Example: England dominates the UK demographically and politically.
