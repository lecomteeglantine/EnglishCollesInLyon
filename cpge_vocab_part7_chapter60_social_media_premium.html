Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse social media: platforms, feeds, virality, influencers, algorithms, moderation, misinformation, polarisation, privacy, surveillance capitalism, mental health, platform regulation, online activism, election interference and the changing relationship between journalism, politics and public opinion.

How to use this chapter

For press articlesFocus on terms such as recommendation algorithm, content moderation, misinformation, echo chamber, surveillance capitalism, microtargeting, attention economy, platform accountability, online activism and election interference.
For oral examsUse this vocabulary to explain social media as both a democratic tool and a risky ecosystem: it can inform, mobilise and connect people, but also polarise, manipulate and harm users.
For essaysMemorise the collocations: to go viral, to spread misinformation, to moderate content, to regulate platforms, to manipulate public opinion.

A. Core social media vocabulary

social media

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl ˈmiːdiə/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl MEE-dee-uh
French: réseaux sociaux

Definition: Websites and apps that allow users to create, share and interact with content.

Example: Social media has transformed communication, politics and journalism.

social network

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl ˈnetwɜːk/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl NET-work
French: réseau social

Definition: An online platform connecting users through profiles, posts and interactions.

Example: A social network can spread information faster than traditional media.

platform

IPA: /ˈplætfɔːm/
Pronunciation: PLAT-form
French: plateforme

Definition: A digital service that connects users, content, advertisers or businesses.

Example: Platforms shape what users see and how they interact.

digital platform

IPA: /ˌdɪdʒɪtəl ˈplætfɔːm/
Pronunciation: DIJ-i-tuhl PLAT-form
French: plateforme numérique

Definition: An online system enabling communication, exchange or content distribution.

Example: Digital platforms have become powerful economic and political actors.

online platform

IPA: /ˌɒnlaɪn ˈplætfɔːm/
Pronunciation: ON-line PLAT-form
French: plateforme en ligne

Definition: A platform accessed through the internet.

Example: Online platforms are central to public debate.

user

IPA: /ˈjuːzə/
Pronunciation: YOO-zuh
French: utilisateur

Definition: A person who uses a digital service or platform.

Example: Users produce much of the content on social media.

account

IPA: /əˈkaʊnt/
Pronunciation: uh-KOWNT
French: compte

Definition: A user profile on a digital platform.

Example: A fake account can be used to spread propaganda.

profile

IPA: /ˈprəʊfaɪl/
Pronunciation: PROH-fyle
French: profil

Definition: A page or account representing a user online.

Example: A profile can reveal personal information.

feed

IPA: /fiːd/
Pronunciation: feed
French: fil d’actualité

Definition: The stream of posts or content shown to a user.

Example: The feed is often organised by algorithms.

timeline

IPA: /ˈtaɪmlaɪn/
Pronunciation: TYME-line
French: fil ; timeline

Definition: A chronological or algorithmic stream of posts.

Example: A user’s timeline can become politically polarised.

post

IPA: /pəʊst/
Pronunciation: pohst
French: publication ; post

Definition: A piece of content shared online.

Example: A post can go viral within hours.

tweet

IPA: /twiːt/
Pronunciation: tweet
French: tweet

Definition: A short post on X, formerly Twitter.

Example: A controversial tweet can trigger a public backlash.

thread

IPA: /θred/
Pronunciation: thread
French: fil de discussion

Definition: A connected series of posts or comments.

Example: A thread can explain a complex issue.

comment

IPA: /ˈkɒment/
Pronunciation: KOM-ent
French: commentaire

Definition: A written response to online content.

Example: Comments can encourage debate or harassment.

like

IPA: /laɪk/
Pronunciation: like
French: like ; j’aime

Definition: A button showing approval or interest in online content.

Example: Likes can reward emotional or extreme content.

share

IPA: /ʃeə/
Pronunciation: share
French: partage

Definition: A repost or act of distributing content to others.

Example: Shares help content spread quickly.

repost

IPA: /ˌriːˈpəʊst/
Pronunciation: ree-POHST
French: republication ; repost

Definition: To share someone else’s content again.

Example: A repost can amplify misinformation.

hashtag

IPA: /ˈhæʃtæɡ/
Pronunciation: HASH-tag
French: hashtag ; mot-dièse

Definition: A word or phrase preceded by # to group online content.

Example: A hashtag can organise a political campaign.

trend

IPA: /trend/
Pronunciation: trend
French: tendance

Definition: A topic or hashtag that becomes popular online.

Example: A trend can influence news coverage.

viral

IPA: /ˈvaɪrəl/
Pronunciation: VY-ruhl
French: viral

Definition: Spreading very quickly online.

Example: A viral video can shape public opinion.

virality

IPA: /vaɪˈræləti/
Pronunciation: vy-RAL-uh-tee
French: viralisation

Definition: The quality of spreading rapidly online.

Example: Virality rewards content that triggers strong emotions.

meme

IPA: /miːm/
Pronunciation: meem
French: mème

Definition: A humorous or symbolic image, video or phrase spread online.

Example: Memes can carry political messages.

GIF

IPA: /ɡɪf/
Pronunciation: gif
French: GIF

Definition: A short looping animated image.

Example: GIFs are used to react quickly online.

short-form video

IPA: /ˌʃɔːt fɔːm ˈvɪdiəʊ/
Pronunciation: short-form VID-ee-oh
French: vidéo courte

Definition: A brief video designed for quick consumption online.

Example: Short-form video dominates many social platforms.

livestream

IPA: /ˈlaɪvstriːm/
Pronunciation: LYVE-stream
French: diffusion en direct

Definition: A live video broadcast online.

Example: A livestream can bypass traditional media.

influencer

IPA: /ˈɪnfluənsə/
Pronunciation: IN-floo-uhn-suh
French: influenceur ; influenceuse

Definition: A person who shapes audiences’ opinions or purchases online.

Example: Influencers can affect politics, fashion and consumer behaviour.

content creator

IPA: /ˈkɒntent kriˌeɪtə/
Pronunciation: KON-tent kree-AY-tuh
French: créateur de contenu

Definition: A person who produces online content for an audience.

Example: Content creators can build large communities.

follower

IPA: /ˈfɒləʊə/
Pronunciation: FOL-oh-uh
French: abonné

Definition: A person who subscribes to another user’s updates.

Example: A large number of followers can create influence.

subscriber

IPA: /səbˈskraɪbə/
Pronunciation: suhb-SKRY-buh
French: abonné

Definition: A person who signs up to receive content from a channel or creator.

Example: Subscribers can support creators financially.

audience

IPA: /ˈɔːdiəns/
Pronunciation: AW-dee-uhns
French: public ; audience

Definition: The people who consume or follow content.

Example: Online audiences are fragmented and highly targeted.

engagement

IPA: /ɪnˈɡeɪdʒmənt/
Pronunciation: in-GAYJ-muhnt
French: engagement

Definition: User interaction with content, such as likes, comments and shares.

Example: Platforms optimise for engagement.

reach

IPA: /riːtʃ/
Pronunciation: reech
French: portée

Definition: The number of people who see content.

Example: A platform’s reach gives it political influence.

impression

IPA: /ɪmˈpreʃən/
Pronunciation: im-PRESH-uhn
French: impression ; affichage

Definition: A single display of online content to a user.

Example: Advertisers pay for impressions.

algorithm

IPA: /ˈælɡərɪðəm/
Pronunciation: AL-guh-rith-uhm
French: algorithme

Definition: A set of rules used by a computer to rank, recommend or filter content.

Example: Algorithms decide which posts users are likely to see.

recommendation algorithm

IPA: /ˌrekəmenˈdeɪʃən ˈælɡərɪðəm/
Pronunciation: rek-uh-men-DAY-shuhn AL-guh-rith-uhm
French: algorithme de recommandation

Definition: An algorithm that suggests content to users.

Example: Recommendation algorithms can push users towards extreme content.

B. Algorithms, moderation and information disorder

algorithmic ranking

IPA: /ˌælɡəˈrɪðmɪk ˈræŋkɪŋ/
Pronunciation: al-guh-RITH-mik RANG-king
French: classement algorithmique

Definition: The ordering of content by algorithms rather than chronology.

Example: Algorithmic ranking shapes public attention.

content moderation

IPA: /ˈkɒntent ˌmɒdəˈreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: KON-tent mod-uh-RAY-shuhn
French: modération de contenu

Definition: The process of reviewing, removing or limiting online content.

Example: Content moderation raises questions about free speech.

moderator

IPA: /ˈmɒdəreɪtə/
Pronunciation: MOD-uh-ray-tuh
French: modérateur

Definition: A person or system that reviews online content or behaviour.

Example: Moderators are exposed to disturbing content.

community guidelines

IPA: /kəˌmjuːnəti ˈɡaɪdlaɪnz/
Pronunciation: kuh-MYOO-nuh-tee GYDE-lynz
French: règles de la communauté

Definition: Rules defining acceptable behaviour on a platform.

Example: Users can be banned for violating community guidelines.

terms of service

IPA: /ˌtɜːmz əv ˈsɜːvɪs/
Pronunciation: terms ov SUR-vis
French: conditions d’utilisation

Definition: The legal rules users accept when using a platform.

Example: Terms of service are often long and difficult to understand.

ban

IPA: /bæn/
Pronunciation: ban
French: interdiction ; bannissement

Definition: A decision preventing a user from using a platform or service.

Example: A ban can be temporary or permanent.

shadow ban

IPA: /ˈʃædəʊ bæn/
Pronunciation: SHAD-oh ban
French: shadow ban ; bannissement invisible

Definition: A hidden reduction in a user’s visibility on a platform.

Example: Users often suspect a shadow ban when engagement falls.

deplatforming

IPA: /ˌdiːˈplætfɔːmɪŋ/
Pronunciation: dee-PLAT-form-ing
French: déplateformisation

Definition: Removing someone’s access to major online platforms.

Example: Deplatforming can limit extremist reach but raises free-speech concerns.

account suspension

IPA: /əˈkaʊnt səˌspenʃən/
Pronunciation: uh-KOWNT suh-SPEN-shuhn
French: suspension de compte

Definition: Temporary or permanent disabling of a user account.

Example: Account suspension can follow repeated rule violations.

fact-checking

IPA: /ˈfækt ˌtʃekɪŋ/
Pronunciation: FAKT-chek-ing
French: vérification des faits

Definition: Verifying whether claims are accurate.

Example: Fact-checking can slow the spread of false claims.

misinformation

IPA: /ˌmɪsɪnfəˈmeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: mis-in-fuh-MAY-shuhn
French: mésinformation

Definition: False information spread without necessarily intending harm.

Example: Misinformation can spread during elections or crises.

disinformation

IPA: /ˌdɪsɪnfəˈmeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: dis-in-fuh-MAY-shuhn
French: désinformation

Definition: False information spread deliberately to mislead.

Example: Disinformation campaigns can undermine trust.

fake news

IPA: /ˌfeɪk ˈnjuːz/
Pronunciation: fake news
French: fausses informations ; fake news

Definition: False or misleading information presented as news.

Example: Fake news spreads easily on social media.

rumour

IPA: /ˈruːmə/
Pronunciation: ROO-muh
French: rumeur

Definition: Unverified information passed from person to person.

Example: A rumour can become viral before it is checked.

conspiracy theory

IPA: /kənˈspɪrəsi ˈθɪəri/
Pronunciation: kuhn-SPIR-uh-see THEER-ee
French: théorie du complot

Definition: A belief that hidden groups secretly control events, often without evidence.

Example: Conspiracy theories thrive in distrustful environments.

propaganda

IPA: /ˌprɒpəˈɡændə/
Pronunciation: prop-uh-GAN-duh
French: propagande

Definition: Biased or misleading information used to promote a political cause.

Example: Propaganda can be disguised as ordinary content.

information disorder

IPA: /ˌɪnfəˈmeɪʃən dɪsˈɔːdə/
Pronunciation: in-fuh-MAY-shuhn dis-OR-duh
French: désordre informationnel

Definition: A broad term for misinformation, disinformation and harmful information flows.

Example: Information disorder makes public debate harder.

echo chamber

IPA: /ˈekəʊ ˌtʃeɪmbə/
Pronunciation: EK-oh CHAYM-buh
French: chambre d’écho

Definition: An environment where people mainly encounter views similar to their own.

Example: Echo chambers can reinforce political beliefs.

filter bubble

IPA: /ˈfɪltə ˌbʌbəl/
Pronunciation: FIL-tuh BUB-uhl
French: bulle de filtres

Definition: A personalised information environment shaped by algorithms.

Example: A filter bubble can narrow exposure to opposing views.

polarisation

IPA: /ˌpəʊləraɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: poh-luh-ry-ZAY-shuhn
French: polarisation

Definition: The division of society into opposing hostile groups.

Example: Social media may intensify polarisation.

radicalisation

IPA: /ˌrædɪkəlaɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: rad-i-kuh-ly-ZAY-shuhn
French: radicalisation

Definition: The process of adopting extreme views, sometimes leading to violence.

Example: Online communities can accelerate radicalisation.

online extremism

IPA: /ˌɒnlaɪn ɪkˈstriːmɪzəm/
Pronunciation: ON-line ik-STREEM-iz-uhm
French: extrémisme en ligne

Definition: Extremist ideology or activity spread through the internet.

Example: Online extremism poses challenges for platforms and governments.

hate speech

IPA: /ˈheɪt spiːtʃ/
Pronunciation: hate speech
French: discours de haine

Definition: Speech attacking people based on identity such as race, religion or gender.

Example: Hate speech can normalise discrimination.

harassment

IPA: /ˈhærəsmənt/
Pronunciation: HARR-uhs-muhnt
French: harcèlement

Definition: Repeated abusive or threatening behaviour.

Example: Online harassment can silence journalists and activists.

cyberbullying

IPA: /ˈsaɪbəˌbʊliɪŋ/
Pronunciation: SY-buh-BULL-ee-ing
French: cyberharcèlement

Definition: Bullying carried out through digital tools or platforms.

Example: Cyberbullying can seriously affect teenagers’ mental health.

trolling

IPA: /ˈtrəʊlɪŋ/
Pronunciation: TROHL-ing
French: trolling ; provocation en ligne

Definition: Posting inflammatory content to provoke or upset people.

Example: Trolling can derail online discussions.

pile-on

IPA: /ˈpaɪl ɒn/
Pronunciation: PYLE-on
French: déferlement de critiques ; meute numérique

Definition: A wave of online attacks or criticism by many users.

Example: A pile-on can become psychologically overwhelming.

cancel culture

IPA: /ˈkænsəl ˌkʌltʃə/
Pronunciation: KAN-suhl KUL-chuh
French: cancel culture ; culture de l’annulation

Definition: Public shaming or boycotting of people for perceived wrongdoing.

Example: Cancel culture is debated as accountability or mob justice.

public shaming

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk ˈʃeɪmɪŋ/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik SHAY-ming
French: humiliation publique

Definition: Publicly humiliating someone for their behaviour or views.

Example: Public shaming can be disproportionate online.

doxxing

IPA: /ˈdɒksɪŋ/
Pronunciation: DOKS-ing
French: doxxing

Definition: Publishing someone’s private information online without consent.

Example: Doxxing can endanger people in real life.

privacy

IPA: /ˈprɪvəsi/
Pronunciation: PRIV-uh-see
French: vie privée

Definition: Protection of personal life and information.

Example: Privacy is difficult to preserve on social media.

data privacy

IPA: /ˈdeɪtə ˈprɪvəsi/
Pronunciation: DAY-tuh PRIV-uh-see
French: confidentialité des données

Definition: Protection of personal data from misuse.

Example: Data privacy is central to platform regulation.

personal data

IPA: /ˌpɜːsənəl ˈdeɪtə/
Pronunciation: PUR-suh-nuhl DAY-tuh
French: données personnelles

Definition: Information relating to an identifiable person.

Example: Platforms collect huge amounts of personal data.

data collection

IPA: /ˈdeɪtə kəˌlekʃən/
Pronunciation: DAY-tuh kuh-LEK-shuhn
French: collecte de données

Definition: The gathering of user information.

Example: Data collection powers targeted advertising.

data harvesting

IPA: /ˈdeɪtə ˌhɑːvɪstɪŋ/
Pronunciation: DAY-tuh HAR-vis-ting
French: collecte massive de données

Definition: Large-scale collection of personal data, often for commercial or political use.

Example: Data harvesting can be opaque and intrusive.

surveillance capitalism

IPA: /səˈveɪləns ˈkæpɪtəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: suh-VAY-luhns KAP-i-tuhl-iz-uhm
French: capitalisme de surveillance

Definition: An economic model based on collecting and monetising personal data.

Example: Surveillance capitalism turns attention and behaviour into profit.

C. Privacy, attention economy and mental health

IPA: /ˈtɑːɡɪtɪd ˈædvətaɪzɪŋ/
Pronunciation: TAR-git-id AD-vuh-ty-zing
French: publicité ciblée

Definition: Advertising aimed at specific users based on data.

Example: Targeted advertising funds many social platforms.

IPA: /ˌmaɪkrəʊˈtɑːɡɪtɪŋ/
Pronunciation: MY-kroh-TAR-git-ing
French: microciblage

IPA: /bɪˌheɪvjərəl ˈædvətaɪzɪŋ/
Pronunciation: bi-HAY-vyuh-ruhl AD-vuh-ty-zing

tracking

IPA: /ˈtrækɪŋ/
Pronunciation: TRAK-ing

cookie

IPA: /ˈkʊki/
