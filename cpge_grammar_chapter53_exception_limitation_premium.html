B2 → C1 grammar for precise scope, qualified claims, academic caution and sophisticated argumentation.

Chapter objective

This premium grammar chapter helps CPGE students express exception and limitation accurately. It covers exclusion, restricted scope, conditional limits, cautious generalisation, focus structures and inversion after limiting expressions.

The golden rule

Exception removes a case; limitation restricts a claim.
All regions improved except the north-east.
The reform succeeded only in urban areas.
Exceptionexcept / except for / apart from / other than / with the exception of
Limitationonly / solely / at least / to some extent / within limits
Conditional limitprovided that / as long as / unless / only if

A. Lesson — Expressing exception and limitation

Exception

Rule / explanation: An exception removes one person, thing or case from a general statement.

Example / answer: All regions experienced growth except the north-east.

Limitation

Rule / explanation: A limitation restricts the scope, validity or strength of a statement.

Example / answer: The reform was successful only in urban areas.

Qualification

Rule / explanation: Academic writing often limits a claim rather than presenting it as universally true.

Example / answer: The policy appears effective, at least in the short term.

CPGE relevance

Rule / explanation: These structures help students avoid overgeneralisation and produce precise, defensible arguments.

Example / answer: With the exception of one indicator, the data support the author’s conclusion.

B. Except, except for and except that

Except + noun

Rule / explanation: Use except before a noun or pronoun to exclude an item from a group.

Example / answer: All parties supported the proposal except the Greens.

Except for + noun

Rule / explanation: Use except for to introduce an exception to a general statement, especially when the sentence is otherwise complete.

Example / answer: The article is convincing except for its final paragraph.

Except that + clause

Rule / explanation: Use except that before a full clause.

Example / answer: The two policies are similar, except that the second provides more funding.

Except when / where / if

Rule / explanation: Use these forms to specify a circumstance in which the general rule does not apply.

Example / answer: The rule applies except when public safety is at risk.

No one except

Rule / explanation: Except may follow indefinite pronouns and negative expressions.

Example / answer: No one except the minister had seen the report.

C. Apart from, aside from and other than

Apart from

Rule / explanation: Apart from can mean except for or, depending on context, in addition to.

Example / answer: Apart from one factual error, the article is reliable.

Aside from

Rule / explanation: Aside from is close to apart from and is slightly more common in American English.

Example / answer: Aside from its cost, the reform raises serious ethical concerns.

Other than

Rule / explanation: Use other than to exclude alternatives or categories.

Example / answer: The policy offers no solution other than increased taxation.

Meaning depends on context

Rule / explanation: Apart from may exclude or add information, so make the intended meaning clear.

Example / answer: Apart from housing, the article also discusses healthcare.

Formal comparison

Rule / explanation: Other than is useful when rejecting all alternatives except one.

Example / answer: There is little evidence other than anecdotal testimony.

D. But, save and barring

But meaning except

Rule / explanation: After words such as all, everyone, nobody, nothing or anywhere, but can mean except.

Example / answer: Everyone but the prime minister supported the compromise.

Save / save for

Rule / explanation: Save and save for are formal or literary alternatives to except.

Example / answer: The streets were empty, save for a few journalists.

Barring

Rule / explanation: Barring means unless something unexpected prevents the stated outcome.

Example / answer: Barring a last-minute crisis, the bill will pass.

Bar

Rule / explanation: Bar can function as a preposition meaning except, especially in British English.

Example / answer: The proposal was supported by all parties bar one.

Register

Rule / explanation: Save, barring and bar are useful, but should be used only when their register suits the context.

Example / answer: Barring unforeseen circumstances, negotiations will resume tomorrow.

E. Only, merely and solely

Only

Rule / explanation: Only limits a statement to one person, thing, condition, degree or time.

Example / answer: The policy benefited only large companies.

Merely

Rule / explanation: Merely means only or no more than and often minimises importance.

Example / answer: The reform merely postponed the crisis.

Solely

Rule / explanation: Solely means exclusively and is common in formal analysis.

Example / answer: The decline cannot be explained solely by inflation.

Only after / only when

Rule / explanation: When an only-expression begins the sentence, inversion is required in the main clause.

Example / answer: Only after the report was published did the government respond.

Not only

Rule / explanation: Do not confuse limiting only with the correlative structure not only...but also.

Example / answer: The policy was not only costly but also ineffective.

F. At least, at most and no more than

At least

Rule / explanation: At least sets a minimum or introduces a limited positive point.

Example / answer: The reform created at least 10,000 jobs.

At least as a qualifier

Rule / explanation: At least can restrict a claim to one perspective, period or condition.

Example / answer: The strategy worked, at least in the short term.

At most

Rule / explanation: At most sets a maximum.

Example / answer: The measure will reduce costs by five per cent at most.

No more than

Rule / explanation: No more than emphasises a small maximum quantity or limited significance.

Example / answer: The proposal received no more than twenty votes.

No less than

Rule / explanation: No less than emphasises a surprisingly large number or degree.

Example / answer: No less than three committees rejected the plan.

G. To some extent, up to a point and within limits

To some extent

Rule / explanation: Use this to express partial validity or agreement.

Example / answer: To some extent, the author’s criticism is justified.

Up to a point

Rule / explanation: This means that an argument is valid only within certain limits.

Example / answer: The policy was successful up to a point, but regional inequality persisted.

Within limits

Rule / explanation: Use within limits to acknowledge restricted usefulness or acceptability.

Example / answer: Market competition can be beneficial within limits.

Within the scope of

Rule / explanation: This formal phrase defines the area covered by an analysis, rule or study.

Example / answer: The issue falls within the scope of environmental law.

Beyond the scope of

Rule / explanation: Use this to exclude material from the present discussion.

Example / answer: A detailed analysis of taxation is beyond the scope of this essay.

H. As far as, insofar as and inasmuch as

As far as...is concerned

Rule / explanation: Use this structure to limit a statement to a particular topic or perspective.

Example / answer: As far as housing is concerned, the reform has achieved little.

Insofar as

Rule / explanation: Insofar as means to the extent that and limits the validity of a claim.

Example / answer: The policy succeeded insofar as it reduced short-term unemployment.

Inasmuch as

Rule / explanation: Inasmuch as is very formal and means to the extent that or because.

Example / answer: The proposal is defensible inasmuch as it protects vulnerable groups.

In terms of

Rule / explanation: Use in terms of to define the dimension being considered.

Example / answer: The reform was successful in terms of cost reduction.

From the standpoint of

Rule / explanation: Use this to limit analysis to a particular viewpoint.

Example / answer: From the standpoint of local authorities, the policy remains impractical.

I. Provided, unless and conditional limitations

Provided / providing that

Rule / explanation: These mean only if and establish a necessary condition.

Example / answer: The reform may succeed provided that sufficient funding is available.

As long as

Rule / explanation: As long as means on condition that.

Example / answer: The policy is acceptable as long as civil liberties are protected.

Unless

Rule / explanation: Unless means except if or if not.

Example / answer: The situation will worsen unless the government acts.

Except if

Rule / explanation: Except if is possible but usually less natural than unless.

Example / answer: The rule applies unless an emergency is declared.

Otherwise

Rule / explanation: Otherwise introduces the consequence if the condition is not met.

Example / answer: Funding must be increased; otherwise, the programme will fail.

J. Limiting generalisations

Most, not all

Rule / explanation: Use most, many, some or a majority instead of making universal claims.

Example / answer: Most voters supported the principle of reform.

Tend to

Rule / explanation: Tend to expresses a general pattern rather than an absolute rule.

Example / answer: Younger voters tend to be more supportive of environmental regulation.

Generally / broadly

Rule / explanation: These adverbs indicate that a claim is true in most cases.

Example / answer: The policy was broadly successful.

In many cases

Rule / explanation: Use this phrase to restrict a generalisation to frequent but non-universal situations.

Example / answer: In many cases, local authorities lacked the necessary resources.

Not necessarily

Rule / explanation: Use not necessarily to reject an automatic conclusion.

Example / answer: Economic growth does not necessarily lead to greater equality.

K. Exception and limitation in article commentary

With the exception of...

Rule / explanation: Use this to identify one item that does not follow the main pattern.

Example / answer: With the exception of the final section, the article is well documented.

The argument applies only to...

Rule / explanation: Use this to expose a restricted scope.

Example / answer: The argument applies only to large urban economies.

The evidence is limited to...

Rule / explanation: Use this to identify a narrow evidence base.

Example / answer: The evidence is limited to data from three regions.

The author overlooks...

Rule / explanation: Use this to identify an excluded case or factor.

Example / answer: The author overlooks several important exceptions.

This conclusion holds only if...

Rule / explanation: Use this to state the condition under which a claim remains valid.

Example / answer: This conclusion holds only if current growth rates continue.

L. Exception and limitation in CPGE essays

Define the scope

Rule / explanation: Clarify what the essay will and will not examine.

Example / answer: This essay will focus primarily on the political consequences of the reform.

Restrict a thesis

Rule / explanation: Limit the main claim to make it more accurate and defensible.

Example / answer: Regulation can promote innovation, provided that it remains proportionate.

Acknowledge exceptions

Rule / explanation: Recognising exceptions strengthens rather than weakens a serious argument.

Example / answer: Although the trend is widespread, several countries have followed a different path.

Challenge an absolute claim

Rule / explanation: Show that a statement is true only under specific conditions.

Example / answer: The market is efficient only when competition is genuine and information is accessible.

Qualified conclusion

Rule / explanation: End with a conclusion that states both validity and limits.

Example / answer: The reform was successful to some extent, but its benefits were largely confined to urban areas.

M. Focus and inversion after limiting expressions

Only then

Rule / explanation: When only then begins the sentence, invert the auxiliary and subject.

Example / answer: Only then did the government recognise the scale of the crisis.

Only by

Rule / explanation: Use inversion after only by + gerund or noun phrase.

Example / answer: Only by investing in public services can the state restore trust.

Only if

Rule / explanation: When only if begins the sentence, invert the main clause, not the if-clause.

Example / answer: Only if funding increases will the reform succeed.

Not until

Rule / explanation: Fronted not until requires inversion in the main clause.

Example / answer: Not until the inquiry ended did the minister resign.

Under no circumstances

Rule / explanation: This strong limiting expression requires inversion when fronted.

Example / answer: Under no circumstances should the data be disclosed.

N. Typical mistakes by French speakers

Except vs except for

Rule / explanation: Except directly excludes an item; except for often introduces a qualification to a complete general statement.

Example / answer: All students passed except Tom. / The essay is excellent except for the conclusion.

Except that + noun

Rule / explanation: Except that must introduce a clause.

Example / answer: Wrong: except that the conclusion. Correct: except for the conclusion.

Only placement

Rule / explanation: Place only immediately before the element it limits.

Example / answer: Only the minister criticised the plan ≠ The minister only criticised the plan.

Missing inversion

Rule / explanation: Use inversion after fronted only, not until and negative limiting expressions.

Example / answer: Only later did the truth emerge.

Unless + negative

Rule / explanation: Unless already contains a negative condition, so avoid unnecessary double negatives.

Example / answer: Wrong: unless the government does not act. Correct: unless the government acts.

Absolute generalisations

Rule / explanation: Avoid everyone, always and never unless the evidence truly supports them.

Example / answer: Prefer most, generally, tends to or in many cases.

O. Exercises

1. Choose except or except for

Rule / explanation: Everyone supported the plan ___ the finance minister.

Example / answer: Everyone supported the plan except the finance minister.

2. Choose except or except for

Rule / explanation: The article is excellent ___ its conclusion.

Example / answer: The article is excellent except for its conclusion.

3. Rewrite with only

Rule / explanation: The policy benefited large companies and no other group.

Example / answer: The policy benefited only large companies.

4. Add inversion

Rule / explanation: The government understood the problem only after the report was published.

Example / answer: Only after the report was published did the government understand the problem.

5. Rewrite with provided that

Rule / explanation: The reform may succeed, but sufficient funding is necessary.

Example / answer: The reform may succeed provided that sufficient funding is available.

6. Correct the sentence

Rule / explanation: Unless the government does not act, the crisis will worsen.

Example / answer: Unless the government acts, the crisis will worsen.

7. Translate

Rule / explanation: À l’exception du dernier paragraphe, l’article est convaincant.

Example / answer: With the exception of the final paragraph, the article is convincing.

8. Mini concours challenge

Rule / explanation: Write a paragraph using except for, solely, to some extent, provided that and only if.

Example / answer: Example: Except for its final recommendation, the article is persuasive. The crisis cannot be explained solely by inflation. To some extent, institutional weakness is also responsible. The reform could work provided that local authorities receive more funding. Only if implementation improves will public trust recover.
