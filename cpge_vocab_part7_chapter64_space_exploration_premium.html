A. Core space exploration and mission vocabulary

space exploration

IPA: /ˌspeɪs ekspləˈreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: space ek-spluh-RAY-shuhn
French: exploration spatiale

Definition: The investigation of outer space through observation, spacecraft, probes and human missions.

Example: Space exploration raises scientific, economic and geopolitical questions.

outer space

IPA: /ˌaʊtə ˈspeɪs/
Pronunciation: OW-tuh space
French: espace extra-atmosphérique

Definition: The region beyond Earth’s atmosphere.

Example: Outer space is governed by international treaties.

space race

IPA: /ˈspeɪs reɪs/
Pronunciation: space race
French: course à l’espace

Definition: Competition between states to achieve superiority in space activities.

Example: The original space race opposed the United States and the Soviet Union.

new space race

IPA: /ˌnjuː ˈspeɪs reɪs/
Pronunciation: nyoo space race
French: nouvelle course à l’espace

Definition: Contemporary competition between states and private companies in space.

Example: The new space race involves China, the United States, India and private firms.

space power

IPA: /ˈspeɪs ˌpaʊə/
Pronunciation: space POW-uh
French: puissance spatiale

Definition: A country with major technological, military or economic capabilities in space.

Example: China has become a major space power.

space agency

IPA: /ˈspeɪs ˌeɪdʒənsi/
Pronunciation: space AY-juhn-see
French: agence spatiale

Definition: An organisation responsible for a space programme.

Example: NASA and ESA are major space agencies.

NASA

IPA: /ˈnæsə/
Pronunciation: NASS-uh
French: NASA

Definition: The United States space agency.

Example: NASA leads many scientific and human spaceflight missions.

ESA

IPA: /ˌiː es ˈeɪ/
Pronunciation: ee-ess-ay
French: ESA ; Agence spatiale européenne

Definition: European Space Agency.

Example: ESA coordinates European space missions.

space programme

IPA: /ˈspeɪs ˌprəʊɡræm/
Pronunciation: space PROH-gram
French: programme spatial

Definition: A planned set of space missions, research and technologies.

Example: A national space programme can project prestige.

space mission

IPA: /ˈspeɪs ˌmɪʃən/
Pronunciation: space MISH-uhn
French: mission spatiale

Definition: A planned operation involving spacecraft, satellites, astronauts or probes.

Example: A space mission requires years of preparation.

launch

IPA: /lɔːntʃ/
Pronunciation: launch
French: lancement

Definition: The act of sending a rocket, spacecraft or satellite into space.

Example: A launch can fail within seconds if the rocket malfunctions.

rocket

IPA: /ˈrɒkɪt/
Pronunciation: ROK-it
French: fusée

Definition: A vehicle using powerful engines to travel into space.

Example: A rocket must escape Earth’s gravity.

launch vehicle

IPA: /ˈlɔːntʃ ˌviːɪkəl/
Pronunciation: launch VEE-i-kuhl
French: lanceur

Definition: A rocket used to carry payloads into space.

Example: A reliable launch vehicle is essential for space access.

payload

IPA: /ˈpeɪləʊd/
Pronunciation: PAY-lohd
French: charge utile

Definition: The cargo carried by a rocket, such as a satellite or probe.

Example: The payload was placed into orbit.

spacecraft

IPA: /ˈspeɪskrɑːft/
Pronunciation: SPACE-kraft
French: engin spatial ; vaisseau spatial

Definition: A vehicle designed to travel or operate in space.

Example: A spacecraft must survive extreme temperatures.

space probe

IPA: /ˈspeɪs prəʊb/
Pronunciation: space probe
French: sonde spatiale

Definition: An uncrewed spacecraft sent to study planets, moons or deep space.

Example: A space probe can send data back from distant worlds.

lander

IPA: /ˈlændə/
Pronunciation: LAN-duh
French: atterrisseur

Definition: A spacecraft designed to land on a planet, moon or asteroid.

Example: A lander must slow down safely before touching the surface.

rover

IPA: /ˈrəʊvə/
Pronunciation: ROH-vuh
French: rover ; astromobile

Definition: A robotic vehicle designed to move across a planet or moon.

Example: A rover can analyse rocks on Mars.

orbiter

IPA: /ˈɔːbɪtə/
Pronunciation: OR-bi-tuh
French: orbiteur

Definition: A spacecraft designed to orbit a planet, moon or other body.

Example: An orbiter can map a planet’s surface.

satellite

IPA: /ˈsætəlaɪt/
Pronunciation: SAT-uh-lyte
French: satellite

Definition: An object placed in orbit around Earth or another body.

Example: Satellites are used for communication, navigation and observation.

artificial satellite

IPA: /ˌɑːtɪˈfɪʃəl ˈsætəlaɪt/
Pronunciation: ar-ti-FISH-uhl SAT-uh-lyte
French: satellite artificiel

Definition: A human-made object placed in orbit.

Example: Artificial satellites support modern life.

orbit

IPA: /ˈɔːbɪt/
Pronunciation: OR-bit
French: orbite

Definition: The curved path of an object around a planet, star or moon.

Example: A satellite can remain in orbit for years.

low Earth orbit

IPA: /ˌləʊ ɜːθ ˈɔːbɪt/
Pronunciation: loh earth OR-bit
French: orbite terrestre basse

Definition: An orbit relatively close to Earth.

Example: Low Earth orbit is becoming increasingly crowded.

LEO

IPA: /ˌel iː ˈəʊ/
Pronunciation: el-ee-oh
French: LEO ; orbite basse

Definition: Abbreviation for low Earth orbit.

Example: Many internet satellites operate in LEO.

geostationary orbit

IPA: /ˌdʒiːəʊˈsteɪʃənəri ˈɔːbɪt/
Pronunciation: jee-oh-STAY-shuhn-uh-ree OR-bit
French: orbite géostationnaire

Definition: An orbit where a satellite appears fixed above one point on Earth.

Example: Weather and communication satellites often use geostationary orbit.

microgravity

IPA: /ˌmaɪkrəʊˈɡrævəti/
Pronunciation: MY-kroh-GRAV-uh-tee
French: microgravité

Definition: A condition in which people or objects appear almost weightless.

Example: Astronauts conduct experiments in microgravity.

weightlessness

IPA: /ˈweɪtləsnəs/
Pronunciation: WAIT-luhs-nuhs
French: apesanteur

Definition: The feeling or condition of having no apparent weight.

Example: Weightlessness affects muscles and bones.

gravity

IPA: /ˈɡrævəti/
Pronunciation: GRAV-uh-tee
French: gravité

Definition: The force that attracts objects towards each other.

Example: Gravity keeps planets in orbit around the Sun.

escape velocity

IPA: /ɪˈskeɪp vəˌlɒsəti/
Pronunciation: i-SKAYP vuh-LOSS-uh-tee
French: vitesse de libération

Definition: The speed needed to escape a celestial body’s gravity.

Example: A rocket must reach escape velocity to leave Earth.

re-entry

IPA: /ˌriː ˈentri/
Pronunciation: ree-EN-tree
French: rentrée atmosphérique

Definition: The return of a spacecraft into a planet’s atmosphere.

Example: Re-entry creates intense heat.

heat shield

IPA: /ˈhiːt ʃiːld/
Pronunciation: heat sheeld
French: bouclier thermique

Definition: A protective layer preventing a spacecraft from burning during re-entry.

Example: The heat shield must survive extreme temperatures.

space station

IPA: /ˈspeɪs ˌsteɪʃən/
Pronunciation: space STAY-shuhn
French: station spatiale

Definition: A large spacecraft where astronauts live and work in orbit.

Example: A space station supports long-term research.

International Space Station

IPA: /ˌɪntəˈnæʃənəl ˈspeɪs ˌsteɪʃən/
Pronunciation: IN-tuh-NASH-uh-nuhl space STAY-shuhn
French: Station spatiale internationale

Definition: A multinational research station in low Earth orbit.

Example: The International Space Station is a symbol of scientific cooperation.

ISS

IPA: /ˌaɪ es ˈes/
Pronunciation: eye-ess-ess
French: ISS

Definition: Abbreviation for International Space Station.

Example: The ISS has hosted astronauts from many countries.

astronaut

IPA: /ˈæstrənɔːt/
Pronunciation: AS-truh-nawt
French: astronaute

Definition: A person trained to travel and work in space.

Example: Astronauts must train for emergencies.

B. Human spaceflight, commercial space and satellite services

cosmonaut

IPA: /ˈkɒzmənɔːt/
Pronunciation: KOZ-muh-nawt
French: cosmonaute

Definition: A Russian or Soviet astronaut.

Example: The first human in space was a Soviet cosmonaut.

taikonaut

IPA: /ˈtaɪkənɔːt/
Pronunciation: TY-kuh-nawt
French: taïkonaute

Definition: A Chinese astronaut.

Example: Chinese taikonauts work on China’s space station.

crew

IPA: /kruː/
Pronunciation: crew
French: équipage

Definition: The group of people operating a spacecraft or mission.

Example: The crew spent six months in orbit.

mission control

IPA: /ˈmɪʃən kənˌtrəʊl/
Pronunciation: MISH-uhn kuhn-TROHL
French: centre de contrôle de mission

Definition: The ground team that manages a space mission.

Example: Mission control monitors spacecraft systems.

spacewalk

IPA: /ˈspeɪswɔːk/
Pronunciation: SPACE-wawk
French: sortie extravéhiculaire

Definition: An activity outside a spacecraft performed by an astronaut.

Example: A spacewalk can last several hours.

spacesuit

IPA: /ˈspeɪssuːt/
Pronunciation: SPACE-soot
French: combinaison spatiale

Definition: A protective suit worn by astronauts in space.

Example: A spacesuit provides oxygen and pressure.

life-support system

IPA: /ˈlaɪf səˌpɔːt ˌsɪstəm/
Pronunciation: life-suh-PORT SIS-tuhm
French: système de survie

Definition: A system providing air, water, pressure and temperature control for humans in space.

Example: Life-support systems are vital for crewed missions.

crewed mission

IPA: /ˌkruːd ˈmɪʃən/
Pronunciation: krood MISH-uhn
French: mission habitée

Definition: A mission carrying human beings.

Example: Crewed missions are expensive and risky.

uncrewed mission

IPA: /ˌʌnˈkruːd ˈmɪʃən/
Pronunciation: un-KROOD MISH-uhn
French: mission non habitée

Definition: A mission without humans on board.

Example: Uncrewed missions can explore dangerous environments.

human spaceflight

IPA: /ˌhjuːmən ˈspeɪsflaɪt/
Pronunciation: HYOO-muhn SPACE-flyte
French: vol spatial habité

Definition: Space travel involving human beings.

Example: Human spaceflight is often linked to national prestige.

space tourism

IPA: /ˈspeɪs ˌtʊərɪzəm/
Pronunciation: space TOOR-iz-uhm
French: tourisme spatial

Definition: Commercial travel into space for private individuals.

Example: Space tourism remains accessible only to the very wealthy.

commercial spaceflight

IPA: /kəˌmɜːʃəl ˈspeɪsflaɪt/
Pronunciation: kuh-MUR-shuhl SPACE-flyte
French: vol spatial commercial

Definition: Space travel or launch services provided by private companies.

Example: Commercial spaceflight has reduced launch costs.

private space company

IPA: /ˌpraɪvət ˈspeɪs ˌkʌmpəni/
Pronunciation: PRY-vuht space KUM-puh-nee
French: entreprise spatiale privée

Definition: A private firm involved in space activities.

Example: Private space companies now compete with state agencies.

NewSpace

IPA: /ˈnjuː speɪs/
Pronunciation: NYOO-space
French: NewSpace

Definition: The modern private-sector-driven space industry.

Example: NewSpace has changed launch markets and satellite deployment.

reusable rocket

IPA: /ˌriːˈjuːzəbəl ˈrɒkɪt/
Pronunciation: ree-YOO-zuh-buhl ROK-it
French: fusée réutilisable

Definition: A rocket designed to be launched, recovered and launched again.

Example: Reusable rockets can lower the cost of access to space.

launch cost

IPA: /ˈlɔːntʃ kɒst/
Pronunciation: launch kost
French: coût de lancement

Definition: The cost of sending a payload into space.

Example: Lower launch costs make more missions possible.

space industry

French: industrie spatiale

Definition: The economic sector producing space technology and services.

Example: The space industry includes satellites, rockets and data services.

space economy

IPA: /ˈspeɪs iˌkɒnəmi/

French: économie spatiale

Definition: Economic activity linked to space technology and services.

Example: Satellite constellations can provide global internet access.

IPA: /ˌmeɡə ˌkɒnstəˈleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: MEG-uh kon-stuh-LAY-shuhn
French: méga-constellation

Definition: A very large constellation containing hundreds or thousands of satellites.

Example: Mega-constellations raise concerns about space debris.

satellite internet

IPA: /ˈsætəlaɪt ˌɪntənet/
Pronunciation: SAT-uh-lyte IN-tuh-net
French: internet par satellite

Example: Satellite internet can connect remote areas.

French: observation de la Terre

French: télédétection

weather satellite

Pronunciation: gal-i-LAY-oh
French: Galileo

Pronunciation: kuh-myoo-ni-KAY-shuhn SAT-uh-lyte
French: satellite de communication

Example: Communication satellites connect distant regions.

space debris

IPA: /ˈspeɪs ˌdebriː/
Pronunciation: space DEB-ree
French: débris spatiaux

Definition: Defunct satellites, fragments and other objects orbiting Earth.

Example: Space debris can damage active satellites.

orbital debris

IPA: /ˌɔːbɪtəl ˈdebriː/
Pronunciation: OR-bi-tuhl DEB-ree
French: débris orbitaux

Definition: Debris orbiting Earth.

Example: Orbital debris is a growing safety concern.

space junk

IPA: /ˈspeɪs dʒʌŋk/
Pronunciation: space junk
French: déchets spatiaux

Definition: Informal term for space debris.

Example: Space junk can travel at extremely high speeds.

IPA: /ˈkeslə ˌsɪndrəʊm/
Pronunciation: KESS-luh SIN-drohm
French: syndrome de Kessler

Definition: A scenario where collisions create debris that causes more collisions in orbit.

Example: Kessler syndrome could make some orbits unusable.

collision avoidance

IPA: /kəˈlɪʒən əˌvɔɪdəns/
Pronunciation: kuh-LIZH-uhn uh-VOYD-uhns
French: évitement de collision

Definition: Actions taken to prevent objects in space from colliding.

Example: Satellites may need collision avoidance manoeuvres.

space traffic management

IPA: /ˈspeɪs ˈtræfɪk ˌmænɪdʒmənt/
Pronunciation: space TRAF-ik MAN-ij-muhnt
French: gestion du trafic spatial

Definition: Rules and systems used to manage the movement of objects in orbit.

Example: Space traffic management is becoming urgent.

space sustainability

IPA: /ˈspeɪs səˌsteɪnəˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: space suh-stay-nuh-BIL-uh-tee
French: durabilité spatiale

Definition: Ensuring space activities remain safe and usable for future generations.

Example: Space sustainability requires debris mitigation.

debris mitigation

IPA: /ˈdebriː ˌmɪtɪˈɡeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: DEB-ree mit-i-GAY-shuhn
French: atténuation des débris spatiaux

Definition: Measures to reduce the creation of space debris.

Example: Debris mitigation includes deorbiting old satellites.

deorbit

IPA: /diːˈɔːbɪt/
Pronunciation: dee-OR-bit
French: désorbiter

Definition: To remove an object from orbit, often so it burns up in the atmosphere.

Example: Satellites should deorbit at the end of their life.

space law

IPA: /ˈspeɪs lɔː/
Pronunciation: space law
French: droit spatial

Definition: The body of law governing activities in outer space.

Example: Space law must adapt to private companies.

C. Space debris, law, security and strategic autonomy

Outer Space Treaty

IPA: /ˌaʊtə ˈspeɪs ˌtriːti/
Pronunciation: OW-tuh space TREE-tee
French: Traité de l’espace

Definition: The 1967 treaty setting basic principles for peaceful use of outer space.

Example: The Outer Space Treaty bans national sovereignty claims in space.

peaceful use of space

IPA: /ˌpiːsfəl ˈjuːs əv ˈspeɪs/
Pronunciation: PEACE-fuhl yooss ov space
French: utilisation pacifique de l’espace

Definition: The principle that space should be used for peaceful purposes.

Example: Peaceful use of space is a central legal principle.

weaponisation of space

IPA: /ˌwepənaɪˈzeɪʃən əv ˈspeɪs/
Pronunciation: wep-uh-ny-ZAY-shuhn ov space
French: militarisation armée de l’espace

Definition: The deployment or use of weapons in or from space.

Example: The weaponisation of space could destabilise international security.

militarisation of space

IPA: /ˌmɪlɪtəraɪˈzeɪʃən əv ˈspeɪs/
Pronunciation: mil-i-tuh-ry-ZAY-shuhn ov space
French: militarisation de l’espace

Definition: The use of space for military purposes, such as surveillance or navigation.

Example: The militarisation of space has already begun through satellites.

anti-satellite weapon

IPA: /ˌænti ˈsætəlaɪt ˈwepən/
Pronunciation: AN-tee SAT-uh-lyte WEP-uhn
French: arme antisatellite

Definition: A weapon designed to disable or destroy satellites.

Example: Anti-satellite weapons can create dangerous debris.

ASAT

IPA: /ˈeɪsæt/
Pronunciation: AY-sat
French: ASAT ; arme antisatellite

Definition: Abbreviation for anti-satellite weapon or test.

Example: ASAT tests are controversial because they create debris.

space security

IPA: /ˈspeɪs sɪˌkjʊərəti/
Pronunciation: space si-KYOOR-uh-tee
French: sécurité spatiale

Definition: Protection of space systems from threats and conflict.

Example: Space security is essential for modern militaries.

dual-use technology

IPA: /ˌdjuːəl juːs tekˈnɒlədʒi/
Pronunciation: DYOO-uhl yooss tek-NOL-uh-jee
French: technologie à double usage

Definition: Technology usable for both civilian and military purposes.

Example: Many space technologies are dual-use technologies.

strategic autonomy

IPA: /strəˌtiːdʒɪk ɔːˈtɒnəmi/
Pronunciation: struh-TEE-jik aw-TON-uh-mee
French: autonomie stratégique

Example: Independent launch capacity supports strategic autonomy.

space sovereignty

IPA: /ˈspeɪs ˈsɒvrənti/
Pronunciation: space SOV-ruhn-tee
French: souveraineté spatiale

Definition: A state’s ability to control and secure its space capabilities.

Example: Space sovereignty depends on satellites, launchers and data.

cosmology

IPA: /kɒzˈmɒlədʒi/
Pronunciation: koz-MOL-uh-jee
French: cosmologie

Definition: The scientific study of the origin, structure and evolution of the universe.

Example: Cosmology asks how the universe began.

universe

IPA: /ˈjuːnɪvɜːs/
Pronunciation: YOO-ni-vurs
French: univers

Example: The universe contains billions of galaxies.

galaxy

IPA: /ˈɡæləksi/
Pronunciation: GAL-uhk-see

black hole

IPA: /ˌblæk ˈhəʊl/
