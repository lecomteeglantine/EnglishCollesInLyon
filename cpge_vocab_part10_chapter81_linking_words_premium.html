Part 10 — Concours Toolkit | Chapter 81 — Linking Words
Essential connectors for essays, syntheses, khôlles and press article analysis.

Chapter objective

This premium toolkit chapter gives CPGE students the linking words and discourse markers needed to build clear, elegant and logical English essays: ordering ideas, adding arguments, comparing, contrasting, conceding, nuancing, explaining causes and consequences, giving examples, reformulating and concluding.

Linking WordsEssay WritingArgumentationConcessionConcours ToolkitKhôlles

How to use this chapter

For essaysUse varied connectors: moreover, nevertheless, whereas, to some extent, as a result, for instance, in other words, on balance.
For khôllesUse them orally to structure your answer clearly: “First of all...”, “However...”, “This is why...”, “To put it simply...”
WarningDo not overuse moreover and however. A strong essay needs logical variety, not connector decoration.

A. Ordering ideas and structuring paragraphs

firstly

IPA: /ˈfɜːstli/
Pronunciation: FURST-lee
French: premièrement

Definition: Used to introduce the first point in a sequence.

Example: Firstly, the policy may reduce inequality.

first of all

IPA: /ˌfɜːst əv ˈɔːl/
Pronunciation: first-ov-AWL
French: tout d’abord

Definition: Used to introduce the first and often most basic point.

Example: First of all, the article highlights a major social divide.

to begin with

IPA: /tə bɪˈɡɪn wɪð/
Pronunciation: tuh bi-GIN with
French: pour commencer

Definition: Used to open an argument or explanation.

Example: To begin with, the figures reveal a worrying trend.

at the outset

IPA: /æt ði ˈaʊtset/
Pronunciation: at thee OWT-set
French: d’emblée ; au départ

Definition: At the beginning; used in formal writing.

Example: At the outset, it is important to define populism.

in the first place

IPA: /ɪn ðə ˈfɜːst pleɪs/
Pronunciation: in thuh FIRST place
French: en premier lieu

Definition: Used to introduce an initial reason or argument.

Example: In the first place, democracy requires trust.

secondly

IPA: /ˈsekəndli/
Pronunciation: SEK-uhnd-lee
French: deuxièmement

Definition: Used to introduce a second point.

Example: Secondly, the reform may be costly.

thirdly

IPA: /ˈθɜːdli/
Pronunciation: THURD-lee
French: troisièmement

Definition: Used to introduce a third point.

Example: Thirdly, public opinion remains divided.

finally

IPA: /ˈfaɪnəli/
Pronunciation: FY-nuh-lee
French: enfin

Definition: Used to introduce the last point in a sequence.

Example: Finally, the article raises ethical questions.

lastly

IPA: /ˈlɑːstli/
Pronunciation: LAHST-lee
French: en dernier lieu

Definition: Used to introduce the last element in a list.

Example: Lastly, the measure could deepen regional inequalities.

above all

IPA: /əˌbʌv ˈɔːl/
Pronunciation: uh-BUV awl
French: surtout ; avant tout

Definition: Most importantly.

Example: Above all, the crisis is political.

more importantly

IPA: /mɔːr ɪmˈpɔːtəntli/
Pronunciation: more im-POR-tuhnt-lee
French: plus important encore

Definition: Used to introduce a more significant point.

Example: More importantly, the law may undermine civil liberties.

significantly

IPA: /sɪɡˈnɪfɪkəntli/
Pronunciation: sig-NIF-i-kuhnt-lee
French: de manière significative

Definition: In a meaningful or important way.

Example: Significantly, young voters reject both major parties.

notably

IPA: /ˈnəʊtəbli/
Pronunciation: NOH-tuh-blee
French: notamment

Definition: Used to mention something important or remarkable.

Example: Notably, the article focuses on rural voters.

in particular

IPA: /ɪn pəˈtɪkjʊlə/
Pronunciation: in puh-TIK-yuh-luh
French: en particulier

Definition: Especially; specifically.

Example: This affects young people in particular.

particularly

IPA: /pəˈtɪkjʊləli/
Pronunciation: puh-TIK-yuh-luh-lee
French: particulièrement

Definition: Especially or to a great degree.

Example: This issue is particularly relevant to democracies.

especially

IPA: /ɪˈspeʃəli/
Pronunciation: i-SPESH-uh-lee
French: surtout ; particulièrement

Definition: More than usual or more than others.

Example: The policy affects poorer households especially.

moreover

IPA: /mɔːrˈəʊvə/
Pronunciation: mor-OH-vuh
French: de plus ; qui plus est

Definition: In addition; used to add a strong point.

Example: Moreover, the reform lacks public support.

Definition: The logical consequence is that.

Example: It follows that reform must be gradual.

we may conclude that

IPA: /wi meɪ kənˈkluːd ðæt/
Pronunciation: wee may kuhn-KLOOD that
French: nous pouvons conclure que

Definition: A formal way to introduce a conclusion.

Example: We may conclude that democracy depends on informed citizens.

this suggests that

IPA: /ðɪs səˈdʒests ðæt/
Pronunciation: this suh-JESTS that
French: cela suggère que

Definition: This indicates a likely conclusion.

Example: This suggests that voters want change.

this points to

IPA: /ðɪs pɔɪnts tə/
Pronunciation: this points tuh
French: cela indique ; cela renvoie à

Definition: This indicates or suggests.

Example: This points to a deeper crisis of representation.

the evidence suggests

IPA: /ði ˈevɪdəns səˈdʒests/
Pronunciation: the EV-i-duhns suh-JESTS
French: les éléments suggèrent

Definition: The available facts point towards a conclusion.

Example: The evidence suggests that inequality has widened.

on balance

IPA: /ɒn ˈbæləns/
Pronunciation: on BAL-uhns
French: tout bien considéré

Definition: After weighing both sides.

Example: On balance, the benefits outweigh the risks.
