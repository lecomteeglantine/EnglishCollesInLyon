Chapter 61 — Reporting Verbs
B2 → C1 grammar for precise source attribution, stance, synthesis and sophisticated academic analysis.

Chapter objective

This premium grammar chapter helps CPGE students master reporting verbs: neutral reporting, argument, evidence, doubt, agreement, criticism, advice, accusation, grammatical patterns and advanced use in summaries, essays and khôlles.

The golden rule

Choose the verb according to meaning, not variety alone.
state = neutral · argue = reasoned position · claim = distance · acknowledge = concession · demonstrate = strong evidence
That-clauseargue / claim / acknowledge / state that...
Infinitivepromise to... / urge someone to... / warn someone not to...
Gerund or prepositionadmit doing / accuse someone of doing / warn against doing

A. Lesson — Why reporting verbs matter

Reporting verb

Rule / explanation: A reporting verb shows not only that someone spoke or wrote, but also the attitude, force and function of the message.

Example / answer: The author argues that the reform has deepened inequality.

Meaning and stance

Rule / explanation: Choosing claim, admit, warn or demonstrate changes how the reader interprets the reported statement.

Example / answer: The government claims that the policy has succeeded.

Source distance

Rule / explanation: Some verbs are neutral, while others signal doubt, agreement, criticism or certainty.

Example / answer: The article alleges that officials concealed the figures.

CPGE relevance

Rule / explanation: Precise reporting verbs are essential for summaries, source comparison, essays, oral commentary and khôlles.

Example / answer: The journalist acknowledges the policy’s benefits but questions its long-term sustainability.

B. Neutral and descriptive reporting verbs

Say

Rule / explanation: Say is neutral and common, but often too vague for advanced academic writing.

Example / answer: The minister said that further reforms were necessary.

State

Rule / explanation: State presents information clearly and formally.

Example / answer: The report states that regional inequality has increased.

Explain

Rule / explanation: Explain introduces clarification, reasons or mechanisms.

Example / answer: The author explains why public confidence has declined.

Report

Rule / explanation: Report presents information, findings or events without necessarily endorsing them.

Example / answer: The newspaper reports that negotiations have resumed.

Note / observe

Rule / explanation: Note and observe draw attention to a fact or pattern.

Example / answer: The researcher notes that younger voters are more politically volatile.

C. Verbs for argument and interpretation

Argue

Rule / explanation: Argue introduces a reasoned position supported by evidence.

Example / answer: The author argues that stronger regulation would protect consumers.

Contend

Rule / explanation: Contend is formal and often introduces a debatable position.

Example / answer: The editorial contends that the reform will weaken democratic accountability.

Maintain

Rule / explanation: Maintain means continue to assert a position, often despite opposition.

Example / answer: The minister maintains that the policy remains effective.

Suggest

Rule / explanation: Suggest presents an interpretation cautiously rather than as proven fact.

Example / answer: The figures suggest that the recovery remains fragile.

Imply

Rule / explanation: Imply reports a meaning that is indirect rather than explicitly stated.

Example / answer: The article implies that the government anticipated the crisis.

D. Verbs expressing certainty and evidence

Demonstrate

Rule / explanation: Demonstrate indicates that evidence strongly supports a conclusion.

Example / answer: The data demonstrate that regional disparities have widened.

Show

Rule / explanation: Show is clear and flexible, but less formal than demonstrate.

Example / answer: The survey shows that trust has fallen sharply.

Establish

Rule / explanation: Establish means prove or confirm something through evidence.

Example / answer: The inquiry established that officials had ignored repeated warnings.

Confirm

Rule / explanation: Confirm supports or verifies a previous claim or suspicion.

Example / answer: The new figures confirm that inflation is slowing.

Prove

Rule / explanation: Use prove only when the evidence is genuinely conclusive.

Example / answer: The study does not prove that regulation causes lower investment.

E. Verbs expressing doubt, uncertainty or distance

Claim

Rule / explanation: Claim reports an assertion whose truth the reporter may not fully accept.

Example / answer: The company claims that no customer data were compromised.

Allege

Rule / explanation: Allege reports a serious but unproven accusation.

Example / answer: The newspaper alleges that officials concealed key evidence.

Purport

Rule / explanation: Purport means claim or appear to do something, often with scepticism.

Example / answer: The report purports to offer an impartial analysis.

Speculate

Rule / explanation: Speculate introduces an uncertain hypothesis.

Example / answer: Commentators speculate that an early election may be called.

Question

Rule / explanation: Question challenges the validity, wisdom or certainty of a claim.

Example / answer: The author questions whether the reform can deliver lasting change.

F. Verbs of agreement and acceptance

Agree

Rule / explanation: Agree reports shared opinion or acceptance.

Example / answer: Both authors agree that public trust has declined.

Concur

Rule / explanation: Concur is a formal alternative to agree.

Example / answer: Several experts concur that the timetable is unrealistic.

Accept

Rule / explanation: Accept indicates recognition of a fact, proposal or argument.

Example / answer: The minister accepts that implementation has been uneven.

Acknowledge

Rule / explanation: Acknowledge recognises a fact, limitation or opposing point.

Example / answer: The article acknowledges that the policy created some jobs.

Concede

Rule / explanation: Concede admits a point reluctantly or as part of a counterargument.

Example / answer: The author concedes that regulation may slow innovation in the short term.

G. Verbs of disagreement, rejection and criticism

Disagree

Rule / explanation: Disagree states that two positions do not coincide.

Example / answer: The second author disagrees with the government’s interpretation.

Reject

Rule / explanation: Reject means refuse to accept a claim, proposal or explanation.

Example / answer: The committee rejected the argument that the crisis was unavoidable.

Dispute

Rule / explanation: Dispute challenges the truth or accuracy of a statement.

Example / answer: The opposition disputes the official unemployment figures.

Challenge

Rule / explanation: Challenge questions the assumptions, evidence or conclusions of another position.

Example / answer: The article challenges the idea that markets regulate themselves.

Criticise

Rule / explanation: Criticise expresses negative evaluation.

Example / answer: The journalist criticises the government for delaying action.

H. Verbs of admission and denial

Admit

Rule / explanation: Admit acknowledges an unwelcome fact or responsibility.

Example / answer: The minister admitted that serious mistakes had been made.

Confess

Rule / explanation: Confess is stronger and often concerns wrongdoing or personal responsibility.

Example / answer: The official confessed to leaking the document.

Deny

Rule / explanation: Deny rejects an accusation or statement.

Example / answer: The company denied misleading investors.

Refute

Rule / explanation: Refute means prove an argument or claim to be false, not merely disagree with it.

Example / answer: The new evidence refutes the allegation that the data were fabricated.

Disclaim

Rule / explanation: Disclaim formally denies responsibility, knowledge or ownership.

Example / answer: The agency disclaimed responsibility for the publication delay.

I. Verbs of warning, advice and recommendation

Warn

Rule / explanation: Warn alerts someone to danger or an undesirable consequence.

Example / answer: Experts warned that further cuts could damage public services.

Advise

Rule / explanation: Advise recommends a course of action to a person or institution.

Example / answer: The committee advised the government to delay implementation.

Urge

Rule / explanation: Urge strongly encourages immediate action.

Example / answer: The report urges ministers to increase regional funding.

Recommend

Rule / explanation: Recommend proposes what should be done.

Example / answer: The inquiry recommends establishing an independent regulator.

Caution

Rule / explanation: Caution warns against excessive confidence or risky action.

Example / answer: The author cautions against drawing conclusions from limited data.

J. Verbs of demand, order and obligation

Demand

Rule / explanation: Demand expresses a strong requirement or public insistence.

Example / answer: Opposition parties demanded that the report be published.

Order

Rule / explanation: Order gives an authoritative command.

Example / answer: The court ordered the ministry to release the documents.

Instruct

Rule / explanation: Instruct gives an official or practical direction.

Example / answer: Officials were instructed not to disclose confidential information.

Require

Rule / explanation: Require states that something is necessary under a rule, condition or system.

Example / answer: The law requires companies to report major data breaches.

Insist

Rule / explanation: Insist may express either forceful assertion or a strong demand.

Example / answer: The minister insisted that the inquiry remain independent.

K. Verbs of promise, intention and commitment

Promise

Rule / explanation: Promise commits the speaker to a future action.

Example / answer: The government promised to increase healthcare funding.

Pledge

Rule / explanation: Pledge is a formal and public promise.

Example / answer: The party pledged to reduce emissions by 40 per cent.

Undertake

Rule / explanation: Undertake formally commits someone to carrying out an action.

Example / answer: The agency undertook to publish annual progress reports.

Vow

Rule / explanation: Vow is a strong and often emotional promise.

Example / answer: The minister vowed to restore public confidence.

Guarantee

Rule / explanation: Guarantee promises a result with a high level of certainty.

Example / answer: The policy cannot guarantee equal access across all regions.

L. Verbs of accusation and responsibility

Accuse someone of

Rule / explanation: Use accuse + object + of + noun or gerund.

Example / answer: The opposition accused the government of hiding the figures.

Blame someone for

Rule / explanation: Use blame + object + for + noun or gerund.

Example / answer: Critics blamed ministers for ignoring local concerns.

Hold someone responsible for

Rule / explanation: Use this formal structure to assign responsibility.

Example / answer: The inquiry held senior officials responsible for the failure.

Charge someone with

Rule / explanation: Use charge + object + with for a formal criminal accusation.

Example / answer: The executive was charged with fraud.

Condemn

Rule / explanation: Condemn expresses strong public criticism of an action or policy.

Example / answer: Human rights groups condemned the decision as discriminatory.

M. Grammar patterns: that-clauses

Verb + that-clause

Rule / explanation: Many reporting verbs are followed by that + subject + verb.

Example / answer: The author argues that the reform is unsustainable.

Optional that

Rule / explanation: That can often be omitted after common verbs, but retaining it may improve clarity in formal writing.

Example / answer: The report states that costs have increased.

Subjunctive after demands

Rule / explanation: After demand, recommend, propose, insist and suggest, formal English may use the base form.

Example / answer: The committee recommended that the law be amended.

Should alternative

Rule / explanation: British English often uses should + base verb after these verbs.

Example / answer: The committee recommended that the law should be amended.

Tense choice

Rule / explanation: The tense in the that-clause reflects time, backshift and current validity.

Example / answer: The author argued that the policy had failed.

N. Grammar patterns: infinitives

Verb + to-infinitive

Rule / explanation: Some verbs are followed directly by to + base verb.

Example / answer: The government promised to review the policy.

Verb + object + to-infinitive

Rule / explanation: Some verbs require an object before the infinitive.

Example / answer: The committee advised ministers to delay the vote.

Common direct-infinitive verbs

Rule / explanation: Agree, offer, promise, refuse, threaten and undertake commonly take a to-infinitive.

Example / answer: The company refused to disclose the figures.

Common object-infinitive verbs

Rule / explanation: Advise, ask, encourage, instruct, order, persuade, remind, tell, urge and warn commonly take object + to-infinitive.

Example / answer: Experts urged the government to act.

Negative infinitive

Rule / explanation: Place not before the infinitive.

Example / answer: Officials were instructed not to publish the data.

O. Grammar patterns: gerunds and prepositions

Verb + gerund

Rule / explanation: Admit, deny, mention, propose, recommend and suggest may be followed by a gerund.

Example / answer: The minister admitted misleading Parliament.

Preposition + gerund

Rule / explanation: When a reporting pattern contains a preposition, use a noun or gerund after it.

Example / answer: The company apologised for delaying the announcement.

Accuse of

Rule / explanation: Accuse requires an object followed by of.

Example / answer: Critics accused the agency of suppressing evidence.

Insist on

Rule / explanation: Insist on + noun or gerund expresses a strong demand.

Example / answer: The committee insisted on receiving the full report.

Warn against

Rule / explanation: Warn against + gerund advises someone not to take an action.

Example / answer: Experts warned against cutting investment.

P. Same verb, different pattern

Advise

Rule / explanation: Advise may take object + to-infinitive or a gerund in impersonal guidance.

Example / answer: The report advised the government to act. / The report advises investing early.

Recommend

Rule / explanation: Recommend can take a gerund, noun or that-clause, but not normally object + to-infinitive.

Example / answer: The inquiry recommended increasing funding.

Suggest

Rule / explanation: Suggest can take a gerund or that-clause, but not suggest someone to do something.

Example / answer: She suggested postponing the vote.

Insist

Rule / explanation: Insist that reports an assertion or demand; insist on introduces a required action.

Example / answer: He insisted that the figures were accurate. / He insisted on seeing the report.

Warn

Rule / explanation: Warn may take that-clause, object + to-infinitive, object + against + gerund or object + of + noun.

Example / answer: Experts warned that costs could rise.

Q. Reporting verbs in article summaries

Opening the summary

Rule / explanation: Choose a verb that accurately represents the author’s central move.

Example / answer: The article argues that regional inequality threatens political stability.

Developing the reasoning

Rule / explanation: Use explain, identify, emphasise and illustrate to map the progression.

Example / answer: The author first identifies the causes, then illustrates their social consequences.

Reporting caution

Rule / explanation: Use suggest, indicate or appear to when the source is tentative.

Example / answer: The evidence suggests that the recovery remains uneven.

Reporting criticism

Rule / explanation: Use challenge, criticise, condemn or question according to intensity.

Example / answer: The journalist questions the government’s optimistic interpretation.

Concluding move

Rule / explanation: Use conclude, recommend, call for or warn to report the final position.

Example / answer: The article concludes by calling for stronger regulation.

R. Reporting verbs in source comparison

Agreement

Rule / explanation: Use agree, concur, echo and support to show common ground.

Example / answer: The second article echoes the first author’s concern about inequality.

Contrast

Rule / explanation: Use challenge, reject, dispute and counter to show opposition.

Example / answer: The second author counters the claim that regulation restricts innovation.

Different emphasis

Rule / explanation: Use focus on, highlight, stress and prioritise.

Example / answer: While the first article stresses efficiency, the second prioritises fairness.

Qualification

Rule / explanation: Use acknowledge, concede and partly accept for nuanced comparison.

Example / answer: The second author concedes that the reform reduced costs but disputes its social benefits.

Synthesis

Rule / explanation: Combine reporting verbs to build a coherent multi-source paragraph.

Example / answer: One text argues that the policy promotes growth, whereas the other warns that it may deepen inequality.

S. Reporting verbs in CPGE oral commentary

Neutral introduction

Rule / explanation: Use the article states, reports or examines when presenting content.

Example / answer: The article examines the political consequences of rising inequality.

Problematic framing

Rule / explanation: Use raises the question of or invites us to consider.

Example / answer: The text raises the question of whether economic growth can remain socially sustainable.

Analytical response

Rule / explanation: Use suggests, reveals, exposes and illustrates to interpret evidence.

Example / answer: The example reveals a widening gap between national policy and local reality.

Evaluation

Rule / explanation: Use convincingly demonstrates, arguably overlooks or fails to establish.

Example / answer: The author convincingly demonstrates the urgency of reform but overlooks its cost.

Personal response

Rule / explanation: Use I would argue, I would question or one may contend for controlled evaluation.

Example / answer: I would argue that the article underestimates the role of institutional trust.

T. Strength scale and register

Tentative

Rule / explanation: Suggest, indicate, imply and appear to express caution.

Example / answer: The figures suggest that confidence may be recovering.

Neutral

Rule / explanation: State, explain, report and note describe information without strong judgement.

Example / answer: The report notes that regional differences persist.

Assertive

Rule / explanation: Argue, maintain, insist and contend express a strong position.

Example / answer: The author maintains that reform is unavoidable.

Evidential

Rule / explanation: Show, demonstrate, establish and confirm indicate evidential support.

Example / answer: The data demonstrate a clear regional divide.

Sceptical

Rule / explanation: Claim, allege and purport create distance from the reported statement.

Example / answer: The company claims that the breach caused no harm.

U. Typical mistakes by French speakers

Say vs tell

Rule / explanation: Tell requires an object; say does not.

Example / answer: Wrong: She told that the policy failed. Correct: She said that... / She told us that...

Suggest someone to

Rule / explanation: Do not use suggest + object + to-infinitive.

Example / answer: Correct: She suggested that they act / suggested acting.

Explain someone

Rule / explanation: Use explain something to someone, not explain someone something.

Example / answer: Correct: She explained the policy to journalists.

Discuss about

Rule / explanation: Discuss takes a direct object without about.

Example / answer: Correct: The article discusses inequality.

Accuse for

Rule / explanation: Use accuse someone of, not accuse someone for.

Example / answer: Correct: They accused officials of hiding evidence.

Claim as fact

Rule / explanation: Do not use demonstrate or prove when the source merely asserts something.

Example / answer: Use claim or argue if the evidence is contested.

V. Exercises

1. Choose a neutral verb

Rule / explanation: The report ___ that unemployment has fallen. (states / accuses)

Example / answer: The report states that unemployment has fallen.

2. Choose a distancing verb

Rule / explanation: The company ___ that no data were lost, but the claim remains unverified.

Example / answer: The company claims that no data were lost.

3. Correct the pattern

Rule / explanation: The committee suggested the government to delay the vote.

Example / answer: The committee suggested that the government delay the vote. / The committee suggested delaying the vote.

4. Use accuse

Rule / explanation: The opposition said the government had hidden the figures.

Example / answer: The opposition accused the government of hiding the figures.

5. Use an object + infinitive

Rule / explanation: The experts said to the government: “Act immediately.”

Example / answer: The experts urged the government to act immediately.

6. Choose the most precise verb

Rule / explanation: The author accepts a weakness in her own argument.

Example / answer: The author acknowledges / concedes a weakness in her own argument.

7. Translate

Rule / explanation: L’article soutient que la réforme est nécessaire, mais reconnaît que sa mise en œuvre a été médiocre.

Example / answer: The article argues that the reform is necessary but acknowledges that its implementation has been poor.

8. Mini concours challenge

Rule / explanation: Write a paragraph using argue, claim, acknowledge, warn and demonstrate.

Example / answer: Example: The government argues that the reform has improved efficiency and claims that regional access is now more equal. However, the report acknowledges that implementation remains uneven and warns that further cuts could deepen existing disparities. The available data demonstrate that rural communities have benefited far less than urban areas.
