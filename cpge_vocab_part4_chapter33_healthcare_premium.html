Essential vocabulary for khôlles, essays, press reviews and competitive exams.

Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse healthcare: public and private systems, the NHS, insurance, hospitals, prevention, public health, mental health, inequalities, ageing populations, medical innovation, ethics and the pressure placed on healthcare systems by rising costs and social change.

How to use this chapter

For press articlesFocus on terms such as universal healthcare, NHS, health insurance, waiting lists, preventive medicine, mental health crisis, health inequalities and ageing population.
For oral examsUse this vocabulary to explain the tension between healthcare as a human right, healthcare as a cost, and healthcare as a system shaped by inequality, technology and demographics.
For essaysMemorise the collocations: to expand access to care, to tackle health inequalities, to reduce waiting times, to strengthen public health, to relieve pressure on the NHS.

A. Core healthcare systems and funding

healthcare

IPA: /ˈhelθkeə/
Pronunciation: HELTH-care
French: soins de santé ; système de santé

Definition: The organised provision of medical services to prevent, diagnose and treat illness.

Example: Healthcare is often considered a basic public good.

healthcare system

IPA: /ˈhelθkeə ˌsɪstəm/
Pronunciation: HELTH-care SIS-tuhm
French: système de santé

Definition: The institutions, services and funding arrangements that provide medical care.

Example: Every healthcare system must balance access, quality and cost.

health system

IPA: /ˈhelθ ˌsɪstəm/
Pronunciation: helth SIS-tuhm
French: système de santé

Definition: The broader network of organisations, workers and policies that affect health.

Example: A strong health system includes prevention, hospitals and public health.

public health

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk ˈhelθ/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik helth
French: santé publique

Definition: The protection and improvement of health at population level.

Example: Public health focuses on prevention rather than only treatment.

universal healthcare

IPA: /ˌjuːnɪˈvɜːsəl ˈhelθkeə/
Pronunciation: yoo-ni-VUR-suhl HELTH-care
French: couverture santé universelle

Definition: A system in which everyone has access to healthcare regardless of income.

Example: Universal healthcare aims to prevent medical care from depending on wealth.

universal coverage

IPA: /ˌjuːnɪˈvɜːsəl ˈkʌvərɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: yoo-ni-VUR-suhl KUV-uh-rij
French: couverture universelle

Definition: Health coverage available to all members of a population.

Example: Universal coverage is a key goal of many healthcare reforms.

healthcare access

IPA: /ˈhelθkeə ˌækses/
Pronunciation: HELTH-care AK-sess
French: accès aux soins

Definition: The ability to obtain needed medical services.

Example: Healthcare access remains unequal between regions and social groups.

access to care

IPA: /ˌækses tə ˈkeə/
Pronunciation: AK-sess tuh care
French: accès aux soins

Definition: The ability to receive medical treatment, advice or support when needed.

Example: Rural patients may have limited access to care.

healthcare coverage

IPA: /ˈhelθkeə ˌkʌvərɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: HELTH-care KUV-uh-rij
French: couverture santé

Definition: Insurance or public protection allowing people to receive healthcare.

Example: Millions of Americans still struggle with insufficient healthcare coverage.

health insurance

IPA: /ˈhelθ ɪnˌʃʊərəns/
Pronunciation: helth in-SHOOR-uhns
French: assurance maladie ; assurance santé

Definition: Insurance that helps pay for medical care.

Example: Health insurance can reduce the risk of catastrophic medical bills.

private health insurance

IPA: /ˌpraɪvət helθ ɪnˈʃʊərəns/
Pronunciation: PRY-vuht helth in-SHOOR-uhns
French: assurance santé privée

Definition: Health insurance bought privately or provided by an employer.

Example: Private health insurance can create unequal access to faster care.

public healthcare

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk ˈhelθkeə/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik HELTH-care
French: système de santé public

Definition: Healthcare funded or provided by the state.

Example: Public healthcare is usually funded through taxation or social insurance.

private healthcare

IPA: /ˌpraɪvət ˈhelθkeə/
Pronunciation: PRY-vuht HELTH-care
French: soins privés ; système de santé privé

Definition: Healthcare funded by private payment or insurance.

Example: Private healthcare can reduce waiting times for those who can afford it.

mixed healthcare system

IPA: /ˌmɪkst ˈhelθkeə ˌsɪstəm/
Pronunciation: mikst HELTH-care SIS-tuhm
French: système de santé mixte

Definition: A system combining public and private healthcare provision or funding.

Example: Most countries have a mixed healthcare system.

National Health Service

IPA: /ˌnæʃənəl ˈhelθ ˌsɜːvɪs/
Pronunciation: NASH-uh-nuhl helth SUR-vis
French: National Health Service ; NHS

Definition: The UK’s publicly funded healthcare system, commonly called the NHS.

Example: The National Health Service is central to the British welfare state.

NHS

IPA: /ˌen eɪtʃ ˈes/
Pronunciation: en-aych-ess
French: NHS

Definition: Abbreviation for the UK National Health Service.

Example: The NHS faces pressure from ageing, staff shortages and waiting lists.

Medicare

IPA: /ˈmedɪkeə/
Pronunciation: MED-i-care
French: Medicare

Definition: A US federal health insurance programme mainly for older people.

Example: Medicare provides health coverage for many elderly Americans.

Medicaid

IPA: /ˈmedɪkeɪd/
Pronunciation: MED-i-kayd
French: Medicaid

Definition: A US public health insurance programme for low-income people.

Example: Medicaid helps many low-income families access care.

single-payer system

IPA: /ˌsɪŋɡəl ˈpeɪə ˌsɪstəm/
Pronunciation: SING-guhl PAY-uh SIS-tuhm
French: système à payeur unique

Definition: A healthcare system where one public body pays for healthcare costs.

Example: Supporters of a single-payer system argue it reduces administrative costs.

IPA: /ˌaʊt əv ˈpɒkɪt ɪkˌspensɪz/
Pronunciation: owt ov POK-it ik-SPEN-siz
French: reste à charge ; dépenses directes

Definition: Medical costs paid directly by patients.

Example: High out-of-pocket expenses can discourage people from seeking care.

co-payment

IPA: /ˌkəʊ ˈpeɪmənt/
Pronunciation: koh-PAY-muhnt
French: ticket modérateur ; quote-part

Definition: A fixed amount paid by a patient for a medical service.

Example: Co-payments may make patients delay treatment.

deductible

IPA: /dɪˈdʌktəbəl/
Pronunciation: di-DUK-tuh-buhl
French: franchise

Definition: The amount a patient must pay before insurance starts covering costs.

Example: High deductibles can make insured patients effectively underinsured.

medical bill

IPA: /ˈmedɪkəl bɪl/
Pronunciation: MED-i-kuhl bil
French: facture médicale

Definition: A charge for medical treatment or services.

Example: Medical bills are a major cause of debt in the United States.

medical debt

IPA: /ˈmedɪkəl det/
Pronunciation: MED-i-kuhl det
French: dette médicale

Definition: Debt caused by the cost of healthcare.

Example: Medical debt can push families into poverty.

uninsured

IPA: /ˌʌnɪnˈʃʊəd/
Pronunciation: un-in-SHOORD
French: non assuré

Definition: Not covered by health insurance.

Example: Uninsured patients may avoid treatment because of cost.

underinsured

IPA: /ˌʌndərɪnˈʃʊəd/
Pronunciation: UN-duh-rin-SHOORD
French: sous-assuré

Definition: Having insurance that does not cover enough medical costs.

Example: Underinsured patients can still face unaffordable bills.

healthcare affordability

IPA: /ˈhelθkeə əˌfɔːdəˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: HELTH-care uh-FOR-duh-BIL-uh-tee
French: accessibilité financière des soins

Definition: The extent to which people can afford healthcare.

Example: Healthcare affordability is a major political issue.

healthcare funding

IPA: /ˈhelθkeə ˌfʌndɪŋ/
Pronunciation: HELTH-care FUN-ding
French: financement de la santé

Definition: The money used to pay for healthcare services.

Example: Healthcare funding is under pressure in ageing societies.

IPA: /ˈtækspeɪə ˌfʌndɪd ˈhelθkeə/
Pronunciation: TAKS-pay-uh FUN-did HELTH-care
French: santé financée par l’impôt

Definition: Healthcare paid for through taxes.

Example: The NHS is largely a taxpayer-funded healthcare system.

healthcare expenditure

IPA: /ˈhelθkeə ɪkˌspendɪtʃə/
Pronunciation: HELTH-care ik-SPEN-di-chuh
French: dépenses de santé

Definition: Money spent on healthcare by government, insurers or individuals.

Example: Healthcare expenditure rises as populations age.

public expenditure

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk ɪkˈspendɪtʃə/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik ik-SPEN-di-chuh
French: dépenses publiques

Definition: Government spending on services, benefits and infrastructure.

Example: Healthcare is a major part of public expenditure.

budget cuts

IPA: /ˈbʌdʒɪt kʌts/
Pronunciation: BUJ-it kuts
French: coupes budgétaires

Definition: Reductions in planned public spending.

Example: Budget cuts can weaken hospitals and community care.

healthcare reform

IPA: /ˈhelθkeə rɪˌfɔːm/
Pronunciation: HELTH-care ri-FORM
French: réforme du système de santé

Definition: Changes designed to improve how healthcare is funded, organised or delivered.

Example: Healthcare reform often triggers fierce political debate.

primary care

IPA: /ˌpraɪməri ˈkeə/
Pronunciation: PRY-muh-ree care
French: soins primaires

Definition: First-contact healthcare, usually provided by GPs, family doctors or clinics.

Example: Strong primary care can reduce pressure on hospitals.

secondary care

IPA: /ˌsekəndəri ˈkeə/
Pronunciation: SEK-uhn-duh-ree care
French: soins secondaires

Definition: Specialist care usually accessed after referral from primary care.

Example: Secondary care includes many hospital-based services.
