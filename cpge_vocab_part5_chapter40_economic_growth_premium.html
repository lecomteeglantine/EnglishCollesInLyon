Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse economic growth: GDP, productivity, innovation, investment, infrastructure, development, living standards, demand, macroeconomic policy, inequality, green growth and the debate over whether growth should remain the main goal of public policy.

How to use this chapter

For press articlesFocus on terms such as GDP, productivity, investment, living standards, fiscal policy, industrial policy, green growth, degrowth and decoupling.
For oral examsUse this vocabulary to explain why growth matters, why it can be uneven, and why GDP alone does not capture inequality, well-being or environmental limits.
For essaysMemorise the collocations: to boost growth, to raise productivity, to invest in infrastructure, to improve living standards, to decouple growth from emissions.

A. Core growth vocabulary and business cycles

economic growth

IPA: /ˌiːkəˈnɒmɪk ɡrəʊθ/
Pronunciation: ee-kuh-NOM-ik growth
French: croissance économique

Definition: An increase in the production of goods and services in an economy.

Example: Economic growth is often used as a key measure of national success.

growth rate

IPA: /ˈɡrəʊθ reɪt/
Pronunciation: growth rayt
French: taux de croissance

Definition: The speed at which an economy, output or indicator increases.

Example: A low growth rate can make debt harder to manage.

GDP

IPA: /ˌdʒiː diː ˈpiː/
Pronunciation: jee-dee-pee
French: PIB

Definition: Gross Domestic Product: the total value of goods and services produced in a country.

Example: GDP is useful, but it does not measure well-being perfectly.

gross domestic product

IPA: /ˌɡrəʊs dəˌmestɪk ˈprɒdʌkt/
Pronunciation: grohss duh-MES-tik PROD-ukt
French: produit intérieur brut

Definition: The total value of goods and services produced within a country.

Example: Gross domestic product can rise even when inequality worsens.

GDP per capita

IPA: /ˌdʒiː diː piː pə ˈkæpɪtə/
Pronunciation: jee-dee-pee puh KAP-i-tuh
French: PIB par habitant

Definition: GDP divided by the population, used as an average income measure.

Example: GDP per capita is often used to compare living standards.

real GDP

IPA: /ˌrɪəl ˌdʒiː diː ˈpiː/
Pronunciation: REE-uhl jee-dee-pee
French: PIB réel

Definition: GDP adjusted for inflation.

Example: Real GDP shows whether output has grown after inflation.

nominal GDP

IPA: /ˌnɒmɪnəl ˌdʒiː diː ˈpiː/
Pronunciation: NOM-i-nuhl jee-dee-pee
French: PIB nominal

Definition: GDP measured in current prices, not adjusted for inflation.

Example: Nominal GDP can rise simply because prices rise.

potential growth

IPA: /pəˌtenʃəl ˈɡrəʊθ/
Pronunciation: puh-TEN-shuhl growth
French: croissance potentielle

Definition: The maximum sustainable growth rate of an economy without creating inflation.

Example: Potential growth depends on labour, capital and productivity.

sustainable growth

IPA: /səˌsteɪnəbəl ˈɡrəʊθ/
Pronunciation: suh-STAY-nuh-buhl growth
French: croissance durable

Definition: Growth that can continue without damaging the economy, society or environment.

Example: Sustainable growth should not destroy natural resources.

inclusive growth

IPA: /ɪnˌkluːsɪv ˈɡrəʊθ/
Pronunciation: in-KLOO-siv growth
French: croissance inclusive

Definition: Growth that benefits a broad range of people, not only elites.

Example: Inclusive growth aims to reduce inequality while expanding the economy.

green growth

IPA: /ˌɡriːn ˈɡrəʊθ/
Pronunciation: green growth
French: croissance verte

Definition: Economic growth that is compatible with environmental protection.

Example: Green growth promises prosperity without rising emissions.

low growth

IPA: /ˌləʊ ˈɡrəʊθ/
Pronunciation: loh growth
French: faible croissance

Definition: A period when the economy expands slowly.

Example: Low growth can fuel political frustration.

slowdown

IPA: /ˈsləʊdaʊn/
Pronunciation: SLOH-down
French: ralentissement

Definition: A reduction in the speed of economic activity or growth.

Example: A global slowdown can reduce exports and investment.

stagnation

IPA: /stæɡˈneɪʃən/
Pronunciation: stag-NAY-shuhn
French: stagnation

Definition: A period of little or no economic growth.

Example: Stagnation can make voters lose trust in governments.

secular stagnation

IPA: /ˌsekjələ stæɡˈneɪʃən/
Pronunciation: SEK-yuh-luh stag-NAY-shuhn
French: stagnation séculaire

Definition: A long-term period of weak growth, low investment and low interest rates.

Example: Secular stagnation is often linked to ageing and weak demand.

recession

IPA: /rɪˈseʃən/
Pronunciation: ri-SESH-uhn
French: récession

Definition: A period of falling economic activity, often defined by falling GDP.

Example: A recession usually increases unemployment.

depression

IPA: /dɪˈpreʃən/
Pronunciation: di-PRESH-uhn
French: dépression économique

Definition: A severe and prolonged economic downturn.

Example: The Great Depression transformed economic policy.

boom

IPA: /buːm/
Pronunciation: boom
French: boom économique

Definition: A period of rapid economic growth.

Example: A boom can create jobs but also inflationary pressure.

bust

IPA: /bʌst/
Pronunciation: bust
French: effondrement ; crise après un boom

Definition: A sudden economic downturn after a boom.

Example: A credit boom can be followed by a painful bust.

business cycle

IPA: /ˈbɪznəs ˌsaɪkəl/
Pronunciation: BIZ-nuhs SY-kuhl
French: cycle économique

Definition: The repeated pattern of expansion and contraction in economic activity.

Example: The business cycle affects employment and investment.

economic expansion

IPA: /ˌiːkəˈnɒmɪk ɪkˈspænʃən/
Pronunciation: ee-kuh-NOM-ik ik-SPAN-shuhn
French: expansion économique

Definition: A period when economic activity is increasing.

Example: Economic expansion can improve public finances.

economic downturn

IPA: /ˌiːkəˈnɒmɪk ˈdaʊntɜːn/
Pronunciation: ee-kuh-NOM-ik DOWN-turn
French: ralentissement économique ; crise

Definition: A period of falling or weakening economic activity.

Example: An economic downturn hits vulnerable workers hardest.

output

IPA: /ˈaʊtpʊt/
Pronunciation: OWT-put
French: production ; output

Definition: The amount of goods and services produced.

Example: Higher output can result from more workers or better productivity.

economic output

IPA: /ˌiːkəˈnɒmɪk ˈaʊtpʊt/
Pronunciation: ee-kuh-NOM-ik OWT-put
French: production économique

Definition: The total production of an economy.

Example: Economic output fell sharply during the crisis.

productivity

IPA: /ˌprɒdʌkˈtɪvəti/
Pronunciation: prod-uk-TIV-uh-tee
French: productivité

Definition: The amount produced per worker or per unit of input.

Example: Productivity growth is crucial for long-term prosperity.

B. Productivity, innovation, capital and infrastructure

labour productivity

IPA: /ˈleɪbə ˌprɒdʌkˈtɪvəti/
Pronunciation: LAY-buh prod-uk-TIV-uh-tee
French: productivité du travail

Definition: Output produced per worker or per hour worked.

Example: Labour productivity rises when workers have better tools and skills.

total factor productivity

IPA: /ˌtəʊtəl ˈfæktə prɒdʌkˈtɪvəti/
Pronunciation: TOH-tuhl FAK-tuh prod-uk-TIV-uh-tee
French: productivité globale des facteurs

Definition: Efficiency gains not explained simply by more labour or capital.

Example: Innovation can increase total factor productivity.

productivity gap

IPA: /ˌprɒdʌkˈtɪvəti ɡæp/
Pronunciation: prod-uk-TIV-uh-tee gap
French: écart de productivité

Definition: A difference in productivity between countries, regions or firms.

Example: The productivity gap explains part of the income gap.

productivity puzzle

IPA: /ˌprɒdʌkˈtɪvəti ˈpʌzəl/
Pronunciation: prod-uk-TIV-uh-tee PUZ-uhl
French: énigme de la productivité

Definition: The difficulty of explaining weak productivity despite technology or investment.

Example: The UK productivity puzzle has worried economists for years.

innovation

IPA: /ˌɪnəˈveɪʃən/
Pronunciation: in-uh-VAY-shuhn
French: innovation

Definition: The creation and use of new ideas, products or methods.

Example: Innovation drives long-term economic growth.

technological progress

IPA: /ˌteknəˈlɒdʒɪkəl ˈprəʊɡres/
Pronunciation: tek-nuh-LOJ-i-kuhl PROH-gress
French: progrès technologique

Definition: Improvement in technology that allows more efficient production.

Example: Technological progress can raise productivity but disrupt jobs.

research and development

IPA: /rɪˌsɜːtʃ ənd dɪˈveləpmənt/
Pronunciation: ri-SURCH and di-VEL-uhp-muhnt
French: recherche et développement

Definition: Work aimed at creating new knowledge, products or technologies.

Example: Research and development is essential for innovation.

R&D

IPA: /ˌɑːr ən ˈdiː/
Pronunciation: ar-and-dee
French: R&D ; recherche et développement

Definition: Abbreviation for research and development.

Example: R&D spending can support future growth.

capital

IPA: /ˈkæpɪtəl/
Pronunciation: KAP-i-tuhl
French: capital

Definition: Resources used to produce goods and services, such as machines, buildings or money.

Example: Investment increases the stock of capital.

human capital

IPA: /ˌhjuːmən ˈkæpɪtəl/
Pronunciation: HYOO-muhn KAP-i-tuhl
French: capital humain

Definition: The skills, education and health of workers.

Example: Education improves human capital and productivity.

physical capital

IPA: /ˌfɪzɪkəl ˈkæpɪtəl/
Pronunciation: FIZ-i-kuhl KAP-i-tuhl
French: capital physique

Definition: Machines, buildings and equipment used in production.

Example: Physical capital helps workers produce more.

capital accumulation

IPA: /ˈkæpɪtəl əˌkjuːmjʊˈleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: KAP-i-tuhl uh-kyoo-myuh-LAY-shuhn
French: accumulation du capital

Definition: The growth of capital stock through investment.

Example: Capital accumulation is a traditional driver of growth.

investment

IPA: /ɪnˈvestmənt/
Pronunciation: in-VEST-muhnt
French: investissement

Definition: Spending on assets, equipment or projects expected to generate future value.

Example: Investment today can raise productivity tomorrow.

public investment

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk ɪnˈvestmənt/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik in-VEST-muhnt
French: investissement public

Definition: Government spending on infrastructure, education or long-term assets.

Example: Public investment can support growth in neglected regions.

private investment

IPA: /ˌpraɪvət ɪnˈvestmənt/
Pronunciation: PRY-vuht in-VEST-muhnt
French: investissement privé

Definition: Investment made by businesses or individuals.

Example: Private investment depends on confidence and profitability.

foreign direct investment

IPA: /ˌfɒrən dəˌrekt ɪnˈvestmənt/
Pronunciation: FOR-uhn di-REKT in-VEST-muhnt
French: investissement direct étranger

Definition: Investment by a foreign company in productive activity in another country.

Example: Foreign direct investment can bring capital and technology.

FDI

IPA: /ˌef diː ˈaɪ/
Pronunciation: eff-dee-eye
French: IDE ; investissement direct étranger

Definition: Abbreviation for foreign direct investment.

Example: FDI can help emerging economies industrialise.

infrastructure

IPA: /ˈɪnfrəˌstrʌktʃə/
Pronunciation: IN-fruh-struk-chuh
French: infrastructures

Definition: Basic systems such as transport, energy, water and digital networks.

Example: Poor infrastructure can limit economic growth.

transport infrastructure

IPA: /ˈtrænspɔːt ˌɪnfrəˌstrʌktʃə/
Pronunciation: TRAN-sport IN-fruh-struk-chuh
French: infrastructures de transport

Definition: Roads, railways, ports and airports supporting movement.

Example: Transport infrastructure connects workers, firms and markets.

digital infrastructure

IPA: /ˌdɪdʒɪtəl ˈɪnfrəˌstrʌktʃə/
Pronunciation: DIJ-i-tuhl IN-fruh-struk-chuh
French: infrastructure numérique

Definition: Internet, data and communication systems supporting the digital economy.

Example: Digital infrastructure is vital for modern growth.

energy infrastructure

IPA: /ˈenədʒi ˌɪnfrəˌstrʌktʃə/
Pronunciation: EN-uh-jee IN-fruh-struk-chuh
French: infrastructures énergétiques

Definition: Systems for producing, transporting and distributing energy.

Example: Green growth requires major energy infrastructure investment.

industrialisation

IPA: /ɪnˌdʌstriəlaɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: in-DUS-tree-uh-ly-ZAY-shuhn
French: industrialisation

Definition: The development of manufacturing and industrial activity.

Example: Industrialisation transformed living standards in the nineteenth century.

deindustrialisation

IPA: /ˌdiːɪnˌdʌstriəlaɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: dee-in-DUS-tree-uh-ly-ZAY-shuhn
French: désindustrialisation

Definition: The decline of manufacturing and industrial employment.

Example: Deindustrialisation can damage towns dependent on factories.

reindustrialisation

IPA: /ˌriːɪnˌdʌstriəlaɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: ree-in-DUS-tree-uh-ly-ZAY-shuhn
French: réindustrialisation

Definition: The revival or rebuilding of industrial capacity.

Example: Reindustrialisation is linked to strategic autonomy and green industry.

manufacturing

IPA: /ˌmænjʊˈfæktʃərɪŋ/
Pronunciation: man-yuh-FAK-chuh-ring
French: industrie manufacturière

Definition: The production of goods in factories.

Example: Manufacturing remains important for exports and productivity.

service economy

IPA: /ˈsɜːvɪs iˌkɒnəmi/
Pronunciation: SUR-vis i-KON-uh-mee
French: économie de services

Definition: An economy dominated by services rather than manufacturing.

Example: Advanced economies are usually service economies.

C. Sectors, development and living standards

knowledge economy

IPA: /ˈnɒlɪdʒ iˌkɒnəmi/
Pronunciation: NOL-ij i-KON-uh-mee
French: économie de la connaissance

Definition: An economy based on knowledge, technology and skilled labour.

Example: Universities play a key role in the knowledge economy.

digital economy

IPA: /ˌdɪdʒɪtəl iˈkɒnəmi/
Pronunciation: DIJ-i-tuhl i-KON-uh-mee
French: économie numérique

Definition: Economic activity based on digital technologies and data.

Example: The digital economy creates growth but raises privacy concerns.

creative economy

IPA: /kriˌeɪtɪv iˈkɒnəmi/
Pronunciation: kree-AY-tiv i-KON-uh-mee
French: économie créative

Definition: Economic activity based on culture, design, media and creativity.

Example: The creative economy can support urban regeneration.

green economy

IPA: /ˌɡriːn iˈkɒnəmi/
Pronunciation: green i-KON-uh-mee
French: économie verte

Definition: An economy designed to reduce environmental risks and carbon emissions.

circular economy

IPA: /ˌsɜːkjələ iˈkɒnəmi/
Pronunciation: SUR-kyuh-luh i-KON-uh-mee
French: économie circulaire

Definition: An economy that reuses, repairs and recycles resources to reduce waste.

Example: A circular economy reduces dependence on raw materials.

informal economy

IPA: /ɪnˌfɔːməl iˈkɒnəmi/
Pronunciation: in-FOR-muhl i-KON-uh-mee
French: économie informelle

Definition: Economic activity not fully regulated or taxed by the state.

Example: The informal economy provides income but little protection.

underground economy

IPA: /ˌʌndəɡraʊnd iˈkɒnəmi/
Pronunciation: UN-duh-ground i-KON-uh-mee
French: économie souterraine

Definition: Hidden or illegal economic activity outside official statistics.

Example: The underground economy reduces tax revenue.

emerging economy

IPA: /ɪˈmɜːdʒɪŋ iˌkɒnəmi/
Pronunciation: i-MUR-jing i-KON-uh-mee
French: économie émergente

Definition: A developing economy becoming more integrated into global markets.

Example: Emerging economies can grow quickly but remain vulnerable.

developed economy

IPA: /dɪˌveləpt iˈkɒnəmi/
Pronunciation: di-VEL-uhpt i-KON-uh-mee
French: économie développée

Definition: A wealthy, industrialised economy with advanced infrastructure and institutions.

Example: Developed economies often face slow growth and ageing populations.

developing economy

IPA: /dɪˌveləpɪŋ iˈkɒnəmi/
Pronunciation: di-VEL-uh-ping i-KON-uh-mee
French: économie en développement

Definition: An economy with lower income levels and ongoing industrial or institutional development.

Example: Developing economies need investment in education and infrastructure.

low-income country

IPA: /ˌləʊ ˈɪnkʌm ˈkʌntri/
Pronunciation: loh IN-kum KUN-tree
French: pays à faible revenu

Definition: A country with a low average income level.

Example: Low-income countries often lack infrastructure and capital.

Pronunciation: MID-uhl IN-kum KUN-tree
French: pays à revenu intermédiaire

Definition: A country with an intermediate average income level.

high-income country

IPA: /ˌhaɪ ˈɪnkʌm ˈkʌntri/
Pronunciation: hy IN-kum KUN-tree
French: pays à revenu élevé

Definition: A wealthy country with a high average income level.

Example: High-income countries often shift towards services and innovation.

IPA: /ˌmɪdəl ˈɪnkʌm træp/
Pronunciation: MID-uhl IN-kum trap
French: piège du revenu intermédiaire

Definition: A situation where a country struggles to move from middle-income to high-income status.

Example: Escaping the middle-income trap requires innovation and better institutions.

economic development

IPA: /ˌiːkəˈnɒmɪk dɪˈveləpmənt/
Pronunciation: ee-kuh-NOM-ik di-VEL-uhp-muhnt
French: développement économique

Definition: Long-term improvement in income, living standards and institutions.

Example: Economic development is broader than GDP growth.

development

IPA: /dɪˈveləpmənt/
Pronunciation: di-VEL-uhp-muhnt
French: développement

Definition: The process of improving economic and social conditions.

Example: Development includes health, education and political stability.

human development

IPA: /ˌhjuːmən dɪˈveləpmənt/
Pronunciation: HYOO-muhn di-VEL-uhp-muhnt
French: développement humain

Definition: Development measured through health, education and living standards.

Example: Human development focuses on people’s capabilities.

human development index

IPA: /ˌhjuːmən dɪˈveləpmənt ˈɪndeks/

French: indice de développement humain

HDI

IPA: /ˌeɪtʃ diː ˈaɪ/
Pronunciation: aych-dee-eye
French: IDH

Definition: Abbreviation for Human Development Index.

Example: HDI rankings highlight differences in life chances.

living standards

French: niveau de vie

standard of living

IPA: /ˌstændəd əv ˈlɪvɪŋ/

French: niveau de vie

prosperity

French: crédit
