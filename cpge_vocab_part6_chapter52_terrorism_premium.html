Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse terrorism: extremist ideologies, radicalisation, attack methods, soft targets, propaganda, recruitment, counterterrorism, surveillance, emergency powers, civil liberties, international cooperation, victims, media coverage and the challenge of protecting society without undermining democratic freedoms.

How to use this chapter

For press articlesFocus on terms such as violent extremism, online radicalisation, lone actor, soft target, extremist propaganda, counterterrorism, mass surveillance, civil liberties, intelligence sharing and community resilience.
For oral examsUse this vocabulary to explain terrorism as a political and social phenomenon, not only as a security problem: ideology, fear, media, identity, policing and rights all matter.
For essaysMemorise the collocations: to prevent attacks, to foil a plot, to counter violent extremism, to balance security and freedom, to protect civil liberties.

A. Core terrorism and extremism vocabulary

terrorism

IPA: /ˈterərɪzəm/
Pronunciation: TERR-uh-riz-uhm
French: terrorisme

Definition: The use or threat of violence against civilians or symbolic targets to pursue political, ideological or religious aims.

Example: Terrorism aims to spread fear beyond the immediate victims.

terrorist attack

IPA: /ˈterərɪst əˌtæk/
Pronunciation: TERR-uh-rist uh-TAK
French: attentat terroriste

Definition: A violent act carried out for terrorist purposes.

Example: The terrorist attack shocked the country and triggered a security response.

terrorist group

IPA: /ˈterərɪst ɡruːp/
Pronunciation: TERR-uh-rist group
French: groupe terroriste

Definition: An organisation using terrorism to pursue its goals.

Example: The terrorist group claimed responsibility for the bombing.

terrorist cell

IPA: /ˈterərɪst sel/
Pronunciation: TERR-uh-rist sel
French: cellule terroriste

Definition: A small organised group within a terrorist network.

Example: Police dismantled a terrorist cell before it could act.

terror network

IPA: /ˈterə ˌnetwɜːk/
Pronunciation: TERR-uh NET-work
French: réseau terroriste

Definition: A connected system of terrorist actors, supporters and resources.

Example: The terror network operated across borders.

extremism

IPA: /ɪkˈstriːmɪzəm/
Pronunciation: ik-STREEM-iz-uhm
French: extrémisme

Definition: Support for radical ideas that reject democratic norms or justify violence.

Example: Extremism can become dangerous when it legitimises violence.

violent extremism

IPA: /ˌvaɪələnt ɪkˈstriːmɪzəm/
Pronunciation: VY-uh-luhnt ik-STREEM-iz-uhm
French: extrémisme violent

Definition: Extremism that supports or uses violence.

Example: Preventing violent extremism requires more than policing.

radicalisation

IPA: /ˌrædɪkəlaɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: rad-i-kuh-ly-ZAY-shuhn
French: radicalisation

Definition: The process by which someone adopts extreme beliefs, sometimes leading to violence.

Example: Online propaganda can accelerate radicalisation.

self-radicalisation

IPA: /ˌself rædɪkəlaɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: self rad-i-kuh-ly-ZAY-shuhn
French: auto-radicalisation

Definition: Radicalisation that occurs largely without direct personal contact with a group.

Example: Self-radicalisation is difficult for authorities to detect.

online radicalisation

IPA: /ˌɒnlaɪn rædɪkəlaɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: ON-line rad-i-kuh-ly-ZAY-shuhn
French: radicalisation en ligne

Definition: Radicalisation through internet content, forums or social media.

Example: Online radicalisation has become a major security concern.

jihadism

IPA: /dʒɪˈhɑːdɪzəm/
Pronunciation: ji-HAH-diz-uhm
French: djihadisme

Definition: A violent extremist ideology claiming to justify violence through a distorted interpretation of jihad.

Example: Jihadism has inspired transnational terrorist networks.

far-right terrorism

IPA: /ˌfɑː raɪt ˈterərɪzəm/
Pronunciation: far-right TERR-uh-riz-uhm
French: terrorisme d’extrême droite

Definition: Terrorism motivated by far-right ideologies such as white supremacy or ultranationalism.

Example: Far-right terrorism has grown as a concern in many democracies.

white supremacism

IPA: /ˌwaɪt suːˈpreməsɪzəm/
Pronunciation: white soo-PREM-uh-siz-uhm
French: suprémacisme blanc

Definition: The racist belief that white people are superior and should dominate society.

Example: White supremacism can fuel violent extremism.

religious extremism

IPA: /rɪˌlɪdʒəs ɪkˈstriːmɪzəm/
Pronunciation: ri-LIJ-uhs ik-STREEM-iz-uhm
French: extrémisme religieux

Definition: Extremism justified through religious claims or identity.

Example: Religious extremism is not representative of a whole faith community.

political violence

IPA: /pəˌlɪtɪkəl ˈvaɪələns/
Pronunciation: puh-LIT-i-kuhl VY-uh-luhns
French: violence politique

Definition: Violence used for political purposes.

Example: Terrorism is one form of political violence.

lone actor

IPA: /ˌləʊn ˈæktə/
Pronunciation: lohn AK-tuh
French: acteur isolé

Definition: A person who carries out violence alone, without direct command from a group.

Example: Lone-actor attacks are hard to prevent.

lone wolf

IPA: /ˌləʊn ˈwʊlf/
Pronunciation: lohn wolf
French: loup solitaire

Definition: Informal term for a lone actor; often avoided because it can romanticise violence.

Example: The phrase lone wolf can obscure online influence.

homegrown terrorism

IPA: /ˌhəʊmɡrəʊn ˈterərɪzəm/
Pronunciation: HOHM-grohn TERR-uh-riz-uhm
French: terrorisme endogène

Definition: Terrorism carried out by people radicalised within the country where they act.

Example: Homegrown terrorism challenges traditional border-focused security.

transnational terrorism

IPA: /trænzˌnæʃənəl ˈterərɪzəm/
Pronunciation: tranz-NASH-uh-nuhl TERR-uh-riz-uhm
French: terrorisme transnational

Definition: Terrorism involving networks, targets or operations across borders.

Example: Transnational terrorism requires international cooperation.

domestic terrorism

IPA: /dəˌmestɪk ˈterərɪzəm/
Pronunciation: duh-MES-tik TERR-uh-riz-uhm
French: terrorisme intérieur

Definition: Terrorism carried out within a country by actors based there.

Example: Domestic terrorism can be politically sensitive to define.

state terrorism

IPA: /ˌsteɪt ˈterərɪzəm/
Pronunciation: state TERR-uh-riz-uhm
French: terrorisme d’État

Definition: Use of terror by a state against populations or opponents.

Example: The term state terrorism is highly contested.

state-sponsored terrorism

IPA: /ˌsteɪt ˌspɒnsəd ˈterərɪzəm/
Pronunciation: state-SPON-suhd TERR-uh-riz-uhm
French: terrorisme soutenu par un État

Definition: Terrorism supported by a government through funding, training or protection.

Example: State-sponsored terrorism can destabilise whole regions.

insurgency

IPA: /ɪnˈsɜːdʒənsi/
Pronunciation: in-SUR-juhn-see
French: insurrection

Definition: An organised rebellion against a government or occupying power.

Example: Some insurgencies use terrorist tactics.

guerrilla tactics

IPA: /ɡəˈrɪlə ˈtæktɪks/
Pronunciation: guh-RIL-uh TAK-tiks
French: tactiques de guérilla

Definition: Irregular warfare methods using small mobile groups and surprise attacks.

Example: Guerrilla tactics differ from indiscriminate attacks on civilians.

asymmetric warfare

IPA: /ˌeɪsɪˈmetrɪk ˈwɔːfeə/
Pronunciation: ay-si-MET-rik WAR-fair
French: guerre asymétrique

Definition: Conflict between actors with very unequal military power.

Example: Terrorism is often used in asymmetric warfare.

suicide bombing

IPA: /ˈsuːɪsaɪd ˌbɒmɪŋ/
Pronunciation: SOO-i-syde BOM-ing
French: attentat-suicide

Definition: An attack in which the attacker deliberately dies while detonating explosives.

Example: Suicide bombing is designed to maximise shock and fear.

B. Attack methods, targets and propaganda

bombing

IPA: /ˈbɒmɪŋ/
Pronunciation: BOM-ing
French: attentat à la bombe ; bombardement

Definition: An attack using explosives.

Example: The bombing targeted a crowded market.

car bomb

IPA: /ˈkɑː bɒm/
Pronunciation: car bom
French: voiture piégée

Definition: A bomb placed in a vehicle.

Example: A car bomb exploded outside the ministry.

improvised explosive device

IPA: /ˌɪmprəvaɪzd ɪkˌspləʊsɪv dɪˈvaɪs/
Pronunciation: IM-pruh-vyzd ik-SPLOH-siv di-VYSS
French: engin explosif improvisé

Definition: A homemade bomb used by non-state actors or insurgents.

Example: Improvised explosive devices caused many casualties.

IED

IPA: /ˌaɪ iː ˈdiː/
Pronunciation: eye-ee-dee
French: EEI ; engin explosif improvisé

Definition: Abbreviation for improvised explosive device.

Example: IEDs are often used in asymmetric conflicts.

hostage-taking

IPA: /ˈhɒstɪdʒ ˌteɪkɪŋ/
Pronunciation: HOS-tij TAY-king
French: prise d’otages

Definition: The act of capturing people to force demands.

Example: Hostage-taking puts governments under intense pressure.

hijacking

IPA: /ˈhaɪdʒækɪŋ/
Pronunciation: HY-jak-ing
French: détournement

Definition: Taking control of a vehicle, aircraft or ship by force.

Example: Aircraft hijacking reshaped aviation security.

assassination

IPA: /əˌsæsɪˈneɪʃən/
Pronunciation: uh-sas-i-NAY-shuhn
French: assassinat

Definition: The deliberate killing of a political or public figure.

Example: Assassination can be used to destabilise a government.

mass shooting

IPA: /ˌmæs ˈʃuːtɪŋ/
Pronunciation: mass SHOO-ting
French: fusillade de masse

Definition: An attack in which multiple people are shot.

Example: Some mass shootings are motivated by extremist ideology.

knife attack

IPA: /ˈnaɪf əˌtæk/
Pronunciation: knife uh-TAK
French: attaque au couteau

Definition: An attack using a knife or blade.

Example: Knife attacks are low-tech but can spread fear.

vehicle-ramming attack

IPA: /ˈviːɪkəl ˌræmɪŋ əˌtæk/
Pronunciation: VEE-i-kuhl RAM-ing uh-TAK
French: attaque au véhicule-bélier

Definition: An attack using a vehicle to hit people deliberately.

Example: Vehicle-ramming attacks are difficult to prevent in open spaces.

soft target

IPA: /ˌsɒft ˈtɑːɡɪt/
Pronunciation: soft TAR-git
French: cible vulnérable

Definition: A civilian or poorly protected target such as a school, concert hall or market.

Example: Terrorists often choose soft targets to maximise fear.

hard target

IPA: /ˌhɑːd ˈtɑːɡɪt/
Pronunciation: hard TAR-git
French: cible protégée

Definition: A protected target such as a military base or government building.

Example: Hard targets require greater planning and resources.

civilian target

IPA: /səˌvɪliən ˈtɑːɡɪt/
Pronunciation: suh-VIL-yuhn TAR-git
French: cible civile

Definition: A non-military target involving civilians.

Example: Attacking civilian targets is morally and legally condemned.

symbolic target

IPA: /sɪmˌbɒlɪk ˈtɑːɡɪt/
Pronunciation: sim-BOL-ik TAR-git
French: cible symbolique

Definition: A target chosen for its political, cultural or religious meaning.

Example: The monument was chosen as a symbolic target.

critical infrastructure

IPA: /ˌkrɪtɪkəl ˈɪnfrəˌstrʌktʃə/
Pronunciation: KRIT-i-kuhl IN-fruh-struk-chuh
French: infrastructures critiques

Definition: Essential systems such as transport, energy, water and communications.

Example: Terrorists may target critical infrastructure.

cyberterrorism

IPA: /ˌsaɪbəˈterərɪzəm/
Pronunciation: SY-buh-TERR-uh-riz-uhm
French: cyberterrorisme

Definition: Use of cyberattacks to cause fear, disruption or political pressure.

Example: Cyberterrorism could target hospitals or power grids.

propaganda

IPA: /ˌprɒpəˈɡændə/
Pronunciation: prop-uh-GAN-duh
French: propagande

Definition: Biased information used to promote a political cause or ideology.

Example: Terrorist propaganda glorifies violence and martyrdom.

extremist propaganda

IPA: /ɪkˌstriːmɪst ˌprɒpəˈɡændə/
Pronunciation: ik-STREEM-ist prop-uh-GAN-duh
French: propagande extrémiste

Definition: Propaganda promoting extremist beliefs or violence.

Example: Extremist propaganda spreads rapidly online.

recruitment

IPA: /rɪˈkruːtmənt/
Pronunciation: ri-KROOT-muhnt
French: recrutement

Definition: The process of attracting people to join a group or cause.

Example: Online recruitment can target isolated young people.

indoctrination

IPA: /ɪnˌdɒktrɪˈneɪʃən/
Pronunciation: in-dok-tri-NAY-shuhn
French: endoctrinement

Definition: Teaching someone to accept beliefs uncritically.

Example: Indoctrination isolates individuals from alternative viewpoints.

incitement

IPA: /ɪnˈsaɪtmənt/
Pronunciation: in-SYTE-muhnt
French: incitation

Definition: Encouragement to commit violence or illegal acts.

Example: Incitement to violence is punishable by law.

glorification of violence

IPA: /ˌɡlɔːrɪfɪˈkeɪʃən əv ˈvaɪələns/
Pronunciation: glor-i-fi-KAY-shuhn ov VY-uh-luhns
French: glorification de la violence

Definition: Presenting violence as heroic or admirable.

Example: Glorification of violence can help radicalise vulnerable people.

martyrdom narrative

IPA: /ˈmɑːtədəm ˈnærətɪv/
Pronunciation: MAR-tuh-duhm NARR-uh-tiv
French: récit de martyr

Definition: A story portraying death for a cause as heroic or sacred.

Example: Martyrdom narratives are used in extremist recruitment.

conspiracy theory

IPA: /kənˈspɪrəsi ˈθɪəri/
Pronunciation: kuhn-SPIR-uh-see THEER-ee
French: théorie du complot

Definition: A belief that hidden groups secretly control events, often without evidence.

Example: Conspiracy theories can feed extremist worldviews.

grievance

IPA: /ˈɡriːvəns/
Pronunciation: GREE-vuhns
French: grief ; ressentiment

Definition: A real or perceived injustice that causes resentment.

Example: Extremist recruiters exploit personal and political grievances.

alienation

IPA: /ˌeɪliəˈneɪʃən/
Pronunciation: ay-lee-uh-NAY-shuhn
French: aliénation ; sentiment d’exclusion

Definition: A feeling of isolation or disconnection from society.

Example: Alienation can make extremist narratives more attractive.

polarisation

IPA: /ˌpəʊləraɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: poh-luh-ry-ZAY-shuhn
French: polarisation

Definition: The division of society into opposing and hostile groups.

Example: Polarisation can create fertile ground for extremism.

hate speech

IPA: /ˈheɪt spiːtʃ/
Pronunciation: hate speech
French: discours de haine

Definition: Speech attacking people based on identity such as race, religion or ethnicity.

Example: Hate speech can normalise violence.

dehumanisation

IPA: /diːˌhjuːmənaɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: dee-HYOO-muh-ny-ZAY-shuhn
French: déshumanisation

Definition: Portraying people as less than human.

Example: Dehumanisation makes violence easier to justify.

counterterrorism

IPA: /ˌkaʊntəˈterərɪzəm/
Pronunciation: KOWN-tuh-TERR-uh-riz-uhm
French: contre-terrorisme ; lutte antiterroriste

Definition: Policies and actions designed to prevent and respond to terrorism.

Example: Counterterrorism must balance security and civil liberties.

anti-terrorism

IPA: /ˌænti ˈterərɪzəm/
Pronunciation: AN-tee TERR-uh-riz-uhm
French: antiterrorisme

Definition: Measures opposing terrorism.

Example: Anti-terrorism laws expanded after major attacks.

national security

IPA: /ˌnæʃənəl sɪˈkjʊərəti/
Pronunciation: NASH-uh-nuhl si-KYOOR-uh-tee
French: sécurité nationale

Definition: Protection of a country’s safety and vital interests.

Example: Terrorism is often framed as a national security threat.

homeland security

IPA: /ˌhəʊmlænd sɪˈkjʊərəti/
Pronunciation: HOHM-land si-KYOOR-uh-tee
French: sécurité intérieure

Definition: Domestic security against threats such as terrorism.

Example: Homeland security agencies coordinate emergency responses.

security services

IPA: /sɪˈkjʊərəti ˈsɜːvɪsɪz/
Pronunciation: si-KYOOR-uh-tee SUR-vis-iz
French: services de sécurité

Definition: Agencies responsible for intelligence and internal security.

Example: Security services monitor extremist networks.

intelligence agency

IPA: /ɪnˈtelɪdʒəns ˌeɪdʒənsi/
Pronunciation: in-TEL-i-juhns AY-juhn-see
French: service de renseignement

Definition: A government body that gathers and analyses information for security.

Example: Intelligence agencies help prevent attacks.

C. Counterterrorism, surveillance and prevention

surveillance

IPA: /səˈveɪləns/
Pronunciation: suh-VAY-luhns
French: surveillance

Definition: Monitoring people, communications or places for security or control.

Example: Mass surveillance raises civil-liberty concerns.

mass surveillance

IPA: /ˌmæs səˈveɪləns/
Pronunciation: mass suh-VAY-luhns
French: surveillance de masse

Definition: Large-scale monitoring of populations or communications.

Example: Mass surveillance is controversial in democracies.

targeted surveillance

IPA: /ˈtɑːɡɪtɪd səˈveɪləns/
Pronunciation: TAR-git-id suh-VAY-luhns
French: surveillance ciblée

Definition: Monitoring specific individuals or groups based on suspicion.

Example: Targeted surveillance requires legal oversight.

intelligence gathering

IPA: /ɪnˈtelɪdʒəns ˌɡæðərɪŋ/
Pronunciation: in-TEL-i-juhns GATH-uh-ring
French: collecte de renseignement

Definition: Collecting information to assess threats.

Example: Intelligence gathering can prevent attacks.

threat assessment

IPA: /ˈθret əˌsesmənt/
Pronunciation: thret uh-SESS-muhnt
French: évaluation de la menace

Definition: Evaluation of the seriousness and likelihood of a threat.

Example: Threat assessment guides police priorities.

risk assessment

IPA: /ˈrɪsk əˌsesmənt/
Pronunciation: risk uh-SESS-muhnt
French: évaluation des risques

Definition: Evaluation of possible risks and their impact.

Example: Risk assessment is used at airports and public events.

security threat

IPA: /sɪˈkjʊərəti θret/
Pronunciation: si-KYOOR-uh-tee thret
French: menace sécuritaire

Definition: A danger to public or national security.

Example: The suspect was considered a security threat.

threat level

IPA: /ˈθret ˌlevəl/
Pronunciation: thret LEV-uhl
French: niveau de menace

Definition: An official assessment of the likelihood of an attack.

Example: The threat level was raised after the attack.

early warning signs

IPA: /ˌɜːli ˈwɔːnɪŋ saɪnz/
Pronunciation: UR-lee WAR-ning synz
French: signaux d’alerte précoces

Definition: Signs indicating a possible future threat.

Example: Teachers and social workers may notice early warning signs.

prevention

IPA: /prɪˈvenʃən/
Pronunciation: pri-VEN-shuhn
French: prévention

Definition: Action taken to stop something from happening.

Example: Prevention is central to counterterrorism.

deradicalisation

IPA: /ˌdiːrædɪkəlaɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: dee-rad-i-kuh-ly-ZAY-shuhn
French: déradicalisation

Definition: The process of helping someone abandon extremist beliefs.

Example: Deradicalisation programmes are difficult to evaluate.

disengagement

IPA: /ˌdɪsɪnˈɡeɪdʒmənt/
Pronunciation: dis-in-GAYJ-muhnt
French: désengagement

Definition: Stopping involvement in extremist activity, even if beliefs change slowly.

Example: Disengagement may be more realistic than full deradicalisation.

community policing

IPA: /kəˌmjuːnəti pəˈliːsɪŋ/
Pronunciation: kuh-MYOO-nuh-tee puh-LEE-sing
French: police de proximité

Definition: Policing based on trust and cooperation with local communities.

Example: Community policing can improve trust and intelligence.

community engagement

IPA: /kəˌmjuːnəti ɪnˈɡeɪdʒmənt/
Pronunciation: kuh-MYOO-nuh-tee in-GAYJ-muhnt
French: implication des communautés

Definition: Working with communities to build trust and prevent harm.

Example: Community engagement can help prevent radicalisation.

resilience

IPA: /rɪˈzɪliəns/
Pronunciation: ri-ZIL-ee-uhns
French: résilience

Definition: The ability of a society to recover from attacks or crises.

Example: Social resilience reduces the long-term impact of terrorism.

emergency response

IPA: /ɪˈmɜːdʒənsi rɪˌspɒns/
Pronunciation: i-MUR-juhn-see ri-SPONS
French: réponse d’urgence

Definition: Immediate action taken after a crisis or attack.

Example: Emergency response saves lives after an attack.

first responders

IPA: /ˌfɜːst rɪˈspɒndəz/
Pronunciation: first ri-SPON-duhz
French: premiers intervenants

Definition: Police, firefighters, paramedics and others who respond first to emergencies.

Example: First responders arrived within minutes.

evacuation

IPA: /ɪˌvækjuˈeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: i-vak-yoo-AY-shuhn
French: évacuation

Definition: Moving people away from danger.

Example: The evacuation of the station was orderly.

lockdown

IPA: /ˈlɒkdaʊn/
Pronunciation: LOK-down
French: confinement de sécurité ; verrouillage

Definition: A security measure preventing people from entering or leaving an area.

Example: The school went into lockdown after the alert.

state of emergency

IPA: /ˌsteɪt əv ɪˈmɜːdʒənsi/
Pronunciation: state ov i-MUR-juhn-see
French: état d’urgence

Definition: A legal situation giving authorities special powers during a crisis.

Example: The government declared a state of emergency.

emergency powers

IPA: /ɪˈmɜːdʒənsi ˈpaʊəz/
Pronunciation: i-MUR-juhn-see POW-uhz
French: pouvoirs d’urgence

Definition: Special powers granted to authorities during a crisis.

Example: Emergency powers must be limited and supervised.

civil liberties

IPA: /ˌsɪvəl ˈlɪbətiz/
Pronunciation: SIV-uhl LIB-uh-teez
French: libertés civiles

Definition: Basic freedoms protected from government interference.

Example: Counterterrorism can threaten civil liberties.

human rights

IPA: /ˌhjuːmən ˈraɪts/
Pronunciation: HYOO-muhn rites
French: droits humains

Definition: Basic rights and freedoms belonging to all people.

Example: Human rights must be protected even during security crises.

rule of law

IPA: /ˌruːl əv ˈlɔː/
Pronunciation: rool ov law
French: État de droit

Definition: The principle that everyone, including the state, is subject to law.

Example: The rule of law limits counterterrorism powers.

due process

IPA: /ˌdjuː ˈprəʊses/
Pronunciation: dyoo PROH-ses
French: procédure régulière

Definition: Fair legal procedures before punishment or restriction.

Example: Suspects must be granted due process.

racial profiling

IPA: /ˌreɪʃəl ˈprəʊfaɪlɪŋ/
Pronunciation: RAY-shuhl PROH-fy-ling
French: profilage racial

Definition: Targeting people based on race or ethnicity rather than evidence.

Example: Racial profiling damages trust in law enforcement.

religious profiling

IPA: /rɪˌlɪdʒəs ˈprəʊfaɪlɪŋ/
Pronunciation: ri-LIJ-uhs PROH-fy-ling
French: profilage religieux

Definition: Targeting people based on religion rather than evidence.

Example: Religious profiling can stigmatise entire communities.

stigmatisation

IPA: /ˌstɪɡmətaɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: stig-muh-ty-ZAY-shuhn
French: stigmatisation

Definition: The process of unfairly marking a group as dangerous or shameful.

Example: Stigmatisation can worsen alienation and mistrust.

Islamophobia

IPA: /ɪzˌlæməˈfəʊbiə/
Pronunciation: iz-lam-uh-FOH-bee-uh
French: islamophobie

Definition: Hostility, prejudice or discrimination against Muslims.

Example: Counterterrorism discourse can fuel Islamophobia if it generalises unfairly.

hate crime

IPA: /ˈheɪt kraɪm/
Pronunciation: hate kryme
French: crime de haine

Definition: A crime motivated by prejudice against identity.

Example: Terrorist attacks can trigger hate crimes against minorities.

backlash

IPA: /ˈbæklæʃ/
Pronunciation: BAK-lash
French: retour de bâton ; réaction hostile

Definition: A strong negative reaction.

Example: Security measures triggered a civil-liberties backlash.

collective punishment
