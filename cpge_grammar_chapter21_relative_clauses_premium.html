Chapter objective

This premium grammar chapter helps CPGE students master relative clauses. It covers who, which, that, whose, where, when, defining and non-defining relative clauses, commas, formal academic alternatives and common French-speaker mistakes.

The golden rule

Relative pronoun + clause
Voters who feel ignored may abstain.
The reform, which was announced yesterday, has been criticised.
Main useUse relative clauses to add essential or extra information about a noun, person, place, idea or whole sentence.
CPGE habitUse relative clauses to build precise academic sentences: Citizens whose wages stagnate feel increasingly insecure.
Key trapDo not use that after a comma in non-defining clauses: The reform, which..., not The reform, that....

A. Lesson — What are relative clauses?

Relative clause

French: proposition relative

Rule / explanation: A relative clause adds information about a noun, person, place, idea or whole sentence.

Example / answer: The article which we studied yesterday focuses on inequality.

Relative pronoun

French: pronom relatif

Rule / explanation: A word such as who, which, that, whose, where or when introducing a relative clause.

Example / answer: The journalist who wrote the article is very critical.

Defining relative clause

French: relative déterminative

Rule / explanation: A clause that gives essential information to identify the noun.

Example / answer: Voters who feel ignored may abstain.

Non-defining relative clause

French: relative explicative

Rule / explanation: A clause that adds extra information; it is separated by commas.

Example / answer: The reform, which was announced yesterday, has been criticised.

People

French: personnes

Rule / explanation: Use who or that for people in defining relative clauses.

Example / answer: Citizens who distrust institutions are less likely to vote.

Things and ideas

French: choses et idées

Rule / explanation: Use which or that for things and ideas in defining relative clauses.

Example / answer: The argument which the author develops is convincing.

Possession

French: possession

Rule / explanation: Use whose to express possession.

Example / answer: Citizens whose wages stagnate feel increasingly insecure.

CPGE use

French: usage CPGE

Rule / explanation: Relative clauses help students write more complex, precise and elegant sentences.

Example / answer: The article examines a crisis that has transformed British politics.

B. Who, which and that

Who for people

French: who pour personnes

Rule / explanation: Use who when the relative clause refers to people.

Example / answer: Politicians who ignore inequality risk losing support.

Which for things or ideas

French: which pour choses/idées

Rule / explanation: Use which when the relative clause refers to things, ideas, events or facts.

Example / answer: The policy which the government proposed is controversial.

That for people or things

French: that

Rule / explanation: Use that in defining relative clauses for people or things.

Example / answer: The reform that the minister defended remains unpopular.

Who as subject

French: who sujet

Rule / explanation: When who is the subject, do not add another subject.

Example / answer: The journalist who wrote the article criticises the government.

Which as subject

French: which sujet

Rule / explanation: When which is the subject, do not add it again.

Example / answer: The law which was passed last year is being challenged.

That as subject

French: that sujet

Rule / explanation: That can be the subject in defining relative clauses.

Example / answer: The issue that matters most is public trust.

Object relative pronoun

French: pronom relatif objet

Rule / explanation: Who/which/that can be the object of the relative clause.

Example / answer: The argument that the author rejects is simplistic.

Omitting object relative pronoun

French: omission du relatif objet

Rule / explanation: In defining relative clauses, an object relative pronoun can often be omitted.

Example / answer: The article we read yesterday was challenging.

C. Whose, where, when and why

Whose

French: dont / dont le

Rule / explanation: Use whose to express possession for people, groups, countries or institutions.

Example / answer: Voters whose jobs are threatened may support protectionism.

Where

French: où

Rule / explanation: Use where for places, institutions or contexts.

Example / answer: Cities where rents are high face a housing crisis.

When

French: où / lorsque

Rule / explanation: Use when for time periods or moments.

Example / answer: The 1980s were a period when inequality increased.

Why

French: pour laquelle

Rule / explanation: Use why after reason.

Example / answer: The reason why voters abstain is complex.

The place where

French: l’endroit où

Rule / explanation: Formal and clear structure for locations.

Example / answer: The place where the protest began became symbolic.

The moment when

French: le moment où

Rule / explanation: Useful for history and turning points.

Example / answer: The moment when the scandal broke changed the campaign.

The reason why

French: la raison pour laquelle

Rule / explanation: Common but can be shortened to the reason.

Example / answer: The reason why the reform failed was poor communication.

In which

French: dans lequel

Rule / explanation: Formal alternative to where/when for academic style.

Example / answer: A context in which trust has collapsed is dangerous for democracy.

D. Defining relative clauses

Essential information

French: information essentielle

Rule / explanation: A defining relative clause identifies exactly who or what is meant.

Example / answer: The voters who opposed the reform were mostly young.

No commas

French: pas de virgules

Rule / explanation: Do not use commas around defining relative clauses.

Example / answer: The article that we analysed was published in The Guardian.

That possible

French: that possible

Rule / explanation: That is common in defining relative clauses.

Example / answer: The problem that the author highlights is structural.

Omission possible

French: omission possible

Rule / explanation: If the relative pronoun is the object, it can be omitted.

Example / answer: The policy the government proposed was rejected.

Subject relative cannot be omitted

French: sujet non supprimable

Rule / explanation: Do not omit who/which/that when it is the subject.

Example / answer: Wrong: The journalist wrote the article is critical. Correct: The journalist who wrote the article is critical.

People affected

French: personnes concernées

Rule / explanation: Useful in social and political analysis.

Example / answer: People who live in rural areas may feel neglected.

Policies discussed

French: politiques discutées

Rule / explanation: Useful in article summaries.

Example / answer: The measure that the article criticises is unpopular.

Arguments developed

French: arguments développés

Rule / explanation: Useful in essays.

Example / answer: The argument that the author develops deserves attention.

E. Non-defining relative clauses

Extra information

French: information supplémentaire

Rule / explanation: A non-defining relative clause adds information that is not essential to identify the noun.

Example / answer: The Prime Minister, who gave a speech yesterday, defended the reform.

Commas required

French: virgules obligatoires

Rule / explanation: Use commas before and after the relative clause.

Example / answer: The reform, which was announced yesterday, has divided Parliament.

No that

French: pas de that

Rule / explanation: Do not use that in non-defining relative clauses.

Example / answer: Wrong: The reform, that was announced yesterday, is controversial. Correct: The reform, which was announced yesterday, is controversial.

Which for whole clause

French: which phrase entière

Rule / explanation: Which can refer to the whole previous clause.

Example / answer: The government ignored warnings, which angered experts.

Formal style

French: style formel

Rule / explanation: Non-defining clauses are useful for formal explanation and nuance.

Example / answer: The article, which relies on recent data, offers a persuasive analysis.

Adding context

French: ajouter du contexte

Rule / explanation: Use them to add dates, sources or background.

Example / answer: The report, which was published in 2024, reveals growing inequality.

Adding evaluation

French: ajouter un commentaire

Rule / explanation: Use them to add a comment without starting a new sentence.

Example / answer: The argument, which is persuasive, still needs more evidence.

CPGE elegance

French: élégance CPGE

Rule / explanation: They allow students to write more compact and sophisticated sentences.

Example / answer: The crisis, which began years ago, has become politically explosive.

F. Relative clauses in essays and article summaries

Defining key groups

French: définir des groupes

Rule / explanation: Use relative clauses to identify social groups precisely.

Example / answer: Citizens who feel excluded may reject mainstream politics.

Explaining causes

French: expliquer les causes

Rule / explanation: Use them to connect a noun with a cause or consequence.

Example / answer: A system that fails to protect the vulnerable loses legitimacy.

Discussing articles

French: analyse d’article

Rule / explanation: Use them to refer to texts, authors and arguments.

Example / answer: The article, which was published after the election, explores voter frustration.

Comparing countries

French: comparaison de pays

Rule / explanation: Use them to add political or historical context.

Example / answer: Countries that invest in education often reduce inequality more effectively.

Data commentary

French: commentaire de données

Rule / explanation: Use them to clarify what a figure or trend represents.

Example / answer: The figures, which cover the past decade, show a widening gap.

Nuancing claims

French: nuancer

Rule / explanation: Use non-defining clauses to add nuance.

Example / answer: The proposal, which may seem attractive, raises serious legal questions.

Avoiding repetition

French: éviter les répétitions

Rule / explanation: Relative clauses help avoid repeating nouns.

Example / answer: The reform that the government proposed has been criticised.

Building complex sentences

French: phrases complexes

Rule / explanation: They are a bridge from B1 grammar to B2/C1 academic writing.

Example / answer: The article describes a society in which trust has become fragile.

G. Typical mistakes by French speakers

Using which for people

French: which pour personnes

Rule / explanation: Use who for people in formal English.

Example / answer: Wrong: voters which feel ignored. Correct: voters who feel ignored.

Using who for things

French: who pour choses

Rule / explanation: Use which or that for things and ideas.

Example / answer: Wrong: the policy who failed. Correct: the policy which failed.

Using that in non-defining clauses

French: that après virgule

Rule / explanation: That is not used after a comma in non-defining relative clauses.

Example / answer: Wrong: The law, that was passed in 2019. Correct: The law, which was passed in 2019.

Forgetting commas

French: oubli des virgules

Rule / explanation: Non-defining relative clauses need commas.

Example / answer: Correct: The article, which is persuasive, lacks data.

Double subject

French: double sujet

Rule / explanation: Do not repeat the subject inside the relative clause.

Example / answer: Wrong: The article which it explains the issue. Correct: The article which explains the issue.

Omitting a subject relative pronoun

French: omission impossible

Rule / explanation: A subject relative pronoun cannot be omitted.

Example / answer: Wrong: The policy affects families is unfair. Correct: The policy that affects families is unfair.

Confusing whose and who’s

French: whose / who’s

Rule / explanation: Whose = possession; who’s = who is / who has.

Example / answer: Citizens whose wages stagnate. / Who’s responsible?

Translating dont mechanically

French: dont

Rule / explanation: French dont can become whose, of which, about which, or that depending on meaning.

Example / answer: The issue that the author talks about / the citizens whose rights are threatened.
