Part 9 — Media & Culture | Chapter 78 — Entertainment and Streaming
Essential vocabulary for khôlles, essays, press reviews and competitive exams.

Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse entertainment and streaming: streaming platforms, subscriptions, binge-watching, franchises, fandom, algorithms, global cultural influence, representation, screen time, creator labour, piracy, AI-generated content and the transformation of film, television and popular culture in the platform age.

How to use this chapter

For press articlesFocus on streaming platform, subscription fatigue, original content, franchise, fandom, binge-watching, recommendation algorithm, creator economy, AI-generated content and representation.
For oral examsUse this vocabulary to explain streaming as an economic, technological and cultural shift: platforms do not just distribute entertainment; they shape tastes, habits, visibility and cultural power.

A. Core entertainment and streaming vocabulary

entertainment

IPA: /ˌentəˈteɪnmənt/
Pronunciation: EN-tuh-TAYN-muhnt
French: divertissement

Definition: Activities, media or performances designed to amuse, inform or engage audiences.

Example: Entertainment has become a global industry.

streaming

IPA: /ˈstriːmɪŋ/
Pronunciation: STREE-ming
French: streaming ; diffusion en continu

Definition: Delivering audio or video content over the internet without downloading it first.

Example: Streaming has transformed how people watch films and series.

streaming platform

IPA: /ˈstriːmɪŋ ˌplætfɔːm/
Pronunciation: STREE-ming PLAT-form
French: plateforme de streaming

Definition: An online service providing streamed films, series, music or live content.

Example: Streaming platforms compete for subscribers.

streaming service

IPA: /ˈstriːmɪŋ ˌsɜːvɪs/
Pronunciation: STREE-ming SUR-vis
French: service de streaming

Definition: A company or platform offering streamed media content.

Example: Many households subscribe to several streaming services.

subscription video on demand

IPA: /səbˈskrɪpʃən ˈvɪdiəʊ ɒn dɪˈmɑːnd/
Pronunciation: suhb-SKRIP-shuhn VID-ee-oh on di-MAHND
French: vidéo à la demande par abonnement

Definition: A model where users pay regularly to access video content on demand.

Example: Subscription video on demand has challenged traditional television.

SVOD

IPA: /ˌes viː əʊ ˈdiː/
Pronunciation: ess-vee-oh-dee
French: SVOD

Definition: Abbreviation for subscription video on demand.

Example: SVOD platforms depend on recurring subscriptions.

video on demand

IPA: /ˌvɪdiəʊ ɒn dɪˈmɑːnd/
Pronunciation: VID-ee-oh on di-MAHND
French: vidéo à la demande

Definition: A system allowing users to watch chosen video content whenever they want.

Example: Video on demand changed audience habits.

VOD

IPA: /ˌviː əʊ ˈdiː/
Pronunciation: vee-oh-dee
French: VOD

Definition: Abbreviation for video on demand.

Example: VOD gives viewers more control.

broadcast television

IPA: /ˈbrɔːdkɑːst ˌtelɪvɪʒən/
Pronunciation: BROD-kast TEL-i-vizh-uhn
French: télévision diffusée ; télévision linéaire

Definition: Television transmitted according to a fixed schedule.

Example: Broadcast television has lost younger audiences.

linear television

IPA: /ˌlɪniə ˈtelɪvɪʒən/
Pronunciation: LIN-ee-uh TEL-i-vizh-uhn
French: télévision linéaire

Definition: Traditional scheduled television where viewers watch at set times.

Example: Linear television still matters for live sport and news.

cable television

IPA: /ˈkeɪbəl ˌtelɪvɪʒən/
Pronunciation: KAY-buhl TEL-i-vizh-uhn
French: télévision câblée

Definition: Television delivered through cable networks, often by subscription.

Example: Cable television has declined in the streaming era.

cord-cutting

IPA: /ˈkɔːd ˌkʌtɪŋ/
Pronunciation: KORD-cut-ing
French: abandon du câble

Definition: Cancelling traditional cable or satellite TV in favour of streaming.

Example: Cord-cutting reshaped the US media market.

pay TV

IPA: /ˌpeɪ tiː ˈviː/
Pronunciation: pay tee-vee
French: télévision payante

Definition: Television channels or services available through paid subscription.

Example: Pay TV faces pressure from cheaper streaming offers.

public broadcaster

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk ˈbrɔːdkɑːstə/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik BROD-kast-uh
French: radiodiffuseur public ; service public audiovisuel

Definition: A broadcaster funded to serve the public interest.

Example: Public broadcasters must compete with global platforms.

audience

IPA: /ˈɔːdiəns/
Pronunciation: AW-dee-uhns
French: public ; audience

Definition: The people who watch, listen to or consume media content.

Example: Streaming platforms track audience behaviour closely.

viewer

IPA: /ˈvjuːə/
Pronunciation: VYOO-uh
French: spectateur ; téléspectateur

Definition: A person who watches television, video or online content.

Example: Viewers expect personalised recommendations.

subscriber

IPA: /səbˈskraɪbə/
Pronunciation: suhb-SKRY-buh
French: abonné

Definition: A person who pays regularly for access to a service.

Example: Platforms try to keep subscribers from cancelling.

subscription

IPA: /səbˈskrɪpʃən/
Pronunciation: suhb-SKRIP-shuhn
French: abonnement

Definition: Regular payment for access to a service.

Example: Subscription fatigue is becoming a problem.

subscription fatigue

IPA: /səbˈskrɪpʃən fəˌtiːɡ/
Pronunciation: suhb-SKRIP-shuhn fuh-TEEG
French: fatigue des abonnements

Definition: Tiredness or frustration caused by too many paid subscriptions.

Example: Subscription fatigue may push viewers back towards piracy or free platforms.

churn

IPA: /tʃɜːn/
Pronunciation: churn
French: désabonnement ; taux d’attrition

Definition: The rate at which subscribers cancel a service.

Example: High churn is a major concern for streaming platforms.

user retention

IPA: /ˈjuːzə rɪˌtenʃən/
Pronunciation: YOO-zuh ri-TEN-shuhn
French: fidélisation des utilisateurs

Definition: Keeping users or subscribers over time.

Example: Exclusive content helps user retention.

content library

IPA: /ˈkɒntent ˌlaɪbrəri/
Pronunciation: KON-tent LY-bruh-ree
French: catalogue de contenus

Definition: The catalogue of films, series or programmes offered by a platform.

Example: A large content library can attract subscribers.

catalogue

IPA: /ˈkætəlɒɡ/
Pronunciation: KAT-uh-log
French: catalogue

Definition: A collection of available content.

Example: The platform removed several films from its catalogue.

original content

IPA: /əˌrɪdʒənəl ˈkɒntent/
Pronunciation: uh-RIJ-uh-nuhl KON-tent
French: contenu original

Definition: Films, series or programmes produced or commissioned by a platform.

Example: Original content is used to differentiate platforms.

exclusive content

IPA: /ɪkˌskluːsɪv ˈkɒntent/
Pronunciation: ik-SKLOO-siv KON-tent
French: contenu exclusif

Definition: Content available only through one platform or provider.

Example: Exclusive content can attract new subscribers.

licensed content

IPA: /ˈlaɪsənst ˈkɒntent/
Pronunciation: LY-suhnst KON-tent
French: contenu sous licence

Definition: Content made by another company and legally distributed under licence.

Example: Licensed content can disappear when contracts expire.

franchise

IPA: /ˈfræntʃaɪz/
Pronunciation: FRAN-chyze
French: franchise

Definition: A series of related films, shows, games or products built around a shared brand or universe.

Example: Major studios rely on franchises to reduce financial risk.

intellectual property

IPA: /ˌɪntəˈlektʃuəl ˈprɒpəti/
Pronunciation: IN-tuh-LEK-choo-uhl PROP-uh-tee
French: propriété intellectuelle

Definition: Creations protected by law, such as stories, characters, music or brands.

Example: Streaming companies invest heavily in intellectual property.

IP

IPA: /ˌaɪ ˈpiː/
Pronunciation: eye-pee
French: PI ; propriété intellectuelle

Definition: Abbreviation for intellectual property.

Example: Hollywood increasingly depends on well-known IP.

adaptation

IPA: /ˌædæpˈteɪʃən/
Pronunciation: ad-ap-TAY-shuhn
French: adaptation

Definition: A film, series or programme based on a book, game, comic or other work.

Example: The adaptation attracted fans of the original novel.

remake

IPA: /ˈriːmeɪk/
Pronunciation: REE-mayk
French: remake ; nouvelle version

Definition: A new version of an older film, series or programme.

Example: Remakes are often criticised for lacking originality.

reboot

IPA: /ˈriːbuːt/
Pronunciation: REE-boot
French: redémarrage ; reboot

Definition: A fresh start for an existing franchise, often with new actors or continuity.

Example: The studio planned a reboot of the franchise.

B. Film, television, franchises and fandom

sequel

IPA: /ˈsiːkwəl/
Pronunciation: SEE-kwuhl
French: suite

Definition: A work continuing the story of an earlier one.

Example: The sequel performed better than expected.

prequel

IPA: /ˈpriːkwəl/
Pronunciation: PREE-kwuhl
French: préquelle

Definition: A work set before the events of an earlier one.

Example: The prequel explains the villain’s origins.

spin-off

IPA: /ˈspɪn ɒf/
Pronunciation: SPIN-off
French: série dérivée ; spin-off

Definition: A new work derived from an existing work, often focused on a secondary character.

Example: The spin-off targeted loyal fans.

cinematic universe

IPA: /ˌsɪnəˈmætɪk ˈjuːnɪvɜːs/
Pronunciation: sin-uh-MAT-ik YOO-ni-vurs
French: univers cinématographique

Definition: A shared fictional world across several films or series.

Example: Cinematic universes encourage long-term fan engagement.

fandom

IPA: /ˈfændəm/
Pronunciation: FAN-duhm
French: communauté de fans

Definition: A community of fans around a cultural product, celebrity or franchise.

Example: Fandom can turn entertainment into identity.

fanbase

IPA: /ˈfænbeɪs/
Pronunciation: FAN-bayss
French: base de fans

Definition: The group of people who strongly support an artist, show or franchise.

Example: A loyal fanbase can make a series profitable.

fan engagement

IPA: /ˈfæn ɪnˌɡeɪdʒmənt/
Pronunciation: FAN in-GAYJ-muhnt
French: engagement des fans

Definition: Interaction between fans and cultural products, creators or communities.

Example: Studios monitor fan engagement online.

shipping

IPA: /ˈʃɪpɪŋ/
Pronunciation: SHIP-ing
French: shipping

Definition: Fans imagining or supporting a romantic relationship between characters.

Example: Shipping can shape online fandom debates.

fan fiction

IPA: /ˈfæn ˌfɪkʃən/
Pronunciation: FAN FIK-shuhn
French: fanfiction

Definition: Stories written by fans using existing characters or worlds.

Example: Fan fiction shows how audiences become creators.

participatory culture

Pronunciation: par-TIS-i-puh-tuh-ree KUL-chuh

Definition: A culture where audiences actively create, share and respond to media.
