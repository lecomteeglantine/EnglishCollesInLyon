The golden rule

Subject pronoun before the verb
Object pronoun after the verb or a preposition
Possessive adjective before a noun
Main usePronouns replace nouns, avoid repetition and create cohesion between sentences.
CPGE habitUse pronouns to connect ideas: The article presents a claim. It then supports it with data.
Key trapDo not confuse its and it’s: its = possessive; it’s = it is / it has.

A. Lesson — What are pronouns?

Pronouns

French: pronoms

Rule / explanation: Pronouns replace nouns to avoid repetition and make sentences clearer.

Example / answer: The article criticises the government. It argues that the policy is unfair.

Subject pronouns

French: pronoms sujets

Rule / explanation: Subject pronouns do the action: I, you, he, she, it, we, they.

Example / answer: They support the reform.

Object pronouns

French: pronoms compléments

Rule / explanation: Object pronouns receive the action: me, you, him, her, it, us, them.

Example / answer: The reform affects them.

Possessive adjectives

French: adjectifs possessifs

Rule / explanation: Possessive adjectives come before a noun: my, your, his, her, its, our, their.

Example / answer: Their argument is convincing.

Possessive pronouns

French: pronoms possessifs

Rule / explanation: Possessive pronouns replace a noun: mine, yours, his, hers, ours, theirs.

Example / answer: This view is theirs, not ours.

Reflexive pronouns

French: pronoms réfléchis

Rule / explanation: Reflexive pronouns refer back to the subject: myself, yourself, himself, herself, itself, ourselves, themselves.

Example / answer: The government blamed itself for the failure.

Demonstrative pronouns

French: pronoms démonstratifs

Rule / explanation: This, that, these, those refer to people, things or ideas near/far in context.

Example / answer: This shows that the issue is complex.

CPGE use

French: usage CPGE

Rule / explanation: Pronouns are essential for avoiding repetition in summaries, essays and khôlles.

Example / answer: The author presents a claim, then supports it with data.

B. Subject and object pronouns

I / me

French: je / moi

Rule / explanation: I is subject; me is object.

Example / answer: I support the idea. / The argument convinced me.

You / you

French: tu/vous

Rule / explanation: You is both subject and object.

Example / answer: You can interpret the chart differently. / The article may surprise you.

He / him

French: il / le/lui

Rule / explanation: He is subject; him is object.

Example / answer: He criticises the policy. / The journalist interviewed him.

She / her

French: elle / la/lui

Rule / explanation: She is subject; her is object.

Example / answer: She supports reform. / The report mentions her.

It / it

French: il/elle/ceci/cela

Rule / explanation: It refers to a thing, idea, institution, article or situation.

Example / answer: The article is convincing because it uses strong evidence.

We / us

French: nous

Rule / explanation: We is subject; us is object.

Example / answer: We must analyse the data. / The crisis affects us all.

They / them

French: ils/elles / les/leur

Rule / explanation: They is subject; them is object.

Example / answer: They oppose the bill. / The reform affects them.

One

French: on

Rule / explanation: One is formal and general, meaning people in general.

Example / answer: One should avoid oversimplification.

C. Possessive forms

My

French: mon / ma / mes

Rule / explanation: Belonging to me.

Example / answer: My interpretation is different.

Your

French: ton / ta / tes / votre / vos

Rule / explanation: Belonging to you.

Example / answer: Your argument needs more evidence.

His

French: son / sa / ses

Rule / explanation: Belonging to him.

Example / answer: His conclusion is too simplistic.

Her

French: son / sa / ses

Rule / explanation: Belonging to her.

Example / answer: Her analysis is nuanced.

Its

French: son / sa / ses pour chose

Rule / explanation: Belonging to it; used for things, institutions and abstract nouns.

Example / answer: The article has its limits.

Our

French: notre / nos

Rule / explanation: Belonging to us.

Example / answer: Our society faces new challenges.

Their

French: leur / leurs

Rule / explanation: Belonging to them.

Example / answer: Their policy failed to reduce poverty.

Mine / yours / hers / ours / theirs

French: le mien / le tien / le leur

Rule / explanation: Possessive pronouns replace noun phrases.

Example / answer: This argument is not mine; it is theirs.

D. This, that, these, those

This

French: ceci / ce / cette

Rule / explanation: Use this for one thing/idea close in text, time or argument.

Example / answer: This suggests that public trust is declining.

That

French: cela / ce / cette

Rule / explanation: Use that for one thing/idea more distant or already mentioned.

Example / answer: That may explain why voters abstained.

These

French: ces-ci / ces

Rule / explanation: Plural of this; close in text or argument.

Example / answer: These figures reveal a worrying trend.

Those

French: ceux-là / ces

Rule / explanation: Plural of that; more distant or already mentioned.

Example / answer: Those who oppose the reform fear higher taxes.

This + noun

French: ce/cette + nom

Rule / explanation: Use this to refer to the current point.

Example / answer: This argument is convincing.

That + noun

French: ce/cette + nom

Rule / explanation: Use that to refer back or create distance.

Example / answer: That claim is not supported by evidence.

This is why

French: c’est pourquoi

Rule / explanation: Useful for consequences.

Example / answer: This is why the issue matters.

That does not mean that

French: cela ne signifie pas que

Rule / explanation: Useful for nuance.

Example / answer: That does not mean that regulation is useless.

E. Relative and indefinite pronouns

Who

French: qui

Rule / explanation: Refers to people in relative clauses.

Example / answer: Voters who feel ignored may abstain.

Which

French: qui / lequel

Rule / explanation: Refers to things, ideas or clauses.

Example / answer: The reform, which was controversial, failed.

That

French: qui / que

Rule / explanation: Can refer to people or things in defining relative clauses.

Example / answer: The argument that the author develops is persuasive.

Whose

French: dont / duquel

Rule / explanation: Expresses possession.

Example / answer: Citizens whose wages stagnate feel insecure.

Someone / somebody

French: quelqu’un

Rule / explanation: An unspecified person.

Example / answer: Someone must take responsibility.

Anyone / anybody

French: quelqu’un / n’importe qui

Rule / explanation: Any person; often in questions and negatives.

Example / answer: Does anyone trust the proposal?

No one / nobody

French: personne

Rule / explanation: No person.

Example / answer: No one can ignore the housing crisis.

Everyone / everybody

French: tout le monde

Rule / explanation: All people in a group.

Example / answer: Everyone is affected by inflation.

F. Pronouns for academic style

It is clear that

French: il est clair que

Rule / explanation: Use it as an impersonal subject.

Example / answer: It is clear that inequality has increased.

It seems that

French: il semble que

Rule / explanation: Use it to soften a claim.

Example / answer: It seems that voters are losing trust.

It could be argued that

French: on pourrait soutenir que

Rule / explanation: Useful for cautious argumentation.

Example / answer: It could be argued that the reform came too late.

There is / there are

French: il y a

Rule / explanation: Use there to introduce existence.

Example / answer: There is a strong link between poverty and health.

One may argue that

French: on peut soutenir que

Rule / explanation: Formal alternative to we/you.

Example / answer: One may argue that the policy is necessary.

We may conclude that

French: nous pouvons conclure que

Rule / explanation: Useful for essays; includes writer and reader.

Example / answer: We may conclude that the reform is insufficient.

They say / people say

French: on dit / ils disent

Rule / explanation: Avoid vague they in formal essays; specify the group if possible.

Example / answer: Weak: They say it is unfair. Better: Critics argue that it is unfair.

This shows that

French: cela montre que

Rule / explanation: Useful for linking evidence to analysis.

Example / answer: This shows that the crisis is structural.

G. Typical mistakes by French speakers

Confusing subject and object pronouns

French: sujet vs complément

Rule / explanation: Use subject pronouns before verbs and object pronouns after verbs/prepositions.

Example / answer: Wrong: Him supports reform. Correct: He supports reform.

Confusing his and her

French: his / her

Rule / explanation: English possessives depend on the possessor, not the object possessed.

Example / answer: Marie and her argument. John and his argument.

Using its with apostrophe

French: its / it’s

Rule / explanation: Its = possessive; it's = it is / it has.

Example / answer: The article has its limits. / It’s convincing.

Repeating the noun too often

French: répétition

Rule / explanation: Use pronouns to avoid repetition, but keep clarity.

Example / answer: The article is interesting. It offers a balanced view.

Using this without clear reference

French: this vague

Rule / explanation: This must clearly refer to an idea or sentence.

Example / answer: Better: This argument / This trend / This problem shows...

Using they vaguely

French: they vague

Rule / explanation: Avoid they if the reference is unclear.

Example / answer: Better: Politicians claim... / Critics argue...

Forgetting object pronouns after prepositions

French: après préposition

Rule / explanation: After about, with, for, to, use object pronouns.

Example / answer: Wrong: for they. Correct: for them.

Confusing who and which

French: who / which

Rule / explanation: Who = people; which = things/ideas.

Example / answer: Voters who... / A policy which...

H. Exercises

Exercise 1 — Choose the right pronoun

French: exercice

Rule / explanation: The article is convincing because ___ uses reliable data. (it / they)

Example / answer: Answer: it

Exercise 2 — Choose the right pronoun

French: exercice

Rule / explanation: Many voters feel ignored, and the reform affects ___. (they / them)

Example / answer: Answer: them

Exercise 3 — Choose the right possessive

French: exercice

Rule / explanation: The government defended ___ policy. (its / it’s)

Example / answer: Answer: its

Exercise 4 — Choose the right pronoun

French: exercice

Rule / explanation: Citizens ___ wages stagnate may feel insecure. (who / whose / which)

Example / answer: Answer: whose

Exercise 5 — Correct the mistake

French: exercice

Rule / explanation: Him argues that democracy is fragile.

Example / answer: Answer: He argues that democracy is fragile.

Exercise 6 — Correct the mistake

French: exercice

Rule / explanation: The article is interesting because the article gives examples.

Example / answer: Answer: The article is interesting because it gives examples.

Exercise 7 — Correct the mistake

French: exercice

Rule / explanation: The government changed it’s position.

Example / answer: Answer: The government changed its position.

Exercise 8 — Correct the mistake

French: exercice

Rule / explanation: This shows that is a serious problem.

Example / answer: Answer: This shows that it is a serious problem. / This shows that the problem is serious.

Exercise 9 — Replace the repeated nouns

French: exercice

Rule / explanation: The reform is controversial because the reform increases taxes.

Example / answer: Answer: The reform is controversial because it increases taxes.

Exercise 10 — Translate

French: exercice

Rule / explanation: L’article soutient que cette réforme a ses limites.

Example / answer: Answer: The article argues that this reform has its limits.

Exercise 11 — Translate

French: exercice

Rule / explanation: Ceux qui se sentent ignorés peuvent s’abstenir.

Example / answer: Answer: Those who feel ignored may abstain.

Exercise 12 — Mini concours challenge

French: exercice

Rule / explanation: Write 6 sentences using: it, they, their, this, those who, it could be argued that.

Example / answer: Possible answer: The article is persuasive because it uses data. Voters feel ignored, and they may abstain. Their frustration is understandable. This reveals a crisis of trust. Those who oppose the reform fear higher taxes. It could be argued that the policy came too late.
