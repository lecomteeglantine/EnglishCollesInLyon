A. Lesson — What are advanced time clauses?

Time clause

Rule / explanation: A time clause situates one action in relation to another: before, after, while, until, once, whenever or by the time.

Example / answer: Once the report is published, Parliament will begin its inquiry.

Sequence

Rule / explanation: Advanced time clauses clarify which event happens first, which continues and which marks a limit.

Example / answer: By the time the reform took effect, public confidence had already declined.

Tense choice

Rule / explanation: The verb tense depends on the time relationship, not only on the connector.

Example / answer: When the minister arrived, the debate had already begun.

CPGE relevance

Rule / explanation: Time clauses are essential for historical narration, policy analysis, article summaries and chronological argumentation.

Example / answer: No sooner had the figures been released than the government changed its position.

B. When, whenever and every time

When

Rule / explanation: Use when for a specific time or a general repeated relationship.

Example / answer: When the bill was introduced, public opposition increased.

Whenever

Rule / explanation: Use whenever for every occasion on which something happens.

Example / answer: Whenever trust declines, political polarisation tends to increase.

Every time

Rule / explanation: Every time is close to whenever but often sounds more concrete.

Example / answer: Every time the government delays reform, costs rise.

Single vs repeated event

Rule / explanation: Context determines whether when refers to one event or a repeated pattern.

Example / answer: When inflation rises, households usually reduce spending.

Future meaning

Rule / explanation: After when, use a present tense for future reference, not will.

Example / answer: When Parliament approves the bill, the law will take effect.

C. While, as and meanwhile

While

Rule / explanation: Use while for two actions or situations continuing at the same time.

Example / answer: While the government was negotiating, protests continued.

As

Rule / explanation: As can express simultaneous development, often with gradual change.

Example / answer: As public confidence declined, support for radical parties increased.

Meanwhile

Rule / explanation: Meanwhile is a sentence adverb linking simultaneous developments.

Example / answer: The government defended the policy. Meanwhile, local authorities demanded more funding.

While vs whereas

Rule / explanation: While can express time or contrast; context must make the meaning clear.

Example / answer: While ministers debated the bill, voters waited for a decision.

Progressive forms

Rule / explanation: Continuous tenses often appear when the time clause describes an ongoing action.

Example / answer: While the committee was reviewing the evidence, new allegations emerged.

D. Before, after and by the time

Before

Rule / explanation: Use before to place one action earlier than another.

Example / answer: The government consulted experts before it introduced the reform.

After

Rule / explanation: Use after to place one action later than another.

Example / answer: After the report was published, Parliament opened an inquiry.

Past perfect

Rule / explanation: Use the past perfect when it helps clarify which past event happened first.

Example / answer: After the committee had reviewed the evidence, it reopened the inquiry.

By the time

Rule / explanation: Use by the time to indicate that one action is already complete when another point is reached.

Example / answer: By the time the law took effect, many firms had already adapted.

Future perfect

Rule / explanation: Use the future perfect in the main clause to describe completion before a future point.

Example / answer: By the time the review ends, the government will have collected new data.

E. Until, till and up to the point when

Until / till

Rule / explanation: Use until or till to mark the endpoint of a continuing action or state.

Example / answer: The government delayed action until public pressure became overwhelming.

Not until

Rule / explanation: Use not until to show that an action did not happen before a particular time.

Example / answer: The minister did not resign until the inquiry ended.

Fronted not until

Rule / explanation: When not until begins the sentence, use inversion in the main clause.

Example / answer: Not until the inquiry ended did the minister resign.

Until vs by

Rule / explanation: Until describes continuation; by describes a deadline or completed action.

Example / answer: The debate continued until midnight. / The vote must be completed by midnight.

Formal alternative

Rule / explanation: Up to the point when is possible but usually more cumbersome.

Example / answer: The policy remained unchanged up to the point when the crisis intensified.

F. Since and ever since

Since + starting point

Rule / explanation: Use since to mark the beginning of a situation continuing to the present or a later reference point.

Example / answer: Public confidence has declined since the scandal began.

Ever since

Rule / explanation: Ever since emphasises continuous development from a past point.

Example / answer: Ever since the reform was introduced, regional tensions have increased.

Present perfect

Rule / explanation: Use the present perfect in the main clause when the situation continues to the present.

Example / answer: The debate has intensified since the report was published.

Past perfect

Rule / explanation: Use the past perfect when the reference point is in the past.

Example / answer: Confidence had fallen steadily since the minister took office.

Since meaning because

Rule / explanation: Since can express time or cause; context must make the meaning clear.

Example / answer: Since the inquiry began, several witnesses have testified.

G. Once, as soon as and the moment

Once

Rule / explanation: Use once to mean when something has been completed or becomes true.

Example / answer: Once the bill is approved, implementation will begin.

As soon as

Rule / explanation: Use as soon as for immediate succession.

Example / answer: As soon as the figures were released, markets reacted.

The moment / minute / instant

Rule / explanation: These expressions emphasise immediate timing.

Example / answer: The moment the minister announced the decision, protests began.

No future will in time clause

Rule / explanation: Use a present tense in future time clauses.

Example / answer: Correct: As soon as the report is published, Parliament will respond.

Perfect form

Rule / explanation: Use a perfect tense when completion matters.

Example / answer: Once the committee has reviewed the evidence, it will issue its findings.

H. Hardly, scarcely and no sooner

Hardly...when

Rule / explanation: Use hardly + past perfect with inversion, followed by when.

Example / answer: Hardly had the debate begun when protesters interrupted it.

Scarcely...when

Rule / explanation: Scarcely follows the same formal pattern.

Example / answer: Scarcely had the figures been published when criticism intensified.

No sooner...than

Rule / explanation: Use no sooner + past perfect with inversion, followed by than.

Example / answer: No sooner had the policy been announced than opposition emerged.

Formal register

Rule / explanation: These structures are highly formal and useful for dramatic or emphatic chronology.

Example / answer: No sooner had the minister apologised than a new allegation appeared.

Word order

Rule / explanation: Fronted negative adverbials require auxiliary–subject inversion.

Example / answer: Wrong: No sooner the report had appeared... Correct: No sooner had the report appeared...

I. As long as, so long as and while

Duration

Rule / explanation: As long as can mean throughout the period during which.

Example / answer: The agreement will remain valid as long as both parties cooperate.

Condition

Rule / explanation: As long as can also mean provided that.

Example / answer: The policy is acceptable as long as civil liberties are protected.

So long as

Rule / explanation: So long as has the same meanings and is slightly more formal or emphatic.

Example / answer: The programme can continue so long as funding remains available.

While for duration

Rule / explanation: While emphasises simultaneous duration.

Example / answer: While the inquiry continued, the minister remained in office.

Context check

Rule / explanation: Distinguish temporal duration from conditional meaning.

Example / answer: As long as inflation remains high, household pressure will continue.

J. By, by then and by the time

By + deadline

Rule / explanation: Use by to mean no later than a specified time.

Example / answer: The committee must publish its report by Friday.

By then

Rule / explanation: Use by then to refer back to a previously mentioned future or past point.

Example / answer: The vote will take place next month. By then, public opinion may have shifted.

By the time + clause

Rule / explanation: Use a full clause when the deadline is an event rather than a date.

Example / answer: By the time Parliament votes, the campaign will have intensified.

Completion

Rule / explanation: By often combines with perfect tenses.

Example / answer: By 2030, the government hopes to have reduced emissions substantially.

Do not confuse with until

Rule / explanation: By marks completion before a deadline; until marks continuation to an endpoint.

Example / answer: The negotiations continued until Friday, but the agreement had been drafted by Thursday.

K. Time clauses with future reference

Present simple after time linker

Rule / explanation: Use the present simple after when, once, before, after, until and as soon as for future meaning.

Example / answer: Once the reform takes effect, costs will begin to fall.

Present perfect for completion

Rule / explanation: Use the present perfect when the time clause must show completed action.

Example / answer: After Parliament has approved the bill, implementation will begin.

Main-clause future

Rule / explanation: The future form normally appears in the main clause.

Example / answer: The government will respond when the inquiry publishes its findings.

Modal result

Rule / explanation: The main clause may use may, might, can or should instead of will.

Example / answer: Once the data are available, the committee may revise its conclusion.

Will for willingness

Rule / explanation: Will may appear in a time clause only when it expresses willingness or insistence, not ordinary future time.

Example / answer: When the government will listen, meaningful reform may become possible.

L. Reduced time clauses

When + participle

Rule / explanation: A time clause may be reduced when both clauses share the same subject.

Example / answer: When questioned, the minister refused to comment.

While + -ing

Rule / explanation: Use while + verb-ing for simultaneous actions.

Example / answer: While analysing the figures, the author overlooks regional differences.

Before / after + -ing

Rule / explanation: Use a gerund after before or after when the subject is shared.

Example / answer: After reviewing the evidence, the committee reopened the inquiry.

Once + past participle

Rule / explanation: Use once + past participle for a completed passive stage.

Example / answer: Once approved, the law will take effect immediately.

Subject safety

Rule / explanation: Do not reduce if the implied subject would be unclear or different.

Example / answer: Correct: After reviewing the report, the committee changed its conclusion.

M. Time relationships in historical narration

Background vs event

Rule / explanation: Use the past continuous for background and the past simple for the main event.

Example / answer: While Parliament was debating the bill, protests erupted outside.

Earlier past

Rule / explanation: Use the past perfect for an event completed before another past event.

Example / answer: By the time the government acted, the crisis had already deepened.

Sequence markers

Rule / explanation: Use initially, subsequently, eventually and in the meantime to organise chronology.

Example / answer: Initially, the policy gained support; subsequently, criticism intensified.

Turning point

Rule / explanation: Use once, after or the moment to mark a decisive transition.

Example / answer: Once the scandal became public, the minister’s position became untenable.

Counterfactual time

Rule / explanation: Use conditionals when discussing what might have happened at an earlier stage.

Example / answer: Had the authorities acted sooner, the damage might have been limited.

N. Time clauses in article summaries

Publication sequence

Rule / explanation: Use time clauses to relate reporting, reaction and consequence.

Example / answer: After the figures were released, the article describes a rapid political response.

Long-term development

Rule / explanation: Use since and over time to trace continuing change.

Example / answer: Since the reform was introduced, public support has steadily declined.

Immediate reaction

Rule / explanation: Use as soon as or the moment for rapid response.

Example / answer: As soon as the decision was announced, opposition parties demanded a vote.

Parallel developments

Rule / explanation: Use while or meanwhile to compare simultaneous trends.

Example / answer: While employment rose, real wages remained stagnant.

Analytical chronology

Rule / explanation: Chronology should support interpretation, not merely list events.

Example / answer: By the time ministers acknowledged the problem, public trust had already collapsed.

O. Time clauses in CPGE essays

Policy implementation

Rule / explanation: Use time clauses to distinguish announcement, implementation and later effects.

Example / answer: Once the policy was implemented, its regional consequences became visible.

Long-term argument

Rule / explanation: Use since and by the time to show development over a period.

Example / answer: Since the financial crisis, demands for stronger regulation have increased.

Cause through sequence

Rule / explanation: Chronology can suggest causation, but sequence alone does not prove it.

Example / answer: After taxes rose, investment fell; however, other factors may also explain the decline.

Forecast

Rule / explanation: Use future time clauses to discuss expected developments.

Example / answer: When demographic pressures intensify, governments will face difficult choices.

Conclusion

Rule / explanation: Use temporal perspective to assess short- and long-term outcomes.

Example / answer: Although the reform produced immediate savings, its social costs became clearer over time.

P. Typical mistakes by French speakers

Will after when

Rule / explanation: Do not use will in an ordinary future time clause.

Example / answer: Wrong: When Parliament will approve the bill... Correct: When Parliament approves the bill...

Since + wrong tense

Rule / explanation: Use a perfect tense when a situation continues from a past point.

Example / answer: Wrong: Confidence declines since 2024. Correct: Confidence has declined since 2024.

Until vs by

Rule / explanation: Use until for continuation and by for a deadline.

Example / answer: The debate continued until midnight. / Submit the report by midnight.

No sooner connector

Rule / explanation: Use than after no sooner, but when after hardly or scarcely.

Example / answer: No sooner...than / Hardly...when

Missing inversion

Rule / explanation: Fronted hardly, scarcely, no sooner and not until require inversion.

Example / answer: Not until the inquiry ended did the minister resign.

Ambiguous as

Rule / explanation: As may mean while, because or in the role of; use a clearer linker when needed.

Example / answer: Prefer while if the intended meaning is purely temporal.

Q. Exercises

1. Future time clause

Rule / explanation: When Parliament ___ the bill, the law will take effect. (approve)

Example / answer: When Parliament approves the bill, the law will take effect.

2. Use by the time

Rule / explanation: The government acted. Before that, the crisis had already deepened.

Example / answer: By the time the government acted, the crisis had already deepened.

3. Choose until or by

Rule / explanation: The committee must publish its report ___ Friday.

Example / answer: The committee must publish its report by Friday.

4. Add inversion

Rule / explanation: The minister resigned only after the inquiry ended.

Example / answer: Not until the inquiry ended did the minister resign.

5. Rewrite with no sooner

Rule / explanation: The policy was announced. Opposition emerged immediately.

Example / answer: No sooner had the policy been announced than opposition emerged.

6. Reduce the clause

Rule / explanation: After the committee reviewed the evidence, it reopened the inquiry.

Example / answer: After reviewing the evidence, the committee reopened the inquiry.

7. Translate

Rule / explanation: D’ici à ce que le Parlement vote, l’opinion publique aura peut-être changé.

Example / answer: By the time Parliament votes, public opinion may have changed.

8. Mini concours challenge

Rule / explanation: Write a paragraph using once, while, by the time, since and no sooner...than.

Example / answer: Example: Once the reform was introduced, criticism intensified. While ministers defended the measure, local authorities reported growing difficulties. By the time Parliament opened an inquiry, public trust had already fallen sharply. Since then, support has continued to decline. No sooner had the government promised revisions than a new controversy emerged.
