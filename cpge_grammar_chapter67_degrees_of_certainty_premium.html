Chapter 67 — Degrees of Certainty
B2 → C1 grammar for probability, evidence, academic caution and precisely calibrated claims.

Chapter objective

This premium grammar chapter develops advanced control of degrees of certainty: certainty adverbs, probability, possibility, doubt, impossibility, evidence markers, hedging, boosters and precise CPGE argumentation.

The golden rule

Match the strength of the language to the strength of the evidence.
must / certainly → strong certainty
probably / likely → probability
may / might / could → possibility
unlikely / cannot → improbability or impossibility
Strong certaintymust, certainly, undoubtedly, clearly
Probabilityprobably, likely, should, presumably
Possibilitymay, might, could, perhaps
Doubt / impossibilityunlikely, doubtful, cannot, by no means

A. Lesson — Why degrees of certainty matter

Certainty scale

Rule / explanation: English offers many ways to show whether a statement is certain, probable, possible, doubtful or impossible.

Example / answer: The reform will certainly affect public spending.

Academic caution

Rule / explanation: A strong claim requires strong evidence; weaker evidence calls for more cautious language.

Example / answer: The figures appear to indicate a gradual recovery.

Speaker stance

Rule / explanation: Certainty markers reveal how strongly the writer or speaker commits to a proposition.

Example / answer: The author clearly regards inequality as a structural issue.

CPGE relevance

Rule / explanation: Precise calibration improves article commentary, essays, summaries, debate and oral analysis.

Example / answer: The policy may reduce costs, but it is unlikely to solve the underlying problem.

B. Absolute and near-absolute certainty

Certainly

Rule / explanation: Use certainly for a conclusion presented with very high confidence.

Example / answer: The crisis will certainly influence the next election.

Definitely

Rule / explanation: Definitely is strong and common, though slightly less formal than certainly.

Example / answer: The reform has definitely changed the political debate.

Undoubtedly

Rule / explanation: Undoubtedly strongly asserts that little reasonable doubt remains.

Example / answer: The scandal has undoubtedly weakened public trust.

Clearly

Rule / explanation: Clearly presents a conclusion as evident from the available information.

Example / answer: The author clearly rejects the official explanation.

Beyond doubt

Rule / explanation: Beyond doubt and beyond dispute express exceptionally strong certainty and should be used sparingly.

Example / answer: The evidence establishes beyond doubt that the figures were altered.

C. Strong probability

Probably

Rule / explanation: Probably presents an outcome or explanation as more likely than not.

Example / answer: The government will probably revise the proposal.

Very likely

Rule / explanation: Very likely expresses strong probability in an adjective phrase.

Example / answer: A further delay is very likely.

In all likelihood

Rule / explanation: This formal phrase introduces a highly probable conclusion.

Example / answer: In all likelihood, the coalition will seek a compromise.

There is every likelihood that

Rule / explanation: Use this formal construction to emphasise strong probability.

Example / answer: There is every likelihood that costs will continue to rise.

Be bound to

Rule / explanation: Be bound to expresses a strong expectation based on circumstances.

Example / answer: The decision is bound to provoke criticism.

D. Reasonable expectation

Should

Rule / explanation: Should can express an expected result under normal conditions.

Example / answer: The new measures should reduce waiting times.

Ought to

Rule / explanation: Ought to can also express reasonable expectation, though it is less frequent.

Example / answer: The additional funding ought to improve access.

Be expected to

Rule / explanation: This neutral formal structure reports an anticipated development.

Example / answer: Inflation is expected to fall gradually.

Be likely to

Rule / explanation: Use be likely to for a probable event or characteristic.

Example / answer: The reform is likely to face legal challenges.

Presumably

Rule / explanation: Presumably marks a conclusion based on what seems reasonable or known.

Example / answer: Presumably, the committee will examine the new evidence.

E. Possibility and uncertainty

May

Rule / explanation: May expresses a real but uncertain possibility.

Example / answer: The policy may produce unintended consequences.

Might

Rule / explanation: Might often sounds more tentative or remote than may.

Example / answer: The decline might reflect demographic change.

Could

Rule / explanation: Could presents one possible explanation or outcome among others.

Example / answer: The discrepancy could result from a technical error.

Possibly

Rule / explanation: Possibly weakens commitment to the proposition.

Example / answer: The government could possibly delay the vote.

It is possible that

Rule / explanation: This explicit structure is useful in formal argument.

Example / answer: It is possible that the survey underestimates rural dissatisfaction.

F. Weak or remote possibility

Perhaps

Rule / explanation: Perhaps introduces a tentative possibility and often softens a claim.

Example / answer: Perhaps the author places too much confidence in national averages.

Conceivably

Rule / explanation: Conceivably means that something is imaginable, though not necessarily likely.

Example / answer: The measure could conceivably increase regional inequality.

It is conceivable that

Rule / explanation: This formal structure introduces a remote but arguable possibility.

Example / answer: It is conceivable that the reform will have no lasting effect.

There is a slight chance that

Rule / explanation: Use this for a low-probability outcome.

Example / answer: There is a slight chance that the vote will be postponed.

Not impossible

Rule / explanation: This understated form presents possibility without strong endorsement.

Example / answer: It is not impossible that public opinion will shift.

G. Doubt and improbability

Unlikely

Rule / explanation: Be unlikely to or it is unlikely that expresses low probability.

Example / answer: The reform is unlikely to satisfy all stakeholders.

Highly unlikely

Rule / explanation: Use this for a very improbable event or explanation.

Example / answer: It is highly unlikely that one factor alone caused the decline.

Doubtful

Rule / explanation: Doubtful expresses scepticism about truth or outcome.

Example / answer: It is doubtful whether the measure will survive parliamentary scrutiny.

I doubt whether

Rule / explanation: Use this to express personal or analytical scepticism.

Example / answer: I doubt whether the evidence supports such a broad conclusion.

There is little chance that

Rule / explanation: This phrase expresses weak probability.

Example / answer: There is little chance that the crisis will disappear without intervention.

H. Impossibility and rejection

Cannot

Rule / explanation: Cannot may express logical or practical impossibility.

Example / answer: Economic growth cannot by itself guarantee social cohesion.

Can’t possibly

Rule / explanation: This strongly rejects a proposition, especially in speech.

Example / answer: The committee can’t possibly have reviewed all the files.

There is no possibility that

Rule / explanation: This explicit phrase states impossibility very strongly.

Example / answer: There is no possibility that the system can be replaced overnight.

It is impossible to

Rule / explanation: Use this for objective or practical impossibility.

Example / answer: It is impossible to assess the policy without reliable data.

By no means

Rule / explanation: By no means strongly rejects an interpretation.

Example / answer: The recovery is by no means complete.

I. Modal verbs and certainty

Must

Rule / explanation: Must expresses a strong positive deduction based on evidence.

Example / answer: The author must regard the situation as urgent.

Can’t / cannot

Rule / explanation: These express strong negative deduction.

Example / answer: The figures cannot be accurate.

May / might / could

Rule / explanation: These express different shades of possibility rather than certainty.

Example / answer: The fall may reflect economic insecurity.

Should

Rule / explanation: Should expresses expectation rather than logical certainty.

Example / answer: The report should be available tomorrow.

Will

Rule / explanation: Will may express confident prediction or characteristic behaviour.

Example / answer: The issue will remain politically sensitive.

J. Adverbs of certainty

Position with be

Rule / explanation: Place many certainty adverbs after be.

Example / answer: The outcome is probably inevitable.

Before a lexical verb

Rule / explanation: Place them before the main verb.

Example / answer: The policy will probably increase costs.

After an auxiliary

Rule / explanation: Place them after the first auxiliary in a verb phrase.

Example / answer: The government has probably underestimated public anger.

Sentence adverbs

Rule / explanation: Apparently, arguably, presumably and undoubtedly may appear initially.

Example / answer: Apparently, negotiations have resumed.

Emphasis

Rule / explanation: Initial position can foreground the writer’s stance.

Example / answer: Undoubtedly, the reform has transformed the debate.

K. Adjectives and noun phrases

Certain / sure

Rule / explanation: Use be certain or be sure for strong confidence.

Example / answer: The committee is certain that the figures were altered.

Likely / unlikely

Rule / explanation: Use adjective + to-infinitive or a that-clause.

Example / answer: The policy is likely to face opposition.

Possible / probable

Rule / explanation: These commonly appear in impersonal structures.

Example / answer: It is probable that turnout will fall.

The likelihood of

Rule / explanation: Use this noun phrase for formal analysis.

Example / answer: The likelihood of early elections has increased.

A possibility that

Rule / explanation: Use this to introduce one option without endorsing it strongly.

Example / answer: There is a possibility that the figures are incomplete.

L. Verbs expressing certainty or uncertainty

Know

Rule / explanation: Know presents information as established from the speaker’s perspective.

Example / answer: We know that regional disparities have widened.

Believe

Rule / explanation: Believe signals commitment without claiming direct proof.

Example / answer: Many analysts believe that the reform will fail.

Assume

Rule / explanation: Assume treats a proposition as true for reasoning, sometimes without proof.

Example / answer: The model assumes that consumer behaviour remains stable.

Suspect

Rule / explanation: Suspect expresses a tentative belief, often based on incomplete evidence.

Example / answer: Researchers suspect that the sample is unrepresentative.

Doubt

Rule / explanation: Doubt signals low confidence in a proposition.

Example / answer: The author doubts that voluntary regulation will be sufficient.

M. Seem, appear and tend

Seem to

Rule / explanation: Seem to presents an inference based on appearance or evidence.

Example / answer: The economy seems to be recovering.

Appear to

Rule / explanation: Appear to is slightly more formal and useful in academic prose.

Example / answer: The policy appears to have widened regional gaps.

It seems / appears that

Rule / explanation: Use an impersonal clause to soften the claim.

Example / answer: It appears that public confidence is declining.

Tend to

Rule / explanation: Tend to describes a general pattern rather than an absolute rule.

Example / answer: Younger voters tend to distrust traditional parties.

Seemingly

Rule / explanation: Seemingly means apparently, sometimes with a contrast between appearance and reality.

Example / answer: A seemingly minor change produced major consequences.

N. Evidence-based certainty

According to the evidence

Rule / explanation: Use this phrase to anchor a claim in available data.

Example / answer: According to the evidence, access has improved in urban areas.

The data indicate that

Rule / explanation: Indicate is cautious and evidence-based.

Example / answer: The data indicate that inequality remains persistent.

The evidence suggests that

Rule / explanation: Suggest presents a plausible conclusion without claiming proof.

Example / answer: The evidence suggests that the policy has had mixed results.

The findings demonstrate that

Rule / explanation: Demonstrate expresses stronger evidential support.

Example / answer: The findings demonstrate that regional gaps have widened.

No evidence that

Rule / explanation: Use this to reject an unsupported assertion carefully.

Example / answer: There is no evidence that the reform caused the decline.

O. Certainty and source attribution

Authorial certainty

Rule / explanation: Make clear when certainty belongs to the source rather than to you.

Example / answer: The author insists that the reform has succeeded.

Your interpretation

Rule / explanation: Use a separate stance marker for your own evaluation.

Example / answer: The evidence, however, appears insufficient to support this conclusion.

Claim vs fact

Rule / explanation: Do not convert a source’s claim into an established fact.

Example / answer: The government claims that costs have fallen.

Distance

Rule / explanation: Allegedly, reportedly and according to can create distance.

Example / answer: Officials reportedly ignored several warnings.

Comparing sources

Rule / explanation: Different certainty levels may reveal disagreement.

Example / answer: One article presents failure as inevitable, whereas the other treats it as merely possible.

P. Academic hedging

Why hedge?

Rule / explanation: Hedging prevents overstatement and leaves room for complexity or uncertainty.

Example / answer: The reform may have contributed to the decline.

Common hedges

Rule / explanation: Use may, might, could, appear to, seem to, suggest and arguably.

Example / answer: The policy could be seen as a partial success.

Double hedging

Rule / explanation: Avoid piling up too many weakeners.

Example / answer: Weak: It might perhaps possibly suggest...

Appropriate strength

Rule / explanation: Hedging should reflect evidence, not fear of taking a position.

Example / answer: The evidence strongly suggests that implementation was uneven.

Balanced confidence

Rule / explanation: A strong essay combines clear claims with carefully calibrated limits.

Example / answer: The measure reduced costs, although its long-term effects remain uncertain.

Q. Boosters and strong commitment

Boosters

Rule / explanation: Boosters increase certainty or emphasis.

Example / answer: The reform clearly failed to address regional inequality.

Common boosters

Rule / explanation: Clearly, undoubtedly, certainly, indeed and demonstrably are frequent examples.

Example / answer: The data demonstrably contradict the official narrative.

Risk

Rule / explanation: Boosters can make weak evidence sound overstated.

Example / answer: Do not write clearly when the conclusion is disputed.

Evidence first

Rule / explanation: Use strong language only after establishing support.

Example / answer: The repeated findings strongly indicate a structural problem.

Contrast with hedges

Rule / explanation: A sophisticated paragraph may hedge one point and strongly assert another.

Example / answer: The policy may have reduced costs, but it clearly failed to improve access.

R. Certainty in article commentary

Describe the author’s stance

Rule / explanation: Identify whether the source sounds certain, cautious, speculative or doubtful.

Example / answer: The journalist presents the reform’s failure as virtually inevitable.

Analyse language

Rule / explanation: Comment on modals, adverbs, verbs and qualifiers.

Example / answer: The repeated use of may and might creates a deliberately cautious tone.

Evaluate support

Rule / explanation: Compare linguistic confidence with the evidence provided.

Example / answer: The author’s certainty appears stronger than the data justify.

Detect strategic uncertainty

Rule / explanation: A writer may hedge to avoid legal or factual overcommitment.

Example / answer: The word allegedly distances the newspaper from the accusation.

Detect strategic certainty

Rule / explanation: Strong certainty can encourage the reader to treat interpretation as fact.

Example / answer: The adverb clearly discourages alternative readings.

S. Certainty in CPGE essays and khôlles

State a thesis

Rule / explanation: Commit clearly when your argument is well supported.

Example / answer: Institutional trust is essential to democratic legitimacy.

Qualify debatable causes

Rule / explanation: Use cautious language for complex causal claims.

Example / answer: Economic insecurity may partly explain the rise in political distrust.

Reject simplistic explanations

Rule / explanation: Use cannot alone or by itself to set limits.

Example / answer: The trend cannot be attributed to social media alone.

Compare hypotheses

Rule / explanation: Use may, might and could to organise alternatives.

Example / answer: The decline may reflect fatigue, but it could also result from disillusionment.

Conclude proportionately

Rule / explanation: Match the conclusion to the evidence gathered.

Example / answer: The reform is likely to produce modest gains rather than a complete transformation.

T. Certainty scale for CPGE writing

100% / fact

Rule / explanation: Use plain declarative form for verified facts.

Example / answer: The bill passed by a narrow majority.

Very strong

Rule / explanation: Use clearly, undoubtedly, must or certainly when justified.

Example / answer: The scandal has undoubtedly weakened the minister.

Probable

Rule / explanation: Use probably, likely, should or in all likelihood.

Example / answer: The controversy is likely to continue.

Possible

Rule / explanation: Use may, might, could, perhaps or it is possible that.

Example / answer: The policy may create new inequalities.

Improbable / impossible

Rule / explanation: Use unlikely, doubtful, cannot or by no means.

Example / answer: The problem is unlikely to disappear quickly.

U. Common mistakes by French speakers

Maybe position

Rule / explanation: Maybe is a sentence adverb; may is a modal verb.

Example / answer: Maybe the policy will work. / The policy may work.

Likely as adverb

Rule / explanation: In standard British English, prefer is likely to rather than will likely.

Example / answer: The government is likely to respond.

Certainty mismatch

Rule / explanation: Do not use must, certainly or prove with weak evidence.

Example / answer: Use may, suggest or appear when uncertainty remains.

Double modal

Rule / explanation: Do not combine two modal auxiliaries directly.

Example / answer: Wrong: may could happen. Correct: may happen / could happen.

Probably placement

Rule / explanation: Place probably after the first auxiliary but before the main lexical verb.

Example / answer: The government will probably act.

Confusing doubt that / doubt whether

Rule / explanation: Use doubt whether for an open alternative; doubt that commonly implies disbelief.

Example / answer: I doubt whether the reform will work.

V. Exercises

1. Strong certainty

Rule / explanation: The evidence is overwhelming. The figures ___ have been altered.

Example / answer: The figures must have been altered.

2. Probability

Rule / explanation: There is a strong chance that the government will revise the plan.

Example / answer: The government is likely to revise the plan.

3. Possibility

Rule / explanation: Perhaps the survey underestimates rural dissatisfaction.

Example / answer: The survey may / might underestimate rural dissatisfaction.

4. Improbability

Rule / explanation: It probably will not satisfy every stakeholder.

Example / answer: It is unlikely to satisfy every stakeholder.

5. Correct the error

Rule / explanation: The reform may could increase costs.

Example / answer: The reform may increase costs. / The reform could increase costs.

6. Calibrate the claim

Rule / explanation: The evidence is limited: The policy definitely caused the decline.

Example / answer: The policy may have contributed to the decline.

7. Translate

Rule / explanation: Il est fort probable que la controverse se poursuive.

Example / answer: The controversy is very likely to continue. / In all likelihood, the controversy will continue.

8. Mini concours challenge

Rule / explanation: Write a paragraph moving from possibility to probability to strong certainty.

Example / answer: Example: The decline may partly reflect economic insecurity, and demographic change could also have contributed. However, the repeated regional pattern makes a structural explanation increasingly likely. What is certain is that the trend cannot be reduced to one isolated event.
