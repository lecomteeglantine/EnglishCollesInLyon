B2 → C1 grammar for source reporting, article synthesis, indirect questions and formal academic analysis.

Chapter objective

This premium grammar chapter develops advanced control of reported speech: tense backshift, viewpoint changes, reported questions, commands, suggestions, modal verbs, reporting verbs, passive reporting structures and CPGE source synthesis.

The golden rule

Report meaning, not words mechanically.
Adapt tense, pronouns, time and viewpoint only when the reporting context requires it.
“We will act tomorrow.” → They said that they would act the following day.
Statementssay / tell / explain / claim / acknowledge + that-clause
Questionsask + wh-word / whether + statement word order
Commands and suggestionstell/ask + object + to-infinitive / suggest + gerund or that-clause

A. Lesson — What is advanced reported speech?

Reported speech

Rule / explanation: Reported speech conveys what someone said, asked, promised, denied or suggested without necessarily repeating the exact words.

Example / answer: The minister said that the reform would take effect the following year.

Direct vs indirect speech

Rule / explanation: Direct speech reproduces the original words; indirect speech adapts grammar, pronouns, time references and perspective.

Example / answer: Direct: “We will act tomorrow.” Reported: They said that they would act the following day.

Reporting perspective

Rule / explanation: Changes depend on who reports the words, when they report them and whether the original statement is still valid.

Example / answer: The scientist explained that the data remain relevant today.

CPGE relevance

Rule / explanation: Advanced reported speech is essential for article summaries, source comparison, oral commentary and academic synthesis.

Example / answer: The author argues that the policy has failed and warns that further delays could worsen the crisis.

B. Backshift of tenses

Present simple → past simple

Rule / explanation: After a past reporting verb, present statements commonly shift into the past.

Example / answer: “The policy is effective.” → She said that the policy was effective.

Present continuous → past continuous

Rule / explanation: Ongoing present actions commonly shift one step back.

Example / answer: “The economy is recovering.” → He said that the economy was recovering.

Present perfect → past perfect

Rule / explanation: A completed present-related action commonly shifts to the past perfect.

Example / answer: “Costs have increased.” → They reported that costs had increased.

Past simple → past perfect

Rule / explanation: Past events often shift to the past perfect to show earlier time.

Example / answer: “The government ignored the warning.” → She claimed that the government had ignored the warning.

Will → would

Rule / explanation: Future forms normally shift from will to would.

Example / answer: “We will publish the report.” → They said that they would publish the report.

C. When backshift is optional or unnecessary

Still true

Rule / explanation: Do not backshift a statement that remains generally or scientifically true.

Example / answer: The researcher explained that inequality affects social mobility.

Current relevance

Rule / explanation: A tense may remain unchanged if the speaker’s statement is still valid at the reporting moment.

Example / answer: The minister said that the reform is still under review.

Immediate reporting

Rule / explanation: Backshift is often unnecessary when the report follows the original statement immediately.

Example / answer: She says that she needs more evidence.

Historical facts

Rule / explanation: Established facts usually remain in their original tense.

Example / answer: The lecturer explained that the Second World War ended in 1945.

Writer’s choice

Rule / explanation: Backshift can signal distance, while no backshift can signal continuing validity.

Example / answer: The article reported that the policy was effective / is effective.

D. Pronouns, possessives and viewpoint

Pronoun change

Rule / explanation: Pronouns change according to the new speaker and listener perspective.

Example / answer: “I support our proposal.” → The minister said that she supported their proposal.

Possessive change

Rule / explanation: Possessive adjectives and pronouns must match the reported viewpoint.

Example / answer: “My department has completed its work.” → He said that his department had completed its work.

You

Rule / explanation: You may become I, we, he, she, they or remain you depending on the addressee.

Example / answer: “You must act.” → She told the government that it had to act.

We

Rule / explanation: We may include or exclude the reporter, so context determines the correct pronoun.

Example / answer: “We will cooperate.” → The two parties said that they would cooperate.

Avoid mechanical change

Rule / explanation: Change only what perspective requires.

Example / answer: The journalist said, “The public deserves answers.” → The journalist said that the public deserved answers.

E. Time and place reference changes

Today

Rule / explanation: Today often becomes that day.

Example / answer: “The vote takes place today.” → He said that the vote took place that day.

Tomorrow

Rule / explanation: Tomorrow often becomes the next day, the following day or the day after.

Example / answer: “We will respond tomorrow.” → They said that they would respond the following day.

Yesterday

Rule / explanation: Yesterday often becomes the previous day or the day before.

Example / answer: “The figures were released yesterday.” → She said that the figures had been released the day before.

Now

Rule / explanation: Now often becomes then or at that time.

Example / answer: “We are reviewing the evidence now.” → They said that they were reviewing the evidence then.

Here / this / these

Rule / explanation: Here may become there; this and these may become that and those.

Example / answer: “This proposal will work here.” → He claimed that that proposal would work there.

F. Reporting statements with say and tell

Say

Rule / explanation: Say is followed by a clause; an addressee requires say to + person.

Example / answer: The minister said that the policy would change. / She said to reporters that negotiations would continue.

Tell

Rule / explanation: Tell normally requires an object referring to the listener.

Example / answer: The minister told reporters that negotiations would continue.

No tell that alone

Rule / explanation: Do not omit the person after tell.

Example / answer: Wrong: She told that the policy would change. Correct: She said that... / She told us that...

Tell + object + infinitive

Rule / explanation: Use tell + person + to-infinitive for orders or instructions.

Example / answer: The committee told the minister to provide further evidence.

Formal alternatives

Rule / explanation: State, explain, announce, declare, observe and point out can replace say.

Example / answer: The report states that regional inequality has increased.

G. Reported questions

Statement word order

Rule / explanation: Reported questions use subject–verb order, not question inversion.

Example / answer: “Why did the reform fail?” → She asked why the reform had failed.

Wh-questions

Rule / explanation: Keep the original question word.

Example / answer: “When will the report appear?” → He asked when the report would appear.

Yes/no questions

Rule / explanation: Use if or whether before the reported clause.

Example / answer: “Is the evidence reliable?” → She asked whether the evidence was reliable.

No question mark

Rule / explanation: A reported question is normally a statement grammatically and does not require a question mark.

Example / answer: The journalist asked why the government had delayed action.

Do-support disappears

Rule / explanation: Do, does and did are not retained as question auxiliaries.

Example / answer: “What does the policy achieve?” → He asked what the policy achieved.

H. Whether, if and alternative questions

Whether or not

Rule / explanation: Use whether or not when both alternatives are explicit or relevant.

Example / answer: The committee asked whether or not the figures were accurate.

Whether...or

Rule / explanation: Use whether before alternative choices.

Example / answer: She asked whether the government would reform the system or abandon it.

After prepositions

Rule / explanation: Use whether, not if, after a preposition.

Example / answer: The debate concerned whether the reform should continue.

Before infinitives

Rule / explanation: Use whether before to + infinitive.

Example / answer: The government had to decide whether to intervene.

Formal preference

Rule / explanation: Whether is often preferred in formal reported questions.

Example / answer: The article asks whether regulation can protect innovation.

I. Reported commands, requests and advice

Command

Rule / explanation: Use tell/order/instruct + object + to-infinitive.

Example / answer: “Publish the report.” → The committee ordered the ministry to publish the report.

Negative command

Rule / explanation: Use not to + infinitive.

Example / answer: “Do not disclose the data.” → They instructed officials not to disclose the data.

Request

Rule / explanation: Use ask + object + to-infinitive.

Example / answer: “Please reconsider the proposal.” → She asked the government to reconsider the proposal.

Advice

Rule / explanation: Use advise/urge/encourage + object + to-infinitive.

Example / answer: “Invest more in public services.” → The report urged the government to invest more in public services.

Warning

Rule / explanation: Use warn + object + not to / that-clause / of + noun.

Example / answer: The experts warned ministers not to delay further.

J. Reported suggestions and proposals

Suggest + gerund

Rule / explanation: Use suggest + verb-ing when no separate subject is needed.

Example / answer: “Let us postpone the vote.” → She suggested postponing the vote.

Suggest + that-clause

Rule / explanation: Use suggest that + subject + base form or should + base form.

Example / answer: She suggested that Parliament postpone the vote.

Propose

Rule / explanation: Propose follows similar patterns in formal contexts.

Example / answer: The committee proposed that the law be amended.

Recommend

Rule / explanation: Recommend may be followed by a gerund or a that-clause.

Example / answer: The report recommended increasing regional funding.

No suggest someone to

Rule / explanation: Do not use suggest + object + to-infinitive.

Example / answer: Wrong: She suggested Parliament to postpone the vote.

K. Modal verbs in reported speech

Can → could

Rule / explanation: Can commonly shifts to could.

Example / answer: “The policy can work.” → He said that the policy could work.

May → might

Rule / explanation: May commonly shifts to might.

Example / answer: “The reform may fail.” → She warned that the reform might fail.

Must → had to

Rule / explanation: Must usually becomes had to when it expresses external obligation.

Example / answer: “We must act now.” → They said that they had to act immediately.

Must may remain must

Rule / explanation: Must may remain unchanged for continuing obligation, prohibition or logical deduction.

Example / answer: The report states that governments must protect civil liberties.

Would, could, should, might

Rule / explanation: These modals often remain unchanged.

Example / answer: “The policy could succeed.” → She said that the policy could succeed.

L. Reporting verbs and meaning

Admit

Rule / explanation: Admit acknowledges an unwelcome or previously denied fact.

Example / answer: The minister admitted that implementation had been poor.

Deny

Rule / explanation: Deny rejects an accusation or claim and is followed by a noun, gerund or that-clause.

Example / answer: The company denied misleading the public.

Claim

Rule / explanation: Claim reports a statement whose truth the reporter may not accept.

Example / answer: The government claimed that the reform had reduced costs.

Insist

Rule / explanation: Insist presents a statement or demand forcefully.

Example / answer: The minister insisted that the policy remained necessary.

Acknowledge

Rule / explanation: Acknowledge recognises a fact or limitation.

Example / answer: The author acknowledges that the evidence is incomplete.

M. Reporting verbs with prepositions

Accuse someone of

Rule / explanation: Use accuse + object + of + gerund.

Example / answer: The opposition accused the government of hiding the figures.

Apologise for

Rule / explanation: Use apologise for + noun or gerund.

Example / answer: The minister apologised for delaying the report.

Blame someone for

Rule / explanation: Use blame + object + for + noun or gerund.

Example / answer: Critics blamed ministers for ignoring local concerns.

Congratulate someone on

Rule / explanation: Use congratulate + object + on + noun or gerund.

Example / answer: The committee congratulated researchers on completing the study.

Warn someone against

Rule / explanation: Use warn + object + against + gerund.

Example / answer: Experts warned the government against cutting investment.

N. Reporting beliefs and impersonal passive

People say that

Rule / explanation: A general reporting clause may be expressed with people say, experts believe or critics argue.

Example / answer: Experts believe that the policy will fail.

It is said that

Rule / explanation: Use an impersonal passive for formal or impersonal reporting.

Example / answer: It is said that the policy will fail.

Subject + is said to

Rule / explanation: Use subject + passive reporting verb + infinitive.

Example / answer: The policy is said to be ineffective.

Perfect infinitive

Rule / explanation: Use to have + past participle when the reported action happened earlier.

Example / answer: The minister is believed to have ignored several warnings.

Common verbs

Rule / explanation: Say, believe, think, consider, expect, report, allege and understand often appear in this pattern.

Example / answer: The company is alleged to have concealed important data.

O. Reported speech in article summaries

Neutral reporting

Rule / explanation: Use state, explain, note and report for relatively neutral presentation.

Example / answer: The article reports that household costs have risen sharply.

Distancing

Rule / explanation: Use claim, allege and contend when the statement is disputed or unverified.

Example / answer: The government claims that the reform has improved access.

Interpretation

Rule / explanation: Use argue, suggest and imply to report reasoning rather than simple facts.

Example / answer: The author argues that deregulation has weakened consumer protection.

Evaluation

Rule / explanation: Use concede, acknowledge and admit for recognised limitations.

Example / answer: The journalist concedes that the evidence remains incomplete.

Rhetorical progression

Rule / explanation: Vary reporting verbs to show how an argument develops.

Example / answer: The author begins by observing that trust is declining, then argues that institutional reform is essential.

P. Reported speech in source comparison

Agreement

Rule / explanation: Use agree, concur and similarly argue to present common ground.

Example / answer: Both authors agree that public trust has declined.

Disagreement

Rule / explanation: Use dispute, reject, challenge and contradict for opposition.

Example / answer: The second article challenges the first author’s explanation.

Different emphasis

Rule / explanation: Use emphasise, focus on and highlight to distinguish priorities.

Example / answer: Whereas the first text emphasises cost, the second highlights fairness.

Qualification

Rule / explanation: Use partly accept, acknowledge but and question whether for nuanced comparison.

Example / answer: The second author acknowledges the problem but questions whether regulation is the solution.

Synthesis

Rule / explanation: Reported speech structures allow several sources to be integrated into one analytical paragraph.

Example / answer: While one author argues that the reform promotes efficiency, the other warns that it may deepen inequality.

Q. Free indirect speech and quotation choices

Direct quotation

Rule / explanation: Use exact quotation when the wording itself is important.

Example / answer: The minister described the situation as “a national emergency”.

Partial quotation

Rule / explanation: Integrate a short distinctive phrase into reported speech.

Example / answer: The author argues that the policy has created a “two-tier system”.

Indirect report

Rule / explanation: Paraphrase when the idea matters more than the precise wording.

Example / answer: The journalist argues that access has become increasingly unequal.

Free indirect style

Rule / explanation: In literary analysis, free indirect speech blends a character’s perspective with third-person narration.

Example / answer: How could the government have failed so completely?

Quotation discipline

Rule / explanation: Avoid excessive quotation; analyse and paraphrase the source.

Example / answer: Use short quotations only when they support a precise linguistic or rhetorical observation.

R. Typical mistakes by French speakers

Question word order

Rule / explanation: Do not retain inversion in reported questions.

Example / answer: Wrong: She asked why had the policy failed. Correct: She asked why the policy had failed.

Say / tell

Rule / explanation: Tell requires a listener; say does not.

Example / answer: She told us that... / She said that...

Suggest to

Rule / explanation: Do not use suggest someone to do something.

Example / answer: Correct: She suggested that they act / suggested acting.

Double past marking

Rule / explanation: Use had + past participle, not had + past simple.

Example / answer: Wrong: had went. Correct: had gone.

Mechanical backshift

Rule / explanation: Do not backshift facts that remain universally true unless distance is intended.

Example / answer: The teacher explained that water boils at 100°C.

Wrong time reference

Rule / explanation: Adapt tomorrow, yesterday, now, here and this only when the reporting context requires it.

Example / answer: Context, not a rigid rule, determines the change.

S. Exercises

1. Report the statement

Rule / explanation: “The reform will take effect next year,” the minister said.

Example / answer: The minister said that the reform would take effect the following year.

2. Report the question

Rule / explanation: “Why did the policy fail?” the journalist asked.

Example / answer: The journalist asked why the policy had failed.

3. Report the yes/no question

Rule / explanation: “Is the evidence reliable?” the committee asked.

Example / answer: The committee asked whether the evidence was reliable.

4. Report the command

Rule / explanation: “Do not publish the figures,” the official said.

Example / answer: The official told them not to publish the figures.

5. Report the suggestion

Rule / explanation: “Let us postpone the vote,” she said.

Example / answer: She suggested postponing the vote. / She suggested that they postpone the vote.

6. Use an impersonal passive

Rule / explanation: People believe that the minister ignored several warnings.

Example / answer: The minister is believed to have ignored several warnings.

7. Translate

Rule / explanation: L’auteur reconnaît que les preuves sont limitées, mais soutient que la réforme reste nécessaire.

Example / answer: The author acknowledges that the evidence is limited but argues that the reform remains necessary.

8. Mini concours challenge

Rule / explanation: Write a paragraph using claim, acknowledge, warn, ask whether and is believed to have.

Example / answer: Example: The government claims that the reform has improved efficiency. However, the minister acknowledges that regional disparities remain. Experts warn that further cuts could weaken public services, while the article asks whether the policy can remain politically sustainable. The administration is also believed to have ignored several early warnings.
