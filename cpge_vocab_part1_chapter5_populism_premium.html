Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse populism, anti-elitism, emotional rhetoric, scapegoating, anti-establishment movements, disinformation and democratic risks. Each entry includes the English term, IPA phonetics, a practical pronunciation guide, an English definition, a French translation, and an exam-ready example sentence.

How to use this chapter

For press articles Focus on terms such as anti-establishment, the elite, scapegoat, backlash, post-truth politics and democratic backsliding.
For oral exams Do not simply say “populism is bad”. Explain the mechanism: resentment, anti-elitism, simplified narratives, emotional rhetoric and distrust of institutions.
For essays Memorise the collocations: to exploit fears, to fuel resentment, to channel discontent, to bypass institutions, to undermine trust.

A. Core concepts and actors

populism

IPA: /ˈpɒpjəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: POP-yuh-liz-uhm
French: populisme

Definition: A political style or ideology that claims to defend ordinary people against a corrupt elite.

Example: Populism often presents politics as a struggle between the people and the elite.

populist

IPA: /ˈpɒpjəlɪst/
Pronunciation: POP-yuh-list
French: populiste

Definition: A politician, party or movement that uses populist ideas or rhetoric.

Example: The populist leader promised to give power back to ordinary citizens.

anti-elitism

IPA: /ˌænti ɪˈliːtɪzəm/
Pronunciation: AN-tee i-LEE-tiz-uhm
French: anti-élitisme

Definition: Hostility towards elites, especially political, economic, cultural or media elites.

Example: Anti-elitism is a central feature of many populist movements.

the elite

IPA: /ði ɪˈliːt/
Pronunciation: thee i-LEET
French: l’élite

Definition: A small group of powerful or privileged people who are seen as controlling society.

Example: Populist speeches often blame the elite for the country’s problems.

ordinary people

IPA: /ˈɔːdənəri ˈpiːpəl/
Pronunciation: OR-duh-nuh-ree PEE-puhl
French: les gens ordinaires

Definition: Common citizens, especially those presented as honest, hard-working and ignored by elites.

Example: The candidate claimed to speak for ordinary people.

the people

IPA: /ðə ˈpiːpəl/
Pronunciation: thuh PEE-puhl
French: le peuple

Definition: In populist rhetoric, the supposedly unified and legitimate body of ordinary citizens.

Example: Populists often claim that only they represent the people.

the establishment

IPA: /ði ɪˈstæblɪʃmənt/
Pronunciation: thee i-STAB-lish-muhnt
French: l’establishment ; le système

Definition: The powerful institutions and groups that are seen as controlling politics and society.

Example: The movement presented itself as a rebellion against the establishment.

the ruling class

IPA: /ðə ˈruːlɪŋ klɑːs/
Pronunciation: thuh ROO-ling klass
French: la classe dirigeante

Definition: The group of people who hold political, economic or social power.

Example: The ruling class was accused of being disconnected from voters.

political outsider

IPA: /pəˈlɪtɪkəl aʊtˈsaɪdə/
Pronunciation: puh-LIT-i-kuhl owts-EYE-duh
French: outsider politique

Definition: A person who does not belong to the traditional political establishment.

Example: The billionaire ran as a political outsider.

insider

IPA: /ɪnˈsaɪdə/
Pronunciation: in-SY-duh
French: initié ; membre du système

Definition: A person who belongs to a powerful organisation, institution or political circle.

Example: Although he attacked the establishment, critics said he was an insider.

anti-establishment

IPA: /ˌænti ɪˈstæblɪʃmənt/
Pronunciation: AN-tee i-STAB-lish-muhnt
French: anti-système

Definition: Opposed to traditional political, economic or cultural elites.

Example: Anti-establishment parties have gained ground in many democracies.

grassroots movement

IPA: /ˈɡrɑːsruːts ˈmuːvmənt/
Pronunciation: GRASS-roots MOOV-muhnt
French: mouvement de base ; mouvement citoyen

Definition: A movement built from ordinary citizens or local activists rather than party elites.

Example: The campaign began as a grassroots movement online.

popular anger

IPA: /ˈpɒpjələ ˈæŋɡə/
Pronunciation: POP-yuh-luh ANG-guh
French: colère populaire

Definition: Public frustration or resentment directed at leaders, institutions or policies.

Example: The party benefited from popular anger over inflation.

resentment

IPA: /rɪˈzentmənt/
Pronunciation: ri-ZENT-muhnt
French: ressentiment

Definition: A feeling of bitterness caused by perceived unfair treatment.

Example: Populist rhetoric often feeds on social resentment.

grievance

IPA: /ˈɡriːvəns/
Pronunciation: GREE-vuhns
French: grief ; motif de mécontentement

Definition: A complaint or feeling of injustice.

Example: The movement gave voice to long-standing grievances.

backlash

IPA: /ˈbæklæʃ/
Pronunciation: BAK-lash
French: réaction hostile ; retour de bâton

Definition: A strong negative reaction against social or political change.

Example: The reform triggered a populist backlash.

anti-politics

IPA: /ˌænti ˈpɒlɪtɪks/
Pronunciation: AN-tee POL-i-tiks
French: rejet de la politique traditionnelle

Definition: Hostility towards professional politicians and traditional political institutions.

Example: Anti-politics can weaken trust in democratic institutions.

political disaffection

IPA: /pəˈlɪtɪkəl ˌdɪsəˈfekʃən/
Pronunciation: puh-LIT-i-kuhl dis-uh-FEK-shuhn
French: désaffection politique

Definition: A feeling of alienation or dissatisfaction with politics.

Example: Political disaffection helped fuel support for outsider candidates.

distrust

IPA: /dɪsˈtrʌst/
Pronunciation: dis-TRUST
French: méfiance

Definition: A lack of trust or confidence.

Example: Distrust of institutions is often exploited by populist leaders.

mistrust

IPA: /ˌmɪsˈtrʌst/
Pronunciation: mis-TRUST
French: méfiance ; défiance

Definition: Suspicion or lack of confidence in someone or something.

Example: Mistrust of the media has grown in polarised societies.

B. Rhetoric, emotions and scapegoating

charismatic leader

IPA: /ˌkærɪzˈmætɪk ˈliːdə/
Pronunciation: kar-iz-MAT-ik LEE-duh
French: leader charismatique

Definition: A leader who attracts followers through personal appeal, confidence and emotional communication.

Example: Populist movements often revolve around a charismatic leader.

strongman

IPA: /ˈstrɒŋmæn/
Pronunciation: STRONG-man
French: homme fort

Definition: A political leader who presents himself as powerful, decisive and willing to bypass institutions.

Example: The strongman promised to restore order and national pride.

demagogue

IPA: /ˈdeməɡɒɡ/
Pronunciation: DEM-uh-gog
French: démagogue

Definition: A political leader who gains support by appealing to emotions, fears and prejudices rather than reason.

Example: Critics described the speaker as a demagogue.

demagoguery

IPA: /ˈdeməɡɒɡəri/
Pronunciation: DEM-uh-gog-uh-ree
French: démagogie

Definition: Political speech or behaviour that manipulates emotions and prejudices.

Example: The campaign relied more on demagoguery than on detailed policies.

rhetoric

IPA: /ˈretərɪk/
Pronunciation: RET-uh-rik
French: rhétorique ; discours

Definition: The style and strategy of persuasive political language.

Example: The leader’s rhetoric was simple, emotional and confrontational.

fiery rhetoric

IPA: /ˈfaɪəri ˈretərɪk/
Pronunciation: FY-uh-ree RET-uh-rik
French: rhétorique enflammée

Definition: Angry, emotional and forceful political language.

Example: His fiery rhetoric energised supporters but alarmed opponents.

divisive rhetoric

IPA: /dɪˈvaɪsɪv ˈretərɪk/
Pronunciation: di-VY-siv RET-uh-rik
French: rhétorique clivante

Definition: Language that creates conflict between groups.

Example: Divisive rhetoric can deepen social tensions.

scapegoat

IPA: /ˈskeɪpɡəʊt/
Pronunciation: SKAYP-goht
French: bouc émissaire

Definition: A person or group unfairly blamed for problems.

Example: Immigrants are often used as scapegoats in populist discourse.

to scapegoat

IPA: /ˈskeɪpɡəʊt/
Pronunciation: SKAYP-goht
French: prendre pour bouc émissaire

Definition: To blame a person or group unfairly for political or social problems.

Example: The party was accused of scapegoating minorities.

enemy within

IPA: /ˈenəmi wɪˈðɪn/
Pronunciation: EN-uh-mee wi-THIN
French: ennemi intérieur

Definition: A group portrayed as dangerous from inside the country or community.

Example: The government portrayed critics as the enemy within.

us versus them

IPA: /ˈʌs ˌvɜːsəs ˈðem/
Pronunciation: uss VUR-suhs them
French: nous contre eux

Definition: A way of presenting politics as a conflict between two opposing groups.

Example: Populist rhetoric often relies on an us-versus-them narrative.

polarisation

IPA: /ˌpəʊləraɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: poh-luh-ry-ZAY-shuhn
French: polarisation

Definition: The process by which political opinions become more divided and extreme.

Example: Populism can intensify political polarisation.

nativism

IPA: /ˈneɪtɪvɪzəm/
Pronunciation: NAY-tiv-iz-uhm
French: nativisme ; préférence nationale

Definition: A political attitude that favours native-born citizens over immigrants.

Example: Nativism often appears in anti-immigration campaigns.

xenophobia

IPA: /ˌzenəˈfəʊbiə/
Pronunciation: zen-uh-FOH-bee-uh
French: xénophobie

Definition: Fear or hostility towards foreigners or people from other countries.

Example: The speech was criticised for encouraging xenophobia.

anti-immigration stance

IPA: /ˌænti ˌɪmɪˈɡreɪʃən stɑːns/
Pronunciation: AN-tee im-i-GRAY-shuhn stans
French: position anti-immigration

Definition: A political position opposed to immigration or favouring stricter immigration controls.

Example: The party adopted a hardline anti-immigration stance.

national sovereignty

IPA: /ˈnæʃənəl ˈsɒvrənti/
Pronunciation: NASH-uh-nuhl SOV-ruhn-tee
French: souveraineté nationale

Definition: The idea that a nation-state should have the final authority over its own laws and policies.

Example: The campaign framed the issue as a matter of national sovereignty.

economic insecurity

IPA: /ˌiːkəˈnɒmɪk ˌɪnsɪˈkjʊərəti/
Pronunciation: ee-kuh-NOM-ik in-si-KYOOR-uh-tee
French: insécurité économique

Definition: A feeling of financial instability or fear about jobs, wages or living standards.

Example: Economic insecurity can make voters more receptive to populist messages.

C. Populist issues and economic grievances

left-wing populism

IPA: /ˌleft wɪŋ ˈpɒpjəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: left wing POP-yuh-liz-uhm
French: populisme de gauche

Definition: A form of populism that focuses on economic inequality and attacks financial or corporate elites.

Example: Left-wing populism often targets banks, billionaires and austerity policies.

right-wing populism

IPA: /ˌraɪt wɪŋ ˈpɒpjəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: right wing POP-yuh-liz-uhm
French: populisme de droite

Definition: A form of populism that often combines anti-elitism with nationalism and hostility to immigration.

Example: Right-wing populism has reshaped party politics in several Western countries.

anti-globalisation

IPA: /ˌænti ˌɡləʊbəlaɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: AN-tee gloh-buh-ly-ZAY-shuhn
French: anti-mondialisation

Definition: Opposition to globalisation, often because of its effects on jobs, sovereignty or local communities.

Example: Anti-globalisation arguments became central to the campaign.

protectionism

IPA: /prəˈtekʃənɪzəm/
Pronunciation: pruh-TEK-shuh-niz-uhm
French: protectionnisme

Definition: The policy of protecting domestic industries from foreign competition, usually through tariffs or restrictions.

Example: The party’s economic programme included strong protectionism.

tariff

IPA: /ˈtærɪf/
Pronunciation: TAR-if
French: droit de douane

Definition: A tax on imported goods.

Example: The government imposed tariffs to protect domestic industries.

anti-austerity

IPA: /ˌænti ɒˈsterəti/
Pronunciation: AN-tee aw-STAIR-uh-tee
French: anti-austérité

Definition: Opposed to cuts in public spending or welfare programmes.

Example: The anti-austerity movement gained support after years of budget cuts.

welfare chauvinism

IPA: /ˈwelfeə ˈʃəʊvɪnɪzəm/
Pronunciation: WEL-fair SHOH-vin-iz-uhm
French: chauvinisme social ; préférence nationale sociale

Definition: The view that welfare benefits should be reserved mainly for native citizens.

Example: Welfare chauvinism links social protection to national identity.

anti-corruption

IPA: /ˌænti kəˈrʌpʃən/
Pronunciation: AN-tee kuh-RUP-shuhn
French: anti-corruption

Definition: Opposed to dishonest or illegal behaviour by people in power.

Example: The populist candidate built his image around an anti-corruption message.

drain the swamp

IPA: /ˌdreɪn ðə ˈswɒmp/
Pronunciation: drayn thuh swamp
French: nettoyer le marécage ; chasser les élites corrompues

Definition: A slogan meaning to remove corruption or entrenched elites from government.

Example: The slogan “drain the swamp” appealed to voters angry with Washington.

take back control

IPA: /ˌteɪk bæk kənˈtrəʊl/
Pronunciation: tayk bak kuhn-TROHL
French: reprendre le contrôle

Definition: A slogan meaning to regain national, political or democratic control.

Example: “Take back control” became a powerful slogan during the Brexit campaign.

will of the people

IPA: /ˌwɪl əv ðə ˈpiːpəl/
Pronunciation: wil ov thuh PEE-puhl
French: volonté du peuple

Definition: The supposed collective desire of ordinary citizens.

Example: Populist leaders often claim to embody the will of the people.

D. Slogans, narratives and communication

silent majority

IPA: /ˌsaɪlənt məˈdʒɒrəti/
Pronunciation: SY-luhnt muh-JOR-uh-tee
French: majorité silencieuse

Definition: A large group of people who are said to hold common views but not express them publicly.

Example: The candidate claimed to represent the silent majority.

forgotten people

IPA: /fəˈɡɒtən ˈpiːpəl/
Pronunciation: fuh-GOT-uhn PEE-puhl
French: les oubliés ; les laissés-pour-compte

Definition: Citizens who feel ignored or neglected by political and economic elites.

Example: The campaign appealed to forgotten people in deindustrialised regions.

left behind

IPA: /ˌleft bɪˈhaɪnd/
Pronunciation: left bi-HYND
French: laissés pour compte

Definition: People or places that have not benefited from economic or social change.

Example: Populist parties often target voters who feel left behind.

people versus elite narrative

IPA: /ˈpiːpəl ˌvɜːsəs ɪˈliːt ˈnærətɪv/
Pronunciation: PEE-puhl VUR-suhs i-LEET NAR-uh-tiv
French: récit peuple contre élites

Definition: A political story that presents society as divided between virtuous ordinary people and corrupt elites.

Example: The people-versus-elite narrative simplified complex economic problems.

simplistic solution

IPA: /sɪmˈplɪstɪk səˈluːʃən/
Pronunciation: sim-PLIS-tik suh-LOO-shuhn
French: solution simpliste

Definition: A solution that makes a complex issue seem easier than it really is.

Example: Critics argued that the proposal was a simplistic solution to a complex crisis.

soundbite politics

IPA: /ˈsaʊndbaɪt ˈpɒlɪtɪks/
Pronunciation: SOWND-byt POL-i-tiks
French: politique de la petite phrase

Definition: Political communication based on short, memorable phrases rather than detailed argument.

Example: Soundbite politics works well on television and social media.

slogan politics

IPA: /ˈsləʊɡən ˈpɒlɪtɪks/
Pronunciation: SLOH-guhn POL-i-tiks
French: politique du slogan

Definition: Politics based on catchy slogans rather than detailed policies.

Example: Slogan politics can mobilise voters but oversimplify problems.

post-truth politics

IPA: /ˌpəʊst ˈtruːθ ˈpɒlɪtɪks/
Pronunciation: pohst trooth POL-i-tiks
French: politique post-vérité

Definition: Politics in which emotion and personal belief influence opinion more than facts.

Example: Post-truth politics makes fact-checking more difficult.

fake news

IPA: /ˌfeɪk ˈnjuːz/
Pronunciation: fayk nyooz
French: fausses informations ; infox

Definition: False information presented as news, or a term used to discredit unfavourable reporting.

Example: The phrase fake news is often used to attack journalists.

conspiracy theory

IPA: /kənˈspɪrəsi ˈθɪəri/
Pronunciation: kuhn-SPIR-uh-see THEE-uh-ree
French: théorie du complot

Definition: A belief that events are secretly controlled by powerful hidden groups, often without evidence.

Example: Conspiracy theories can spread rapidly during political crises.

echo chamber

IPA: /ˈekəʊ ˌtʃeɪmbə/
Pronunciation: EK-oh CHAYM-buh
French: chambre d’écho

Definition: An environment in which people mainly hear opinions that confirm their existing beliefs.

Example: Social media can create political echo chambers.

filter bubble

IPA: /ˈfɪltə ˌbʌbəl/
Pronunciation: FIL-tuh BUB-uhl
French: bulle de filtres

Definition: An online environment shaped by algorithms that show users information similar to what they already believe.

Example: Filter bubbles can reinforce polarisation.

viral message

IPA: /ˈvaɪrəl ˈmesɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: VY-ruhl MESS-ij
French: message viral

Definition: A message that spreads very quickly online.

Example: The campaign’s viral message reached millions of users.

social media outrage

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl ˈmiːdiə ˈaʊtreɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl MEE-dee-uh OWT-rayj
French: indignation sur les réseaux sociaux

Definition: Strong anger expressed and amplified on social media.

Example: Social media outrage forced the party to respond quickly.

to mobilise voters

IPA: /ˈməʊbɪlaɪz ˈvəʊtəz/
Pronunciation: MOH-bi-lyz VOH-tuhz
French: mobiliser les électeurs

Definition: To encourage voters to take action, especially to vote or campaign.

Example: The party used social media to mobilise young voters.

E. Media, disinformation and mobilisation verbs

to exploit fears

IPA: /ɪkˈsplɔɪt ˈfɪəz/
Pronunciation: ik-SPLOYT fearz
French: exploiter les peurs

Definition: To use people’s anxieties for political advantage.

Example: Critics accused the campaign of exploiting fears about immigration.

to fuel resentment

IPA: /ˈfjuːəl rɪˈzentmənt/
Pronunciation: FYOO-uhl ri-ZENT-muhnt
French: alimenter le ressentiment

Definition: To intensify feelings of bitterness or injustice.

Example: Inflammatory speeches can fuel resentment against minorities.

to stir up anger

IPA: /stɜːr ʌp ˈæŋɡə/
Pronunciation: stur up ANG-guh
French: attiser la colère

Definition: To deliberately make people angry.

Example: The leader tried to stir up anger against the government.

to tap into frustration

IPA: /tæp ˈɪntuː frʌˈstreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: tap IN-too frus-TRAY-shuhn
French: exploiter ; capter la frustration

Definition: To make use of existing public frustration.

Example: The campaign tapped into frustration in rural areas.

to channel discontent

IPA: /ˈtʃænəl ˌdɪskənˈtent/
Pronunciation: CHAN-uhl dis-kuhn-TENT
French: canaliser le mécontentement

Definition: To direct public dissatisfaction towards a political cause or movement.

Example: Populist parties channelled discontent into electoral gains.

to denounce the elite

IPA: /dɪˈnaʊns ði ɪˈliːt/
Pronunciation: di-NOWNSS thee i-LEET
French: dénoncer l’élite

Definition: To publicly criticise powerful groups.

Example: The candidate denounced the elite as corrupt and out of touch.

to bypass institutions

IPA: /ˈbaɪpɑːs ˌɪnstɪˈtjuːʃənz/
Pronunciation: BY-pass in-sti-TOO-shuhnz
French: contourner les institutions

Definition: To avoid or ignore normal institutional procedures.

Example: The leader tried to bypass institutions by appealing directly to the people.

to undermine trust

IPA: /ˌʌndəˈmaɪn ˈtrʌst/
Pronunciation: un-duh-MYNE trust
French: saper la confiance

Definition: To weaken confidence in institutions, media or democratic processes.

Example: Constant attacks on the media can undermine trust in journalism.
