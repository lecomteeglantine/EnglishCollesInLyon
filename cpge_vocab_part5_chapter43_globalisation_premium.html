Essential vocabulary for khôlles, essays, press reviews and competitive exams.

Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse globalisation: economic integration, trade, supply chains, multinational corporations, outsourcing, cultural globalisation, migration, global inequality, labour exploitation, deindustrialisation, protectionist backlash, strategic autonomy, de-risking and the debate over whether globalisation is slowing down or changing shape.

How to use this chapter

For press articlesFocus on terms such as global supply chain, outsourcing, offshoring, multinational corporation, tax haven, cultural homogenisation, left-behind communities, strategic autonomy, de-risking and slowbalisation.
For oral examsUse this vocabulary to explain both sides: globalisation can lower prices, spread technology and reduce poverty, but it can also increase dependency, inequality, exploitation and political backlash.
For essaysMemorise the collocations: to integrate into the global economy, to remove trade barriers, to disrupt supply chains, to create winners and losers, to balance openness and security.

A. Core globalisation and integration vocabulary

globalisation

IPA: /ˌɡləʊbəlaɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: GLOH-buh-ly-ZAY-shuhn
French: mondialisation

Definition: The growing connection and interdependence of economies, societies and cultures across the world.

Example: Globalisation has increased trade, migration and cultural exchange.

globalization

IPA: /ˌɡləʊbəlaɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: GLOH-buh-ly-ZAY-shuhn
French: mondialisation

Definition: US spelling of globalisation.

Example: Globalization is often associated with free trade and multinational companies.

global economy

IPA: /ˌɡləʊbəl iˈkɒnəmi/
Pronunciation: GLOH-buhl i-KON-uh-mee
French: économie mondiale

Definition: The world economy considered as an interconnected system.

Example: The global economy is vulnerable to financial crises and supply-chain shocks.

economic integration

IPA: /ˌiːkəˈnɒmɪk ˌɪntɪˈɡreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: ee-kuh-NOM-ik in-ti-GRAY-shuhn
French: intégration économique

Definition: The process by which economies become more connected through trade, investment and common rules.

Example: Economic integration has deepened within the European Union.

interdependence

IPA: /ˌɪntədɪˈpendəns/
Pronunciation: IN-tuh-di-PEN-duhns
French: interdépendance

Definition: A situation where countries, firms or people depend on each other.

Example: Global interdependence can promote cooperation but also spread crises.

economic interdependence

IPA: /ˌiːkəˈnɒmɪk ˌɪntədɪˈpendəns/
Pronunciation: ee-kuh-NOM-ik in-tuh-di-PEN-duhns
French: interdépendance économique

Definition: Mutual economic reliance between countries or regions.

Example: Economic interdependence makes conflict costly but creates vulnerabilities.

global interconnectedness

IPA: /ˌɡləʊbəl ˌɪntəkəˈnektɪdnəs/
Pronunciation: GLOH-buhl in-tuh-kuh-NEK-tid-nuhs
French: interconnexion mondiale

Definition: The condition of being connected across borders through trade, technology, migration and communication.

Example: The pandemic revealed the extent of global interconnectedness.

cross-border

IPA: /ˌkrɒs ˈbɔːdə/
Pronunciation: cross-BOR-duh
French: transfrontalier

Definition: Taking place across national borders.

Example: Cross-border trade depends on transport and customs rules.

borderless world

IPA: /ˈbɔːdələs wɜːld/
Pronunciation: BOR-duh-luhs world
French: monde sans frontières

Definition: The idea that globalisation weakens national borders.

Example: The borderless world is more myth than reality.

global village

IPA: /ˌɡləʊbəl ˈvɪlɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: GLOH-buhl VIL-ij
French: village planétaire

Definition: The idea that communication technology makes the world feel smaller and more connected.

Example: The internet has strengthened the idea of a global village.

worldwide

IPA: /ˌwɜːldˈwaɪd/
Pronunciation: world-WYDE
French: mondial ; à l’échelle mondiale

Definition: Existing or happening across the world.

Example: The company sells its products worldwide.

worldwide market

IPA: /ˌwɜːldwaɪd ˈmɑːkɪt/
Pronunciation: WORLD-wide MAR-kit
French: marché mondial

Definition: A market that operates across the world.

Example: Digital platforms can reach a worldwide market.

global market

IPA: /ˌɡləʊbəl ˈmɑːkɪt/
Pronunciation: GLOH-buhl MAR-kit
French: marché mondial

Definition: A market operating internationally.

Example: Global markets create opportunities and competition.

international trade

IPA: /ˌɪntəˈnæʃənəl treɪd/
Pronunciation: IN-tuh-NASH-uh-nuhl trade
French: commerce international

Definition: The exchange of goods and services across national borders.

Example: International trade is one of the main drivers of globalisation.

free trade

IPA: /ˌfriː ˈtreɪd/
Pronunciation: free trade
French: libre-échange

Definition: Trade with few or no tariffs, quotas or restrictions.

Example: Free trade can lower prices but expose workers to competition.

trade liberalisation

IPA: /ˈtreɪd ˌlɪbərəlaɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: trade LIB-uh-ruh-ly-ZAY-shuhn
French: libéralisation des échanges

Definition: The reduction of barriers to international trade.

Example: Trade liberalisation expanded after the Cold War.

capital flows

IPA: /ˈkæpɪtəl fləʊz/
Pronunciation: KAP-i-tuhl flohz
French: flux de capitaux

Definition: Movements of money for investment across borders.

Example: Capital flows can finance growth but also create instability.

financial globalisation

IPA: /faɪˌnænʃəl ˌɡləʊbəlaɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: fy-NAN-shuhl GLOH-buh-ly-ZAY-shuhn
French: mondialisation financière

Definition: The integration of financial markets across countries.

Example: Financial globalisation can spread crises rapidly.

foreign direct investment

IPA: /ˌfɒrən dəˌrekt ɪnˈvestmənt/
Pronunciation: FOR-uhn di-REKT in-VEST-muhnt
French: investissement direct étranger

Definition: Investment by a foreign company in productive activity in another country.

Example: Foreign direct investment can bring jobs, technology and capital.

FDI

IPA: /ˌef diː ˈaɪ/
Pronunciation: eff-dee-eye
French: IDE

Definition: Abbreviation for foreign direct investment.

Example: FDI often flows towards countries with stable institutions and cheap labour.

portfolio investment

IPA: /pɔːtˈfəʊliəʊ ɪnˈvestmənt/
Pronunciation: port-FOH-lee-oh in-VEST-muhnt
French: investissement de portefeuille

Definition: Investment in financial assets such as stocks and bonds rather than direct business control.

Example: Portfolio investment can leave a country quickly during crises.

global supply chain

IPA: /ˌɡləʊbəl səˈplaɪ tʃeɪn/
Pronunciation: GLOH-buhl suh-PLY chayn
French: chaîne d’approvisionnement mondiale

Definition: A worldwide network of suppliers, producers and transport used to make and deliver products.

Example: Global supply chains lowered costs but increased vulnerability.

supply chain

IPA: /səˈplaɪ tʃeɪn/
Pronunciation: suh-PLY chayn
French: chaîne d’approvisionnement

Definition: The network involved in producing and delivering goods.

Example: Supply chains can be disrupted by war, disease or natural disasters.

value chain

IPA: /ˈvæljuː tʃeɪn/
Pronunciation: VAL-yoo chayn
French: chaîne de valeur

Definition: The stages through which value is added to a product or service.

Example: Modern products are often made through complex value chains.

B. Trade, supply chains and international institutions

global value chain

IPA: /ˌɡləʊbəl ˈvæljuː tʃeɪn/
Pronunciation: GLOH-buhl VAL-yoo chayn
French: chaîne de valeur mondiale

Definition: A production process spread across several countries.

Example: Global value chains make trade statistics harder to interpret.

outsourcing

IPA: /ˈaʊtsɔːsɪŋ/
Pronunciation: OWT-sor-sing
French: externalisation ; sous-traitance

Definition: Contracting work to another company, often to reduce costs.

Example: Outsourcing can reduce costs but weaken job security.

offshoring

IPA: /ˌɒfˈʃɔːrɪŋ/
Pronunciation: OFF-shor-ing
French: délocalisation

Definition: Moving production or services to another country.

Example: Offshoring shifted many manufacturing jobs abroad.

nearshoring

IPA: /ˌnɪəˈʃɔːrɪŋ/
Pronunciation: NEER-shor-ing
French: délocalisation de proximité

Definition: Moving production closer to the home market.

Example: Nearshoring can reduce supply-chain risks.

reshoring

IPA: /ˌriːˈʃɔːrɪŋ/
Pronunciation: ree-SHOR-ing
French: relocalisation

Definition: Bringing production back to the home country.

Example: Reshoring has become more attractive after supply-chain disruptions.

friend-shoring

IPA: /ˌfrend ˈʃɔːrɪŋ/
Pronunciation: friend-SHOR-ing
French: relocalisation vers des pays alliés

Definition: Moving supply chains to politically allied or trusted countries.

Example: Friend-shoring aims to reduce geopolitical dependency.

just-in-time production

IPA: /ˌdʒʌst ɪn ˈtaɪm prəˈdʌkʃən/
Pronunciation: just-in-time pruh-DUK-shuhn
French: production à flux tendu

Definition: A production system with minimal stocks and fast delivery of inputs.

Example: Just-in-time production is efficient but fragile.

inventory

IPA: /ˈɪnvəntri/
Pronunciation: IN-vuhn-tree
French: stock ; inventaire

Definition: Stocks of goods, materials or products held by a firm.

Example: Firms increased inventory after supply chains became less reliable.

logistics

IPA: /ləˈdʒɪstɪks/
Pronunciation: luh-JIS-tiks
French: logistique

Definition: The organisation of transport, storage and delivery of goods.

Example: Logistics is essential to global trade.

container shipping

IPA: /kənˈteɪnə ˈʃɪpɪŋ/
Pronunciation: kuhn-TAY-nuh SHIP-ing
French: transport maritime par conteneurs

Definition: Transporting goods by standardised shipping containers.

Example: Container shipping made globalisation cheaper and faster.

freight

IPA: /freɪt/
Pronunciation: frayt
French: fret ; marchandises transportées

Definition: Goods transported by ship, train, lorry or plane.

Example: Freight costs surged during the pandemic.

shipping cost

IPA: /ˈʃɪpɪŋ kɒst/
Pronunciation: SHIP-ing kost
French: coût du transport

Definition: The cost of transporting goods.

tariff

Definition: A tax on imported goods.

Example: Tariffs are used to protect domestic industries.

quota

IPA: /ˈkwəʊtə/
Pronunciation: KWOH-tuh
French: quota

Definition: A limit on the quantity of goods that can be imported or exported.

trade barrier

IPA: /ˈtreɪd ˌbæriə/

French: barrière commerciale

Definition: A rule, tax or restriction that makes trade harder.

Pronunciation: non-TAR-if BARR-ee-uh
French: barrière non tarifaire

Definition: A trade restriction that is not a tax, such as rules or standards.

trade agreement

French: accord commercial

IPA: /ˌfriː ˈtreɪd əˌɡriːmənt/
Pronunciation: free trade uh-GREE-muhnt
French: accord de libre-échange

IPA: /ˈkʌstəmz ˌjuːniən/
Pronunciation: KUS-tuhmz YOO-nee-uhn
French: union douanière

Example: A customs union requires more integration than a simple trade deal.

single market

IPA: /ˌsɪŋɡəl ˈmɑːkɪt/
Pronunciation: SING-guhl MAR-kit
French: marché unique

Definition: A market allowing free movement of goods, services, capital and people.

Example: The EU single market is one of the deepest forms of economic integration.

C. Multinationals, taxation, labour and ethical issues

tax evasion

IPA: /ˈtæks ɪˌveɪʒən/
Pronunciation: taks i-VAY-zhuhn
French: fraude fiscale

Definition: Illegal non-payment or underpayment of taxes.

Example: Tax evasion deprives states of revenue.

tax haven

IPA: /ˈtæks ˌheɪvən/
Pronunciation: taks HAY-vuhn
French: paradis fiscal

Definition: A country or territory with very low taxes and secrecy.

Example: Tax havens help firms and wealthy individuals reduce tax bills.

profit shifting

IPA: /ˈprɒfɪt ˌʃɪftɪŋ/
Pronunciation: PROF-it SHIFT-ing
French: transfert de bénéfices

Definition: Moving profits to low-tax jurisdictions to reduce taxes.

Example: Profit shifting is a major challenge for tax authorities.

IPA: /ˌreɪs tə ðə ˈbɒtəm/
Pronunciation: race tuh thuh BOT-uhm
French: course vers le bas

Definition: Competition that lowers wages, taxes or standards to attract business.

Example: Critics say globalisation can create a race to the bottom.

social dumping

IPA: /ˈsəʊʃəl ˌdʌmpɪŋ/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl DUMP-ing
French: dumping social

Definition: Competing by using lower wages or weaker labour protections.

Example: Social dumping is a common fear in trade debates.

environmental dumping

IPA: /ɪnˌvaɪrənmentəl ˈdʌmpɪŋ/
Pronunciation: in-VY-ruhn-MEN-tuhl DUMP-ing
French: dumping environnemental

Example: Environmental dumping can undermine climate policy.

sweatshop

IPA: /ˈswetʃɒp/
Pronunciation: SWET-shop
French: atelier de misère

Definition: A workplace with very poor pay and conditions, often in garment production.

Example: Sweatshops reveal the darker side of cheap consumer goods.
