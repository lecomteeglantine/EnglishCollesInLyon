Essential vocabulary for khôlles, essays, press reviews and competitive exams.

Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse race relations in the United States: slavery, segregation, civil rights, systemic racism, policing, voting rights, socio-economic inequalities, affirmative action, identity politics and contemporary culture wars. Each entry includes the English term, IPA phonetics, a practical pronunciation guide, an English definition, a French translation, and an exam-ready example sentence.

How to use this chapter

For press articles Focus on terms such as systemic racism, civil rights movement, police brutality, racial wealth gap, voter suppression, affirmative action and critical race theory.
For oral exams Use this vocabulary to avoid vague moral comments and explain concrete mechanisms: law, policing, housing, education, voting access and historical legacy.
For essays Memorise the collocations: to address racial inequality, to fight discrimination, to reform policing, to expand voting access, to dismantle systemic racism.

A. Core vocabulary: race, identity and discrimination

race relations

IPA: /ˈreɪs rɪˌleɪʃənz/
Pronunciation: rays ri-LAY-shuhnz
French: relations raciales

Definition: The relationships, tensions and inequalities between racial groups in society.

Example: Race relations remain a central issue in American politics.

racial inequality

IPA: /ˌreɪʃəl ˌɪnɪˈkwɒləti/
Pronunciation: RAY-shuhl in-i-KWOL-uh-tee
French: inégalité raciale

Definition: Unequal treatment or outcomes between racial groups.

Example: Racial inequality can be seen in housing, education, healthcare and criminal justice.

racial discrimination

IPA: /ˌreɪʃəl dɪˌskrɪmɪˈneɪʃən/
Pronunciation: RAY-shuhl dis-krim-i-NAY-shuhn
French: discrimination raciale

Definition: Unfair treatment of people because of their race or ethnicity.

Example: The lawsuit alleged racial discrimination in hiring.

racism

IPA: /ˈreɪsɪzəm/
Pronunciation: RAY-siz-uhm
French: racisme

Definition: Beliefs, practices or systems that treat people unfairly because of race.

Example: Racism can be individual, institutional or structural.

systemic racism

IPA: /sɪˌstemɪk ˈreɪsɪzəm/
Pronunciation: sis-TEM-ik RAY-siz-uhm
French: racisme systémique

Definition: Racism embedded in institutions, policies and social structures.

Example: Activists argue that systemic racism shapes policing and housing.

structural racism

IPA: /ˌstrʌktʃərəl ˈreɪsɪzəm/
Pronunciation: STRUK-chuh-ruhl RAY-siz-uhm
French: racisme structurel

Definition: Racism produced by long-term social, economic and political structures.

Example: Structural racism can persist even without explicit racist laws.

institutional racism

IPA: /ˌɪnstɪˈtjuːʃənəl ˈreɪsɪzəm/
Pronunciation: in-sti-TOO-shuh-nuhl RAY-siz-uhm
French: racisme institutionnel

Definition: Racist outcomes produced by the policies or practices of institutions.

Example: The report accused the police department of institutional racism.

racial bias

IPA: /ˌreɪʃəl ˈbaɪəs/
Pronunciation: RAY-shuhl BY-uhs
French: préjugé racial ; biais racial

Definition: A prejudice or unfair tendency affecting judgement about racial groups.

Example: Racial bias can influence sentencing decisions.

implicit bias

IPA: /ɪmˌplɪsɪt ˈbaɪəs/
Pronunciation: im-PLIS-it BY-uhs
French: biais implicite

Definition: Unconscious attitudes or stereotypes that affect behaviour or decisions.

Example: Implicit bias training is used in some workplaces.

racial prejudice

IPA: /ˌreɪʃəl ˈpredʒədɪs/
Pronunciation: RAY-shuhl PREJ-uh-dis
French: préjugé racial

Definition: A negative judgement or attitude towards people because of their race.

Example: Racial prejudice has often been reinforced by stereotypes.

stereotype

IPA: /ˈsteriətaɪp/
Pronunciation: STAIR-ee-uh-type
French: stéréotype

Definition: A fixed and oversimplified idea about a group of people.

Example: Media representation can reinforce racial stereotypes.

minority group

IPA: /maɪˈnɒrəti ɡruːp/
Pronunciation: my-NOR-uh-tee group
French: groupe minoritaire ; minorité

Definition: A group that is smaller in number or less powerful than the dominant group.

Example: Minority groups have often faced discrimination in the United States.

ethnic minority

IPA: /ˌeθnɪk maɪˈnɒrəti/
Pronunciation: ETH-nik my-NOR-uh-tee
French: minorité ethnique

Definition: A group with a shared cultural, national or ethnic identity that differs from the majority.

Example: Ethnic minorities are often underrepresented in political institutions.

people of colour

IPA: /ˌpiːpəl əv ˈkʌlə/
Pronunciation: PEE-puhl ov KUL-uh
French: personnes racisées ; personnes non blanches

Definition: A broad term for people who are not white, especially in discussions of racism and inequality.

Example: People of colour are disproportionately affected by some policing practices.

African American

IPA: /ˌæfrɪkən əˈmerɪkən/
Pronunciation: AF-ri-kuhn uh-MER-i-kuhn
French: Afro-Américain ; Afro-Américaine

Definition: An American person with African ancestry, especially descended from enslaved Africans in the United States.

Example: African American history is central to understanding the United States.

Black American

IPA: /ˌblæk əˈmerɪkən/
Pronunciation: blak uh-MER-i-kuhn
French: Américain noir ; Américaine noire

Definition: An American person identified as Black.

Example: Black Americans have played a major role in the struggle for civil rights.

Latino

IPA: /ləˈtiːnəʊ/
Pronunciation: luh-TEE-noh
French: Latino

Definition: A person in the US with Latin American origin or ancestry, especially male or used generically.

Example: Latino voters are a diverse and growing electorate.

Latina

IPA: /ləˈtiːnə/
Pronunciation: luh-TEE-nuh
French: Latina

Definition: A woman or girl in the US with Latin American origin or ancestry.

Example: Latina activists campaigned for immigration reform.

Hispanic

IPA: /hɪˈspænɪk/
Pronunciation: his-PAN-ik
French: Hispanique

Definition: A person with Spanish-speaking origin or ancestry.

Example: The term Hispanic refers mainly to language background.

Native American

IPA: /ˌneɪtɪv əˈmerɪkən/
Pronunciation: NAY-tiv uh-MER-i-kuhn
French: Amérindien ; Autochtone américain

Definition: A member of one of the Indigenous peoples of the United States.

Example: Native American communities continue to fight for land and sovereignty rights.

Indigenous peoples

IPA: /ɪnˌdɪdʒənəs ˈpiːpəlz/
Pronunciation: in-DIJ-uh-nuhs PEE-puhlz
French: peuples autochtones

Definition: The original peoples of a territory before colonisation.

Example: Indigenous peoples have faced dispossession and cultural erasure.

Asian American

IPA: /ˌeɪʒən əˈmerɪkən/
Pronunciation: AY-zhuhn uh-MER-i-kuhn
French: Américain d’origine asiatique

Definition: An American person with Asian origin or ancestry.

Example: Asian Americans have faced both stereotypes and anti-Asian racism.

white Americans

IPA: /ˌwaɪt əˈmerɪkənz/
Pronunciation: white uh-MER-i-kuhnz
French: Américains blancs

Definition: Americans socially identified as white.

Example: White Americans remain the largest racial group in the United States.

racial identity

IPA: /ˌreɪʃəl aɪˈdentəti/
Pronunciation: RAY-shuhl eye-DEN-tuh-tee
French: identité raciale

Definition: A person’s or group’s identity in relation to socially defined race.

Example: Racial identity can shape political experience and social belonging.

ethnicity

IPA: /eθˈnɪsəti/
Pronunciation: eth-NIS-uh-tee
French: ethnicité ; origine ethnique

Definition: Shared cultural background, ancestry, language or traditions.

Example: Ethnicity is not the same as race, though the two often overlap.

segregation

IPA: /ˌseɡrɪˈɡeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: seg-ri-GAY-shuhn
French: ségrégation

Definition: The enforced separation of racial groups in schools, housing or public spaces.

Example: Segregation was legally enforced in the American South.

B. Historical background: slavery, segregation and civil rights

racial segregation

IPA: /ˌreɪʃəl ˌseɡrɪˈɡeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: RAY-shuhl seg-ri-GAY-shuhn
French: ségrégation raciale

Definition: The separation of people based on race.

Example: Racial segregation shaped housing and education for generations.

Jim Crow laws

IPA: /ˌdʒɪm ˈkrəʊ lɔːz/
Pronunciation: jim kroh lawz
French: lois Jim Crow

Definition: State and local laws that enforced racial segregation in the American South.

Example: Jim Crow laws denied Black Americans equal citizenship.

slavery

IPA: /ˈsleɪvəri/
Pronunciation: SLAY-vuh-ree
French: esclavage

Definition: A system in which people are treated as property and forced to work without freedom.

Example: The legacy of slavery continues to shape racial inequality.

enslavement

IPA: /ɪnˈsleɪvmənt/
Pronunciation: in-SLAYV-muhnt
French: mise en esclavage ; esclavage

Definition: The act or condition of being made a slave.

Example: The enslavement of Africans was foundational to the US economy.

abolition

IPA: /ˌæbəˈlɪʃən/
Pronunciation: ab-uh-LISH-uhn
French: abolition

Definition: The ending of slavery or another institution by law.

Example: Abolition did not immediately bring racial equality.

abolitionist

IPA: /ˌæbəˈlɪʃənɪst/
Pronunciation: ab-uh-LISH-uhn-ist
French: abolitionniste

Definition: A person who campaigned to end slavery.

Example: Abolitionists fought against slavery before the Civil War.

Civil War

IPA: /ˌsɪvəl ˈwɔː/
Pronunciation: SIV-uhl war
French: guerre de Sécession

Definition: The 1861–1865 war between the Union and the Confederacy over slavery, secession and federal authority.

Example: The Civil War ended slavery but not racism.

Reconstruction

IPA: /ˌriːkənˈstrʌkʃən/
Pronunciation: ree-kuhn-STRUK-shuhn
French: Reconstruction

Definition: The period after the Civil War when the US tried to rebuild the South and define the rights of formerly enslaved people.

Example: Reconstruction briefly expanded Black political rights.

sharecropping

IPA: /ˈʃeəkrɒpɪŋ/
Pronunciation: SHAIR-krop-ing
French: métayage

Definition: A farming system in which tenants worked land in exchange for a share of crops, often trapping Black farmers in debt.

Example: Sharecropping replaced slavery with economic dependency for many families.

lynching

IPA: /ˈlɪntʃɪŋ/
Pronunciation: LIN-ching
French: lynchage

Definition: The illegal killing of someone by a mob, often used as racial terror against Black Americans.

Example: Lynching was used to intimidate Black communities.

racial terror

IPA: /ˌreɪʃəl ˈterə/
Pronunciation: RAY-shuhl TERR-uh
French: terreur raciale

Definition: Violence and intimidation used to control racial groups.

Example: Racial terror enforced white supremacy after Reconstruction.

white supremacy

IPA: /ˌwaɪt səˈpreməsi/
Pronunciation: white suh-PREM-uh-see
French: suprémacisme blanc

Definition: The racist belief or system that white people should dominate other racial groups.

Example: White supremacy was built into many laws and institutions.

civil rights

IPA: /ˌsɪvəl ˈraɪts/
Pronunciation: SIV-uhl rites
French: droits civiques

Definition: Rights protecting people from discrimination and guaranteeing equal treatment under the law.

Example: Civil rights are central to American constitutional history.

civil rights movement

IPA: /ˌsɪvəl ˈraɪts ˌmuːvmənt/
Pronunciation: SIV-uhl rites MOOV-muhnt
French: mouvement des droits civiques

Definition: The movement, especially in the 1950s and 1960s, that fought racial segregation and discrimination.

Example: The civil rights movement transformed American law and society.

civil rights activist

IPA: /ˌsɪvəl ˈraɪts ˈæktɪvɪst/
Pronunciation: SIV-uhl rites AK-ti-vist
French: militant des droits civiques

Definition: A person who campaigns for equal civil rights.

Example: Civil rights activists organised boycotts, marches and legal challenges.

desegregation

IPA: /ˌdiːseɡrɪˈɡeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: dee-seg-ri-GAY-shuhn
French: déségrégation

Definition: The process of ending racial segregation.

Example: School desegregation faced fierce resistance.

integration

IPA: /ˌɪntɪˈɡreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: in-ti-GRAY-shuhn
French: intégration

Definition: The inclusion of different racial groups in the same schools, workplaces or public spaces.

Example: Integration was a major goal of civil rights activists.

Brown v. Board of Education

IPA: /braʊn vɜːsəs bɔːd əv ˌedjʊˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: brown VUR-suhs board ov ej-yoo-KAY-shuhn
French: Brown v. Board of Education

Definition: The 1954 Supreme Court ruling that declared racial segregation in public schools unconstitutional.

Example: Brown v. Board of Education overturned school segregation.

Montgomery bus boycott

IPA: /mɒntˈɡʌməri bʌs ˈbɔɪkɒt/
Pronunciation: mont-GUM-uh-ree bus BOY-kot
French: boycott des bus de Montgomery

Definition: A 1955–1956 protest against bus segregation in Montgomery, Alabama.

Example: The Montgomery bus boycott became a landmark civil rights protest.

March on Washington

IPA: /ˌmɑːtʃ ɒn ˈwɒʃɪŋtən/
Pronunciation: march on WOSH-ing-tuhn
French: Marche sur Washington

Definition: The 1963 civil rights demonstration where Martin Luther King Jr. delivered his famous speech.

Example: The March on Washington demanded jobs and freedom.

Civil Rights Act

IPA: /ˌsɪvəl ˈraɪts ækt/
Pronunciation: SIV-uhl rites akt
French: Civil Rights Act ; loi sur les droits civiques

Definition: The 1964 law banning discrimination in public places, employment and federally funded programmes.

Example: The Civil Rights Act outlawed many forms of segregation.

Voting Rights Act

IPA: /ˈvəʊtɪŋ raɪts ækt/
Pronunciation: VOH-ting rites akt
French: Voting Rights Act ; loi sur le droit de vote

Definition: The 1965 law designed to protect minority voting rights.

Example: The Voting Rights Act targeted discriminatory voting practices.

equal protection

IPA: /ˌiːkwəl prəˈtekʃən/
Pronunciation: EE-kwuhl pruh-TEK-shuhn
French: égale protection de la loi

Definition: The constitutional principle that the law must protect people equally.

Example: Equal protection is central to civil rights cases.

C. Policing, criminal justice and racial justice

Fourteenth Amendment

IPA: /ˌfɔːˈtiːnθ əˈmendmənt/
Pronunciation: for-TEENTH uh-MEND-muhnt
French: Quatorzième amendement

Definition: The constitutional amendment guaranteeing citizenship, due process and equal protection.

Example: The Fourteenth Amendment underpins many civil rights decisions.

Fifteenth Amendment

IPA: /ˌfɪfˈtiːnθ əˈmendmənt/
Pronunciation: fif-TEENTH uh-MEND-muhnt
French: Quinzième amendement

Definition: The amendment prohibiting denial of the vote based on race.

Example: The Fifteenth Amendment aimed to protect Black male suffrage.

affirmative action

IPA: /əˌfɜːmətɪv ˈækʃən/
Pronunciation: uh-FUR-muh-tiv AK-shuhn
French: discrimination positive

Definition: Policies designed to increase opportunities for groups historically discriminated against.

Example: Affirmative action remains controversial in university admissions.

racial justice

IPA: /ˌreɪʃəl ˈdʒʌstɪs/
Pronunciation: RAY-shuhl JUS-tis
French: justice raciale

Definition: Fair treatment and equal outcomes across racial groups.

Example: Protesters demanded racial justice after the killing.

social justice

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl ˈdʒʌstɪs/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl JUS-tis
French: justice sociale

Definition: The pursuit of fairness in social, economic and political life.

Example: Social justice movements often address race, class and gender together.

Black Lives Matter

IPA: /ˌblæk laɪvz ˈmætə/
Pronunciation: black lives MAT-uh
French: Black Lives Matter ; les vies noires comptent

Definition: A movement protesting anti-Black racism and police violence.

Example: Black Lives Matter gained global attention after George Floyd’s murder.

BLM

IPA: /ˌbiː el ˈem/
Pronunciation: bee el em
French: BLM

Definition: Abbreviation for Black Lives Matter.

Example: BLM protests called for police accountability.

police brutality

IPA: /pəˈliːs bruːˈtæləti/
Pronunciation: puh-LEESS broo-TAL-uh-tee
French: violences policières

Definition: Excessive or violent force used by police officers.

Example: Police brutality has sparked nationwide protests.

police accountability

IPA: /pəˈliːs əˌkaʊntəˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: puh-LEESS uh-kown-tuh-BIL-uh-tee
French: responsabilité de la police ; redevabilité policière

Definition: The principle that police officers and departments should answer for misconduct.

Example: Campaigners demanded stronger police accountability.

racial profiling

IPA: /ˌreɪʃəl ˈprəʊfaɪlɪŋ/
Pronunciation: RAY-shuhl PROH-fy-ling
French: profilage racial

Definition: Police or security targeting based on race rather than evidence.

Example: Racial profiling can damage trust between communities and police.

stop-and-frisk

IPA: /ˌstɒp ən ˈfrɪsk/
Pronunciation: stop uhn frisk
French: contrôle et fouille ; stop-and-frisk

Definition: A policing practice involving stopping and searching people in public, often criticised for racial bias.

Example: Stop-and-frisk disproportionately affected Black and Latino communities.

mass incarceration

IPA: /ˌmæs ɪnˌkɑːsəˈreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: mass in-kar-suh-RAY-shuhn
French: incarcération de masse

Definition: The large-scale imprisonment of people, especially in the United States.

Example: Mass incarceration has disproportionately affected Black Americans.

D. Socio-economic inequalities and voting rights

criminal justice reform

IPA: /ˌkrɪmɪnəl ˈdʒʌstɪs rɪˌfɔːm/
Pronunciation: KRIM-i-nuhl JUS-tis ri-FORM
French: réforme de la justice pénale

Definition: Changes aimed at making the policing, courts and prison system fairer.

Example: Criminal justice reform became a bipartisan issue in some states.

sentencing disparity

IPA: /ˈsentənsɪŋ dɪˈspærəti/
Pronunciation: SEN-tuhn-sing di-SPAR-uh-tee
French: disparité des peines

Definition: Unequal punishment for similar crimes, often affecting racial groups differently.

Example: Sentencing disparities have contributed to racial inequality.

school-to-prison pipeline

IPA: /ˌskuːl tə ˈprɪzən ˈpaɪplaɪn/
Pronunciation: school tuh PRIZ-uhn PYPE-line
French: pipeline école-prison

Definition: The process by which harsh school discipline pushes students, especially minority students, into the criminal justice system.

Example: The school-to-prison pipeline is a major concern for civil rights groups.

wealth gap

IPA: /ˈwelθ ɡæp/
Pronunciation: welth gap
French: écart de patrimoine

Definition: The difference in wealth between groups.

Example: The racial wealth gap reflects generations of unequal opportunity.

racial wealth gap

IPA: /ˌreɪʃəl ˈwelθ ɡæp/
Pronunciation: RAY-shuhl welth gap
French: écart de patrimoine racial

Definition: The difference in accumulated wealth between racial groups.

Example: The racial wealth gap is much larger than the income gap.

income gap

IPA: /ˈɪnkʌm ɡæp/
Pronunciation: IN-kum gap
French: écart de revenus

Definition: The difference in income between groups.

Example: The income gap affects access to housing and education.

housing discrimination

IPA: /ˈhaʊzɪŋ dɪˌskrɪmɪˈneɪʃən/
Pronunciation: HOW-zing dis-krim-i-NAY-shuhn
French: discrimination au logement

Definition: Unfair treatment in housing based on race or other protected characteristics.

Example: Housing discrimination helped create segregated neighbourhoods.

redlining

IPA: /ˈredlaɪnɪŋ/
Pronunciation: RED-line-ing
French: redlining ; discrimination bancaire territoriale

Definition: A discriminatory practice denying loans or services to neighbourhoods based on racial composition.

Example: Redlining prevented many Black families from building wealth.

residential segregation

IPA: /ˌrezɪˈdenʃəl ˌseɡrɪˈɡeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: rez-i-DEN-shuhl seg-ri-GAY-shuhn
French: ségrégation résidentielle

Definition: The separation of racial groups into different neighbourhoods.

Example: Residential segregation affects school funding and health outcomes.

food desert

IPA: /ˈfuːd ˌdezət/
Pronunciation: food DEZ-uht
French: désert alimentaire

Definition: An area with limited access to affordable and healthy food.

Example: Food deserts are often found in low-income minority neighbourhoods.

educational inequality

IPA: /ˌedjʊˈkeɪʃənəl ˌɪnɪˈkwɒləti/
Pronunciation: ej-yoo-KAY-shuh-nuhl in-i-KWOL-uh-tee
French: inégalité éducative

Definition: Unequal access to quality education.

Example: Educational inequality often reflects housing segregation.

school funding

IPA: /ˈskuːl ˌfʌndɪŋ/
Pronunciation: school FUN-ding
French: financement des écoles

Definition: Money available to schools, often linked to local taxes in the US.

Example: School funding varies widely between districts.

achievement gap

IPA: /əˈtʃiːvmənt ɡæp/
Pronunciation: uh-CHEEV-muhnt gap
French: écart de réussite scolaire

Definition: Differences in educational performance between groups.

Example: The achievement gap is linked to poverty and unequal resources.

health disparities

IPA: /ˈhelθ dɪˌspærətiz/
Pronunciation: helth di-SPAR-uh-teez
French: disparités de santé

Definition: Differences in health outcomes between groups.

Example: Health disparities were exposed during the pandemic.

environmental racism

IPA: /ɪnˌvaɪrənˈmentəl ˈreɪsɪzəm/
Pronunciation: in-vy-ruhn-MEN-tuhl RAY-siz-uhm
French: racisme environnemental

Definition: Environmental harm that disproportionately affects racial minorities.

Example: Environmental racism places polluting industries near minority communities.

voter suppression

IPA: /ˈvəʊtə səˌpreʃən/
Pronunciation: VOH-tuh suh-PRESH-uhn
French: entraves au vote ; suppression du vote

Definition: Actions or laws that make it harder for certain groups to vote.

Example: Civil rights groups denounced the law as voter suppression.

voting access

IPA: /ˈvəʊtɪŋ ˈækses/
Pronunciation: VOH-ting AK-sess
French: accès au vote

voter ID law

IPA: /ˌvəʊtər aɪ ˈdiː lɔː/
Pronunciation: VOH-tuhr eye-dee law
French: loi exigeant une pièce d’identité pour voter

Definition: A law requiring voters to show identification before voting.

felony disenfranchisement

IPA: /ˈfeləni ˌdɪsɪnˈfræntʃaɪzmənt/
Pronunciation: FEL-uh-nee dis-in-FRAN-chyz-muhnt
French: privation du droit de vote des personnes condamnées

gerrymandering

Pronunciation: JER-ee-man-duh-ring
French: charcutage électoral ; découpage partisan

Example: Racial gerrymandering can weaken minority representation.

minority representation

IPA: /maɪˈnɒrəti ˌreprɪzenˈteɪʃən/
Pronunciation: my-NOR-uh-tee rep-ri-zen-TAY-shuhn
French: représentation des minorités

Definition: The presence and political voice of minority groups in institutions.

Example: Minority representation has increased but remains uneven.

E. Culture wars, policy debates and high-value verbs

descriptive representation

IPA: /dɪˈskrɪptɪv ˌreprɪzenˈteɪʃən/
Pronunciation: di-SKRIP-tiv rep-ri-zen-TAY-shuhn
French: représentation descriptive

Definition: Representation in which elected officials share characteristics with the people they represent.

Example: Descriptive representation can increase trust in institutions.

substantive representation

IPA: /ˈsʌbstəntɪv ˌreprɪzenˈteɪʃən/
Pronunciation: SUB-stuhn-tiv rep-ri-zen-TAY-shuhn
French: représentation substantielle

Definition: Representation based on defending a group’s interests, regardless of the representative’s identity.

Example: Substantive representation matters as much as symbolic diversity.

identity politics

IPA: /aɪˈdentəti ˈpɒlɪtɪks/
Pronunciation: eye-DEN-tuh-tee POL-i-tiks
French: politique identitaire

Definition: Politics based on the interests and experiences of identity groups.

Example: Debates over race are often framed as identity politics.

woke

IPA: /wəʊk/
Pronunciation: wohk
French: woke ; conscient des injustices sociales

Definition: Originally meaning alert to racial injustice; now often used politically and sometimes pejoratively.

Example: The term woke has become a culture-war label.

critical race theory

IPA: /ˌkrɪtɪkəl ˈreɪs ˈθɪəri/
Pronunciation: KRIT-i-kuhl rays THEE-uh-ree
French: théorie critique de la race

Definition: An academic framework examining how law and institutions reproduce racial inequality.
