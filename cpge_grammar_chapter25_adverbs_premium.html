B1 → B2 grammar for nuance, precision, data commentary and academic argumentation.

Chapter objective

This premium grammar chapter helps CPGE students master adverbs and their position in English sentences. It covers frequency, manner, degree, sentence adverbs, data-commentary adverbs, word order and common French-speaker mistakes.

The golden rule

Adverb placement depends on function
Frequency: before main verb / after be
Manner: often after verb or object
Sentence adverb: often at the beginning
Main useAdverbs modify verbs, adjectives, other adverbs or whole sentences. They add nuance, precision and logical force.
CPGE habitUse adverbs to avoid simplistic claims: arguably, somewhat, significantly, gradually, clearly.
Key trapDo not confuse actually and currently: currently = now; actually = in fact.

A. Lesson — What are adverbs?

Adverbs

French: adverbes

Rule / explanation: Adverbs modify verbs, adjectives, other adverbs or whole sentences.

Example / answer: The article clearly explains the causes of the crisis.

Manner

French: manière

Rule / explanation: Adverbs of manner explain how something happens.

Example / answer: The government reacted slowly.

Frequency

French: fréquence

Rule / explanation: Adverbs of frequency explain how often something happens.

Example / answer: Politicians often promise reform.

Degree

French: degré

Rule / explanation: Adverbs of degree intensify or soften meaning.

Example / answer: The argument is highly convincing.

Sentence adverbs

French: adverbes de phrase

Rule / explanation: Some adverbs comment on the whole sentence.

Example / answer: Clearly, the policy needs more funding.

CPGE use

French: usage CPGE

Rule / explanation: Adverbs help students nuance claims and write more precise academic English.

Example / answer: The author cautiously suggests that the reform may fail.

B. Adverbs of frequency

Always

French: toujours

Rule / explanation: Means 100% of the time; placed before the main verb but after be.

Example / answer: Politicians always promise change.

Usually

French: habituellement

Rule / explanation: Means most of the time.

Example / answer: The press usually focuses on conflict.

Often

French: souvent

Rule / explanation: Means frequently.

Example / answer: The article often refers to recent data.

Sometimes

French: parfois

Rule / explanation: Means occasionally.

Example / answer: Governments sometimes ignore expert warnings.

Rarely

French: rarement

Rule / explanation: Means not often.

Example / answer: The author rarely mentions rural voters.

Never

French: jamais

Rule / explanation: Means 0%; do not use with another negative.

Example / answer: The article never claims that reform is easy.

C. Adverbs of manner, place and data

Carefully

French: soigneusement

Rule / explanation: With attention and precision.

Example / answer: Students should read the question carefully.

Clearly

French: clairement

Rule / explanation: In a clear way.

Example / answer: The article clearly identifies the problem.

Gradually

French: progressivement

Rule / explanation: Little by little; useful for trends.

Example / answer: Public trust has gradually declined.

Sharply

French: fortement / brusquement

Rule / explanation: Suddenly or strongly; useful for data.

Example / answer: Prices rose sharply after the energy crisis.

Nationwide

French: dans tout le pays

Rule / explanation: Across the whole country.

Example / answer: Protests spread nationwide.

Approximately

French: environ

Rule / explanation: Useful for rounded figures.

Example / answer: Approximately 40% of respondents supported the proposal.

D. Adverbs of degree and nuance

Quite

French: assez / tout à fait

Rule / explanation: In British English, often means fairly; sometimes completely depending on adjective.

Example / answer: The argument is quite convincing.

Rather

French: plutôt

Rule / explanation: Useful for cautious criticism.

Example / answer: The conclusion is rather simplistic.

Fairly

French: assez

Rule / explanation: Moderate intensifier; useful for nuance.

Example / answer: The article is fairly balanced.

Highly

French: hautement / très

Rule / explanation: Formal intensifier with likely, effective, controversial.

Example / answer: The reform is highly controversial.

Somewhat

French: quelque peu

Rule / explanation: Formal softener meaning to some extent.

Example / answer: The proposal is somewhat unrealistic.

Deeply

French: profondément

Rule / explanation: Strong emotional or structural intensity.

Example / answer: The country is deeply divided.

E. Sentence adverbs for argumentation

Arguably

French: on peut soutenir que

Rule / explanation: Means one can argue that; useful for nuanced claims.

Example / answer: Arguably, the housing crisis is now a generational issue.

Undoubtedly

French: sans aucun doute

Rule / explanation: Means without doubt; strong academic claim.

Example / answer: Undoubtedly, education remains central to social mobility.

Apparently

French: apparemment

Rule / explanation: Means it seems, based on available evidence.

Example / answer: Apparently, the policy failed to reassure voters.

Presumably

French: vraisemblablement

Rule / explanation: Means probably, based on reasonable assumption.

Example / answer: Presumably, the author wanted to challenge conventional views.

Importantly

French: fait important

Rule / explanation: Introduces a significant point.

Example / answer: Importantly, the article does not blame voters themselves.

Interestingly

French: fait intéressant

Rule / explanation: Introduces a noteworthy observation.

Example / answer: Interestingly, the graph shows a different trend after 2016.

F. Adverb position and word order

Before the main verb

French: avant le verbe principal

Rule / explanation: Many adverbs of frequency go before the main verb.

Example / answer: Voters often distrust institutions.

After be

French: après be

Rule / explanation: Frequency adverbs usually go after be.

Example / answer: The issue is often misunderstood.

Between auxiliary and main verb

French: entre auxiliaire et verbe

Rule / explanation: Adverbs often go between auxiliary and main verb.

Example / answer: The government has recently announced a reform.

End position

French: position finale

Rule / explanation: Adverbs of manner, place and time often go at the end.

Example / answer: The minister spoke clearly yesterday.

Front position

French: position initiale

Rule / explanation: Sentence adverbs and time adverbials can go at the beginning.

Example / answer: Recently, the debate has intensified.

CPGE word order

French: ordre des mots CPGE

Rule / explanation: Adverb placement should support clarity, not imitate French word order.

Example / answer: The author carefully distinguishes freedom from impunity.

G. Typical mistakes by French speakers

Never with another negative

French: double négation

Rule / explanation: Never already has negative meaning.

Example / answer: Wrong: The author does not never mention it. Correct: The author never mentions it.

Actually vs currently

French: actually / currently

Rule / explanation: Actually means in fact; currently means at the moment.

Example / answer: Currently, the bill is being debated. Actually, the cost is higher than expected.

Eventually vs éventuellement

French: eventually

Rule / explanation: Eventually means in the end, not possibly.

Example / answer: Possibly = possibly / maybe. Eventually = in the end.

Hardly vs hard

French: hard / hardly

Rule / explanation: Hardly means almost not; hard means with effort or intensity.

Example / answer: The policy hardly worked. / The government worked hard.

Late vs lately

French: late / lately

Rule / explanation: Late means not on time; lately means recently.

Example / answer: The report arrived late. / The issue has attracted attention lately.

Too vs also

French: too / also

Rule / explanation: Too before adjectives often means excessive; also means addition.

Example / answer: The reform is too expensive. / The article also discusses poverty.
