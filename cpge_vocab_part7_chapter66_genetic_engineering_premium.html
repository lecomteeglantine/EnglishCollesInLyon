Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse genetic engineering: DNA, genes, mutations, CRISPR, gene editing, gene therapy, germline editing, designer babies, genetic privacy, GMOs, gene drives, ecological risks, biosafety, biosecurity, public trust and the balance between scientific progress and precaution.

A. Core genetic engineering and DNA vocabulary

genetic engineering

IPA: /dʒəˌnetɪk ˌendʒɪˈnɪərɪŋ/
Pronunciation: juh-NET-ik en-ji-NEER-ing
French: génie génétique

Definition: The deliberate modification of an organism’s genetic material to change its traits or functions.

Example: Genetic engineering can be used in medicine, agriculture and industry.

genome editing

IPA: /ˈdʒiːnəʊm ˌedɪtɪŋ/
Pronunciation: JEE-nohm ED-it-ing
French: édition du génome

Definition: Precise modification of DNA within an organism’s genome.

Example: Genome editing could correct harmful mutations.

gene editing

IPA: /ˈdʒiːn ˌedɪtɪŋ/
Pronunciation: jeen ED-it-ing
French: édition génétique

Definition: Changing the DNA sequence of a gene.

Example: Gene editing raises hopes for treating inherited diseases.

genetic modification

IPA: /dʒəˌnetɪk ˌmɒdɪfɪˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: juh-NET-ik mod-i-fi-KAY-shuhn
French: modification génétique

Definition: Changing the genetic material of an organism.

Example: Genetic modification is debated in food production.

genetically modified

IPA: /dʒəˌnetɪkəli ˈmɒdɪfaɪd/
Pronunciation: juh-NET-i-kuh-lee MOD-i-fyd
French: génétiquement modifié

Definition: Having genetic material deliberately changed by biotechnology.

Example: Genetically modified crops are common in some countries.

genetically modified organism

IPA: /dʒəˌnetɪkəli ˈmɒdɪfaɪd ˈɔːɡənɪzəm/
Pronunciation: juh-NET-i-kuh-lee MOD-i-fyd OR-guh-niz-uhm
French: organisme génétiquement modifié

Definition: An organism whose DNA has been deliberately altered.

Example: A genetically modified organism may resist disease or pests.

GMO

IPA: /ˌdʒiː em ˈəʊ/
Pronunciation: jee-em-oh
French: OGM

Definition: Abbreviation for genetically modified organism.

Example: GMOs remain controversial in public debate.

transgenic organism

IPA: /trænzˌdʒenɪk ˈɔːɡənɪzəm/
Pronunciation: tranz-JEN-ik OR-guh-niz-uhm
French: organisme transgénique

Definition: An organism containing genetic material from another species.

Example: A transgenic organism may produce a useful protein.

transgene

IPA: /ˈtrænzˌdʒiːn/
Pronunciation: TRANZ-jeen
French: transgène

Definition: A gene transferred from one organism to another.

Example: A transgene can give a plant a new trait.

recombinant DNA

IPA: /rɪˈkɒmbɪnənt ˌdiː en ˈeɪ/
Pronunciation: ri-KOM-bi-nuhnt dee-en-ay
French: ADN recombinant

Definition: DNA formed by combining genetic material from different sources.

Example: Recombinant DNA technology made modern biotechnology possible.

DNA

IPA: /ˌdiː en ˈeɪ/
Pronunciation: dee-en-ay
French: ADN

Definition: Deoxyribonucleic acid, the molecule carrying genetic information.

Example: DNA contains the instructions inherited from parents.

RNA

IPA: /ˌɑːr en ˈeɪ/
Pronunciation: ar-en-ay
French: ARN

Definition: Ribonucleic acid, a molecule involved in gene expression and protein production.

Example: RNA carries genetic messages inside cells.

gene

IPA: /dʒiːn/
Pronunciation: jeen
French: gène

Definition: A unit of heredity made of DNA that influences traits or functions.

Example: A gene can affect eye colour or disease risk.

genome

IPA: /ˈdʒiːnəʊm/
Pronunciation: JEE-nohm
French: génome

Definition: The complete set of genetic material in an organism.

Example: The genome can be edited with modern tools.

chromosome

IPA: /ˈkrəʊməsəʊm/
Pronunciation: KROH-muh-sohm
French: chromosome

Definition: A DNA structure carrying many genes.

Example: Humans usually have 46 chromosomes.

allele

IPA: /əˈliːl/
Pronunciation: uh-LEEL
French: allèle

Definition: A version of a gene.

Example: Different alleles can produce different traits.

trait

IPA: /treɪt/
Pronunciation: trayt
French: caractère ; trait

Definition: A characteristic of an organism influenced by genes and environment.

Example: Height is a trait influenced by many genes.

phenotype

IPA: /ˈfiːnətaɪp/
Pronunciation: FEE-noh-type
French: phénotype

Definition: The observable traits of an organism.

Example: The phenotype may change with the environment.

genotype

IPA: /ˈdʒenətaɪp/
Pronunciation: JEN-oh-type
French: génotype

Definition: The genetic makeup of an organism.

Example: The genotype influences the phenotype.

mutation

IPA: /mjuːˈteɪʃən/
Pronunciation: myoo-TAY-shuhn
French: mutation

Definition: A change in the DNA sequence.

Example: A mutation can be harmful, neutral or beneficial.

point mutation

IPA: /ˌpɔɪnt mjuːˈteɪʃən/
Pronunciation: point myoo-TAY-shuhn
French: mutation ponctuelle

Definition: A mutation affecting a single DNA base.

Example: A point mutation can change a protein.

insertion

IPA: /ɪnˈsɜːʃən/
Pronunciation: in-SUR-shuhn
French: insertion

Definition: A mutation where extra DNA is added.

Example: An insertion can disrupt a gene.

deletion

IPA: /dɪˈliːʃən/
Pronunciation: di-LEE-shuhn
French: délétion

Definition: A mutation where DNA is removed.

Example: A deletion can cause a genetic disorder.

DNA sequencing

IPA: /ˌdiː en ˈeɪ ˈsiːkwənsɪŋ/
Pronunciation: dee-en-ay SEE-kwuhn-sing
French: séquençage de l’ADN

Definition: Determining the order of DNA bases.

Example: DNA sequencing can identify mutations.

genome sequencing

IPA: /ˈdʒiːnəʊm ˈsiːkwənsɪŋ/
Pronunciation: JEE-nohm SEE-kwuhn-sing
French: séquençage du génome

Definition: Determining the complete DNA sequence of a genome.

Example: Genome sequencing is used in research and medicine.

CRISPR

IPA: /ˈkrɪspə/
Pronunciation: KRIS-puh
French: CRISPR

Definition: A gene-editing technology allowing targeted changes to DNA.

Example: CRISPR has made genetic engineering more precise.

CRISPR-Cas9

IPA: /ˈkrɪspə kæs naɪn/
Pronunciation: KRIS-puh cass-nine
French: CRISPR-Cas9

Definition: A gene-editing system using Cas9 to cut DNA at a targeted location.

Example: CRISPR-Cas9 can cut DNA like molecular scissors.

Cas9

IPA: /ˈkæs naɪn/
Pronunciation: cass-nine
French: Cas9

Definition: An enzyme used in CRISPR systems to cut DNA.

Example: Cas9 is guided to a specific DNA sequence.

guide RNA

IPA: /ˌɡaɪd ɑːr en ˈeɪ/
Pronunciation: guide ar-en-ay
French: ARN guide

Definition: RNA that directs CRISPR machinery to a target DNA sequence.

Example: Guide RNA tells Cas9 where to cut.

molecular scissors

IPA: /məˌlekjʊlə ˈsɪzəz/
Pronunciation: muh-LEK-yuh-luh SIZ-uhz
French: ciseaux moléculaires

Definition: A metaphor for tools that cut DNA at specific points.

Example: CRISPR is often described as molecular scissors.

target sequence

IPA: /ˈtɑːɡɪt ˈsiːkwəns/
Pronunciation: TAR-git SEE-kwuhns
French: séquence cible

Definition: The DNA sequence selected for editing.

Example: The target sequence must be chosen carefully.

off-target effect

IPA: /ˌɒf ˈtɑːɡɪt ɪˌfekt/
Pronunciation: off-TAR-git i-FEKT
French: effet hors cible

Definition: An unintended genetic change at the wrong location.

Example: Off-target effects are a safety concern.

B. CRISPR, editing tools and gene-function techniques

precision

IPA: /prɪˈsɪʒən/
Pronunciation: pri-SIZH-uhn
French: précision

Definition: The quality of being exact or accurate.

Example: Greater precision reduces unintended genetic damage.

accuracy

IPA: /ˈækjʊrəsi/
Pronunciation: AK-yuh-ruh-see
French: exactitude ; précision

Definition: The quality of being correct or reliable.

Example: Accuracy matters in medical gene editing.

base editing

IPA: /ˈbeɪs ˌedɪtɪŋ/
Pronunciation: bayce ED-it-ing
French: édition de base

Definition: A gene-editing technique changing a single DNA base without cutting both DNA strands.

Example: Base editing may correct some point mutations.

prime editing

IPA: /ˈpraɪm ˌedɪtɪŋ/
Pronunciation: prime ED-it-ing
French: prime editing ; édition primaire

Definition: A gene-editing technique able to make precise DNA changes without double-strand breaks.

Example: Prime editing is sometimes called a search-and-replace tool for DNA.

gene drive

IPA: /ˈdʒiːn draɪv/
Pronunciation: jeen dryv
French: forçage génétique

Definition: A genetic system designed to spread a trait rapidly through a population.

Example: Gene drives could be used to control disease-carrying mosquitoes.

horizontal gene transfer

IPA: /ˌhɒrɪˈzɒntəl ˈdʒiːn ˌtrænsfɜː/
Pronunciation: hor-i-ZON-tuhl jeen TRANS-fur
French: transfert horizontal de gènes

Definition: Transfer of genetic material between organisms outside reproduction.

Example: Horizontal gene transfer is common in bacteria.

vector

IPA: /ˈvektə/
Pronunciation: VEK-tuh
French: vecteur

Definition: A carrier used to deliver genetic material into cells.

Example: Viruses can be used as vectors in gene therapy.

viral vector

IPA: /ˈvaɪrəl ˌvektə/
Pronunciation: VY-ruhl VEK-tuh
French: vecteur viral

Definition: A virus modified to deliver genetic material safely.

Example: A viral vector can carry a therapeutic gene.

plasmid

IPA: /ˈplæzmɪd/
Pronunciation: PLAZ-mid
French: plasmide

Definition: A small circular DNA molecule often used in genetic engineering.

Example: Scientists use plasmids to transfer genes into bacteria.

restriction enzyme

IPA: /rɪˈstrɪkʃən ˈenzaɪm/
Pronunciation: ri-STRIK-shuhn EN-zyme
French: enzyme de restriction

Definition: An enzyme that cuts DNA at specific sequences.

Example: Restriction enzymes are important tools in molecular biology.

DNA ligase

IPA: /ˌdiː en ˈeɪ ˈlaɪɡeɪs/
Pronunciation: dee-en-ay LYE-gayz
French: ADN ligase

Definition: An enzyme that joins DNA fragments together.

Example: DNA ligase helps build recombinant DNA.

polymerase chain reaction

IPA: /pəˈlɪməreɪz tʃeɪn riˈækʃən/
Pronunciation: puh-LIM-uh-rayz chain ree-AK-shuhn
French: réaction en chaîne par polymérase

Definition: A laboratory technique used to amplify DNA.

Example: PCR can produce millions of copies of a DNA sequence.

PCR

IPA: /ˌpiː siː ˈɑː/
Pronunciation: pee-see-ar
French: PCR

Definition: Abbreviation for polymerase chain reaction.

Example: PCR is widely used in testing and research.

gene expression

IPA: /ˈdʒiːn ɪkˌspreʃən/
Pronunciation: jeen ik-SPRESH-uhn
French: expression génique

Definition: The process by which information in a gene is used to make a functional product.

Example: Gene expression can be turned up or down.

gene silencing

IPA: /ˈdʒiːn ˌsaɪlənsɪŋ/
Pronunciation: jeen SY-luhn-sing
French: silencement génique

Definition: Reducing or stopping the activity of a gene.

Example: Gene silencing can block harmful protein production.

knockout

IPA: /ˈnɒkaʊt/
Pronunciation: NOK-owt
French: inactivation d’un gène ; knockout

Definition: A genetic modification that disables a gene.

Example: A knockout mouse can help researchers study gene function.

knock-in

IPA: /ˈnɒk ɪn/
Pronunciation: NOK-in
French: insertion ciblée ; knock-in

Definition: A genetic modification that inserts a gene or sequence into a genome.

Example: A knock-in can introduce a disease-related mutation.

gene therapy

IPA: /ˈdʒiːn ˌθerəpi/
Pronunciation: jeen THERR-uh-pee
French: thérapie génique

Definition: Treatment that modifies, replaces or adds genes to treat disease.

Example: Gene therapy may cure some inherited disorders.

somatic gene therapy

IPA: /səʊˌmætɪk ˈdʒiːn ˌθerəpi/
Pronunciation: soh-MAT-ik jeen THERR-uh-pee
French: thérapie génique somatique

Definition: Gene therapy targeting body cells, not reproductive cells.

Example: Somatic gene therapy affects only the treated patient.

germline editing

IPA: /ˈdʒɜːmlaɪn ˌedɪtɪŋ/
Pronunciation: JURM-line ED-it-ing
French: édition de la lignée germinale

Definition: Editing genes in eggs, sperm or embryos so changes can be inherited.

Example: Germline editing is ethically controversial.

somatic cell

IPA: /səʊˌmætɪk ˈsel/
Pronunciation: soh-MAT-ik sel
French: cellule somatique

Definition: A body cell that is not a reproductive cell.

Example: Somatic cells do not pass genetic changes to offspring.

germ cell

IPA: /ˈdʒɜːm sel/
Pronunciation: jurm sel
French: cellule germinale

Definition: A reproductive cell or precursor cell involved in inheritance.

Example: Changes in germ cells can be inherited.

embryo

IPA: /ˈembriəʊ/
Pronunciation: EM-bree-oh
French: embryon

Definition: An early stage of development after fertilisation.

Example: Editing an embryo raises major ethical concerns.

designer baby

IPA: /dɪˌzaɪnə ˈbeɪbi/
Pronunciation: di-ZY-nuh BAY-bee
French: bébé sur mesure

Definition: A child whose genetic traits have been selected or modified, especially for non-medical reasons.

Example: The idea of designer babies worries many ethicists.

C. Human gene editing, ethics and social risks

enhancement

IPA: /ɪnˈhɑːnsmənt/
Pronunciation: in-HAHNS-muhnt
French: amélioration ; enhancement

Definition: Improving traits beyond normal health or treatment.

Example: Genetic enhancement raises questions about fairness.

therapy versus enhancement

IPA: /ˈθerəpi ˈvɜːsəs ɪnˈhɑːnsmənt/
Pronunciation: THERR-uh-pee VUR-suhs in-HAHNS-muhnt
French: thérapie contre amélioration

Definition: The distinction between treating disease and improving traits beyond normal function.

Example: The line between therapy and enhancement can be hard to draw.

eugenics

IPA: /juːˈdʒenɪks/
Pronunciation: yoo-JEN-iks
French: eugénisme

Definition: The discredited practice or ideology of trying to improve human populations through selective breeding or genetic control.

Example: Genetic engineering revives fears of eugenics.

inherited change

IPA: /ɪnˌherɪtɪd ˈtʃeɪndʒ/
Pronunciation: in-HERR-i-tid chaynj
French: changement héréditaire

Definition: A genetic change passed to future generations.

Example: Germline editing creates inherited changes.

informed consent

IPA: /ɪnˌfɔːmd kənˈsent/
Pronunciation: in-FORMD kuhn-SENT
French: consentement éclairé

Definition: Permission given after understanding risks, benefits and alternatives.

Example: Future generations cannot give informed consent to germline editing.

bioethics

IPA: /ˌbaɪəʊˈeθɪks/
Pronunciation: BY-oh-ETH-iks
French: bioéthique

Definition: The study of ethical issues in biology, medicine and biotechnology.

Example: Bioethics is central to debates over human gene editing.

ethical dilemma

IPA: /ˌeθɪkəl dɪˈlemə/
Pronunciation: ETH-i-kuhl di-LEM-uh
French: dilemme éthique

Definition: A situation involving difficult moral choices.

Example: Editing embryos creates ethical dilemmas.

moral boundary

IPA: /ˌmɒrəl ˈbaʊndəri/
Pronunciation: MOR-uhl BOWN-duh-ree
French: limite morale

Definition: A limit that society considers morally important.

Example: Human enhancement may cross a moral boundary.

slippery slope

IPA: /ˌslɪpəri ˈsləʊp/
Pronunciation: SLIP-uh-ree slohp
French: pente glissante

Definition: A situation where one step may lead to more extreme consequences.

Example: Critics warn of a slippery slope from therapy to enhancement.

genetic privacy

IPA: /dʒəˌnetɪk ˈprɪvəsi/
Pronunciation: juh-NET-ik PRIV-uh-see
French: confidentialité génétique

Definition: Protection of a person’s genetic information.

Example: Genetic privacy is difficult because DNA reveals family information.

genetic data

IPA: /dʒəˌnetɪk ˈdeɪtə/
Pronunciation: juh-NET-ik DAY-tuh
French: données génétiques

Definition: Information derived from DNA or genetic testing.

patent

intellectual property
