Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse fake news: misinformation, disinformation, propaganda, conspiracy theories, deepfakes, bots, troll farms, algorithmic amplification, echo chambers, fact-checking, media literacy, platform accountability, free speech and the democratic consequences of a polluted information ecosystem.

A. Core fake news and information disorder vocabulary

fake news

IPA: /ˌfeɪk ˈnjuːz/
Pronunciation: fayk NYOOZ
French: fausses nouvelles ; infox

Definition: False or misleading information presented as news.

Example: Fake news can spread faster than corrections.

misinformation

IPA: /ˌmɪsɪnfəˈmeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: mis-in-fuh-MAY-shuhn
French: mésinformation

Definition: False information spread without necessarily intending to deceive.

Example: Misinformation spreads quickly during crises.

disinformation

IPA: /ˌdɪsɪnfəˈmeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: dis-in-fuh-MAY-shuhn
French: désinformation

Definition: False information deliberately spread to deceive or manipulate.

Example: Disinformation campaigns can target elections.

malinformation

IPA: /ˌmælɪnfəˈmeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: mal-in-fuh-MAY-shuhn
French: information malveillante

Definition: True information used maliciously or out of context to harm someone.

Example: Leaked private data can become malinformation.

information disorder

IPA: /ˌɪnfəˈmeɪʃən dɪsˌɔːdə/
Pronunciation: in-fuh-MAY-shuhn dis-OR-duh
French: désordre informationnel

Definition: The ecosystem of false, misleading or harmful information.

Example: Information disorder weakens democratic debate.

rumour

IPA: /ˈruːmə/
Pronunciation: ROO-muh
French: rumeur

Definition: Unverified information circulating among people.

Example: Rumours often spread after disasters.

hoax

IPA: /həʊks/
Pronunciation: hohks
French: canular

Definition: A deliberately fabricated story designed to trick people.

Example: The viral story turned out to be a hoax.

fabrication

IPA: /ˌfæbrɪˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: fab-ri-KAY-shuhn
French: invention ; fabrication

Definition: Something invented rather than based on fact.

Example: The article was a complete fabrication.

false claim

IPA: /ˌfɔːls ˈkleɪm/
Pronunciation: false klaym
French: affirmation fausse

Definition: An assertion that is not true.

Example: The politician repeated a false claim.

misleading claim

IPA: /ˌmɪsˈliːdɪŋ kleɪm/
Pronunciation: mis-LEE-ding klaym
French: affirmation trompeuse

Definition: A claim that gives a false impression.

Example: A true statistic can still be misleading.

baseless claim

IPA: /ˌbeɪsləs ˈkleɪm/
Pronunciation: BAYSS-luhs klaym
French: affirmation sans fondement

Definition: A claim with no evidence.

Example: Baseless claims can influence voters.

unverified claim

IPA: /ʌnˌverɪfaɪd ˈkleɪm/
Pronunciation: un-VER-i-fyd klaym
French: affirmation non vérifiée

Definition: A claim that has not been checked or confirmed.

Example: Unverified claims should be labelled clearly.

conspiracy theory

IPA: /kənˈspɪrəsi ˌθɪəri/
Pronunciation: kuhn-SPIR-uh-see THEE-uh-ree
French: théorie du complot

Definition: A belief that hidden groups secretly control events, often without evidence.

Example: Conspiracy theories flourish when trust is low.

conspiracist

IPA: /kənˈspɪrəsɪst/
Pronunciation: kuhn-SPIR-uh-sist
French: complotiste

Definition: A person who believes or spreads conspiracy theories.

Example: Conspiracists often reject mainstream evidence.

denialism

IPA: /dɪˈnaɪəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: di-NY-uh-liz-uhm
French: déni organisé

Definition: The rejection of established facts or scientific consensus.

Example: Denialism can delay public action.

climate denial

IPA: /ˈklaɪmət dɪˌnaɪəl/
Pronunciation: KLY-muht di-NY-uhl
French: déni climatique

Definition: Rejection of well-established climate science.

Example: Climate denial has slowed policy responses.

science denial

IPA: /ˈsaɪəns dɪˌnaɪəl/
Pronunciation: SY-uhns di-NY-uhl
French: déni de la science

Definition: Rejection of scientific evidence or consensus.

Example: Science denial can be politically profitable.

health misinformation

IPA: /ˈhelθ ˌmɪsɪnfəˈmeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: health mis-in-fuh-MAY-shuhn
French: mésinformation sanitaire

Definition: False or inaccurate health-related information.

Example: Health misinformation can put lives at risk.

anti-vaccine misinformation

IPA: /ˌænti ˈvæksiːn ˌmɪsɪnfəˈmeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: AN-tee VAK-seen mis-in-fuh-MAY-shuhn
French: désinformation antivaccin

Definition: False or misleading information about vaccines.

Example: Anti-vaccine misinformation undermines public health.

election misinformation

IPA: /ɪˈlekʃən ˌmɪsɪnfəˈmeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: i-LEK-shuhn mis-in-fuh-MAY-shuhn
French: mésinformation électorale

Definition: False or misleading information about elections or voting.

Example: Election misinformation can suppress turnout.

propaganda

IPA: /ˌprɒpəˈɡændə/
Pronunciation: prop-uh-GAN-duh
French: propagande

Definition: Information designed to influence people, often biased or misleading.

Example: Propaganda can be used by states or political groups.

state propaganda

IPA: /ˌsteɪt ˌprɒpəˈɡændə/
Pronunciation: state prop-uh-GAN-duh
French: propagande d’État

Definition: Propaganda produced or promoted by a government.

Example: State propaganda can justify repression.

influence operation

IPA: /ˈɪnfluəns ˌɒpəˈreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: IN-floo-uhns op-uh-RAY-shuhn
French: opération d’influence

Definition: A coordinated attempt to shape public opinion covertly.

Example: Influence operations can target elections.

information warfare

IPA: /ˌɪnfəˈmeɪʃən ˈwɔːfeə/
Pronunciation: in-fuh-MAY-shuhn WOR-fair
French: guerre informationnelle

Definition: The strategic use of information to disrupt or weaken opponents.

Example: Information warfare is part of modern conflict.

troll

IPA: /trɒl/
Pronunciation: trol
French: troll

Definition: A person or account posting inflammatory content to provoke or disrupt.

Example: Trolls try to derail online debate.

troll farm

IPA: /ˈtrɒl fɑːm/
Pronunciation: trol farm
French: usine à trolls

Definition: An organised group producing manipulative online comments or posts.

Example: Troll farms can amplify propaganda.

bot

IPA: /bɒt/
Pronunciation: bot
French: bot

Definition: An automated account or program posting online content.

Example: Bots can boost hashtags artificially.

bot network

IPA: /ˈbɒt ˌnetwɜːk/
Pronunciation: bot NET-work
French: réseau de bots

Definition: A coordinated group of automated accounts.

Example: A bot network can make a fringe idea appear popular.

B. Digital manipulation, bias and viral dynamics

sock puppet account

IPA: /ˈsɒk ˌpʌpɪt əˌkaʊnt/
Pronunciation: sok PUP-it uh-kownt
French: faux compte

Definition: A fake online identity used to deceive or manipulate.

Example: Sock puppet accounts can fake grassroots support.

astroturfing

IPA: /ˈæstrəʊˌtɜːfɪŋ/
Pronunciation: AS-troh-TURF-ing
French: faux mouvement citoyen

Definition: Fake grassroots campaigning designed to look spontaneous.

Example: Astroturfing hides who is behind a campaign.

deepfake

IPA: /ˈdiːpfeɪk/
Pronunciation: DEEP-fayk
French: hypertrucage ; deepfake

Definition: AI-generated fake audio, video or image designed to look real.

Example: Deepfakes make visual evidence harder to trust.

synthetic media

IPA: /sɪnˌθetɪk ˈmiːdiə/
Pronunciation: sin-THET-ik MEE-dee-uh
French: média synthétique

Definition: Media generated or manipulated by artificial intelligence.

Example: Synthetic media can be creative or deceptive.

cheapfake

IPA: /ˈtʃiːpfeɪk/
Pronunciation: CHEEP-fayk
French: faux contenu manipulé simplement

Definition: Low-tech manipulated media, such as misleadingly edited video.

Example: Cheapfakes can be persuasive.

doctored image

IPA: /ˌdɒktəd ˈɪmɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: DOK-tuhd IM-ij
French: image truquée

Definition: An image altered to deceive.

Example: A doctored image went viral.

manipulated video

IPA: /məˌnɪpjʊleɪtɪd ˈvɪdiəʊ/
Pronunciation: muh-NIP-yuh-lay-tid VID-ee-oh
French: vidéo manipulée

Definition: A video edited or altered to mislead viewers.

Example: Manipulated videos can distort what someone said.

out-of-context clip

IPA: /ˌaʊt əv ˈkɒntekst klɪp/
Pronunciation: out-ov-KON-tekst klip
French: extrait sorti de son contexte

Definition: A short extract presented without necessary context.

Example: Out-of-context clips can damage reputations.

cherry-picking

IPA: /ˈtʃeri ˌpɪkɪŋ/
Pronunciation: CHERR-ee PIK-ing
French: sélection biaisée des données

Definition: Selecting only evidence that supports one’s argument.

Example: Cherry-picking statistics misleads readers.

misleading statistics

IPA: /ˌmɪsˈliːdɪŋ stəˈtɪstɪks/
Pronunciation: mis-LEE-ding stuh-TIS-tiks
French: statistiques trompeuses

Definition: Statistics presented in a way that creates a false impression.

Example: Misleading statistics can distort a debate.

false equivalence

IPA: /ˌfɔːls ɪˈkwɪvələns/
Pronunciation: false i-KWIV-uh-luhns
French: fausse équivalence

Definition: Treating two things as equal when they are not comparable.

Example: False equivalence can distort political debate.

false balance

IPA: /ˌfɔːls ˈbæləns/
Pronunciation: false BAL-uhns
French: faux équilibre

Definition: Giving equal weight to unequal evidence or positions.

Example: False balance can make fringe claims appear credible.

confirmation bias

IPA: /ˌkɒnfəˈmeɪʃən ˈbaɪəs/
Pronunciation: kon-fuh-MAY-shuhn BY-uhs
French: biais de confirmation

Definition: The tendency to accept information that confirms existing beliefs.

Example: Confirmation bias helps fake news spread.

cognitive bias

IPA: /ˌkɒɡnɪtɪv ˈbaɪəs/
Pronunciation: KOG-ni-tiv BY-uhs
French: biais cognitif

Definition: A systematic pattern of thinking that can distort judgement.

Example: Cognitive bias makes people vulnerable to manipulation.

motivated reasoning

IPA: /ˌməʊtɪveɪtɪd ˈriːzənɪŋ/
Pronunciation: MOH-ti-vay-tid REE-zuh-ning
French: raisonnement motivé

Definition: Reasoning shaped by what someone wants to believe.

Example: Motivated reasoning makes corrections less effective.

illusory truth effect

IPA: /ɪˌluːsəri ˈtruːθ ɪˌfekt/
Pronunciation: i-LOO-suh-ree trooth i-FEKT
French: effet de vérité illusoire

Definition: The tendency to believe repeated claims are true.

Example: Repeated lies can start to feel true.

emotional manipulation

IPA: /ɪˌməʊʃənəl məˌnɪpjʊˈleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: i-MOH-shuhn-uhl muh-nip-yuh-LAY-shuhn
French: manipulation émotionnelle

Definition: Using emotions to influence belief or behaviour deceptively.

Example: Fake news often relies on emotional manipulation.

fearmongering

IPA: /ˈfɪəmʌŋɡərɪŋ/
Pronunciation: FEER-mung-guh-ring
French: exploitation de la peur

Definition: Spreading frightening claims to influence people.

Example: Fearmongering can drive clicks and votes.

outrage bait

IPA: /ˈaʊtreɪdʒ beɪt/
Pronunciation: OWT-rayj bayt
French: contenu conçu pour provoquer l’indignation

Definition: Content designed to provoke anger and engagement.

Example: Outrage bait performs well online.

clickbait

IPA: /ˈklɪkbeɪt/
Pronunciation: KLIK-bayt
French: piège à clics

Definition: Misleading or exaggerated content designed to attract clicks.

Example: Clickbait rewards attention rather than accuracy.

viral content

IPA: /ˈvaɪrəl ˈkɒntent/
Pronunciation: VY-ruhl KON-tent
French: contenu viral

Definition: Content that spreads rapidly online.

Example: Viral content is not necessarily reliable.

virality

IPA: /vaɪˈræləti/
Pronunciation: vy-RAL-uh-tee
French: viralisation

Definition: The quality of spreading rapidly online.

Example: Virality can reward shock over truth.

algorithm

IPA: /ˈælɡərɪðəm/
Pronunciation: AL-guh-rith-uhm
French: algorithme

Definition: A set of rules used by platforms to rank or recommend content.

Example: Algorithms influence what people see online.

algorithmic amplification

IPA: /ˌælɡəˈrɪðmɪk ˌæmplɪfɪˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: al-guh-RITH-mik am-pli-fi-KAY-shuhn
French: amplification algorithmique

Definition: The process by which algorithms make certain content more visible.

Example: Algorithmic amplification can reward outrage.

recommendation system

IPA: /ˌrekəmenˈdeɪʃən ˈsɪstəm/
Pronunciation: rek-uh-men-DAY-shuhn SIS-tuhm
French: système de recommandation

Definition: An algorithm suggesting content to users.

Example: Recommendation systems can push extreme content.

engagement

IPA: /ɪnˈɡeɪdʒmənt/
Pronunciation: in-GAYJ-muhnt
French: engagement

Definition: User interaction such as clicks, likes, comments and shares.

Example: Platforms often optimise for engagement.

attention economy

IPA: /əˈtenʃən iˌkɒnəmi/
Pronunciation: uh-TEN-shuhn i-KON-uh-mee
French: économie de l’attention

Definition: An economy where human attention is captured and monetised.

Example: Fake news thrives in the attention economy.

echo chamber

IPA: /ˈekəʊ ˌtʃeɪmbə/
Pronunciation: EK-oh CHAYM-buh
French: chambre d’écho

Definition: A space where people mostly hear views they already agree with.

Example: Echo chambers can radicalise opinions.

filter bubble

IPA: /ˈfɪltə ˌbʌbəl/
Pronunciation: FIL-tuh BUH-buhl
French: bulle de filtres

Definition: A personalised online environment limiting exposure to diverse views.

Example: Filter bubbles can narrow understanding.

polarisation

IPA: /ˌpəʊləraɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: poh-luh-ry-ZAY-shuhn
French: polarisation

Definition: Growing division between opposing groups or views.

Example: Misinformation can deepen polarisation.

trust deficit

IPA: /ˈtrʌst ˌdefɪsɪt/
Pronunciation: trust DEF-i-sit
French: déficit de confiance

Definition: A lack of trust in institutions, media or experts.

Example: Fake news exploits a trust deficit.

media literacy

IPA: /ˈmiːdiə ˌlɪtərəsi/
Pronunciation: MEE-dee-uh LIT-uh-ruh-see
French: éducation aux médias

Definition: The ability to understand and evaluate media critically.

Example: Media literacy helps citizens resist manipulation.

digital literacy

IPA: /ˈdɪdʒɪtəl ˌlɪtərəsi/
Pronunciation: DIJ-i-tuhl LIT-uh-ruh-see
French: littératie numérique

Definition: The ability to use and evaluate digital information effectively.

Example: Digital literacy is crucial online.

C. Media literacy, verification and fact-checking

critical thinking

IPA: /ˌkrɪtɪkəl ˈθɪŋkɪŋ/
Pronunciation: KRIT-i-kuhl THINK-ing
French: esprit critique

Definition: The ability to analyse information carefully and logically.

Example: Critical thinking is a defence against fake news.

source evaluation

IPA: /ˈsɔːs ɪˌvæljʊˈeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: sorss i-val-yoo-AY-shuhn
French: évaluation des sources

Definition: Assessing whether a source is reliable and relevant.

Example: Source evaluation should be taught in schools.

fact-checking

IPA: /ˈfækt ˌtʃekɪŋ/
Pronunciation: FAKT-chek-ing
French: vérification des faits

Definition: The process of checking whether claims are true.

Example: Fact-checking can slow misinformation.

fact-checker

IPA: /ˈfækt ˌtʃekə/
Pronunciation: FAKT-chek-uh
French: vérificateur des faits

Definition: A person or organisation that verifies claims.

Example: Fact-checkers rated the claim as false.

debunking

IPA: /ˌdiːˈbʌŋkɪŋ/
Pronunciation: dee-BUNK-ing
French: réfutation

Definition: Showing that a claim is false or misleading.

Example: Debunking works best when it is clear and timely.

prebunking

IPA: /ˌpriːˈbʌŋkɪŋ/
Pronunciation: pree-BUNK-ing
French: pré-réfutation

Definition: Warning people about manipulation techniques before they encounter them.

Example: Prebunking can build resistance to disinformation.

verification

IPA: /ˌverɪfɪˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: ver-i-fi-KAY-shuhn
French: vérification

Definition: Confirming whether information is accurate or authentic.

Example: Verification is essential before sharing news.

reverse image search

IPA: /rɪˌvɜːs ˈɪmɪdʒ sɜːtʃ/
Pronunciation: ri-VURS IM-ij surch
French: recherche d’image inversée

Definition: Searching an image online to find its origin or earlier uses.

Example: Reverse image search can expose old images reused as new.

metadata

IPA: /ˈmetəˌdeɪtə/
Pronunciation: MET-uh-DAY-tuh
French: métadonnées

Definition: Data giving information about a file, image or document.

Example: Metadata can help verify a photo.

geolocation

IPA: /ˌdʒiːəʊləʊˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: jee-oh-loh-KAY-shuhn
French: géolocalisation

Definition: Identifying the real-world location of an image, video or device.

Example: Geolocation helps verify war footage.

OSINT

IPA: /ˌəʊ es aɪ en ˈtiː/
Pronunciation: oh-ess-eye-en-tee
French: OSINT ; renseignement de sources ouvertes

Definition: Open-source intelligence gathered from publicly available sources.

Example: OSINT investigators analyse videos and satellite images.

content moderation

IPA: /ˈkɒntent ˌmɒdəˈreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: KON-tent mod-uh-RAY-shuhn
French: modération des contenus

Definition: Rules and actions used by platforms to manage user content.

Example: Content moderation is politically controversial.

platform accountability

IPA: /ˈplætfɔːm əˌkaʊntəˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: PLAT-form uh-kown-tuh-BIL-uh-tee
French: responsabilité des plateformes

Definition: Responsibility of digital platforms for online harms.

Example: Platform accountability is central to disinformation debates.

content removal

IPA: /ˈkɒntent rɪˌmuːvəl/
Pronunciation: KON-tent ri-MOO-vuhl
French: suppression de contenu

Definition: Deleting or taking down online content.

Example: Content removal can reduce harm but raises concerns.

deplatforming

IPA: /ˌdiːˈplætfɔːmɪŋ/
Pronunciation: dee-PLAT-form-ing
French: déplateformisation

Definition: Removing someone from a platform or denying them an audience.

Example: Deplatforming can limit extremist reach.

labelling

IPA: /ˈleɪbəlɪŋ/
Pronunciation: LAY-buhl-ing
French: étiquetage

Definition: Adding a warning or context label to online content.

Example: Labelling can warn users about disputed claims.

warning label

IPA: /ˈwɔːnɪŋ ˌleɪbəl/
Pronunciation: WOR-ning LAY-buhl
French: avertissement

Definition: A label warning users about misleading or harmful content.

Example: Warning labels can reduce sharing.

transparency report

IPA: /trænˈspærənsi rɪˌpɔːt/
Pronunciation: tran-SPARR-uhn-see ri-PORT
French: rapport de transparence

Definition: A report explaining platform moderation and enforcement.

Example: Transparency reports help researchers.

regulation

IPA: /ˌreɡjʊˈleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: reg-yuh-LAY-shuhn
French: réglementation

Definition: Rules made by authorities to control platforms or behaviour.

Example: Regulation must balance safety and free speech.

censorship

IPA: /ˈsensəʃɪp/
Pronunciation: SEN-suh-ship
French: censure

Definition: Suppression or control of information.

free speech

IPA: /ˌfriː ˈspiːtʃ/
Pronunciation: free speech
French: liberté d’expression

freedom of expression

public sphere

democratic debate

truth decay

to go viral

IPA: /ɡəʊ ˈvaɪrəl/
Pronunciation: goh VY-ruhl
French: devenir viral

Definition: To spread rapidly online.

Example: The misleading video went viral.

to sow distrust

IPA: /səʊ dɪsˈtrʌst/
Pronunciation: soh dis-TRUST
French: semer la méfiance

Definition: To create suspicion and reduce trust.

Example: Propaganda aims to sow distrust.
