Essential vocabulary for khôlles, essays, press reviews and competitive exams.

Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse gun control in the United States: the Second Amendment, firearm regulation, background checks, assault weapons, concealed carry, mass shootings, school shootings, gun violence, the gun lobby, Supreme Court rulings and the tension between rights and public safety. Each entry includes the English term, IPA phonetics, a practical pronunciation guide, an English definition, a French translation, and an exam-ready example sentence.

How to use this chapter

For press articles Focus on terms such as Second Amendment, background check, assault weapons ban, red flag law, mass shooting, NRA and Supreme Court ruling.
For oral exams Use this vocabulary to explain the tension between constitutional rights, self-defence, public safety, political lobbying and the trauma caused by mass shootings.
For essays Memorise the collocations: to regulate firearms, to restrict gun access, to close loopholes, to prevent mass shootings, to balance rights and safety.

A. Constitutional vocabulary and core debate

gun control

IPA: /ˈɡʌn kənˌtrəʊl/
Pronunciation: gun kuhn-TROHL
French: contrôle des armes à feu

Definition: Laws and policies regulating the sale, ownership and use of firearms.

Example: Gun control remains one of the most divisive issues in American politics.

firearm

IPA: /ˈfaɪərɑːm/
Pronunciation: FY-uhr-arm
French: arme à feu

Definition: A gun or weapon from which bullets are fired.

Example: Federal law regulates who can buy a firearm.

gun ownership

IPA: /ˈɡʌn ˌəʊnəʃɪp/
Pronunciation: gun OH-nuh-ship
French: possession d’armes à feu

Definition: The possession of firearms by private individuals.

Example: Gun ownership is often defended as a constitutional right.

gun rights

IPA: /ˈɡʌn raɪts/
Pronunciation: gun rites
French: droit de posséder ou porter des armes

Definition: The claimed legal or constitutional rights to own, carry or use firearms.

Example: Gun rights activists oppose many restrictions on firearms.

right to bear arms

IPA: /ˌraɪt tə beər ˈɑːmz/
Pronunciation: rite tuh bair armz
French: droit de porter des armes

Definition: The constitutional right, linked to the Second Amendment, to keep and carry weapons.

Example: The right to bear arms is central to Second Amendment debates.

Second Amendment

IPA: /ˌsekənd əˈmendmənt/
Pronunciation: SEK-uhnd uh-MEND-muhnt
French: Deuxième amendement

Definition: The amendment to the US Constitution often interpreted as protecting the right to keep and bear arms.

Example: The Second Amendment is frequently invoked by gun rights supporters.

well-regulated militia

IPA: /ˌwel ˌreɡjʊleɪtɪd məˈlɪʃə/
Pronunciation: wel REG-yuh-lay-tid muh-LISH-uh
French: milice bien organisée

Definition: A phrase from the Second Amendment referring to an organised citizen militia.

Example: The phrase well-regulated militia is central to debates over constitutional interpretation.

individual right

IPA: /ˌɪndɪˈvɪdʒuəl raɪt/
Pronunciation: in-di-VIJ-oo-uhl rite
French: droit individuel

Definition: A right held by a person as an individual rather than by a collective group.

Example: The Supreme Court has interpreted gun ownership as an individual right.

collective right

IPA: /kəˌlektɪv ˈraɪt/
Pronunciation: kuh-LEK-tiv rite
French: droit collectif

Definition: A right held by a group or community rather than by individuals alone.

Example: Some scholars once argued that the Second Amendment protected a collective right linked to militias.

constitutional right

IPA: /ˌkɒnstɪˈtjuːʃənəl raɪt/
Pronunciation: kon-sti-TOO-shuh-nuhl rite
French: droit constitutionnel

Definition: A right protected by a constitution.

Example: Gun rights advocates describe firearm ownership as a constitutional right.

firearms regulation

IPA: /ˈfaɪərɑːmz ˌreɡjʊˈleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: FY-uhr-armz reg-yuh-LAY-shuhn
French: réglementation des armes à feu

Definition: Rules controlling the manufacture, sale, possession or use of firearms.

Example: Firearms regulation varies widely from state to state.

gun legislation

IPA: /ˈɡʌn ˌledʒɪsˈleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: gun lej-is-LAY-shuhn
French: législation sur les armes à feu

Definition: Laws dealing with firearms.

Example: Gun legislation often faces strong opposition in Congress.

gun law

IPA: /ˈɡʌn lɔː/
Pronunciation: gun law
French: loi sur les armes à feu

Definition: A law regulating firearms.

Example: Gun laws are much stricter in some states than in others.

state gun law

IPA: /ˌsteɪt ˈɡʌn lɔː/
Pronunciation: stayt gun law
French: loi d’un État sur les armes à feu

Definition: A firearm law passed by a US state.

Example: State gun laws differ dramatically across the country.

federal gun law

IPA: /ˌfedərəl ˈɡʌn lɔː/
Pronunciation: FED-uh-ruhl gun law
French: loi fédérale sur les armes à feu

Definition: A firearm law passed by the national government.

Example: Federal gun law sets minimum rules for firearm purchases.

background check

IPA: /ˈbækɡraʊnd tʃek/
Pronunciation: BAK-ground chek
French: vérification des antécédents

Definition: A check of a person’s criminal or mental health record before allowing a gun purchase.

Example: Many voters support universal background checks.

universal background checks

IPA: /ˌjuːnɪˈvɜːsəl ˈbækɡraʊnd tʃeks/
Pronunciation: yoo-ni-VUR-suhl BAK-ground cheks
French: vérifications universelles des antécédents

Definition: Background checks required for nearly all gun sales, including private sales.

Example: Universal background checks are a common gun control proposal.

waiting period

IPA: /ˈweɪtɪŋ ˌpɪəriəd/
Pronunciation: WAY-ting PEER-ee-uhd
French: délai d’attente

Definition: A required delay between buying a firearm and receiving it.

Example: A waiting period may reduce impulsive gun violence.

licensing

IPA: /ˈlaɪsənsɪŋ/
Pronunciation: LY-suhn-sing
French: système de licence ; permis

Definition: A system requiring official permission to own, buy or carry a firearm.

Example: Licensing rules can make gun ownership more regulated.

permit

IPA: /ˈpɜːmɪt/
Pronunciation: PUR-mit
French: permis ; autorisation

Definition: An official document allowing someone to own, buy or carry a gun.

Example: Some states require a permit to carry a handgun.

registration

IPA: /ˌredʒɪˈstreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: rej-i-STRAY-shuhn
French: enregistrement

Definition: The official recording of firearm ownership.

Example: Gun registration is controversial among gun rights supporters.

gun registry

IPA: /ˈɡʌn ˌredʒɪstri/
Pronunciation: gun REJ-i-stree
French: registre des armes

Definition: An official list of firearms or firearm owners.

Example: Opponents fear that a gun registry could lead to confiscation.

B. Firearms, carrying rules and safety measures

assault weapon

IPA: /əˈsɔːlt ˌwepən/
Pronunciation: uh-SAWLT WEP-uhn
French: arme d’assaut

Definition: A politically contested term for certain semi-automatic firearms with military-style features.

Example: An assault weapon ban is often proposed after mass shootings.

assault weapons ban

IPA: /əˈsɔːlt ˌwepənz bæn/
Pronunciation: uh-SAWLT WEP-uhnz ban
French: interdiction des armes d’assaut

Definition: A law banning certain semi-automatic firearms defined as assault weapons.

Example: Democrats have repeatedly called for an assault weapons ban.

semi-automatic weapon

IPA: /ˌsemi ɔːtəˈmætɪk ˈwepən/
Pronunciation: SEM-ee aw-tuh-MAT-ik WEP-uhn
French: arme semi-automatique

Definition: A firearm that fires one round per trigger pull and reloads automatically.

Example: Semi-automatic weapons are often used in mass shootings.

automatic weapon

IPA: /ˌɔːtəˈmætɪk ˈwepən/
Pronunciation: aw-tuh-MAT-ik WEP-uhn
French: arme automatique

Definition: A weapon that continues firing while the trigger is held down.

Example: Automatic weapons are heavily restricted under federal law.

handgun

IPA: /ˈhændɡʌn/
Pronunciation: HAND-gun
French: arme de poing

Definition: A small firearm designed to be held in one hand.

Example: Handguns are involved in many firearm deaths.

rifle

IPA: /ˈraɪfəl/
Pronunciation: RY-fuhl
French: fusil

Definition: A long firearm designed to be fired from the shoulder.

Example: Some rifles are used for hunting or sport shooting.

shotgun

IPA: /ˈʃɒtɡʌn/
Pronunciation: SHOT-gun
French: fusil de chasse

Definition: A long firearm that fires shells, often used for hunting or home defence.

Example: Shotguns are commonly owned in rural areas.

AR-15-style rifle

IPA: /ˌeɪ ɑː ˌfɪfˈtiːn staɪl ˈraɪfəl/
Pronunciation: ay ar fif-TEEN style RY-fuhl
French: fusil de type AR-15

Definition: A civilian semi-automatic rifle model often discussed in mass shooting debates.

Example: AR-15-style rifles have become symbols in the gun control debate.

high-capacity magazine

IPA: /ˌhaɪ kəˈpæsəti ˌmæɡəˈziːn/
Pronunciation: hy kuh-PAS-uh-tee mag-uh-ZEEN
French: chargeur grande capacité

Definition: A device holding many rounds of ammunition for a firearm.

Example: High-capacity magazines can increase the number of shots fired without reloading.

ammunition

IPA: /ˌæmjəˈnɪʃən/
Pronunciation: am-yuh-NISH-uhn
French: munitions

Definition: Bullets or other projectiles used in firearms.

Example: Some proposals seek to regulate ammunition sales.

bullet

IPA: /ˈbʊlɪt/
Pronunciation: BUL-it
French: balle

Definition: A projectile fired from a gun.

Example: The bullet was recovered as evidence.

bump stock

IPA: /ˈbʌmp stɒk/
Pronunciation: bump stok
French: crosse bump stock

Definition: A device that allows a semi-automatic weapon to fire more rapidly.

Example: Bump stocks became controversial after the Las Vegas shooting.

silencer

IPA: /ˈsaɪlənsə/
Pronunciation: SY-luhn-suh
French: silencieux

Definition: A device attached to a firearm to reduce noise, also called a suppressor.

Example: Silencers are regulated under federal law.

concealed carry

IPA: /kənˌsiːld ˈkæri/
Pronunciation: kuhn-SEELD KARR-ee
French: port d’arme dissimulé

Definition: The practice of carrying a hidden firearm in public.

Example: Concealed carry laws vary by state.

open carry

IPA: /ˌəʊpən ˈkæri/
Pronunciation: OH-puhn KARR-ee
French: port d’arme visible

Definition: The practice of visibly carrying a firearm in public.

Example: Open carry is legal in some states.

permitless carry

IPA: /ˌpɜːmɪtləs ˈkæri/
Pronunciation: PUR-mit-less KARR-ee
French: port d’arme sans permis

Definition: The legal ability to carry a firearm without a permit.

Example: Several states have adopted permitless carry.

campus carry

IPA: /ˈkæmpəs ˈkæri/
Pronunciation: KAM-puhs KARR-ee
French: port d’arme sur les campus

Definition: Laws allowing firearms on college or university campuses.

Example: Campus carry has sparked debate over safety and academic freedom.

stand-your-ground law

IPA: /ˌstænd jə ˈɡraʊnd lɔː/
Pronunciation: stand yuh ground law
French: loi Stand Your Ground ; loi de légitime défense élargie

Definition: A law allowing people to use force without retreating if they believe they face a threat.

Example: Stand-your-ground laws are criticised for encouraging violent confrontations.

castle doctrine

IPA: /ˈkɑːsəl ˌdɒktrɪn/
Pronunciation: KAR-suhl DOK-trin
French: doctrine du château ; défense du domicile

Definition: A legal principle allowing people to use force to defend their home.

Example: The castle doctrine is often cited in self-defence cases.

self-defence

IPA: /ˌself dɪˈfens/
Pronunciation: self di-FENSS
French: légitime défense

Definition: The use of force to protect oneself from harm.

Example: Many gun owners cite self-defence as their main reason for owning a gun.

home defence

IPA: /ˌhəʊm dɪˈfens/
Pronunciation: hohm di-FENSS
French: défense du domicile

Definition: Protection of one’s home against intruders.

Example: Home defence is a common argument for gun ownership.

responsible gun ownership

IPA: /rɪˌspɒnsəbəl ˈɡʌn ˌəʊnəʃɪp/
Pronunciation: ri-SPON-suh-buhl gun OH-nuh-ship
French: possession responsable d’armes

Definition: Safe and lawful possession and use of firearms.

Example: Supporters say responsible gun ownership should not be punished.

gun safety

IPA: /ˈɡʌn ˌseɪfti/
Pronunciation: gun SAYF-tee
French: sécurité liée aux armes à feu

Definition: Practices or policies designed to prevent firearm accidents and violence.

Example: Gun safety education can prevent accidental shootings.

safe storage

IPA: /ˌseɪf ˈstɔːrɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: sayf STOR-ij
French: stockage sécurisé

Definition: Rules or practices requiring guns to be stored securely.

Example: Safe storage laws aim to protect children from accidental shootings.

trigger lock

IPA: /ˈtrɪɡə lɒk/
Pronunciation: TRIG-uh lok
French: verrou de détente

Definition: A device that prevents a gun from being fired.

Example: A trigger lock can reduce accidental firearm use.

red flag law

IPA: /ˌred ˈflæɡ lɔː/
Pronunciation: red flag law
French: loi d’alerte ; red flag law

Definition: A law allowing authorities to temporarily remove guns from people judged dangerous to themselves or others.

Example: Red flag laws are meant to prevent suicides and mass shootings.

extreme risk protection order

IPA: /ɪkˌstriːm rɪsk prəˈtekʃən ˌɔːdə/
Pronunciation: ik-STREEM risk pruh-TEK-shuhn OR-duh
French: ordonnance de protection contre les risques extrêmes

Definition: A court order temporarily restricting firearm access for a person considered dangerous.

Example: Extreme risk protection orders are often linked to red flag laws.

domestic abuser

IPA: /dəˌmestɪk əˈbjuːzə/
Pronunciation: duh-MES-tik uh-BYOO-zuh
French: auteur de violences domestiques

Definition: A person who commits abuse against a partner or family member.

Example: Many advocates want to keep guns away from domestic abusers.

mental health

IPA: /ˌmentəl ˈhelθ/
Pronunciation: MEN-tuhl helth
French: santé mentale

Definition: A person’s psychological and emotional well-being.

Example: Mental health is often mentioned after mass shootings, though most people with mental illness are not violent.

gun violence

IPA: /ˈɡʌn ˌvaɪələns/
Pronunciation: gun VY-uh-luhns
French: violence par armes à feu

Definition: Violence involving firearms.

Example: Gun violence kills tens of thousands of Americans each year.

firearm death

IPA: /ˈfaɪərɑːm deθ/
Pronunciation: FY-uhr-arm deth
French: mort par arme à feu

Definition: A death caused by a gun.

Example: Firearm deaths include homicides, suicides and accidents.

C. Gun violence, mass shootings and public health

gun homicide

IPA: /ˈɡʌn ˈhɒmɪsaɪd/
Pronunciation: gun HOM-i-syde
French: homicide par arme à feu

Definition: A murder committed with a firearm.

Example: Gun homicides are concentrated in some urban areas.

gun suicide

IPA: /ˈɡʌn ˈsuːɪsaɪd/
Pronunciation: gun SOO-i-syde
French: suicide par arme à feu

Definition: A suicide committed with a firearm.

Example: Gun suicides make up a large share of firearm deaths.

accidental shooting

IPA: /ˌæksɪdentəl ˈʃuːtɪŋ/
Pronunciation: ak-si-DEN-tuhl SHOO-ting
French: tir accidentel

Definition: A shooting that happens unintentionally.

Example: Safe storage can reduce accidental shootings.

mass shooting

IPA: /ˌmæs ˈʃuːtɪŋ/
Pronunciation: mass SHOO-ting
French: fusillade de masse

Definition: A shooting incident involving multiple victims, though definitions vary.

Example: Mass shootings often reignite the gun control debate.

school shooting

IPA: /ˈskuːl ˌʃuːtɪŋ/
Pronunciation: school SHOO-ting
French: fusillade en milieu scolaire

Definition: A shooting that takes place in or near a school.

Example: School shootings have traumatised communities and shaped political debate.

active shooter

IPA: /ˌæktɪv ˈʃuːtə/
Pronunciation: AK-tiv SHOO-tuh
French: tireur actif

Definition: A person actively engaged in killing or attempting to kill people in a populated area.

Example: Schools now practise active shooter drills.

active shooter drill

IPA: /ˌæktɪv ˈʃuːtə drɪl/
Pronunciation: AK-tiv SHOO-tuh dril
French: exercice en cas de tireur actif

Definition: A practice exercise preparing people for an active shooter situation.

Example: Active shooter drills have become common in American schools.

survivor

IPA: /səˈvaɪvə/
Pronunciation: suh-VY-vuh
French: survivant ; survivante

Definition: A person who lives through a violent or traumatic event.

Example: Survivors of school shootings often campaign for reform.

victim

IPA: /ˈvɪktɪm/
Pronunciation: VIK-tim
French: victime

Definition: A person harmed, injured or killed by violence or crime.

Example: Victims’ families called for stronger gun laws.

public safety

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk ˈseɪfti/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik SAYF-tee
French: sécurité publique

Definition: The protection of people from crime, violence or danger.

Example: Gun control supporters frame the issue as a matter of public safety.

public health crisis

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk ˈhelθ ˈkraɪsɪs/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik helth KRY-sis
French: crise de santé publique

Definition: A widespread threat to health requiring collective action.

Example: Many doctors describe gun violence as a public health crisis.

trauma

IPA: /ˈtrɔːmə/
Pronunciation: TRAW-muh
French: traumatisme

Definition: Severe emotional or psychological harm after a disturbing event.

Example: Gun violence leaves long-lasting trauma.

community violence

IPA: /kəˌmjuːnəti ˈvaɪələns/
Pronunciation: kuh-MYOO-nuh-tee VY-uh-luhns
French: violence communautaire ; violence dans les quartiers

Definition: Violence affecting everyday life in a local community.

Example: Community violence is often overlooked compared with mass shootings.

urban violence

IPA: /ˌɜːbən ˈvaɪələns/
Pronunciation: UR-buhn VY-uh-luhns
French: violence urbaine

Definition: Violence occurring in city environments, often linked to poverty and inequality.

Example: Urban violence is frequently politicised in gun debates.

domestic violence

IPA: /dəˌmestɪk ˈvaɪələns/
Pronunciation: duh-MES-tik VY-uh-luhns
French: violence domestique ; violences conjugales

Definition: Violence within a household or intimate relationship.

Example: Guns make domestic violence more deadly.

suicide prevention

IPA: /ˈsuːɪsaɪd prɪˌvenʃən/
Pronunciation: SOO-i-syde pri-VEN-shuhn
French: prévention du suicide

Definition: Measures designed to reduce suicides.

Example: Gun safety policies can be part of suicide prevention.

NRA

IPA: /ˌen ɑːr ˈeɪ/
Pronunciation: en ar ay
French: NRA ; National Rifle Association

Definition: The National Rifle Association, a powerful US gun rights organisation.

Example: The NRA has long influenced Republican politics.

gun lobby

IPA: /ˈɡʌn ˌlɒbi/
Pronunciation: gun LOB-ee
French: lobby des armes

Definition: Groups that lobby to protect gun rights and oppose firearm restrictions.

Example: The gun lobby opposes most federal gun control measures.

gun control advocate

IPA: /ˈɡʌn kənˌtrəʊl ˈædvəkət/
Pronunciation: gun kuhn-TROHL AD-vuh-kuht
French: partisan du contrôle des armes

Definition: A person who supports stricter firearm regulation.

Example: Gun control advocates called for action after the shooting.

gun rights activist

IPA: /ˈɡʌn raɪts ˈæktɪvɪst/
Pronunciation: gun rites AK-ti-vist
French: militant pro-armes

Definition: A person who campaigns to protect or expand gun rights.

Example: Gun rights activists argued that the law violated the Second Amendment.

Second Amendment absolutist

IPA: /ˌsekənd əˈmendmənt ˈæbsəluːtɪst/
Pronunciation: SEK-uhnd uh-MEND-muhnt AB-suh-loot-ist
French: absolutiste du Deuxième amendement

Definition: A person who believes firearm rights should face almost no restrictions.

Example: Second Amendment absolutists reject most gun control proposals.

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk əˈpɪnjən/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik uh-PIN-yuhn
French: opinion publique

Definition: The views of the public on an issue.

Example: Public opinion often supports background checks.

political gridlock

IPA: /pəˌlɪtɪkəl ˈɡrɪdlɒk/
Pronunciation: puh-LIT-i-kuhl GRID-lok
French: blocage politique

Example: Political gridlock has blocked major gun reform.

partisan divide

IPA: /ˌpɑːtɪzən dɪˈvaɪd/
Pronunciation: PAR-ti-zuhn di-VYDE
French: clivage partisan

Example: The partisan divide on gun control has widened.

culture war

IPA: /ˈkʌltʃə wɔː/

French: guerre culturelle

rural voters

IPA: /ˌrʊərəl ˈvəʊtəz/
Pronunciation: ROOR-uhl VOH-tuhz
French: électeurs ruraux

Definition: Voters living outside cities, often in countryside areas.

Example: Rural voters are often more supportive of gun rights.

urban voters

IPA: /ˌɜːbən ˈvəʊtəz/
Pronunciation: UR-buhn VOH-tuhz
French: électeurs urbains

Definition: Voters living in cities.

Example: Urban voters are often more supportive of gun control.

constitutional challenge

IPA: /ˌkɒnstɪˈtjuːʃənəl ˈtʃælɪndʒ/
Pronunciation: kon-sti-TOO-shuh-nuhl CHAL-inj
French: recours constitutionnel

Supreme Court ruling

IPA: /suːˌpriːm ˈkɔːt ˈruːlɪŋ/
Pronunciation: soo-PREEM court ROO-ling
French: décision de la Cour suprême

Definition: A decision made by the US Supreme Court.

Example: A Supreme Court ruling expanded gun rights.

District of Columbia v. Heller

IPA: /ˈdɪstrɪkt əv kəˈlʌmbiə vɜːsəs ˈhelə/
Pronunciation: DIS-trikt ov kuh-LUM-bee-uh VUR-suhs HEL-uh
French: District of Columbia v. Heller

Definition: The 2008 Supreme Court case recognising an individual right to possess firearms for lawful purposes such as self-defence.

Example: District of Columbia v. Heller reshaped Second Amendment law.

New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen

IPA: /njuː ˈjɔːk steɪt ˈraɪfəl ənd ˈpɪstəl əˌsəʊsiˈeɪʃən vɜːsəs ˈbruːən/
Pronunciation: nyoo york state RY-fuhl and PIS-tuhl uh-soh-see-AY-shuhn VUR-suhs BROO-uhn
French: New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen

Definition: The 2022 Supreme Court case limiting states’ ability to restrict public carry of firearms.

Example: Bruen made it harder for states to justify some gun restrictions.

constitutional interpretation

IPA: /ˌkɒnstɪˈtjuːʃənəl ɪnˌtɜːprɪˈteɪʃən/
Pronunciation: kon-sti-TOO-shuh-nuhl in-tur-pri-TAY-shuhn
French: interprétation constitutionnelle

Definition: The process of deciding what constitutional text means.

Example: Gun cases often depend on constitutional interpretation.

originalism

IPA: /əˈrɪdʒənəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: uh-RIJ-uh-nuhl-iz-uhm
French: originalisme

Definition: A method of constitutional interpretation based on original meaning.

Example: Originalism plays a major role in Second Amendment cases.

history-and-tradition test

IPA: /ˌhɪstəri ənd trəˈdɪʃən test/
Pronunciation: HIS-tuh-ree and truh-DISH-uhn test
French: test de l’histoire et de la tradition

Definition: A legal test asking whether gun restrictions are consistent with historical firearm regulation.

Example: The history-and-tradition test changed how courts assess gun laws.

to regulate firearms

IPA: /ˈreɡjʊleɪt ˈfaɪərɑːmz/
Pronunciation: REG-yuh-layt FY-uhr-armz
French: réglementer les armes à feu

Definition: To make rules controlling firearms.

Example: States have different powers to regulate firearms.

to restrict gun access

IPA: /rɪˈstrɪkt ˈɡʌn ˈækses/
Pronunciation: ri-STRIKT gun AK-sess
French: restreindre l’accès aux armes

Definition: To limit who can obtain firearms.

Example: Gun control advocates want to restrict gun access for dangerous individuals.

to expand gun rights

IPA: /ɪkˈspænd ˈɡʌn raɪts/
Pronunciation: ik-SPAND gun rites
French: élargir le droit aux armes

Definition: To broaden legal protections for firearm possession or carry.

Example: Some Supreme Court rulings have expanded gun rights.

to close loopholes

IPA: /kləʊz ˈluːphəʊlz/
Pronunciation: klohz LOOP-hohlz
French: combler les failles juridiques

Definition: To remove gaps or weaknesses in the law.

Example: Reformers want to close loopholes in background check laws.

gun show loophole

IPA: /ˈɡʌn ʃəʊ ˈluːphəʊl/
Pronunciation: gun show LOOP-hohl
French: faille des foires aux armes

Definition: A term for gaps allowing some firearm sales at gun shows without background checks.

Example: The gun show loophole is frequently mentioned in reform debates.

E. High-value verbs and exam-ready expressions

private sale loophole

IPA: /ˌpraɪvət ˈseɪl ˈluːphəʊl/
Pronunciation: PRY-vuht sayl LOOP-hohl
French: faille des ventes privées

Definition: A gap allowing some private firearm sales without standard background checks.

Example: Universal background checks would close the private sale loophole.

to ban assault weapons

IPA: /bæn əˈsɔːlt ˌwepənz/
Pronunciation: ban uh-SAWLT WEP-uhnz
French: interdire les armes d’assaut

Definition: To prohibit the sale or possession of certain semi-automatic firearms.

Example: Many Democrats want to ban assault weapons.

to arm teachers

IPA: /ɑːm ˈtiːtʃəz/
Pronunciation: arm TEE-chuhz
French: armer les enseignants

Definition: To give teachers firearms or allow them to carry guns in schools.

Example: Some conservatives have suggested arming teachers after school shootings.

to harden schools

IPA: /ˈhɑːdən ˈskuːlz/
Pronunciation: HAR-duhn schoolz
French: renforcer la sécurité des écoles

Definition: To increase school security through guards, doors, cameras or armed staff.

Example: Critics say hardening schools does not address easy gun access.

to prevent mass shootings

IPA: /prɪˈvent mæs ˈʃuːtɪŋz/
Pronunciation: pri-VENT mass SHOO-tingz
French: prévenir les fusillades de masse

Definition: To take measures to stop mass shooting events.

Example: Red flag laws are designed to prevent mass shootings.

to reduce gun deaths

IPA: /rɪˈdjuːs ˈɡʌn deθs/
Pronunciation: ri-DYOOSS gun deths
French: réduire les morts par armes à feu

Definition: To lower the number of deaths caused by firearms.

Example: Public health experts focus on reducing gun deaths.

to protect public safety

IPA: /prəˈtekt ˌpʌblɪk ˈseɪfti/
Pronunciation: pruh-TEKT PUB-lik SAYF-tee
French: protéger la sécurité publique

Definition: To keep people safe from harm or violence.

Example: Supporters argue that gun restrictions protect public safety.

to infringe on rights

IPA: /ɪnˈfrɪndʒ ɒn raɪts/
Pronunciation: in-FRINJ on rites
French: porter atteinte aux droits

Definition: To violate or limit rights unfairly.

Example: Opponents say gun control infringes on constitutional rights.

to balance rights and safety

IPA: /ˈbæləns raɪts ənd ˈseɪfti/
Pronunciation: BAL-uhns rites and SAYF-tee
French: concilier droits et sécurité

Definition: To reconcile individual freedoms with collective security.

Example: The challenge is to balance rights and safety.
