Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse crime and justice: policing, investigations, types of crime, courts, trials, sentencing, prisons, rehabilitation, miscarriages of justice, civil liberties, police reform, mass incarceration and the tension between public safety and individual rights.

How to use this chapter

For press articlesFocus on terms such as law enforcement, violent crime, knife crime, cybercrime, trial, sentencing, mass incarceration, racial profiling and criminal justice reform.
For oral examsUse this vocabulary to explain how justice systems must protect citizens, respect rights, avoid discrimination and decide whether punishment or rehabilitation is more effective.
For essaysMemorise the collocations: to commit a crime, to stand trial, to serve a sentence, to reduce reoffending, to uphold the rule of law.

A. Core crime, policing and investigation vocabulary

crime

IPA: /kraɪm/
Pronunciation: kryme
French: crime ; criminalité

Definition: Illegal behaviour punishable by law.

Example: Crime is often used by politicians to discuss security and social order.

criminal justice

IPA: /ˌkrɪmɪnəl ˈdʒʌstɪs/
Pronunciation: KRIM-i-nuhl JUS-tis
French: justice pénale

Definition: The system of police, courts and prisons dealing with crime.

Example: Criminal justice systems must balance punishment, prevention and rights.

justice system

IPA: /ˈdʒʌstɪs ˌsɪstəm/
Pronunciation: JUS-tis SIS-tuhm
French: système judiciaire

Definition: The institutions that apply the law and resolve legal disputes.

Example: Trust in the justice system depends on fairness and transparency.

law enforcement

IPA: /ˈlɔː ɪnˌfɔːsmənt/
Pronunciation: law in-FORSS-muhnt
French: application de la loi ; maintien de l’ordre

Definition: The work of making sure laws are obeyed, especially by police.

Example: Law enforcement is controversial when it is seen as discriminatory.

policing

IPA: /pəˈliːsɪŋ/
Pronunciation: puh-LEE-sing
French: maintien de l’ordre ; action policière

Definition: The work, methods and organisation of the police.

Example: Policing methods can affect trust between citizens and the state.

police force

IPA: /pəˈliːs fɔːs/
Pronunciation: puh-LEESS force
French: force de police

Definition: An organised body of police officers.

Example: The police force has faced criticism over institutional bias.

police officer

IPA: /pəˈliːs ˌɒfɪsə/
Pronunciation: puh-LEESS OFF-i-suh
French: policier ; policière

Definition: A person whose job is to enforce the law.

Example: Police officers are expected to protect the public.

constable

IPA: /ˈkʌnstəbəl/
Pronunciation: KUN-stuh-buhl
French: agent de police britannique

Definition: A police officer, especially in Britain.

Example: A constable has powers of arrest.

detective

IPA: /dɪˈtektɪv/
Pronunciation: di-TEK-tiv
French: inspecteur ; enquêteur

Definition: A police officer who investigates crimes.

Example: Detectives investigate serious offences.

investigation

IPA: /ɪnˌvestɪˈɡeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: in-ves-ti-GAY-shuhn
French: enquête

Definition: A formal inquiry into a crime or event.

Example: The investigation revealed failures in police procedure.

criminal investigation

IPA: /ˌkrɪmɪnəl ɪnˌvestɪˈɡeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: KRIM-i-nuhl in-ves-ti-GAY-shuhn
French: enquête pénale

Definition: An inquiry into suspected criminal behaviour.

Example: A criminal investigation must gather reliable evidence.

suspect

IPA: /ˈsʌspekt/
Pronunciation: SUS-pekt
French: suspect

Definition: A person believed to have committed a crime.

Example: A suspect is innocent until proven guilty.

victim

IPA: /ˈvɪktɪm/
Pronunciation: VIK-tim
French: victime

Definition: A person harmed by a crime or wrongdoing.

Example: Victims often need protection and support during trials.

witness

IPA: /ˈwɪtnəs/
Pronunciation: WIT-nuhs
French: témoin

Definition: A person who saw an event or can give evidence.

Example: A witness may be called to testify in court.

evidence

IPA: /ˈevɪdəns/
Pronunciation: EV-i-duhns
French: preuves

Definition: Information, objects or testimony used to prove facts in a case.

Example: Evidence must be collected legally to be admissible.

forensic evidence

IPA: /fəˈrenzɪk ˈevɪdəns/
Pronunciation: fuh-REN-zik EV-i-duhns
French: preuves médico-légales ; preuves scientifiques

Definition: Scientific evidence used in a criminal investigation.

Example: Forensic evidence can strengthen a prosecution case.

DNA evidence

IPA: /ˌdiː en ˈeɪ ˈevɪdəns/
Pronunciation: dee-en-ay EV-i-duhns
French: preuve ADN

Definition: Genetic evidence used to identify people in investigations.

Example: DNA evidence has overturned wrongful convictions.

fingerprint

IPA: /ˈfɪŋɡəprɪnt/
Pronunciation: FIN-guh-print
French: empreinte digitale

Definition: A mark left by the pattern of a person’s finger.

Example: Fingerprints can link a suspect to a crime scene.

crime scene

IPA: /ˈkraɪm siːn/
Pronunciation: kryme seen
French: scène de crime

Definition: The place where a crime happened.

Example: The crime scene must be protected from contamination.

alibi

IPA: /ˈælɪbaɪ/
Pronunciation: AL-i-by
French: alibi

Definition: Proof or claim that someone was elsewhere when a crime occurred.

Example: The defendant had a strong alibi.

motive

IPA: /ˈməʊtɪv/
Pronunciation: MOH-tiv
French: mobile ; motif

Definition: The reason why someone commits a crime.

Example: Investigators looked for a possible motive.

offence

IPA: /əˈfens/
Pronunciation: uh-FENSS
French: infraction

Definition: A crime or illegal act, especially in British English.

Example: The offence carries a maximum prison sentence.

felony

IPA: /ˈfeləni/
Pronunciation: FEL-uh-nee
French: crime grave américain

Definition: A serious crime in the US legal system.

Example: A felony can lead to a long prison sentence.

misdemeanour

IPA: /ˌmɪsdɪˈmiːnə/
Pronunciation: mis-di-MEE-nuh
French: délit mineur américain

Definition: A less serious crime in the US legal system.

Example: A misdemeanour is less serious than a felony.

B. Types of crime and criminal behaviour

violent crime

IPA: /ˌvaɪələnt ˈkraɪm/
Pronunciation: VY-uh-luhnt kryme
French: crime violent

Definition: Crime involving force or threat of force.

Example: Violent crime often receives strong media attention.

property crime

IPA: /ˈprɒpəti kraɪm/
Pronunciation: PROP-uh-tee kryme
French: atteinte aux biens

Definition: Crime involving theft or damage to property.

Example: Property crime includes burglary and vandalism.

white-collar crime

IPA: /ˌwaɪt ˈkɒlə kraɪm/
Pronunciation: white KOL-uh kryme
French: criminalité en col blanc

Definition: Financial or corporate crime committed by professionals.

Example: White-collar crime can cause enormous economic harm.

organised crime

IPA: /ˌɔːɡənaɪzd ˈkraɪm/
Pronunciation: OR-guh-nyzd kryme
French: crime organisé

Definition: Crime committed by structured criminal groups.

Example: Organised crime often involves trafficking and money laundering.

gang crime

IPA: /ˈɡæŋ kraɪm/
Pronunciation: gang kryme
French: criminalité de gangs

Definition: Crime linked to street gangs or organised groups.

Example: Gang crime is often connected to poverty and exclusion.

knife crime

IPA: /ˈnaɪf kraɪm/
Pronunciation: nyfe kryme
French: criminalité au couteau

Definition: Crime involving knives, especially assaults or threats.

Example: Knife crime has become a major concern in Britain.

gun crime

IPA: /ˈɡʌn kraɪm/
Pronunciation: gun kryme
French: criminalité armée

Definition: Crime involving firearms.

Example: Gun crime is central to US debates about violence.

hate crime

IPA: /ˈheɪt kraɪm/
Pronunciation: hate kryme
French: crime de haine

Definition: A crime motivated by hostility towards identity such as race, religion or sexuality.

Example: Hate crime can make whole communities feel unsafe.

domestic violence

IPA: /dəˌmestɪk ˈvaɪələns/
Pronunciation: duh-MES-tik VY-uh-luhns
French: violence domestique ; violences conjugales

Definition: Violence or abuse within a family or intimate relationship.

Example: Domestic violence often remains hidden.

sexual assault

IPA: /ˌsekʃuəl əˈsɔːlt/
Pronunciation: SEK-shoo-uhl uh-SAWLT
French: agression sexuelle

Definition: A sexual act committed without consent.

Example: Sexual assault cases require sensitive handling.

rape

IPA: /reɪp/
Pronunciation: rayp
French: viol

Definition: Forced sexual intercourse or sexual penetration without consent.

Example: Rape victims often face stigma and disbelief.

harassment

IPA: /ˈhærəsmənt/
Pronunciation: HARR-uhs-muhnt
French: harcèlement

Definition: Repeated behaviour that intimidates, threatens or humiliates someone.

Example: Online harassment can silence public voices.

stalking

IPA: /ˈstɔːkɪŋ/
Pronunciation: STAW-king
French: harcèlement obsessionnel ; stalking

Definition: Repeated unwanted following or monitoring of someone.

Example: Stalking can escalate into violence.

burglary

IPA: /ˈbɜːɡləri/
Pronunciation: BUR-gluh-ree
French: cambriolage

Definition: Entering a building illegally to steal.

Example: Burglary can leave victims feeling unsafe at home.

robbery

IPA: /ˈrɒbəri/
Pronunciation: ROB-uh-ree
French: vol avec violence

Definition: Stealing from a person using force or threats.

Example: Robbery is treated as a serious violent offence.

theft

IPA: /θeft/
Pronunciation: theft
French: vol

Definition: The act of stealing property.

Example: Theft can range from shoplifting to car theft.

shoplifting

IPA: /ˈʃɒplɪftɪŋ/
Pronunciation: SHOP-lif-ting
French: vol à l’étalage

Definition: Stealing goods from a shop.

Example: Shoplifting often rises during periods of hardship.

fraud

IPA: /frɔːd/
Pronunciation: fraud
French: fraude ; escroquerie

Definition: Deception for financial or personal gain.

Example: Fraud is increasingly committed online.

scam

IPA: /skæm/
Pronunciation: skam
French: arnaque

Definition: A dishonest scheme designed to trick people out of money.

Example: Elderly people are often targeted by scams.

identity theft

IPA: /aɪˈdentəti θeft/
Pronunciation: eye-DEN-tuh-tee theft
French: usurpation d’identité

Definition: Stealing someone’s personal information to commit fraud.

Example: Identity theft is a growing cybercrime.

money laundering

IPA: /ˈmʌni ˌlɔːndərɪŋ/
Pronunciation: MUN-ee LAWN-duh-ring
French: blanchiment d’argent

Definition: Hiding the illegal origin of money.

Example: Money laundering allows criminal profits to enter the legal economy.

tax evasion

IPA: /ˈtæks ɪˌveɪʒən/
Pronunciation: taks i-VAY-zhuhn
French: fraude fiscale

Definition: Illegal non-payment or underpayment of taxes.

Example: Tax evasion deprives the state of public revenue.

bribery

IPA: /ˈbraɪbəri/
Pronunciation: BRY-buh-ree
French: corruption ; pot-de-vin

Definition: Giving or receiving money or favours to influence someone dishonestly.

Example: Bribery undermines trust in public institutions.

corruption

IPA: /kəˈrʌpʃən/
Pronunciation: kuh-RUP-shuhn
French: corruption

Definition: Dishonest abuse of power for personal gain.

Example: Corruption weakens democracy and the rule of law.

cybercrime

IPA: /ˈsaɪbəkraɪm/
Pronunciation: SY-buh-kryme
French: cybercriminalité

hacking

IPA: /ˈhækɪŋ/

French: piratage informatique

Definition: Gaining unauthorised access to computer systems.

Example: Hacking can target companies, governments or individuals.

phishing

IPA: /ˈfɪʃɪŋ/

French: hameçonnage ; phishing

Definition: Fraudulent messages designed to steal information or money.

human trafficking

Pronunciation: HYOO-muhn TRAF-i-king
French: traite des êtres humains

Definition: The exploitation of people through force, fraud or coercion.

Example: Human trafficking is a serious form of organised crime.

drug trafficking

IPA: /ˈdrʌɡ ˌtræfɪkɪŋ/
Pronunciation: drug TRAF-i-king
French: trafic de drogue

Definition: The illegal trade in drugs.

Example: Drug trafficking funds organised crime networks.

smuggling

IPA: /ˈsmʌɡlɪŋ/
Pronunciation: SMUG-ling
French: contrebande ; trafic

Definition: Illegally moving goods or people across borders.

Example: Smuggling networks exploit vulnerable migrants.

criminal record

IPA: /ˌkrɪmɪnəl ˈrekɔːd/
Pronunciation: KRIM-i-nuhl REK-awd
French: casier judiciaire

Definition: An official record of someone’s crimes or convictions.

Example: A criminal record can limit employment opportunities.

C. Arrest, trial and court procedure

arrest

IPA: /əˈrest/
Pronunciation: uh-REST
French: arrestation

Definition: The act of legally taking someone into custody.

Example: An arrest must usually be based on reasonable suspicion.

to arrest

IPA: /əˈrest/
Pronunciation: uh-REST
French: arrêter

Definition: To take someone into police custody.

Example: Police arrested the suspect after the attack.

custody

IPA: /ˈkʌstədi/
Pronunciation: KUS-tuh-dee
French: garde à vue ; détention

Definition: Legal detention by police or authorities.

Example: The suspect was held in custody overnight.

detention

IPA: /dɪˈtenʃən/
Pronunciation: di-TEN-shuhn
French: détention ; rétention

Definition: The state of being held by authorities.

Example: Pre-trial detention should not become punishment before conviction.

bail

IPA: /beɪl/
Pronunciation: bayl
French: libération sous caution

Definition: Temporary release while waiting for trial, sometimes with conditions.

Example: The judge granted bail with strict conditions.

charge

IPA: /tʃɑːdʒ/
Pronunciation: charj
French: chef d’accusation ; inculpation

Definition: A formal accusation that someone has committed a crime.

Example: The suspect faced several charges.

to charge someone with

IPA: /tʃɑːdʒ ˈsʌmwʌn wɪð/
Pronunciation: charj SUM-wun with
French: inculper quelqu’un de

Definition: To formally accuse someone of a crime.

Example: The police charged him with burglary.

prosecution

IPA: /ˌprɒsɪˈkjuːʃən/
Pronunciation: pros-i-KYOO-shuhn
French: poursuites ; accusation

Definition: The legal process of bringing a criminal case against someone.

Example: The prosecution must prove guilt beyond reasonable doubt.

prosecutor

IPA: /ˈprɒsɪkjuːtə/
Pronunciation: PROS-i-kyoo-tuh
French: procureur ; procureure

Definition: A lawyer who brings a criminal case on behalf of the state.

Example: The prosecutor presented evidence to the jury.

defence

IPA: /dɪˈfens/
Pronunciation: di-FENSS
French: défense

Definition: The argument or lawyers representing the accused.

Example: The defence challenged the reliability of the witness.

defendant

IPA: /dɪˈfendənt/
Pronunciation: di-FEN-duhnt
French: prévenu ; accusé

Definition: A person accused in a court case.

Example: The defendant pleaded not guilty.

accused

IPA: /əˈkjuːzd/
Pronunciation: uh-KYOOZD
French: accusé

Definition: The person charged with or suspected of a crime.

Example: The accused has the right to a fair trial.

trial

IPA: /ˈtraɪəl/
Pronunciation: TRY-uhl
French: procès

Definition: A court process deciding whether someone is guilty.

Example: A trial must be fair and impartial.

court

IPA: /kɔːt/
Pronunciation: court
French: tribunal ; cour

Definition: A place or institution where legal cases are heard.

Example: The case went to court after a long investigation.

courtroom

IPA: /ˈkɔːtruːm/

Definition: The room where a trial or hearing takes place.

judge

French: juge

jury

IPA: /ˈdʒʊəri/
Pronunciation: JOOR-ee
French: jury

juror

IPA: /ˈdʒʊərə/

French: juré

verdict

IPA: /ˈvɜːdɪkt/

guilty

French: coupable

not guilty

IPA: /ˌnɒt ˈɡɪlti/
Pronunciation: not GIL-tee
French: non coupable

innocent

IPA: /ˈɪnəsənt/
Pronunciation: IN-uh-suhnt
French: innocent

Definition: Not guilty of a crime.

Example: The presumption of innocence protects the accused.

French: présomption d’innocence

French: charge de la preuve

punishment

probation
