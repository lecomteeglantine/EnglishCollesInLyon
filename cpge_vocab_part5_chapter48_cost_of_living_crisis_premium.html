Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse the cost-of-living crisis: inflation, purchasing power, food and energy prices, household budgets, fuel poverty, rent and mortgage pressure, debt, government support, wages, strikes, corporate pricing, inequality and the difficult balance between helping households and controlling inflation.

How to use this chapter

For press articlesFocus on terms such as purchasing power, real wages, energy price shock, food inflation, fuel poverty, heat or eat dilemma, price cap, targeted support, greedflation and windfall tax.
For oral examsUse this vocabulary to explain how rising prices affect households, workers, businesses, public finances and political trust.
For essaysMemorise the collocations: to make ends meet, to erode purchasing power, to squeeze household budgets, to subsidise energy bills, to balance support and inflation.

A. Core cost-of-living vocabulary

cost-of-living crisis

IPA: /ˌkɒst əv ˈlɪvɪŋ ˈkraɪsɪs/
Pronunciation: kost-ov-LIV-ing KRY-sis
French: crise du coût de la vie

Definition: A situation where essential costs rise faster than incomes.

Example: The cost-of-living crisis has pushed many households into financial hardship.

cost of living

IPA: /ˌkɒst əv ˈlɪvɪŋ/
Pronunciation: kost ov LIV-ing
French: coût de la vie

Definition: The amount of money needed to pay for basic goods and services.

Example: The cost of living is higher in large cities.

living costs

IPA: /ˈlɪvɪŋ kɒsts/
Pronunciation: LIV-ing kosts
French: coûts de la vie quotidienne

Definition: The costs of everyday life, such as food, housing, energy and transport.

Example: Living costs have risen faster than wages.

essential costs

IPA: /ɪˌsenʃəl ˈkɒsts/
Pronunciation: i-SEN-shuhl kosts
French: dépenses essentielles

Definition: Costs linked to basic needs such as food, energy, rent and transport.

Example: Essential costs take up most of a low-income household’s budget.

household budget

IPA: /ˌhaʊshəʊld ˈbʌdʒɪt/
Pronunciation: HOWSS-hohld BUJ-it
French: budget du ménage

Definition: The money a household has available and how it is spent.

Example: Inflation has put pressure on household budgets.

family budget

IPA: /ˌfæməli ˈbʌdʒɪt/
Pronunciation: FAM-uh-lee BUJ-it
French: budget familial

Definition: The money available to a family and its planned expenses.

Example: Many families have had to cut non-essential items from the family budget.

squeezed households

IPA: /ˌskwiːzd ˈhaʊshəʊldz/
Pronunciation: skweezd HOWSS-hohldz
French: ménages sous pression

Definition: Households under financial pressure because costs are rising faster than income.

Example: Squeezed households often reduce heating or leisure spending.

household finances

IPA: /ˌhaʊshəʊld ˈfaɪnænsɪz/
Pronunciation: HOWSS-hohld FY-nan-siz
French: finances du ménage

Definition: The income, spending, debts and savings of a household.

Example: Household finances deteriorated during the energy crisis.

financial pressure

IPA: /faɪˌnænʃəl ˈpreʃə/
Pronunciation: fy-NAN-shuhl PRESH-uh
French: pression financière

Definition: Stress caused by money difficulties.

Example: Financial pressure can damage mental health.

financial strain

IPA: /faɪˌnænʃəl ˈstreɪn/
Pronunciation: fy-NAN-shuhl strayn
French: tension financière

Definition: Serious pressure on someone’s finances.

Example: Rent and energy bills create financial strain.

financial hardship

IPA: /faɪˌnænʃəl ˈhɑːdʃɪp/
Pronunciation: fy-NAN-shuhl HARD-ship
French: difficultés financières

Definition: Severe difficulty caused by lack of money.

Example: Financial hardship forces families to make painful choices.

economic hardship

IPA: /ˌiːkəˈnɒmɪk ˈhɑːdʃɪp/
Pronunciation: ee-kuh-NOM-ik HARD-ship
French: difficultés économiques

Definition: Difficulty caused by unemployment, low income or rising prices.

Example: Economic hardship has increased demand for welfare support.

hardship

IPA: /ˈhɑːdʃɪp/
Pronunciation: HARD-ship
French: épreuve ; difficulté

Definition: Severe difficulty, especially financial suffering.

Example: Many pensioners face hardship during winter.

affordability

IPA: /əˌfɔːdəˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: uh-FOR-duh-BIL-uh-tee
French: accessibilité financière

Definition: The extent to which people can pay for something without hardship.

Example: Housing affordability has become a major political issue.

unaffordable

IPA: /ˌʌnəˈfɔːdəbəl/
Pronunciation: un-uh-FOR-duh-buhl
French: inabordable

Definition: Too expensive for someone to pay for.

Example: Childcare is unaffordable for many low-income families.

basic necessities

IPA: /ˌbeɪsɪk nəˈsesətiz/
Pronunciation: BAY-sik nuh-SES-uh-teez
French: nécessités de base

Definition: Essential things people need to live, such as food, shelter and warmth.

Example: Some households struggle to afford basic necessities.

essentials

IPA: /ɪˈsenʃəlz/
Pronunciation: i-SEN-shuhlz
French: produits de première nécessité

Definition: Necessary goods or services.

Example: Poor households spend most of their money on essentials.

non-essential spending

IPA: /ˌnɒn ɪˌsenʃəl ˈspendɪŋ/
Pronunciation: non-i-SEN-shuhl SPEN-ding
French: dépenses non essentielles

Definition: Spending on things that are not basic needs.

Example: Non-essential spending is often cut first during a crisis.

discretionary spending

IPA: /dɪˈskreʃənəri ˈspendɪŋ/
Pronunciation: di-SKRESH-uh-nuh-ree SPEN-ding
French: dépenses discrétionnaires

Definition: Money spent on non-essential goods and services.

Example: Discretionary spending falls when households are squeezed.

purchasing power

IPA: /ˈpɜːtʃəsɪŋ ˌpaʊə/
Pronunciation: PUR-chuh-sing POW-uh
French: pouvoir d’achat

Definition: The amount of goods and services money can buy.

Example: Inflation erodes purchasing power.

real income

IPA: /ˌrɪəl ˈɪnkʌm/
Pronunciation: REE-uhl IN-kum
French: revenu réel

Definition: Income adjusted for inflation.

Example: Real income falls when wages lag behind prices.

real wages

IPA: /ˌrɪəl ˈweɪdʒɪz/
Pronunciation: REE-uhl WAY-jiz
French: salaires réels

Definition: Wages adjusted for inflation.

Example: Real wages declined during the inflation shock.

nominal wages

IPA: /ˌnɒmɪnəl ˈweɪdʒɪz/
Pronunciation: NOM-i-nuhl WAY-jiz
French: salaires nominaux

Definition: Wages before adjusting for inflation.

Example: Nominal wages may rise while real wages fall.

wage growth

IPA: /ˈweɪdʒ ɡrəʊθ/
Pronunciation: wayj growth
French: croissance des salaires

Definition: An increase in wages over time.

Example: Wage growth has not always kept pace with inflation.

pay rise

IPA: /ˈpeɪ raɪz/
Pronunciation: pay ryze
French: augmentation de salaire

Definition: An increase in wages or salary.

Example: Workers demanded a pay rise to match inflation.

below-inflation pay rise

IPA: /bɪˌləʊ ɪnˈfleɪʃən ˈpeɪ raɪz/
Pronunciation: bi-LOH in-FLAY-shuhn pay ryze
French: augmentation inférieure à l’inflation

Definition: A wage increase smaller than the inflation rate.

Example: A below-inflation pay rise is a real-terms pay cut.

real-terms pay cut

IPA: /ˌrɪəl tɜːmz ˈpeɪ kʌt/
Pronunciation: REE-uhl-termz pay kut
French: baisse de salaire en termes réels

Definition: A fall in pay after inflation is taken into account.

Example: Many public-sector workers experienced a real-terms pay cut.

income squeeze

IPA: /ˈɪnkʌm skwiːz/
Pronunciation: IN-kum skweez
French: compression des revenus

Definition: Pressure on income caused by rising prices or stagnant wages.

Example: The income squeeze hit low-paid workers hardest.

living standards squeeze

IPA: /ˈlɪvɪŋ ˌstændədz skwiːz/
Pronunciation: LIV-ing STAN-duhdz skweez
French: baisse sous pression du niveau de vie

Definition: A decline in material comfort because prices rise faster than income.

Example: The living standards squeeze became a major electoral issue.

inflation

IPA: /ɪnˈfleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: in-FLAY-shuhn
French: inflation

Definition: A general rise in prices.

Example: Inflation is the main driver of the cost-of-living crisis.

B. Inflation, prices and corporate behaviour

inflation rate

IPA: /ɪnˈfleɪʃən reɪt/
Pronunciation: in-FLAY-shuhn rayt
French: taux d’inflation

Definition: The speed at which prices rise.

Example: The inflation rate rose sharply after energy prices surged.

headline inflation

IPA: /ˌhedlaɪn ɪnˈfleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: HED-lyne in-FLAY-shuhn
French: inflation globale

Definition: Overall inflation including all goods and services.

Example: Headline inflation can be driven by food and energy prices.

core inflation

IPA: /ˌkɔːr ɪnˈfleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: kor in-FLAY-shuhn
French: inflation sous-jacente

Definition: Inflation excluding volatile items such as food and energy.

Example: Core inflation shows whether price pressures are persistent.

food inflation

IPA: /ˈfuːd ɪnˌfleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: food in-FLAY-shuhn
French: inflation alimentaire

Definition: Rising food prices.

Example: Food inflation harms low-income households disproportionately.

energy inflation

IPA: /ˈenədʒi ɪnˌfleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: EN-uh-jee in-FLAY-shuhn
French: inflation énergétique

Definition: Rising energy prices.

Example: Energy inflation increased heating bills.

rent inflation

IPA: /ˈrent ɪnˌfleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: rent in-FLAY-shuhn
French: inflation des loyers

Definition: Rising rents over time.

Example: Rent inflation worsens housing insecurity.

price shock

IPA: /ˈpraɪs ʃɒk/
Pronunciation: price shok
French: choc de prix

Definition: A sudden sharp rise in prices.

Example: A price shock can quickly reduce living standards.

IPA: /ˈenədʒi praɪs ʃɒk/
Pronunciation: EN-uh-jee price shok
French: choc des prix de l’énergie

Definition: A sudden sharp rise in energy prices.

Example: The energy price shock increased fuel poverty.

supply shock

IPA: /səˈplaɪ ʃɒk/
Pronunciation: suh-PLY shok
French: choc d’offre

Definition: A sudden disruption to supply that affects prices or output.

Example: The war created a supply shock in energy markets.

bottleneck

IPA: /ˈbɒtəlnek/
Pronunciation: BOT-uhl-nek
French: goulot d’étranglement

Definition: A point where supply or production is blocked or slowed.

Example: Supply-chain bottlenecks pushed up goods prices.

shortage

IPA: /ˈʃɔːtɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: SHOR-tij
French: pénurie

Definition: A lack of enough goods, workers or resources.

Example: Shortages can drive prices upward.

scarcity

IPA: /ˈskeəsəti/
Pronunciation: SKAIR-suh-tee
French: rareté

Definition: Limited availability of resources or goods.

Example: Scarcity increases bargaining power for suppliers.

price gouging

IPA: /ˈpraɪs ˌɡaʊdʒɪŋ/
Pronunciation: price GOW-jing
French: prix abusifs

Definition: Charging excessively high prices during a crisis or shortage.

Example: Price gouging is often condemned during emergencies.

greedflation

IPA: /ˌɡriːdˈfleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: greed-FLAY-shuhn
French: inflation par avidité

Definition: A critical term claiming firms raise prices excessively to increase profits.

Example: Greedflation became a popular explanation for rising prices.

shrinkflation

IPA: /ʃrɪŋkˈfleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: shrink-FLAY-shuhn
French: réduflation

Definition: Reducing product size while keeping the same price.

Example: Shrinkflation makes price increases less visible.

skimpflation

IPA: /skɪmpˈfleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: skimp-FLAY-shuhn
French: baisse de qualité masquée

Definition: Reducing product or service quality while keeping the price similar.

Example: Skimpflation frustrates consumers.

profit margin

IPA: /ˈprɒfɪt ˌmɑːdʒɪn/
Pronunciation: PROF-it MAR-jin
French: marge bénéficiaire

Definition: The share of revenue kept as profit.

Example: Rising profit margins fuelled accusations of greedflation.

excess profits

IPA: /ˌekses ˈprɒfɪts/
Pronunciation: EK-sess PROF-its
French: surprofits

Definition: Profits considered unusually high.

Example: Energy firms were accused of making excess profits.

windfall profits

IPA: /ˈwɪndfɔːl ˌprɒfɪts/
Pronunciation: WIND-fawl PROF-its
French: profits exceptionnels

Definition: Unexpectedly high profits caused by unusual circumstances.

Example: Windfall profits led to calls for a special tax.
