Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse education: school systems, public and private education, curriculum, exams, inequality, funding, teacher shortages, inclusion, higher education, student debt, culture wars, social mobility and the role of education in democracy and the labour market.

How to use this chapter

For press articlesFocus on terms such as state education, private school, tuition fees, student debt, achievement gap, teacher shortage, critical race theory and book bans.
For oral examsUse this vocabulary to explain how education can promote opportunity but also reproduce inequality through funding gaps, private tutoring, elite admissions and unequal access to resources.
For essaysMemorise the collocations: to widen access, to close the attainment gap, to level the playing field, to reproduce inequality, to promote critical thinking.

A. School systems and types of institutions

education

IPA: /ˌedjʊˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: ej-yoo-KAY-shuhn
French: éducation ; enseignement

Definition: The process of teaching, learning and developing knowledge, skills and values.

Example: Education is often presented as the key to social mobility.

schooling

IPA: /ˈskuːlɪŋ/
Pronunciation: SKOO-ling
French: scolarisation

Definition: Formal education received at school.

Example: Schooling is compulsory for children in Britain and the United States.

state education

IPA: /ˌsteɪt ˌedjʊˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: state ej-yoo-KAY-shuhn
French: enseignement public

Definition: Education provided or funded by the government.

Example: State education is supposed to provide equal opportunity.

public education

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk ˌedjʊˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik ej-yoo-KAY-shuhn
French: enseignement public

Definition: Government-funded education; in the US, this means state schools.

Example: Public education is central to democratic citizenship.

state school

IPA: /ˈsteɪt skuːl/
Pronunciation: state school
French: école publique britannique

Definition: In Britain, a school funded by the government and free to attend.

Example: Most British children attend state schools.

public school

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk ˈskuːl/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik school
French: école privée prestigieuse britannique ; école publique américaine

Definition: In Britain, an expensive private school; in the US, a government-funded school.

Example: The term public school means very different things in Britain and the United States.

private school

IPA: /ˌpraɪvət ˈskuːl/
Pronunciation: PRY-vuht school
French: école privée

Definition: A fee-paying school not funded by the state.

Example: Private schools often benefit from smaller classes and extensive resources.

independent school

IPA: /ˌɪndɪˈpendənt skuːl/
Pronunciation: in-di-PEN-duhnt school
French: école indépendante ; école privée

Definition: A British private school funded mainly by fees rather than the state.

Example: Independent schools educate a small but influential minority.

fee-paying school

IPA: /ˈfiː peɪɪŋ skuːl/
Pronunciation: fee-PAY-ing school
French: école payante

Definition: A school for which families pay tuition fees.

Example: Fee-paying schools raise questions about privilege.

boarding school

IPA: /ˈbɔːdɪŋ skuːl/
Pronunciation: BOR-ding school
French: internat

Definition: A school where pupils live during term time.

Example: Boarding schools are often associated with Britain’s elite.

grammar school

IPA: /ˈɡræmə skuːl/
Pronunciation: GRAM-uh school
French: grammar school ; lycée sélectif public

Definition: A selective state school in Britain admitting pupils based on academic ability.

Example: Grammar schools remain controversial because they select pupils at a young age.

comprehensive school

IPA: /ˌkɒmprɪˈhensɪv skuːl/
Pronunciation: kom-pri-HEN-siv school
French: établissement secondaire public non sélectif

Definition: A non-selective state secondary school for pupils of all abilities.

Example: Comprehensive schools were designed to reduce class barriers.

academy

IPA: /əˈkædəmi/
Pronunciation: uh-KAD-uh-mee
French: academy ; école publique autonome

Definition: In England, a state-funded school with greater independence from local authority control.

Example: Academies have more freedom over budgets and curriculum.

free school

IPA: /ˈfriː skuːl/
Pronunciation: free school
French: free school ; école publique autonome

Definition: In England, a state-funded school set up by groups such as parents, charities or trusts.

Example: Free schools were promoted as a way to increase choice.

faith school

IPA: /ˈfeɪθ skuːl/
Pronunciation: faith school
French: école confessionnelle

Definition: A school linked to a religious group or tradition.

Example: Faith schools raise debates about religion and social cohesion.

single-sex school

IPA: /ˌsɪŋɡəl seks ˈskuːl/
Pronunciation: SING-guhl seks school
French: école non mixte

Definition: A school for either boys or girls only.

Example: Single-sex schools are sometimes defended as improving confidence.

co-educational school

IPA: /ˌkəʊ edjʊˈkeɪʃənəl skuːl/
Pronunciation: koh ej-yoo-KAY-shuh-nuhl school
French: école mixte

Definition: A school for both boys and girls.

Example: Most modern schools are co-educational.

nursery school

IPA: /ˈnɜːsəri skuːl/
Pronunciation: NUR-suh-ree school
French: école maternelle ; crèche éducative

Definition: A school or setting for very young children before primary school.

Example: Early nursery education can improve later outcomes.

primary school

IPA: /ˈpraɪməri skuːl/
Pronunciation: PRY-muh-ree school
French: école primaire

Definition: A school for younger children, before secondary education.

Example: Primary school builds the foundations of literacy and numeracy.

secondary school

IPA: /ˈsekəndəri skuːl/
Pronunciation: SEK-uhn-duh-ree school
French: collège/lycée ; établissement secondaire

Definition: A school for older children and teenagers.

Example: Secondary school prepares pupils for exams and further study.

sixth form

IPA: /ˈsɪksθ fɔːm/
Pronunciation: sixth form
French: sixth form ; classes de 16 à 18 ans

Definition: The final two years of secondary education in England, usually for ages 16 to 18.

Example: Students often take A-levels in sixth form.

college

IPA: /ˈkɒlɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: KOL-ij
French: college ; établissement post-16 ou université selon contexte

Definition: In Britain, often a post-16 or vocational institution; in the US, a university-level institution.

Example: The word college has different meanings in Britain and America.

university

IPA: /ˌjuːnɪˈvɜːsəti/
Pronunciation: yoo-ni-VUR-suh-tee
French: université

Definition: An institution offering higher education and degrees.

Example: University is often seen as a route to better employment.

higher education

IPA: /ˌhaɪər ˌedjʊˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: HY-uhr ej-yoo-KAY-shuhn
French: enseignement supérieur

Definition: Education at university or equivalent level.

Example: Higher education has expanded dramatically in recent decades.

further education

IPA: /ˌfɜːðər ˌedjʊˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: FUR-thuh ej-yoo-KAY-shuhn
French: formation post-secondaire ; further education

Definition: Education after compulsory schooling but below or outside university level.

Example: Further education colleges provide vocational and academic courses.

vocational education

IPA: /vəʊˌkeɪʃənəl ˌedjʊˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: voh-KAY-shuh-nuhl ej-yoo-KAY-shuhn
French: enseignement professionnel

Definition: Education focused on practical skills and specific jobs.

Example: Vocational education is crucial for technical careers.

technical education

IPA: /ˈteknɪkəl ˌedjʊˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: TEK-ni-kuhl ej-yoo-KAY-shuhn
French: enseignement technique

Definition: Education focused on technical knowledge and applied skills.

Example: Technical education can help address skills shortages.

apprenticeship

IPA: /əˈprentɪsʃɪp/
Pronunciation: uh-PREN-tis-ship
French: apprentissage

Definition: A programme combining paid work with training.

Example: Apprenticeships offer an alternative to university.

degree

IPA: /dɪˈɡriː/
Pronunciation: di-GREE
French: diplôme universitaire

Definition: A qualification awarded by a university or college.

Example: A degree can improve job prospects but may also create debt.

undergraduate

IPA: /ˌʌndəˈɡrædʒuət/
Pronunciation: UN-duh-GRAJ-oo-uht
French: étudiant de licence ; undergraduate

Definition: A student studying for a first university degree.

Example: Undergraduates often pay tuition fees.

postgraduate

IPA: /ˌpəʊstˈɡrædʒuət/
Pronunciation: post-GRAJ-oo-uht
French: étudiant de master/doctorat ; postgraduate

Definition: A student studying after a first degree.

Example: Postgraduate study can be expensive.

tuition fees

IPA: /tjuːˈɪʃən fiːz/
Pronunciation: tyoo-ISH-uhn feez
French: frais de scolarité

Definition: Money paid for teaching, especially at university or private school.

Example: Tuition fees have made higher education more financially risky.

student debt

IPA: /ˈstjuːdənt det/
Pronunciation: STYOO-duhnt det
French: dette étudiante

Definition: Money borrowed to pay for education.

Example: Student debt can delay home ownership and family formation.

student loan

IPA: /ˈstjuːdənt ləʊn/
Pronunciation: STYOO-duhnt lohn
French: prêt étudiant

Definition: Money borrowed by a student to pay for education and living costs.

Example: Many graduates repay student loans for years.

grant

IPA: /ɡrɑːnt/
Pronunciation: grant
French: bourse ; subvention

Definition: Money given to support study, research or institutions, usually not repaid.

Example: Grants can make education more accessible.

scholarship

IPA: /ˈskɒləʃɪp/
Pronunciation: SKOL-uh-ship
French: bourse d’études

Definition: Financial support awarded to a student, often based on merit or need.

Example: Scholarships help talented students from poorer backgrounds.

bursary

IPA: /ˈbɜːsəri/
Pronunciation: BUR-suh-ree
French: bourse d’aide sociale

Definition: Financial support for students, often based on need.

Example: Bursaries can reduce barriers to higher education.

curriculum

IPA: /kəˈrɪkjʊləm/
Pronunciation: kuh-RIK-yuh-luhm
French: programme scolaire

Definition: The subjects and content taught in a school or course.

Example: The curriculum often becomes a political battleground.

B. Curriculum, learning methods and assessment

national curriculum

IPA: /ˌnæʃənəl kəˈrɪkjʊləm/
Pronunciation: NASH-uh-nuhl kuh-RIK-yuh-luhm
French: programme national

Definition: The official set of subjects and standards taught in state schools.

Example: The national curriculum defines what pupils are expected to learn.

syllabus

IPA: /ˈsɪləbəs/
Pronunciation: SIL-uh-buhs
French: programme de cours ; syllabus

Definition: The detailed content of a course or exam.

Example: The syllabus lists the texts and topics for the exam.

core subject

IPA: /ˌkɔː ˈsʌbdʒɪkt/
Pronunciation: core SUB-jikt
French: matière fondamentale

Definition: A central compulsory subject, such as English, maths or science.

Example: Maths and English are core subjects.

STEM

IPA: /stem/
Pronunciation: stem
French: STEM ; sciences, technologie, ingénierie et mathématiques

Definition: Science, technology, engineering and mathematics.

Example: Governments often promote STEM to improve competitiveness.

humanities

IPA: /hjuːˈmænətiz/
Pronunciation: hyoo-MAN-uh-teez
French: sciences humaines

Definition: Subjects such as history, literature, philosophy and languages.

Example: The humanities help students think critically about society.

literacy

IPA: /ˈlɪtərəsi/
Pronunciation: LIT-uh-ruh-see
French: maîtrise de la lecture et de l’écriture

Definition: The ability to read and write effectively.

Example: Literacy is a foundation for educational success.

numeracy

IPA: /ˈnjuːmərəsi/
Pronunciation: NYOO-muh-ruh-see
French: maîtrise du calcul

Definition: The ability to use numbers and basic mathematics effectively.

Example: Numeracy matters in everyday life and employment.

critical thinking

IPA: /ˌkrɪtɪkəl ˈθɪŋkɪŋ/
Pronunciation: KRIT-i-kuhl THINK-ing
French: esprit critique

Definition: The ability to analyse information carefully and judge arguments.

Example: Critical thinking is essential in the age of misinformation.

rote learning

IPA: /ˌrəʊt ˈlɜːnɪŋ/
Pronunciation: roht LUR-ning
French: apprentissage par cœur

Definition: Learning by repetition and memorisation, often without deep understanding.

Example: Critics argue that excessive testing encourages rote learning.

active learning

IPA: /ˌæktɪv ˈlɜːnɪŋ/
Pronunciation: AK-tiv LUR-ning
French: apprentissage actif

Definition: Learning through participation, problem-solving and discussion.

Example: Active learning can improve engagement.

blended learning

IPA: /ˌblendɪd ˈlɜːnɪŋ/
Pronunciation: BLEN-did LUR-ning
French: apprentissage hybride

Definition: A mix of online and face-to-face learning.

Example: Blended learning expanded after the pandemic.

remote learning

IPA: /rɪˌməʊt ˈlɜːnɪŋ/
Pronunciation: ri-MOHT LUR-ning
French: enseignement à distance

Definition: Learning from home or another location using digital tools.

Example: Remote learning exposed inequalities in access to technology.

online learning

IPA: /ˌɒnlaɪn ˈlɜːnɪŋ/
Pronunciation: ON-lyne LUR-ning
French: apprentissage en ligne

Definition: Learning delivered through the internet.

Example: Online learning can be flexible but isolating.

assessment

IPA: /əˈsesmənt/
Pronunciation: uh-SESS-muhnt
French: évaluation

Definition: The process of evaluating learning or performance.

Example: Assessment can be formative or summative.

exam

IPA: /ɪɡˈzæm/
Pronunciation: ig-ZAM
French: examen

Definition: A formal test of knowledge or skills.

Example: High-stakes exams can put pressure on pupils.

standardised test

IPA: /ˈstændədaɪzd test/
Pronunciation: STAN-duh-dyzd test
French: test standardisé

Definition: A test administered and scored in the same way for all candidates.

Example: Standardised tests are often criticised for narrowing the curriculum.

high-stakes testing

IPA: /ˌhaɪ steɪks ˈtestɪŋ/
Pronunciation: hy-stayks TEST-ing
French: évaluation à forts enjeux

Definition: Testing with important consequences for pupils, teachers or schools.

Example: High-stakes testing can distort teaching priorities.

grade

IPA: /ɡreɪd/
Pronunciation: grayd
French: note ; niveau

Definition: A mark or level awarded for schoolwork or an exam.

Example: Grades influence access to higher education.

GCSE

IPA: /ˌdʒiː siː es ˈiː/
Pronunciation: jee-see-ess-ee
French: GCSE ; examens de fin de secondaire inférieur

Definition: Exams usually taken in England, Wales and Northern Ireland around age 16.

Example: GCSE results affect future study options.

A-level

IPA: /ˈeɪ levəl/
Pronunciation: AY-level
French: A-level ; examen de fin de secondaire

Definition: Advanced exams usually taken in England, Wales and Northern Ireland around age 18.

Example: A-levels are used for university admissions.

SAT

IPA: /ˌes eɪ ˈtiː/
Pronunciation: ess-ay-tee
French: SAT ; test d’entrée universitaire américain

Definition: A standardised test used in US college admissions.

Example: The SAT has become optional at many US colleges.

GPA

IPA: /ˌdʒiː piː ˈeɪ/
Pronunciation: jee-pee-ay
French: moyenne générale américaine

Definition: Grade point average, a measure of academic performance in the US.

Example: A high GPA can improve college applications.

league table

IPA: /ˈliːɡ ˌteɪbəl/
Pronunciation: leeg TAY-buhl
French: classement

Definition: A ranking of schools or universities by performance.

Example: School league tables can shape parental choice.

school inspection

IPA: /ˈskuːl ɪnˌspekʃən/

French: inspection scolaire

Definition: An official evaluation of a school’s quality.

Example: School inspections can affect reputation and funding.

Ofsted

IPA: /ˈɒfsted/
Pronunciation: OF-sted
French: Ofsted ; organisme d’inspection scolaire anglais

accountability

IPA: /əˌkaʊntəˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: uh-kown-tuh-BIL-uh-tee
French: responsabilité ; redevabilité

Example: Accountability systems can improve standards but also create pressure.

school funding

IPA: /ˈskuːl ˌfʌndɪŋ/
Pronunciation: school FUN-ding
French: financement des écoles

Definition: Money available to schools from government, fees or other sources.

Example: Unequal school funding affects educational outcomes.

funding gap

IPA: /ˈfʌndɪŋ ɡæp/

French: écart de financement

Example: The funding gap widens educational inequality.

underfunded school

IPA: /ˌʌndəˈfʌndɪd skuːl/
Pronunciation: UN-duh-FUN-did school
French: école sous-financée

overcrowded classroom

IPA: /ˌəʊvəˌkraʊdɪd ˈklɑːsruːm/

C. Funding, teachers, inclusion and inequality

teacher shortage

IPA: /ˈtiːtʃə ˌʃɔːtɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: TEE-chuh SHOR-tij
French: pénurie d’enseignants

Definition: A lack of enough qualified teachers.

Example: Teacher shortages are severe in some subjects.

teacher retention

IPA: /ˈtiːtʃə rɪˌtenʃən/
Pronunciation: TEE-chuh ri-TEN-shuhn
French: maintien des enseignants dans le métier

Definition: The ability to keep teachers in the profession.

Example: Workload affects teacher retention.

workload

IPA: /ˈwɜːkləʊd/
Pronunciation: WURK-lohd
French: charge de travail

Definition: The amount of work someone has to do.

Example: Teacher workload is a major cause of burnout.

burnout

IPA: /ˈbɜːnaʊt/
Pronunciation: BURN-owt
French: épuisement professionnel

Definition: Exhaustion caused by long-term stress and overwork.

Example: Teacher burnout threatens the quality of education.

special educational needs

IPA: /ˌspeʃəl ˌedjʊˈkeɪʃənəl niːdz/
Pronunciation: SPESH-uhl ej-yoo-KAY-shuh-nuhl needz
French: besoins éducatifs particuliers

Definition: Learning or support needs requiring additional help in education.

Example: Pupils with special educational needs often need tailored support.

SEN

IPA: /ˌes iː ˈen/
Pronunciation: ess-ee-en
French: BEP ; besoins éducatifs particuliers

Definition: Abbreviation for special educational needs.

Example: SEN provision is under pressure in many schools.

inclusive education

IPA: /ɪnˌkluːsɪv ˌedjʊˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: in-KLOO-siv ej-yoo-KAY-shuhn
French: éducation inclusive

Definition: Education designed to include all learners, regardless of need or background.

Example: Inclusive education requires resources and training.

disability support

IPA: /ˌdɪsəˈbɪləti səˈpɔːt/
Pronunciation: dis-uh-BIL-uh-tee suh-PORT
French: accompagnement du handicap

Definition: Assistance provided to students with disabilities.

Example: Disability support is essential for equal access.

achievement gap

IPA: /əˈtʃiːvmənt ɡæp/
Pronunciation: uh-CHEEV-muhnt gap
French: écart de réussite scolaire

Definition: A difference in academic performance between groups.

Example: The achievement gap often reflects poverty and unequal resources.

attainment gap

IPA: /əˈteɪnmənt ɡæp/
Pronunciation: uh-TAYN-muhnt gap
French: écart de niveau scolaire

Definition: A difference in educational results between groups.

Example: The attainment gap widened during school closures.

opportunity gap

IPA: /ˌɒpəˈtjuːnəti ɡæp/
Pronunciation: op-uh-TYOO-nuh-tee gap
French: écart d’opportunités

Definition: Unequal access to learning opportunities and resources.

Example: The opportunity gap begins before children enter school.

educational inequality

IPA: /ˌedjʊˈkeɪʃənəl ˌɪnɪˈkwɒləti/
Pronunciation: ej-yoo-KAY-shuh-nuhl in-i-KWOL-uh-tee
French: inégalité éducative

Definition: Unequal access to quality education or unequal outcomes.

Example: Educational inequality undermines social mobility.

postcode lottery

IPA: /ˈpəʊstkəʊd ˌlɒtəri/
Pronunciation: POST-kohd LOT-uh-ree
French: loterie du code postal ; inégalités territoriales

Example: School quality can become a postcode lottery.

catchment area

IPA: /ˈkætʃmənt ˌeəriə/

French: secteur scolaire

school choice

Pronunciation: school choice
French: choix de l’école

parental involvement

IPA: /pəˌrentəl ɪnˈvɒlvmənt/
Pronunciation: puh-REN-tuhl in-VOLV-muhnt
French: implication des parents

Example: Parental involvement strongly affects school success.

private tutoring

IPA: /ˌpraɪvət ˈtjuːtərɪŋ/
Pronunciation: PRY-vuht TYOO-tuh-ring
French: cours particuliers

Example: Private tutoring can widen inequality.

shadow education

IPA: /ˌʃædəʊ ˌedjʊˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: SHAD-oh ej-yoo-KAY-shuhn
French: éducation parallèle ; soutien privé

Example: Shadow education gives richer pupils an advantage.

social mobility

Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl moh-BIL-uh-tee
French: mobilité sociale

Example: Education is often seen as a driver of social mobility.

Pronunciation: mer-i-TOK-ruh-see
French: méritocratie

legacy admission

Example: Legacy admissions are criticised as unfair and elitist.

college admissions

IPA: /ˈkɒlɪdʒ ədˌmɪʃənz/
Pronunciation: KOL-ij ad-MISH-uhnz
French: admissions universitaires

Definition: The process of applying and being accepted to university or college, especially in the US.

Ivy League

IPA: /ˈaɪvi liːɡ/
