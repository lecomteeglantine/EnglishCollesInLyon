Part 4 — Society | Chapter 31 — Poverty and Inequality
Essential vocabulary for khôlles, essays, press reviews and competitive exams.

Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse poverty and inequality: absolute and relative poverty, deprivation, homelessness, food insecurity, income and wealth gaps, welfare, low-paid work, taxation, redistribution, structural inequality and policies designed to reduce hardship.

PovertyInequalityWelfareFood banksRedistributionKhôlles

How to use this chapter

For press articlesFocus on terms such as relative poverty, food insecurity, cost-of-living crisis, wealth gap, Universal Credit, poverty premium, austerity and redistribution.
For oral examsUse this vocabulary to explain how poverty is not only a lack of income, but also a lack of security, services, housing, health, opportunity and political voice.
For essaysMemorise the collocations: to lift people out of poverty, to reduce inequality, to widen the gap, to make ends meet, to address root causes.

A. Core poverty and inequality vocabulary

poverty

IPA: /ˈpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: POV-uh-tee
French: pauvreté

Definition: The state of lacking enough money or resources to meet basic needs.

Example: Poverty affects health, education, housing and life chances.

inequality

IPA: /ˌɪnɪˈkwɒləti/
Pronunciation: in-i-KWOL-uh-tee
French: inégalité

Definition: An unfair or uneven distribution of resources, opportunities or outcomes.

Example: Inequality has become a central issue in modern democracies.

economic inequality

IPA: /ˌiːkəˈnɒmɪk ˌɪnɪˈkwɒləti/
Pronunciation: ee-kuh-NOM-ik in-i-KWOL-uh-tee
French: inégalité économique

Definition: Unequal distribution of income, wealth and economic opportunities.

Example: Economic inequality can weaken trust in institutions.

social inequality

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl ˌɪnɪˈkwɒləti/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl in-i-KWOL-uh-tee
French: inégalité sociale

Definition: Unequal access to status, rights, services or opportunities in society.

Example: Social inequality is often reproduced through education and housing.

income inequality

IPA: /ˈɪnkʌm ˌɪnɪˈkwɒləti/
Pronunciation: IN-kum in-i-KWOL-uh-tee
French: inégalité des revenus

Definition: Unequal distribution of income across people or households.

Example: Income inequality has widened in many developed economies.

wealth inequality

IPA: /ˈwelθ ˌɪnɪˈkwɒləti/
Pronunciation: welth in-i-KWOL-uh-tee
French: inégalité patrimoniale

Definition: Unequal distribution of assets such as property, savings and investments.

Example: Wealth inequality is often greater than income inequality.

absolute poverty

IPA: /ˌæbsəluːt ˈpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: AB-suh-loot POV-uh-tee
French: pauvreté absolue

Definition: Poverty measured against a fixed basic standard of living.

Example: Absolute poverty means being unable to meet basic needs.

relative poverty

IPA: /ˌrelətɪv ˈpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: REL-uh-tiv POV-uh-tee
French: pauvreté relative

Definition: Poverty defined in relation to average living standards in a society.

Example: Relative poverty can exist even in wealthy countries.

extreme poverty

IPA: /ɪkˌstriːm ˈpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: ik-STREEM POV-uh-tee
French: extrême pauvreté

Definition: Severe poverty involving very low income and deprivation.

Example: Extreme poverty is often linked to hunger, unsafe housing and poor health.

deep poverty

IPA: /ˌdiːp ˈpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: deep POV-uh-tee
French: grande pauvreté

Definition: A very severe form of poverty far below the poverty line.

Example: Families in deep poverty often rely on emergency support.

persistent poverty

IPA: /pəˌsɪstənt ˈpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: puh-SIS-tuhnt POV-uh-tee
French: pauvreté persistante

Definition: Poverty that continues over a long period.

Example: Persistent poverty can damage children’s long-term prospects.

child poverty

IPA: /ˌtʃaɪld ˈpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: child POV-uh-tee
French: pauvreté infantile

Definition: Poverty experienced by children.

Example: Child poverty affects educational attainment and mental health.

working poverty

IPA: /ˌwɜːkɪŋ ˈpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: WUR-king POV-uh-tee
French: pauvreté laborieuse

Definition: Poverty among people who are employed.

Example: Working poverty shows that employment does not always ensure security.

working poor

IPA: /ˌwɜːkɪŋ ˈpɔː/
Pronunciation: WUR-king poor
French: travailleurs pauvres

Definition: People who work but remain poor.

Example: The working poor often face low wages and high housing costs.

in-work poverty

IPA: /ˌɪn wɜːk ˈpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: in-work POV-uh-tee
French: pauvreté au travail

Definition: Poverty affecting households where at least one adult works.

Example: In-work poverty has increased with insecure employment.

food poverty

IPA: /ˈfuːd ˌpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: food POV-uh-tee
French: précarité alimentaire

Definition: Lack of money to buy enough nutritious food.

Example: Food poverty can affect children’s concentration at school.

food insecurity

IPA: /ˈfuːd ˌɪnsɪˈkjʊərəti/
Pronunciation: food in-si-KYOOR-uh-tee
French: insécurité alimentaire

Definition: Unreliable access to enough affordable and nutritious food.

Example: Food insecurity has risen during the cost-of-living crisis.

fuel poverty

IPA: /ˈfjuːəl ˌpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: FYOO-uhl POV-uh-tee
French: précarité énergétique

Definition: Inability to afford adequate heating or energy.

Example: Fuel poverty forces families to choose between heating and eating.

energy poverty

IPA: /ˈenədʒi ˌpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: EN-uh-jee POV-uh-tee
French: pauvreté énergétique

Definition: Lack of affordable access to energy for basic needs.

Example: Energy poverty became more visible after energy prices surged.

period poverty

IPA: /ˈpɪəriəd ˌpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: PEER-ee-uhd POV-uh-tee
French: précarité menstruelle

Definition: Lack of access to menstrual products because of poverty.

Example: Period poverty can make girls miss school.

housing poverty

IPA: /ˈhaʊzɪŋ ˌpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: HOW-zing POV-uh-tee
French: pauvreté liée au logement

Definition: Poverty linked to unaffordable, unsafe or inadequate housing.

Example: Housing poverty is worsened by high rents.

homelessness

IPA: /ˈhəʊmləsnəs/
Pronunciation: HOHM-luhs-nuhs
French: sans-abrisme ; absence de logement

Definition: The condition of having no stable or safe home.

Example: Homelessness is the most visible form of housing exclusion.

rough sleeping

IPA: /ˌrʌf ˈsliːpɪŋ/
Pronunciation: ruff SLEE-ping
French: sans-abrisme de rue

Definition: Sleeping outside because of homelessness.

Example: Rough sleeping is dangerous and strongly linked to poor health.

hidden homelessness

IPA: /ˌhɪdən ˈhəʊmləsnəs/
Pronunciation: HID-uhn HOHM-luhs-nuhs
French: sans-abrisme caché

Definition: Homelessness not visible in official statistics, such as sofa-surfing.

Example: Hidden homelessness affects many young people.

B. Deprivation, hardship and measurement

sofa-surfing

IPA: /ˈsəʊfə ˌsɜːfɪŋ/
Pronunciation: SOH-fuh SUR-fing
French: hébergement temporaire chez des proches

Definition: Staying temporarily with friends or relatives because one has no home.

Example: Sofa-surfing can hide the true scale of homelessness.

deprivation

IPA: /ˌdeprɪˈveɪʃən/
Pronunciation: dep-ri-VAY-shuhn
French: privation ; défavorisation

Definition: A serious lack of resources, services or living conditions.

Example: Deprivation is often concentrated in specific neighbourhoods.

material deprivation

IPA: /məˌtɪəriəl ˌdeprɪˈveɪʃən/
Pronunciation: muh-TEER-ee-uhl dep-ri-VAY-shuhn
French: privation matérielle

Definition: Lack of basic goods and living conditions considered necessary.

Example: Material deprivation may include lack of heating, food or clothing.

severe deprivation

IPA: /sɪˌvɪə ˌdeprɪˈveɪʃən/
Pronunciation: si-VEER dep-ri-VAY-shuhn
French: privation sévère

Definition: Extreme lack of basic resources and living standards.

Example: Severe deprivation requires urgent public intervention.

multiple deprivation

IPA: /ˌmʌltɪpəl ˌdeprɪˈveɪʃən/
Pronunciation: MUL-ti-puhl dep-ri-VAY-shuhn
French: défavorisation multiple

Definition: Several forms of disadvantage occurring together, such as poor health, low income and bad housing.

Example: Multiple deprivation is difficult to solve with one policy alone.

social exclusion

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl ɪkˈskluːʒən/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl ik-SKLOO-zhuhn
French: exclusion sociale

Definition: Being cut off from opportunities, services and participation in society.

Example: Poverty often leads to social exclusion.

marginalisation

IPA: /ˌmɑːdʒɪnəlaɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: mar-ji-nuh-ly-ZAY-shuhn
French: marginalisation

Definition: The process of pushing groups to the edge of society.

Example: Marginalisation reduces voice and access to power.

vulnerability

IPA: /ˌvʌlnərəˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: vul-nuh-ruh-BIL-uh-tee
French: vulnérabilité

Definition: Exposure to harm, insecurity or hardship.

Example: Poverty increases vulnerability to illness and exploitation.

precariousness

IPA: /prɪˈkeəriəsnəs/
Pronunciation: pri-KAIR-ee-uhs-nuhs
French: précarité

Definition: A state of insecurity and instability.

Example: Precariousness affects work, housing and mental health.

precarity

IPA: /prɪˈkeərəti/
Pronunciation: pri-KAIR-uh-tee
French: précarité

Definition: A condition of unstable work, income or living conditions.

Example: Precarity has become a defining feature of many low-income lives.

hardship

IPA: /ˈhɑːdʃɪp/
Pronunciation: HARD-ship
French: difficultés matérielles ; épreuve

Definition: Serious difficulty caused by lack of money, resources or support.

Example: Many households face hardship during inflationary periods.

financial hardship

IPA: /faɪˌnænʃəl ˈhɑːdʃɪp/
Pronunciation: fy-NAN-shuhl HARD-ship
French: difficultés financières

Definition: Difficulty paying for basic needs because of lack of money.

Example: Financial hardship can push families into debt.

cost-of-living crisis

IPA: /ˌkɒst əv ˈlɪvɪŋ ˈkraɪsɪs/
Pronunciation: kost ov LIV-ing KRY-sis
French: crise du coût de la vie

Definition: A period when everyday expenses rise faster than incomes.

Example: The cost-of-living crisis has increased demand for food banks.

living standards

IPA: /ˈlɪvɪŋ ˌstændədz/
Pronunciation: LIV-ing STAN-duhdz
French: niveau de vie

Definition: The material conditions in which people live.

Example: Falling living standards fuel public anger.

standard of living

IPA: /ˌstændəd əv ˈlɪvɪŋ/
Pronunciation: STAN-duhd ov LIV-ing
French: niveau de vie

Definition: The level of comfort and material well-being available to a person or group.

Example: A decent standard of living should include housing, food and healthcare.

subsistence

IPA: /səbˈsɪstəns/
Pronunciation: suhb-SIS-tuhns
French: subsistance

Definition: The minimum resources needed to stay alive.

Example: Some wages barely cover subsistence.

destitution

IPA: /ˌdestɪˈtjuːʃən/
Pronunciation: des-ti-TYOO-shuhn
French: dénuement extrême

Definition: Extreme poverty with no means to meet basic needs.

Example: Destitution is a failure of the social safety net.

poverty line

IPA: /ˈpɒvəti laɪn/
Pronunciation: POV-uh-tee line
French: seuil de pauvreté

Definition: The income level below which someone is considered poor.

Example: Many families live below the poverty line.

low-income household

IPA: /ˌləʊ ˈɪnkʌm ˈhaʊshəʊld/
Pronunciation: loh IN-kum HOWSS-hohld
French: ménage à faible revenu

Definition: A household with limited income compared with the wider population.

Example: Low-income households are most affected by rising food prices.

household income

IPA: /ˌhaʊshəʊld ˈɪnkʌm/
Pronunciation: HOWSS-hohld IN-kum
French: revenu du ménage

Definition: The total income of all people living in a household.

Example: Household income determines access to housing and services.

disposable income

IPA: /dɪˌspəʊzəbəl ˈɪnkʌm/
Pronunciation: di-SPOH-zuh-buhl IN-kum
French: revenu disponible

Definition: Income left after taxes and essential deductions.

Example: Disposable income falls when energy bills rise.

real income

IPA: /ˌrɪəl ˈɪnkʌm/
Pronunciation: REE-uhl IN-kum
French: revenu réel

Definition: Income adjusted for inflation.

Example: Real incomes can fall even when wages rise slightly.

median income

IPA: /ˌmiːdiən ˈɪnkʌm/
Pronunciation: MEE-dee-uhn IN-kum
French: revenu médian

Definition: The income level in the middle of the income distribution.

Example: Relative poverty is often measured against median income.

income distribution

IPA: /ˈɪnkʌm ˌdɪstrɪˈbjuːʃən/
Pronunciation: IN-kum dis-tri-BYOO-shuhn
French: répartition des revenus

Definition: The way income is spread across a population.

Example: Income distribution has become more unequal in many countries.

wealth distribution

IPA: /ˈwelθ ˌdɪstrɪˈbjuːʃən/
Pronunciation: welth dis-tri-BYOO-shuhn
French: répartition du patrimoine

Definition: The way assets are spread across a population.

Example: Wealth distribution is shaped by inheritance and housing markets.

wealth gap

IPA: /ˈwelθ ɡæp/
Pronunciation: welth gap
French: écart de patrimoine

Definition: The difference in wealth between groups.

Example: The wealth gap is a major barrier to social mobility.

racial wealth gap

IPA: /ˌreɪʃəl ˈwelθ ɡæp/
Pronunciation: RAY-shuhl welth gap
French: écart de patrimoine racial

Definition: The difference in accumulated wealth between racial groups.

Example: The racial wealth gap reflects historical discrimination.

gender pay gap

IPA: /ˌdʒendə ˈpeɪ ɡæp/
Pronunciation: JEN-duh pay gap
French: écart salarial entre hommes et femmes

Definition: The difference between men’s and women’s average earnings.

Example: The gender pay gap contributes to women’s lower lifetime wealth.

underclass

IPA: /ˈɡɪɡ iˌkɒnəmi/
Pronunciation: gig i-KON-uh-mee
French: économie des petits boulots ; gig economy

Definition: A labour market based on short-term, freelance or platform work.

Example: The gig economy can create flexibility but also insecurity.

job insecurity

IPA: /ˈdʒɒb ˌɪnsɪˈkjʊərəti/
Pronunciation: job in-si-KYOOR-uh-tee
French: insécurité de l’emploi

Definition: Uncertainty about keeping one’s job.

Example: Job insecurity damages mental health and planning.

wage stagnation

IPA: /ˈweɪdʒ stæɡˌneɪʃən/
Pronunciation: wayj stag-NAY-shuhn
French: stagnation des salaires

Definition: A situation where wages do not rise significantly over time.

Example: Wage stagnation contributes to inequality.

underemployment

IPA: /ˌʌndərɪmˈplɔɪmənt/
Pronunciation: UN-duh-rim-PLOY-muhnt
French: sous-emploi

Definition: Working fewer hours or in lower-skilled work than desired or qualified for.

Example: Underemployment can hide economic hardship.

unemployment

IPA: /ˌʌnɪmˈplɔɪmənt/
Pronunciation: un-im-PLOY-muhnt
French: chômage

Definition: The state of being without a job while seeking work.

Example: Unemployment is a major cause of poverty.

long-term unemployment

IPA: /ˌlɒŋ tɜːm ˌʌnɪmˈplɔɪmənt/
Pronunciation: long-term un-im-PLOY-muhnt
French: chômage de longue durée

Definition: Unemployment lasting for an extended period.

Example: Long-term unemployment can erode skills and confidence.

austerity

IPA: /ɒˈsterəti/
Pronunciation: aw-STAIR-uh-tee
French: austérité

Definition: Policies aimed at reducing public spending, often through cuts to services and benefits.

Example: Austerity policies increased pressure on public services.

public spending

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk ˈspendɪŋ/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik SPEN-ding
French: dépenses publiques

Definition: Money spent by government on services, benefits and infrastructure.

Example: Public spending choices shape inequality.

spending cuts

IPA: /ˈspendɪŋ kʌts/
Pronunciation: SPEN-ding kuts
French: coupes budgétaires

Definition: Reductions in government spending.

Example: Spending cuts can hit the poorest communities hardest.

taxation

IPA: /tækˈseɪʃən/
Pronunciation: tak-SAY-shuhn
French: fiscalité ; imposition

Definition: The system of collecting money from people or companies to fund the state.

Example: Taxation is central to redistribution.

progressive taxation

IPA: /prəˌɡresɪv tækˈseɪʃən/
Pronunciation: pruh-GRESS-iv tak-SAY-shuhn
French: impôt progressif

Definition: A tax system where richer people pay a higher share of income.

Example: Progressive taxation can reduce inequality.

regressive tax

IPA: /rɪˌɡresɪv ˈtæks/
Pronunciation: ri-GRESS-iv taks
French: impôt régressif

Definition: A tax that takes a larger share of income from poorer people.

Example: Sales taxes can be regressive.

tax avoidance

IPA: /ˈtæks əˌvɔɪdəns/
Pronunciation: taks uh-VOID-uhns
French: évitement fiscal

Definition: Legal methods used to reduce tax payments.

Example: Tax avoidance by the wealthy can undermine public trust.

tax evasion

IPA: /ˈtæks ɪˌveɪʒən/
Pronunciation: taks i-VAY-zhuhn
French: fraude fiscale

Definition: Illegal non-payment or underpayment of taxes.

Example: Tax evasion reduces money available for public services.

tax haven

IPA: /ˈtæks ˌheɪvən/
Pronunciation: taks HAY-vuhn
French: paradis fiscal

Definition: A place with very low taxes used to reduce tax bills.

Example: Tax havens allow wealth to be hidden offshore.

E. Policy, structural inequality and high-value verbs

redistribution

IPA: /ˌriːdɪstrɪˈbjuːʃən/
Pronunciation: ree-dis-tri-BYOO-shuhn
French: redistribution

Definition: The transfer of resources through taxes and public spending.

Example: Redistribution can reduce poverty and inequality.

universal basic income

IPA: /ˌjuːnɪvɜːsəl ˌbeɪsɪk ˈɪnkʌm/
Pronunciation: yoo-ni-VUR-suhl BAY-sik IN-kum
French: revenu universel de base

Definition: A regular payment given to all citizens regardless of work or income.

Example: Universal basic income is proposed as a response to insecurity.

wealth tax

IPA: /ˈwelθ tæks/
Pronunciation: welth taks
French: impôt sur la fortune ; taxe sur le patrimoine

Definition: A tax on accumulated wealth rather than income.

Example: A wealth tax is often proposed to reduce inequality.

inheritance tax

IPA: /ɪnˈherɪtəns tæks/
Pronunciation: in-HER-i-tuhns taks
French: droits de succession

Definition: A tax on wealth passed on after death.

Example: Inheritance tax is politically sensitive because it affects family wealth.

public services

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk ˈsɜːvɪsɪz/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik SUR-vis-iz
French: services publics

Definition: Services provided or funded by the state, such as health, education and transport.

Example: Strong public services can reduce inequality.

health inequality

IPA: /ˈhelθ ˌɪnɪˈkwɒləti/
Pronunciation: helth in-i-KWOL-uh-tee
French: inégalité de santé

Definition: Unequal health outcomes between groups.

Example: Health inequality is strongly linked to poverty.

educational inequality

IPA: /ˌedjʊˈkeɪʃənəl ˌɪnɪˈkwɒləti/
Pronunciation: ej-yoo-KAY-shuh-nuhl in-i-KWOL-uh-tee
French: inégalité éducative

Definition: Unequal access to quality education or unequal educational outcomes.

Example: Educational inequality reproduces class divisions.

digital divide

IPA: /ˌdɪdʒɪtəl dɪˈvaɪd/
Pronunciation: DIJ-i-tuhl di-VYDE
French: fracture numérique

Definition: Unequal access to internet, devices and digital skills.

Example: The digital divide worsens educational inequality.

intersectionality

IPA: /ˌɪntəsekʃəˈnæləti/
Pronunciation: IN-tuh-sek-shuh-NAL-uh-tee
French: intersectionnalité

Definition: The way different forms of inequality combine, such as class, race and gender.

Example: Intersectionality helps explain why poverty affects groups differently.

structural inequality

IPA: /ˌstrʌktʃərəl ˌɪnɪˈkwɒləti/
Pronunciation: STRUK-chuh-ruhl in-i-KWOL-uh-tee
French: inégalité structurelle

Definition: Inequality produced by institutions, policies and social structures.

Example: Structural inequality cannot be solved by individual effort alone.

systemic disadvantage

IPA: /sɪˌstemɪk ˌdɪsədˈvɑːntɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: si-STEM-ik dis-ad-VAN-tij
French: désavantage systémique

Definition: Disadvantage built into systems and institutions.

Example: Systemic disadvantage limits social mobility.

discrimination

IPA: /dɪˌskrɪmɪˈneɪʃən/
Pronunciation: dis-krim-i-NAY-shuhn
French: discrimination

Definition: Unfair treatment based on identity, background or status.

Example: Discrimination can deepen economic inequality.

social mobility

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl məʊˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl moh-BIL-uh-tee
French: mobilité sociale

Definition: Movement between social classes or positions.

Example: High inequality often reduces social mobility.

to live in poverty

IPA: /lɪv ɪn ˈpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: liv in POV-uh-tee
French: vivre dans la pauvreté

Definition: To lack enough resources for basic needs.

Example: Millions of children live in poverty.

IPA: /fɔːl ˈɪntuː ˈpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: fall IN-too POV-uh-tee
French: tomber dans la pauvreté

Definition: To become poor after a change such as job loss or illness.

Example: A sudden rent increase can make families fall into poverty.

to lift people out of poverty

IPA: /lɪft ˈpiːpəl aʊt əv ˈpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: lift PEE-puhl owt ov POV-uh-tee
French: sortir les gens de la pauvreté

Definition: To help people escape poverty.

Example: Child benefits can lift families out of poverty.

to tackle poverty

IPA: /ˈtækəl ˈpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: TAK-uhl POV-uh-tee
French: lutter contre la pauvreté

Definition: To take action to reduce poverty.

Example: Governments must tackle poverty with housing, wages and services.

to reduce inequality

IPA: /rɪˈdjuːs ˌɪnɪˈkwɒləti/
Pronunciation: ri-DYOOSS in-i-KWOL-uh-tee
French: réduire les inégalités

Definition: To make inequality smaller.

Example: Progressive taxation can reduce inequality.

Pronunciation: WY-duhn in-i-KWOL-uh-tee
French: creuser les inégalités

Example: Austerity can widen inequality.

to bridge the gap

IPA: /brɪdʒ ðə ɡæp/
Pronunciation: brij thuh gap
French: combler l’écart

Definition: To reduce a difference between groups.

Example: Better school funding can bridge the gap.

to widen the gap

IPA: /ˈwaɪdən ðə ɡæp/

French: creuser l’écart

Example: More families now rely on food banks.

to be priced out

French: être exclu par les prix

Definition: To be unable to afford something because prices have become too high.

French: piéger les gens dans la pauvreté

French: renforcer le filet de sécurité sociale

Example: A crisis should be a reason to strengthen the safety net.

to redistribute wealth

IPA: /ˌriːdɪˈstrɪbjuːt welθ/
Pronunciation: ree-dis-TRIB-yoot welth
French: redistribuer les richesses

to address root causes

Example: Poverty policy must address root causes, not just symptoms.
