Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse cybersecurity: cyberattacks, ransomware, phishing, vulnerabilities, authentication, encryption, incident response, cyber resilience, critical infrastructure, cybercrime, state-sponsored attacks, cyber espionage, digital sovereignty and the balance between security, privacy and international stability.

A. Core cybersecurity concepts and threat actors

cybersecurity

IPA: /ˌsaɪbə sɪˈkjʊərəti/
Pronunciation: SY-buh si-KYOOR-uh-tee
French: cybersécurité

Definition: The protection of computer systems, networks, devices and data from digital attacks or misuse.

Example: Cybersecurity is now a national security and business priority.

cybersecurity threat

IPA: /ˌsaɪbə sɪˈkjʊərəti θret/
Pronunciation: SY-buh si-KYOOR-uh-tee thret
French: menace de cybersécurité

Definition: A danger to digital systems, networks or data.

Example: Ransomware is a major cybersecurity threat.

cyber threat

IPA: /ˈsaɪbə θret/
Pronunciation: SY-buh thret
French: cybermenace

Definition: A possible malicious action targeting digital systems.

Example: Cyber threats can come from criminals, states or insiders.

cyber risk

IPA: /ˈsaɪbə rɪsk/
Pronunciation: SY-buh risk
French: cyber-risque

Definition: The likelihood and impact of harm caused by cyber incidents.

Example: Companies must assess cyber risk before launching new services.

cyberattack

IPA: /ˈsaɪbərətæk/
Pronunciation: SY-buh-uh-tak
French: cyberattaque

Definition: A malicious attempt to damage, disrupt or gain unauthorised access to digital systems.

Example: A cyberattack can paralyse hospitals, banks or public services.

cyber incident

IPA: /ˈsaɪbər ˌɪnsɪdənt/
Pronunciation: SY-buh IN-si-duhnt
French: incident cyber

Definition: An event that threatens the confidentiality, integrity or availability of systems or data.

Example: A cyber incident must be investigated quickly.

data breach

IPA: /ˈdeɪtə briːtʃ/
Pronunciation: DAY-tuh breech
French: violation de données

Definition: Unauthorised access to or exposure of personal or confidential data.

Example: A data breach can damage trust and trigger fines.

security breach

IPA: /sɪˈkjʊərəti briːtʃ/
Pronunciation: si-KYOOR-uh-tee breech
French: faille de sécurité

Definition: A failure allowing unauthorised access to a system or network.

Example: The security breach exposed customer records.

vulnerability

IPA: /ˌvʌlnərəˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: vul-nuh-ruh-BIL-uh-tee
French: vulnérabilité

Definition: A weakness in software, hardware, processes or behaviour that attackers can exploit.

Example: Unpatched software can create a vulnerability.

exploit

IPA: /ˈeksplɔɪt/
Pronunciation: EK-sployt
French: exploit

Definition: Code or a technique used to take advantage of a vulnerability.

Example: Hackers used an exploit to access the system.

zero-day vulnerability

IPA: /ˌzɪərəʊ deɪ ˌvʌlnərəˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: ZEER-oh-day vul-nuh-ruh-BIL-uh-tee
French: vulnérabilité zero-day

Definition: A vulnerability unknown to the vendor and therefore not yet patched.

Example: Zero-day vulnerabilities are valuable to criminal groups and intelligence agencies.

zero-day exploit

IPA: /ˌzɪərəʊ deɪ ˈeksplɔɪt/
Pronunciation: ZEER-oh-day EK-sployt
French: exploit zero-day

Definition: An exploit targeting a vulnerability before a fix is available.

Example: A zero-day exploit can be extremely dangerous.

patch

IPA: /pætʃ/
Pronunciation: patch
French: correctif ; patch

Definition: A software update fixing a bug or vulnerability.

Example: Installing a security patch can prevent attacks.

software update

IPA: /ˈsɒftweər ˌʌpdeɪt/
Pronunciation: SOFT-wair UP-dayt
French: mise à jour logicielle

Definition: A new version of software that improves function or security.

Example: Regular software updates reduce security risks.

patch management

IPA: /ˈpætʃ ˌmænɪdʒmənt/
Pronunciation: PATCH MAN-ij-muhnt
French: gestion des correctifs

Definition: The process of identifying, testing and installing software patches.

Example: Patch management is essential for large organisations.

attack surface

IPA: /əˈtæk ˌsɜːfɪs/
Pronunciation: uh-TAK SUR-fis
French: surface d’attaque

Definition: All the possible points where an attacker could enter or affect a system.

Example: Remote work expanded the attack surface.

threat actor

IPA: /ˈθret ˌæktə/
Pronunciation: thret AK-tuh
French: acteur malveillant ; acteur de menace

Definition: A person, group or state responsible for a cyber threat or attack.

Example: Threat actors may be financially or politically motivated.

malicious actor

IPA: /məˈlɪʃəs ˈæktə/
Pronunciation: muh-LISH-uhs AK-tuh
French: acteur malveillant

Definition: A person or group acting with harmful intent.

Example: Malicious actors target weak passwords.

hacker

IPA: /ˈhækə/
Pronunciation: HAK-uh
French: hacker ; pirate informatique

Definition: A person skilled at using computers, sometimes legally, sometimes maliciously.

Example: Not every hacker is a criminal.

ethical hacker

IPA: /ˌeθɪkəl ˈhækə/
Pronunciation: ETH-i-kuhl HAK-uh
French: hacker éthique

Definition: A security professional authorised to find vulnerabilities.

Example: Ethical hackers help organisations improve security.

black-hat hacker

IPA: /ˌblæk hæt ˈhækə/
Pronunciation: black-hat HAK-uh
French: hacker malveillant

Definition: A hacker who breaks into systems illegally or maliciously.

Example: Black-hat hackers may steal data or money.

white-hat hacker

IPA: /ˌwaɪt hæt ˈhækə/
Pronunciation: white-hat HAK-uh
French: hacker éthique

Definition: A hacker who works legally to improve security.

Example: White-hat hackers report vulnerabilities responsibly.

insider threat

IPA: /ˌɪnˈsaɪdə θret/
Pronunciation: in-SY-duh thret
French: menace interne

Definition: A security risk caused by someone inside an organisation.

Example: An insider threat may be malicious or accidental.

cybercriminal

IPA: /ˌsaɪbəˈkrɪmɪnəl/
Pronunciation: SY-buh-KRIM-i-nuhl
French: cybercriminel

Definition: A person who commits crimes using digital tools.

Example: Cybercriminals often target weakly protected organisations.

cybercrime

IPA: /ˈsaɪbəkraɪm/
Pronunciation: SY-buh-kryme
French: cybercriminalité

Definition: Crime committed through or against computer systems.

Example: Cybercrime has become highly professionalised.

organised cybercrime

IPA: /ˌɔːɡənaɪzd ˈsaɪbəkraɪm/
Pronunciation: OR-guh-nyzd SY-buh-kryme
French: cybercriminalité organisée

Definition: Cybercrime carried out by structured criminal groups.

Example: Organised cybercrime can operate like a business.

ransomware

IPA: /ˈrænsəmweə/
Pronunciation: RAN-suhm-wair
French: rançongiciel

Definition: Malware that encrypts or blocks data until a ransom is paid.

Example: Ransomware attacks can shut down hospitals and schools.

B. Malware, phishing, identity theft and authentication

ransom

IPA: /ˈrænsəm/
Pronunciation: RAN-suhm
French: rançon

Definition: Money demanded to restore access or stop harm.

Example: The attackers demanded a ransom in cryptocurrency.

malware

IPA: /ˈmælweə/
Pronunciation: MAL-wair
French: logiciel malveillant

Definition: Malicious software designed to damage, disrupt or access systems.

Example: Malware can steal data or spy on users.

spyware

IPA: /ˈspaɪweə/
Pronunciation: SPY-wair
French: logiciel espion

Definition: Malware designed to secretly monitor activity or collect data.

Example: Spyware can turn a phone into a surveillance tool.

trojan horse

IPA: /ˌtrəʊdʒən ˈhɔːs/
Pronunciation: TROH-juhn horse
French: cheval de Troie

Definition: Malware disguised as legitimate software.

Example: A trojan horse may look like a normal app.

worm

IPA: /wɜːm/
Pronunciation: wurm
French: ver informatique

Definition: Malware that spreads automatically across networks.

Example: A worm can infect thousands of machines quickly.

virus

IPA: /ˈvaɪrəs/
Pronunciation: VY-ruhs
French: virus informatique

Definition: Malware that attaches to files or programs and spreads when executed.

Example: A virus can corrupt files.

botnet

IPA: /ˈbɒtnet/
Pronunciation: BOT-net
French: réseau de bots ; botnet

Definition: A network of infected devices controlled by an attacker.

Example: A botnet can launch massive attacks.

phishing

IPA: /ˈfɪʃɪŋ/
Pronunciation: FISH-ing
French: hameçonnage ; phishing

Definition: Fraudulent messages designed to trick people into revealing information or clicking links.

Example: Phishing remains one of the most common attack methods.

spear phishing

IPA: /ˈspɪə ˌfɪʃɪŋ/
Pronunciation: SPEER FISH-ing
French: hameçonnage ciblé

Definition: Phishing targeted at a specific person or organisation.

Example: Spear phishing often uses personalised information.

whaling

IPA: /ˈweɪlɪŋ/
Pronunciation: WAY-ling
French: hameçonnage de cadres dirigeants

Definition: Phishing targeting senior executives or important figures.

Example: Whaling attacks may target CEOs or finance directors.

social engineering

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl ˌendʒɪˈnɪərɪŋ/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl en-ji-NEER-ing
French: ingénierie sociale

Definition: Manipulating people into revealing information or taking unsafe actions.

Example: Social engineering exploits trust and urgency.

scam

IPA: /skæm/
Pronunciation: skam
French: arnaque

Definition: A dishonest scheme to steal money or information.

Example: Online scams often imitate trusted brands.

fraud

IPA: /frɔːd/
Pronunciation: fraud
French: fraude

Definition: Deliberate deception for financial or personal gain.

Example: Cyber fraud can target bank accounts and identities.

identity theft

IPA: /aɪˈdentəti θeft/
Pronunciation: eye-DEN-tuh-tee theft
French: usurpation d’identité

Definition: Using someone’s personal information fraudulently.

Example: Identity theft can follow a data breach.

IPA: /krɪˈdenʃəl θeft/
Pronunciation: kri-DEN-shuhl theft
French: vol d’identifiants

Definition: Stealing usernames, passwords or authentication details.

Example: Credential theft allows attackers to access accounts.

password

IPA: /ˈpɑːswɜːd/
Pronunciation: PASS-word
French: mot de passe

Definition: A secret word or phrase used to access an account.

Example: Weak passwords are easy to guess.

IPA: /ˈpɑːsfreɪz/
Pronunciation: PASS-frayz
French: phrase de passe

Definition: A longer phrase used as a password.

Example: A passphrase can be stronger and easier to remember.

IPA: /ˈpɑːswɜːd ˌmænɪdʒə/
Pronunciation: PASS-word MAN-ij-uh
French: gestionnaire de mots de passe

Definition: A tool that stores and generates secure passwords.

Example: A password manager helps users avoid reusing passwords.

IPA: /ˌtuː ef ˈeɪ/

French: 2FA

Definition: Abbreviation for two-factor authentication.
