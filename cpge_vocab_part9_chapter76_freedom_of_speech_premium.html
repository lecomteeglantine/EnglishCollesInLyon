Essential vocabulary for khôlles, essays, press reviews and competitive exams.

Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse freedom of speech: civil liberties, censorship, hate speech, offensive speech, academic freedom, cancel culture, platform moderation, whistleblowing, press freedom, national security, public order and the democratic tension between liberty, harm, dignity and responsibility.

How to use this chapter

For press articlesFocus on freedom of expression, censorship, hate speech, chilling effect, academic freedom, no-platforming, content moderation, press freedom and public interest defence.
For oral examsUse this vocabulary to explain free speech as a democratic right that becomes difficult when expression harms dignity, security, equality or public order.
For essaysMemorise: to defend free speech, to restrict speech, to draw the line, to foster open debate, to balance rights and responsibilities.

A. Core free speech and democratic principles

freedom of speech

IPA: /ˌfriːdəm əv ˈspiːtʃ/
Pronunciation: FREE-duhm ov SPEECH
French: liberté d’expression

Definition: The right to express opinions and ideas without undue censorship or punishment.

Example: Freedom of speech is a cornerstone of liberal democracy.

free speech

IPA: /ˌfriː ˈspiːtʃ/
Pronunciation: free speech
French: liberté d’expression

Definition: Short form for freedom of speech.

Example: Free speech debates often intensify on university campuses.

freedom of expression

IPA: /ˌfriːdəm əv ɪkˈspreʃən/
Pronunciation: FREE-duhm ov ik-SPRESH-uhn
French: liberté d’expression

Definition: The right to express ideas, opinions, beliefs and information.

Example: Freedom of expression protects unpopular views as well as popular ones.

civil liberty

IPA: /ˌsɪvəl ˈlɪbəti/
Pronunciation: SIV-uhl LIB-uh-tee
French: liberté civile

Definition: A basic freedom protected from excessive government interference.

Example: Free speech is a civil liberty.

civil liberties

IPA: /ˌsɪvəl ˈlɪbətiz/
Pronunciation: SIV-uhl LIB-uh-teez
French: libertés civiles

Definition: Basic freedoms such as speech, assembly, religion and privacy.

Example: Civil liberties can be restricted during crises.

constitutional right

IPA: /ˌkɒnstɪˈtjuːʃənəl raɪt/
Pronunciation: kon-sti-TYOO-shuhn-uhl right
French: droit constitutionnel

Definition: A right protected by a constitution.

Example: Freedom of speech is a constitutional right in many democracies.

fundamental right

IPA: /ˌfʌndəˈmentəl raɪt/
Pronunciation: fun-duh-MEN-tuhl right
French: droit fondamental

Definition: A basic right considered essential to human dignity or democracy.

Example: Freedom of expression is often treated as a fundamental right.

human right

IPA: /ˌhjuːmən ˈraɪt/
Pronunciation: HYOO-muhn right
French: droit humain

Definition: A basic right belonging to all people.

Example: Freedom of expression is recognised as a human right.

First Amendment

IPA: /ˌfɜːst əˈmendmənt/
Pronunciation: first uh-MEND-muhnt
French: Premier amendement

Definition: The part of the US Constitution protecting speech, religion, press, assembly and petition.

Example: The First Amendment strongly protects political speech.

Article 10

IPA: /ˌɑːtɪkəl ˈten/
Pronunciation: AR-ti-kuhl ten
French: article 10

Definition: Article of the European Convention on Human Rights protecting freedom of expression.

Example: Article 10 allows restrictions when they are necessary and proportionate.

public debate

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk dɪˈbeɪt/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik di-BAYT
French: débat public

Definition: Open discussion of issues that matter to society.

Example: Free speech enables public debate.

democratic debate

IPA: /ˌdeməˈkrætɪk dɪˈbeɪt/
Pronunciation: dem-uh-KRAT-ik di-BAYT
French: débat démocratique

Definition: Discussion necessary for citizens to make democratic choices.

Example: Censorship can impoverish democratic debate.

marketplace of ideas

IPA: /ˈmɑːkɪtpleɪs əv aɪˈdɪəz/
Pronunciation: MAR-kit-place ov eye-DEE-uhz
French: marché des idées

Definition: The idea that truth emerges from open competition between ideas.

Example: The marketplace of ideas is a classic defence of free speech.

pluralism

IPA: /ˈplʊərəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: PLOOR-uh-liz-uhm
French: pluralisme

Definition: The coexistence of diverse opinions, beliefs and groups.

Example: Pluralism is essential to democratic life.

dissent

IPA: /dɪˈsent/
Pronunciation: di-SENT
French: dissidence ; désaccord

Definition: Public disagreement with official or dominant views.

Example: Free societies must protect dissent.

dissenter

IPA: /dɪˈsentə/
Pronunciation: di-SEN-tuh
French: dissident

Definition: A person who publicly disagrees with an official or dominant view.

Example: Dissenters often test the limits of free speech.

minority opinion

IPA: /maɪˌnɒrəti əˈpɪnjən/
Pronunciation: my-NOR-uh-tee uh-PIN-yuhn
French: opinion minoritaire

Definition: A view held by a smaller group of people.

Example: Minority opinions need protection from majority pressure.

unpopular opinion

IPA: /ʌnˌpɒpjʊlər əˈpɪnjən/
Pronunciation: un-POP-yuh-luh uh-PIN-yuhn
French: opinion impopulaire

Definition: A view many people dislike or reject.

Example: Free speech matters most when opinions are unpopular.

offensive speech

IPA: /əˌfensɪv ˈspiːtʃ/
Pronunciation: uh-FEN-siv speech
French: propos offensants

Definition: Speech that insults, shocks or upsets people.

Example: Offensive speech is not always illegal.

controversial speech

IPA: /ˌkɒntrəˈvɜːʃəl ˈspiːtʃ/
Pronunciation: kon-truh-VUR-shuhl speech
French: propos controversés

Definition: Speech that causes strong disagreement.

Example: Universities often host controversial speech.

political speech

IPA: /pəˌlɪtɪkəl ˈspiːtʃ/
Pronunciation: puh-LIT-i-kuhl speech
French: discours politique

Definition: Speech about politics, government or public affairs.

Example: Political speech receives strong protection.

artistic expression

IPA: /ɑːˌtɪstɪk ɪkˈspreʃən/
Pronunciation: ar-TIS-tik ik-SPRESH-uhn
French: expression artistique

Definition: Expression through art, literature, music, theatre or film.

Example: Artistic expression may provoke controversy.

religious expression

IPA: /rɪˌlɪdʒəs ɪkˈspreʃən/
Pronunciation: ri-LIJ-uhs ik-SPRESH-uhn
French: expression religieuse

Definition: Expression of religious belief or identity.

Example: Religious expression may conflict with secular norms.

academic freedom

IPA: /ˌækəˈdemɪk ˈfriːdəm/
Pronunciation: ak-uh-DEM-ik FREE-duhm
French: liberté académique

Definition: The freedom of scholars to teach, research and debate without undue interference.

Example: Academic freedom protects controversial research.

campus speech

IPA: /ˈkæmpəs spiːtʃ/
Pronunciation: KAM-puhs speech
French: expression sur les campus

Definition: Speech and debate taking place on university campuses.

Example: Campus speech has become politically contentious.

no-platforming

IPA: /ˌnəʊ ˈplætfɔːmɪŋ/
Pronunciation: noh-PLAT-form-ing
French: refus de tribune ; déplateformisation

Definition: Refusing to give someone a platform to speak because their views are considered harmful.

Example: No-platforming is defended by some and criticised by others.

deplatforming

IPA: /ˌdiːˈplætfɔːmɪŋ/
Pronunciation: dee-PLAT-form-ing
French: déplateformisation

Definition: Removing someone from a platform or denying them access to an audience.

Example: Deplatforming can reduce extremist reach but raises free speech concerns.

cancel culture

IPA: /ˈkænsəl ˌkʌltʃə/
Pronunciation: KAN-suhl KUL-chuh
French: cancel culture ; culture de l’annulation

Definition: A contested term for public pressure to punish people for offensive or controversial behaviour.

Example: Cancel culture is often used as a political weapon in debates.

public shaming

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk ˈʃeɪmɪŋ/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik SHAY-ming
French: humiliation publique

Definition: Public criticism or humiliation of someone for perceived wrongdoing.

Example: Public shaming can silence people.

online mob

IPA: /ˌɒnlaɪn ˈmɒb/
Pronunciation: ON-lyne mob
French: foule en ligne ; meute numérique

Definition: A large group attacking someone online.

Example: An online mob can destroy reputations quickly.

B. Censorship, harm, hate speech and legal limits

pile-on

IPA: /ˈpaɪl ɒn/
Pronunciation: PYLE-on
French: déferlement de critiques

Definition: A wave of criticism or abuse directed at someone online.

Example: A pile-on can intimidate ordinary users.

chilling effect

IPA: /ˈtʃɪlɪŋ ɪˌfekt/
Pronunciation: CHIL-ing i-FEKT
French: effet dissuasif

Definition: A situation where people avoid lawful expression because of fear of consequences.

Example: Fear of backlash can create a chilling effect.

self-censorship

IPA: /ˌself ˈsensəʃɪp/
Pronunciation: self SEN-suh-ship
French: autocensure

Definition: Avoiding expression because of fear, pressure or social consequences.

Example: Self-censorship can be invisible but powerful.

censorship

IPA: /ˈsensəʃɪp/
Pronunciation: SEN-suh-ship
French: censure

Definition: Suppression or control of speech, information or art.

Example: Censorship can be imposed by states or institutions.

state censorship

IPA: /ˌsteɪt ˈsensəʃɪp/
Pronunciation: state SEN-suh-ship
French: censure d’État

Definition: Censorship imposed by government authorities.

Example: State censorship is common in authoritarian regimes.

speech restriction

IPA: /ˈspiːtʃ rɪˌstrɪkʃən/
Pronunciation: speech ri-STRIK-shuhn
French: restriction de la parole

Definition: A rule limiting what can be said or expressed.

Example: Speech restrictions must be justified carefully.

proportionate restriction

IPA: /prəˈpɔːʃənət rɪˌstrɪkʃən/
Pronunciation: pruh-POR-shuh-nuht ri-STRIK-shuhn
French: restriction proportionnée

Definition: A restriction that is limited and appropriate to the harm prevented.

Example: Human rights law often requires proportionate restrictions.

legitimate aim

IPA: /lɪˌdʒɪtɪmət ˈeɪm/
Pronunciation: li-JIT-i-muht aim
French: objectif légitime

Definition: A valid public interest reason for limiting a right.

Example: Protecting national security can be a legitimate aim.

public order

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk ˈɔːdə/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik OR-duh
French: ordre public

Definition: Social peace and safety in public spaces.

Example: Public order is often invoked to limit demonstrations.

national security

IPA: /ˌnæʃənəl sɪˈkjʊərəti/
Pronunciation: NASH-uh-nuhl si-KYOOR-uh-tee
French: sécurité nationale

Definition: The protection of a country from threats.

Example: National security can justify some secrecy.

incitement

IPA: /ɪnˈsaɪtmənt/
Pronunciation: in-SYTE-muhnt
French: incitation

Definition: Encouraging others to commit violence, hatred or illegal acts.

Example: Incitement to violence is not protected in many legal systems.

incitement to violence

IPA: /ɪnˈsaɪtmənt tə ˈvaɪələns/
Pronunciation: in-SYTE-muhnt tuh VY-uh-luhns
French: incitation à la violence

Definition: Speech encouraging people to commit violent acts.

Example: Incitement to violence can be criminalised.

hate speech

IPA: /ˈheɪt spiːtʃ/
Pronunciation: hayt speech
French: discours de haine

Definition: Speech attacking people because of identity such as race, religion, gender or sexuality.

Example: Hate speech laws differ sharply between the US and Europe.

hateful rhetoric

IPA: /ˌheɪtfəl ˈretərɪk/
Pronunciation: HAYT-fuhl RET-uh-rik
French: rhétorique haineuse

Definition: Language expressing or encouraging hostility towards a group.

Example: Hateful rhetoric can normalise discrimination.

harassment

IPA: /ˈhærəsmənt/
Pronunciation: HARR-uhs-muhnt
French: harcèlement

Definition: Repeated behaviour intended to intimidate, humiliate or distress someone.

Example: Online harassment can silence women and minorities.

online harassment

IPA: /ˌɒnlaɪn ˈhærəsmənt/
Pronunciation: ON-lyne HARR-uhs-muhnt
French: harcèlement en ligne

Definition: Harassment occurring through digital platforms.

Example: Online harassment is a major free speech issue.

threat

IPA: /θret/
Pronunciation: thret
French: menace

Definition: A statement or action showing intent to harm someone.

Example: True threats may be punished by law.

defamation

IPA: /ˌdefəˈmeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: def-uh-MAY-shuhn
French: diffamation

Definition: False statements damaging someone’s reputation.

Example: Defamation law can protect reputations but chill journalism.

libel

IPA: /ˈlaɪbəl/
Pronunciation: LY-buhl
French: diffamation écrite

Definition: Written defamation.

Example: The newspaper faced a libel lawsuit.

privacy

IPA: /ˈprɪvəsi/
Pronunciation: PRIV-uh-see
French: vie privée

Definition: The right to keep personal life and information private.

Example: Privacy may conflict with press freedom.

blasphemy

IPA: /ˈblæsfəmi/
Pronunciation: BLAS-fuh-mee
French: blasphème

Definition: Speech or action considered insulting to religion or sacred things.

Example: Blasphemy remains a sensitive issue in free speech debates.

satire

IPA: /ˈsætaɪə/
Pronunciation: SAT-eye-uh
French: satire

Definition: Humour or irony used to criticise people, politics or society.

Example: Satire often pushes the boundaries of acceptable speech.

parody

IPA: /ˈpærədi/
Pronunciation: PARR-uh-dee
French: parodie

Definition: Imitation for humorous or critical effect.

Example: Parody is often protected as expression.

political cartoon

IPA: /pəˌlɪtɪkəl kɑːˈtuːn/
Pronunciation: puh-LIT-i-kuhl kar-TOON
French: dessin politique

Definition: A cartoon commenting on politics or public affairs.

Example: Political cartoons are a classic form of free expression.

offence

IPA: /əˈfens/
Pronunciation: uh-FENS
French: offense

Definition: The feeling of being insulted, upset or outraged.

Example: A right not to be offended would severely limit debate.

harm

IPA: /hɑːm/
Pronunciation: harm
French: préjudice ; tort

Definition: Damage or injury caused to a person or group.

Example: Free speech debates often distinguish offence from harm.

harm principle

IPA: /ˈhɑːm ˌprɪnsəpəl/
Pronunciation: HARM PRIN-suh-puhl
French: principe de non-nuisance

Definition: The idea that liberty should be limited only to prevent harm to others.

Example: The harm principle is often used to defend broad free speech.

dignity

IPA: /ˈdɪɡnəti/
Pronunciation: DIG-nuh-tee
French: dignité

Definition: The inherent worth and respect owed to every person.

Example: Hate speech laws often invoke human dignity.

equality

IPA: /ɪˈkwɒləti/
Pronunciation: i-KWOL-uh-tee
French: égalité

Definition: The principle that people should have equal rights and respect.

Example: Equality can sometimes conflict with unrestricted speech.

tolerance

IPA: /ˈtɒlərəns/
Pronunciation: TOL-uh-ruhns
French: tolérance

Definition: Willingness to accept views or behaviour one dislikes.

Example: Free speech requires tolerance of disagreement.

safe space

IPA: /ˈseɪf speɪs/
Pronunciation: sayf spayss
French: espace sûr

Definition: A place intended to protect people from hostility or discrimination.

Example: Critics argue that safe spaces can reduce open debate.

trigger warning

IPA: /ˈtrɪɡə ˌwɔːnɪŋ/
Pronunciation: TRIG-uh WOR-ning
French: avertissement de contenu sensible

Definition: A notice warning that content may be distressing.

Example: Trigger warnings are debated in universities.

content moderation

IPA: /ˈkɒntent ˌmɒdəˈreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: KON-tent mod-uh-RAY-shuhn
French: modération des contenus

Definition: Rules and actions used by platforms to manage user content.

Example: Content moderation raises free speech questions.

community guidelines

IPA: /kəˌmjuːnəti ˈɡaɪdlaɪnz/
Pronunciation: kuh-MYOO-nuh-tee GYDE-lynes
French: règles de communauté

Definition: Rules set by platforms for acceptable user behaviour.

Example: Community guidelines define what users may post.

terms of service

IPA: /ˌtɜːmz əv ˈsɜːvɪs/
Pronunciation: turmz ov SUR-vis
French: conditions d’utilisation

Definition: Rules users accept when using a platform.

Example: Terms of service can restrict speech beyond the law.

C. Online speech, platforms and moderation

content removal

IPA: /ˈkɒntent rɪˌmuːvəl/
Pronunciation: KON-tent ri-MOO-vuhl
French: suppression de contenu

Definition: Taking down online content.

Example: Content removal can reduce harm but fuel censorship claims.

account suspension

IPA: /əˈkaʊnt səˌspenʃən/
Pronunciation: uh-KOWNT suh-SPEN-shuhn
French: suspension de compte

Definition: Temporary blocking of a user account.

Example: Account suspension is a common moderation tool.

permanent ban

IPA: /ˌpɜːmənənt ˈbæn/
Pronunciation: PUR-muh-nuhnt ban
French: bannissement définitif

Definition: A lasting ban from a platform or space.

Example: A permanent ban can be controversial for public figures.

shadow banning

IPA: /ˈʃædəʊ ˌbænɪŋ/
Pronunciation: SHAD-oh BAN-ing
French: bannissement furtif

Definition: Reducing visibility without clearly notifying the user.

Example: Shadow banning is hard to prove.

algorithmic moderation

IPA: /ˌælɡəˈrɪðmɪk ˌmɒdəˈreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: al-guh-RITH-mik mod-uh-RAY-shuhn
French: modération algorithmique

Definition: Automated systems used to detect or limit content.

Example: Algorithmic moderation can make serious mistakes.

platform accountability

IPA: /ˈplætfɔːm əˌkaʊntəˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: PLAT-form uh-kown-tuh-BIL-uh-tee
French: responsabilité des plateformes

Definition: Responsibility of platforms for speech-related harms and decisions.

Example: Platform accountability is central to online speech debates.

digital rights

IPA: /ˌdɪdʒɪtəl ˈraɪts/
Pronunciation: DIJ-i-tuhl rights
French: droits numériques

Definition: Rights related to internet access, privacy and online expression.

Example: Digital rights groups monitor online censorship.

whistleblower

IPA: /ˈwɪsəlˌbləʊə/
Pronunciation: WIS-uhl-bloh-uh
French: lanceur d’alerte

Definition: A person who exposes wrongdoing inside an organisation.

Example: Whistleblowers often need legal protection.

public interest disclosure

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk ˈɪntrəst dɪsˌkləʊʒə/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik IN-truhst dis-KLOH-zhuh
French: divulgation d’intérêt public

Definition: Revealing information because it serves the public interest.

Example: Public interest disclosure can expose corruption.

classified information

IPA: /ˌklæsɪfaɪd ˌɪnfəˈmeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: KLAS-i-fyd in-fuh-MAY-shuhn
French: information classifiée

Definition: Official information kept secret for security reasons.

Example: Publishing classified information can create legal risks.

press freedom

IPA: /ˈpres ˌfriːdəm/
Pronunciation: press FREE-duhm
French: liberté de la presse

Definition: The freedom of journalists and media organisations to report news.

Example: Press freedom protects democratic scrutiny.

source protection

IPA: /ˈsɔːs prəˌtekʃən/
Pronunciation: sorss pruh-TEK-shuhn
French: protection des sources

Definition: Keeping journalistic sources confidential and safe.

Example: Source protection is essential for investigative journalism.

public interest defence

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk ˈɪntrəst dɪˌfens/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik IN-truhst di-FENS
French: défense fondée sur l’intérêt public

Definition: An argument that publication was justified because it served society.

Example: The public interest defence is vital in investigative reporting.

extremist speech

IPA: /ɪkˈstriːmɪst ˈspiːtʃ/
Pronunciation: ik-STREE-mist speech
French: discours extrémiste

Definition: Speech promoting extreme political or religious views, sometimes violence.

Example: Extremist speech creates hard moderation dilemmas.
