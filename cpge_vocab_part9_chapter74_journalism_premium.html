Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse journalism: newsrooms, sources, verification, investigative reporting, editorial lines, bias, framing, sensationalism, press freedom, censorship, disinformation, deepfakes, platform power, media ownership and the economic crisis of journalism in the digital age.

A. Core journalism and media vocabulary

journalism

IPA: /ˈdʒɜːnəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: JUR-nuh-liz-uhm
French: journalisme

Definition: The work of collecting, verifying, writing and publishing news for the public.

Example: Journalism is essential to democratic accountability.

journalist

IPA: /ˈdʒɜːnəlɪst/
Pronunciation: JUR-nuh-list
French: journaliste

Definition: A person who researches, writes or reports news stories.

Example: A journalist must verify information before publication.

reporter

IPA: /rɪˈpɔːtə/
Pronunciation: ri-POR-tuh
French: reporter

Definition: A journalist who gathers and reports news, often from the scene.

Example: The reporter interviewed witnesses after the flood.

correspondent

IPA: /ˌkɒrəˈspɒndənt/
Pronunciation: kor-uh-SPON-duhnt
French: correspondant

Definition: A journalist reporting from a particular place or on a specific topic.

Example: The foreign correspondent reported from Washington.

editor

IPA: /ˈedɪtə/
Pronunciation: ED-i-tuh
French: rédacteur en chef ; éditeur

Definition: A person who selects, revises and approves material for publication.

Example: The editor decided to run the story.

newsroom

IPA: /ˈnjuːzruːm/
Pronunciation: NYOOZ-room
French: rédaction

Definition: The place or team where journalists produce news.

Example: The newsroom worked through the night.

press

IPA: /pres/
Pronunciation: press
French: presse

Definition: Newspapers, journalists and news organisations collectively.

Example: The press scrutinises those in power.

media outlet

IPA: /ˈmiːdiə ˌaʊtlet/
Pronunciation: MEE-dee-uh OUT-let
French: média ; organe de presse

Definition: A newspaper, website, TV channel or radio station publishing news.

Example: Different media outlets framed the story differently.

legacy media

IPA: /ˈleɡəsi ˌmiːdiə/
Pronunciation: LEG-uh-see MEE-dee-uh
French: médias traditionnels

Definition: Traditional media organisations such as newspapers, TV and radio.

Example: Legacy media face competition from digital platforms.

mainstream media

IPA: /ˈmeɪnstriːm ˌmiːdiə/
Pronunciation: MAYN-streem MEE-dee-uh
French: grands médias ; médias dominants

Definition: Large, widely recognised media organisations.

Example: Mainstream media are often accused of bias.

independent media

IPA: /ˌɪndɪˈpendənt ˈmiːdiə/
Pronunciation: in-di-PEN-duhnt MEE-dee-uh
French: médias indépendants

Definition: Media organisations not controlled by governments or major corporate interests.

Example: Independent media can expose corruption.

public service broadcasting

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk ˈsɜːvɪs ˈbrɔːdkɑːstɪŋ/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik SUR-vis BROD-kast-ing
French: audiovisuel public

Definition: Broadcasting intended to serve the public interest rather than profit alone.

Example: Public service broadcasting aims to inform citizens.

tabloid

IPA: /ˈtæblɔɪd/
Pronunciation: TAB-loyd
French: tabloïd

Definition: A newspaper or style of journalism using sensational stories.

Example: Tabloids often focus on scandals.

broadsheet

IPA: /ˈbrɔːdʃiːt/
Pronunciation: BROD-sheet
French: journal de référence

Definition: A serious newspaper traditionally printed on large pages.

Example: Broadsheets usually provide detailed political analysis.

digital journalism

IPA: /ˈdɪdʒɪtəl ˌdʒɜːnəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: DIJ-i-tuhl JUR-nuh-liz-uhm
French: journalisme numérique

Definition: Journalism produced and distributed online.

Example: Digital journalism allows rapid updates.

breaking news

IPA: /ˌbreɪkɪŋ ˈnjuːz/
Pronunciation: BRAY-king NYOOZ
French: information de dernière minute

Definition: Newly received information about an important event.

Example: Breaking news can be fast but incomplete.

headline

IPA: /ˈhedlaɪn/
Pronunciation: HED-lyne
French: titre

Definition: The title of a news story.

Example: A misleading headline can distort the article.

lead

IPA: /liːd/
Pronunciation: leed
French: attaque ; introduction d’article

Definition: The opening sentence or paragraph of a news story.

Example: A good lead gives the key facts immediately.

byline

IPA: /ˈbaɪlaɪn/
Pronunciation: BY-lyne
French: signature d’un article

Definition: The line naming the journalist who wrote an article.

Example: The byline identified the investigative reporter.

front page

IPA: /ˌfrʌnt ˈpeɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: front payj
French: une ; première page

Definition: The first page of a newspaper, featuring major stories.

Example: The scandal made the front page.

scoop

IPA: /skuːp/
Pronunciation: skoop
French: scoop

Definition: An exclusive news story published before competitors.

Example: The newspaper got a scoop.

exclusive

IPA: /ɪkˈskluːsɪv/
Pronunciation: ik-SKLOO-siv
French: exclusivité

Definition: A story or interview obtained by one media outlet only.

Example: The interview was an exclusive.

source

IPA: /sɔːs/
Pronunciation: sorss
French: source

Definition: A person, document or organisation providing information to journalists.

Example: Journalists must protect sensitive sources.

anonymous source

IPA: /əˌnɒnɪməs ˈsɔːs/
Pronunciation: uh-NON-i-muhs sorss
French: source anonyme

Definition: A source whose identity is not revealed.

Example: Anonymous sources must be handled carefully.

whistleblower

IPA: /ˈwɪsəlˌbləʊə/
Pronunciation: WIS-uhl-bloh-uh
French: lanceur d’alerte

Definition: A person who exposes wrongdoing inside an organisation.

Example: Whistleblowers often rely on journalists.

leak

IPA: /liːk/
Pronunciation: leek
French: fuite

Definition: Secret information given to the press without official permission.

Example: The leak embarrassed the government.

leaked document

IPA: /ˌliːkt ˈdɒkjʊmənt/
Pronunciation: leekt DOK-yuh-muhnt
French: document divulgué

Definition: A confidential document released to journalists or the public.

Example: The leaked document contradicted the minister.

press release

IPA: /ˈpres rɪˌliːs/
Pronunciation: press ri-LEESS
French: communiqué de presse

Definition: An official statement sent to journalists by an organisation.

Example: The company issued a press release.

press conference

IPA: /ˈpres ˌkɒnfərəns/
Pronunciation: press KON-fuh-ruhns
French: conférence de presse

Definition: A meeting where officials answer journalists’ questions.

Example: The prime minister held a press conference.

interview

IPA: /ˈɪntəvjuː/
Pronunciation: IN-tuh-vyoo
French: interview ; entretien

Definition: A conversation in which a journalist asks questions to obtain information.

Example: The interview revealed contradictions.

eyewitness account

IPA: /ˈaɪˌwɪtnəs əˌkaʊnt/
Pronunciation: EYE-wit-nuhs uh-KOWNT
French: témoignage oculaire

Definition: A description of an event by someone who saw it.

Example: Eyewitness accounts can be powerful but unreliable.

fact-checking

IPA: /ˈfækt ˌtʃekɪŋ/
Pronunciation: FAKT-chek-ing
French: vérification des faits

Definition: The process of verifying claims, facts and statements.

Example: Fact-checking is crucial during elections.

verification

IPA: /ˌverɪfɪˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: ver-i-fi-KAY-shuhn
French: vérification

Definition: The process of confirming whether information is true or accurate.

Example: Verification prevents rumours from becoming news.

corroboration

IPA: /kəˌrɒbəˈreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: kuh-rob-uh-RAY-shuhn
French: corroboration

Definition: Evidence from another source confirming information.

Example: A serious allegation needs corroboration.

evidence

IPA: /ˈevɪdəns/
Pronunciation: EV-i-duhns
French: preuves ; éléments probants

Definition: Information or material supporting a claim.

Example: The article presented documentary evidence.

B. Verification, ethics and journalistic formats

accuracy

IPA: /ˈækjʊrəsi/
Pronunciation: AK-yuh-ruh-see
French: exactitude

Definition: The quality of being correct and reliable.

Example: Accuracy matters more than speed.

reliability

IPA: /rɪˌlaɪəˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: ri-ly-uh-BIL-uh-tee
French: fiabilité

Definition: The quality of being trustworthy and dependable.

Example: The reliability of the source was questioned.

credibility

IPA: /ˌkredəˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: kred-uh-BIL-uh-tee
French: crédibilité

Definition: The quality of being believable and trusted.

Example: Credibility is a journalist’s most valuable asset.

transparency

IPA: /trænˈspærənsi/
Pronunciation: tran-SPARR-uhn-see
French: transparence

Definition: Openness about methods, sources or interests.

Example: Transparency can build trust with readers.

accountability

IPA: /əˌkaʊntəˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: uh-kown-tuh-BIL-uh-tee
French: responsabilité ; redevabilité

Definition: Responsibility for actions and decisions.

Example: Journalism holds powerful people accountable.

public interest

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk ˈɪntrəst/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik IN-truhst
French: intérêt public

Definition: What benefits society or citizens, especially in democratic debate.

Example: Publishing the information was justified by public interest.

watchdog journalism

IPA: /ˈwɒtʃdɒɡ ˌdʒɜːnəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: WATCH-dog JUR-nuh-liz-uhm
French: journalisme de surveillance ; contre-pouvoir

Definition: Journalism that monitors power and exposes wrongdoing.

Example: Watchdog journalism is vital in a democracy.

investigative journalism

IPA: /ɪnˈvestɪɡətɪv ˌdʒɜːnəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: in-VES-ti-guh-tiv JUR-nuh-liz-uhm
French: journalisme d’investigation

Definition: In-depth reporting that uncovers hidden information or wrongdoing.

Example: Investigative journalism can take months.

data journalism

IPA: /ˈdeɪtə ˌdʒɜːnəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: DAY-tuh JUR-nuh-liz-uhm
French: journalisme de données

Definition: Journalism using data analysis to find and explain stories.

Example: Data journalism can reveal hidden patterns.

solutions journalism

IPA: /səˈluːʃənz ˌdʒɜːnəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: suh-LOO-shuhnz JUR-nuh-liz-uhm
French: journalisme de solutions

Definition: Journalism that reports on responses to social problems.

Example: Solutions journalism avoids pure doom.

explanatory journalism

IPA: /ɪkˈsplænətəri ˌdʒɜːnəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: ik-SPLAN-uh-tuh-ree JUR-nuh-liz-uhm
French: journalisme explicatif

Definition: Journalism that explains complex issues clearly.

Example: Explanatory journalism helps readers understand policy.

long-form journalism

IPA: /ˈlɒŋ fɔːm ˌdʒɜːnəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: long-form JUR-nuh-liz-uhm
French: journalisme long format

Definition: Extended, detailed journalism often using narrative techniques.

Example: Long-form journalism gives space for nuance.

opinion piece

IPA: /əˈpɪnjən piːs/
Pronunciation: uh-PIN-yuhn peess
French: tribune ; article d’opinion

Definition: An article expressing a personal or institutional viewpoint.

Example: An opinion piece should not be confused with news.

editorial

IPA: /ˌedɪˈtɔːriəl/
Pronunciation: ed-i-TOR-ee-uhl
French: éditorial

Definition: An article expressing the official view of a publication.

Example: The editorial criticised the government.

column

IPA: /ˈkɒləm/
Pronunciation: KOL-uhm
French: chronique

Definition: A regular opinion article written by the same journalist or commentator.

Example: The columnist writes a weekly column.

feature article

IPA: /ˈfiːtʃər ˌɑːtɪkəl/
Pronunciation: FEE-chuh AR-ti-kuhl
French: article de fond

Definition: A longer article focusing on context, people or analysis.

Example: The feature article explored rural poverty.

profile

IPA: /ˈprəʊfaɪl/
Pronunciation: PROH-fyle
French: portrait

Definition: An article describing a person in depth.

Example: The profile examined the billionaire’s influence.

news report

IPA: /ˈnjuːz rɪˌpɔːt/
Pronunciation: NYOOZ ri-PORT
French: reportage ; article d’information

Definition: A factual account of a news event.

Example: A news report should separate fact from opinion.

analysis

IPA: /əˈnæləsɪs/
Pronunciation: uh-NAL-uh-sis
French: analyse

Definition: A piece explaining causes, consequences or meaning of events.

Example: The analysis placed the election in context.

commentary

IPA: /ˈkɒməntəri/
Pronunciation: KOM-uhn-tuh-ree
French: commentaire

Definition: Opinion or interpretation of events.

Example: Commentary should be clearly labelled.

editorial line

IPA: /ˌedɪˈtɔːriəl laɪn/
Pronunciation: ed-i-TOR-ee-uhl lyne
French: ligne éditoriale

Definition: The general political or ideological orientation of a publication.

Example: The newspaper’s editorial line is centre-left.

angle

IPA: /ˈæŋɡəl/
Pronunciation: ANG-guhl
French: angle

Definition: The perspective or focus chosen for a story.

Example: The journalist chose a human-interest angle.

framing

IPA: /ˈfreɪmɪŋ/
Pronunciation: FRAY-ming
French: cadrage

Definition: The way information is presented, shaping interpretation.

Example: Framing can influence how readers understand migration.

narrative

IPA: /ˈnærətɪv/
Pronunciation: NARR-uh-tiv
French: récit ; narration

Definition: A storyline or interpretation used to make sense of events.

Example: Political narratives shape news coverage.

spin

IPA: /spɪn/
Pronunciation: spin
French: présentation orientée ; spin

Definition: A biased presentation of information to influence public perception.

Example: Politicians use spin to control damaging stories.

bias

IPA: /ˈbaɪəs/
Pronunciation: BY-uhs
French: biais

Definition: A tendency to favour one side or perspective unfairly.

Example: Media bias can appear in word choice.

political bias

IPA: /pəˌlɪtɪkəl ˈbaɪəs/
Pronunciation: puh-LIT-i-kuhl BY-uhs
French: biais politique

Definition: Bias linked to political opinions or interests.

Example: The outlet was accused of political bias.

confirmation bias

IPA: /ˌkɒnfəˈmeɪʃən ˈbaɪəs/
Pronunciation: kon-fuh-MAY-shuhn BY-uhs
French: biais de confirmation

Definition: The tendency to accept information that confirms existing beliefs.

Example: Confirmation bias affects audiences and journalists.

false balance

IPA: /ˌfɔːls ˈbæləns/
Pronunciation: false BAL-uhns
French: faux équilibre

Definition: Giving equal weight to positions not equally supported by evidence.

Example: False balance once distorted climate reporting.

sensationalism

IPA: /senˈseɪʃənəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: sen-SAY-shuhn-uh-liz-uhm
French: sensationnalisme

Definition: Presenting stories in an exaggerated way to attract attention.

Example: Sensationalism can undermine trust.

clickbait

IPA: /ˈklɪkbeɪt/

French: piège à clics

Pronunciation: OWT-rayj bayt
French: contenu conçu pour provoquer l’indignation

Definition: Content designed to provoke anger and engagement.

infotainment

IPA: /ˌɪnfəʊˈteɪnmənt/
Pronunciation: IN-foh-TAYN-muhnt
French: infodivertissement

Definition: Media content mixing information and entertainment.

Example: Infotainment can make politics feel like a show.

human-interest story

IPA: /ˌhjuːmən ˈɪntrəst ˌstɔːri/
Pronunciation: HYOO-muhn IN-truhst STOR-ee
French: article à dimension humaine

Definition: A story focusing on personal experience and emotion.

Example: Human-interest stories make abstract issues relatable.

agenda-setting

IPA: /əˈdʒendə ˌsetɪŋ/
Pronunciation: uh-JEN-duh SET-ing
French: mise à l’agenda

Definition: The media’s ability to influence which issues people consider important.

gatekeeping

IPA: /ˈɡeɪtˌkiːpɪŋ/

French: filtrage de l’information

IPA: /ˈnjuːzˌwɜːðinəs/
Pronunciation: NYOOZ-wur-thee-nuhs
French: valeur journalistique

media storm

IPA: /ˈmiːdiə stɔːm/

French: tempête médiatique

right of reply

IPA: /ˌraɪt əv rɪˈplaɪ/
Pronunciation: right ov ri-PLY
French: droit de réponse

IPA: /rɪˈtrækʃən/

journalistic ethics

slander
