Chapter 28 — Noun Phrases
B1 → B2 grammar for academic precision, dense argumentation and elegant essay style.

Chapter objective

This premium grammar chapter helps CPGE students build precise noun phrases. It covers head nouns, pre-modification, post-modification, academic noun phrases, article-summary patterns, nominal style and common French-speaker mistakes.

The golden rule

Determiner + modifier(s) + noun + extra information
The growing economic pressure on young households is worrying.
Main useNoun phrases allow students to express complex ideas concisely and precisely.
CPGE habitUse high-value phrases: the impact of, the role of, the lack of, the rise in, the risk of.
Key trapMost English adjectives come before nouns: a convincing argument, not an argument convincing.

A. Lesson — What are noun phrases?

Noun phrase

French: groupe nominal

Rule / explanation: A noun phrase is a group of words built around a noun.

Example / answer: The rapid decline in public trust worries analysts.

Head noun

French: nom noyau

Rule / explanation: The head noun is the central noun in the phrase.

Example / answer: The decline in public trust is significant.

Pre-modification

French: modification avant le nom

Rule / explanation: Words before the noun add information: determiners, adjectives, nouns or participles.

Example / answer: This controversial housing policy has divided voters.

Post-modification

French: modification après le nom

Rule / explanation: Words after the noun add detail: prepositional phrases, relative clauses or participial clauses.

Example / answer: The policy proposed by the government remains controversial.

B. Basic noun phrase structure

Determiner + noun

French: déterminant + nom

Rule / explanation: The simplest noun phrase often starts with a determiner.

Example / answer: This article is persuasive.

Determiner + adjective + noun

French: déterminant + adjectif + nom

Rule / explanation: Add adjectives before the noun.

Example / answer: This recent article is persuasive.

Noun + prepositional phrase

French: nom + groupe prépositionnel

Rule / explanation: A phrase after the noun adds precision.

Example / answer: The consequences of austerity remain visible.

Noun + relative clause

French: nom + relative

Rule / explanation: A relative clause identifies or adds information.

Example / answer: The voters who feel ignored may abstain.

C. Pre-modification before the noun

Adjectives before nouns

French: adjectifs avant le nom

Rule / explanation: Most English adjectives come before the noun.

Example / answer: The article presents a convincing argument.

Noun as adjective

French: nom adjectival

Rule / explanation: A noun can modify another noun.

Example / answer: Housing policy affects young adults.

Participles before nouns

French: participes adjectivaux

Rule / explanation: Present or past participles can work like adjectives.

Example / answer: Rising rents affect students. / A divided society is fragile.

Hyphenated modifiers

French: modificateurs composés

Rule / explanation: Compound modifiers before nouns often use a hyphen.

Example / answer: A long-term policy is needed.

D. Post-modification after the noun

Of-phrase

French: groupe en of

Rule / explanation: Use of to express relation, content, possession or abstract connection.

Example / answer: The decline of public trust is worrying.

In-phrase

French: groupe en in

Rule / explanation: Use in to specify area, field, place or trend.

Example / answer: The rise in inequality is well documented.

Past participle phrase

French: participe passé

Rule / explanation: Use it for passive meaning.

Example / answer: The figures published yesterday are alarming.

Present participle phrase

French: participe présent

Rule / explanation: Use it for active or ongoing meaning.

Example / answer: Voters living in rural areas may feel neglected.

E. Noun phrases for argumentation

The impact of

French: l’impact de

Rule / explanation: Useful for cause and consequence.

Example / answer: The impact of inflation on households is severe.

The role of

French: le rôle de

Rule / explanation: Useful for analysis.

Example / answer: The role of social media should not be ignored.

The lack of

French: le manque de

Rule / explanation: Useful for criticism.

Example / answer: The lack of evidence weakens the argument.

The risk of

French: le risque de

Rule / explanation: Useful for warnings.

Example / answer: The risk of social unrest should be taken seriously.

F. Article-summary patterns

The author’s main argument

French: argument principal de l’auteur

Rule / explanation: Useful for presenting the thesis.

Example / answer: The author’s main argument is that inequality threatens democracy.

The article’s central claim

French: thèse centrale de l’article

Rule / explanation: Formal summary phrase.

Example / answer: The article’s central claim is persuasive.

A sign of

French: un signe de

Rule / explanation: Useful for interpretation.

Example / answer: Low turnout is a sign of democratic fatigue.

An illustration of

French: une illustration de

Rule / explanation: Useful for commentary.

Example / answer: The case is an illustration of institutional failure.

G. Typical mistakes by French speakers

Adjective after noun

French: adjectif après nom

Rule / explanation: Most English adjectives come before nouns.

Example / answer: Wrong: an argument convincing. Correct: a convincing argument.

Too many of-phrases

French: trop de of

Rule / explanation: French often uses de repeatedly; English may need restructuring.

Example / answer: Heavy: the problem of the cost of housing. Better: the cost of housing.

Missing hyphen

French: trait d’union

Rule / explanation: Compound modifiers before nouns often need a hyphen.

Example / answer: A long-term solution.

Unclear this

French: this vague

Rule / explanation: This should often be followed by a noun.

Example / answer: Weak: This is worrying. Better: This trend is worrying.

H. Exercises

Exercise 1 — Correct

French: exercice

Rule / explanation: The author presents an argument convincing.

Example / answer: The author presents a convincing argument.

Exercise 2 — Transform

French: exercice

Rule / explanation: Rents are rising. This affects students.

Example / answer: The rise in rents affects students.

Exercise 3 — Use an academic phrase

French: exercice

Rule / explanation: Use: the lack of + evidence.

Example / answer: The lack of evidence weakens the argument.

Exercise 4 — Translate

French: exercice

Rule / explanation: L’impact de l’inflation sur les ménages est important.

Example / answer: The impact of inflation on households is significant.

Exercise 5 — Mini concours challenge

French: exercice

Rule / explanation: Write 6 noun phrases using: the role of, the risk of, the lack of, the rise in, a sign of, long-term.

Example / answer: The role of education is central. The risk of unrest is real. A long-term solution is needed.
