## 1. ESSENTIAL FACTS
- **Official name:** the United States of America (USA).
- **Capital:** Washington, D.C. **Population:** ~335 million (the third most populous country).
- **Currency:** the US dollar ($) — the world's main reserve currency.
- **System:** a federal presidential republic, made up of **50 states**.
- **Head of state and government:** the President (**Donald Trump**, 47th president, since January 2025 — to be checked).
- **Language:** no official language at the federal level (English is dominant in practice).
- **International standing:** the world's leading military and economic power; permanent member of the UN Security Council; a founding member of NATO.

> ⚠️ **Key idea:** the US is built on a tension between a **strong federal government** and **powerful individual states** — many debates (abortion, guns, the death penalty) come down to "who decides: Washington or the states?"

---

## 2. GEOGRAPHY AND THE FEDERAL STRUCTURE
- A continental country of **50 states** + the federal district of Washington, D.C. (plus territories such as Puerto Rico).
- Great regional contrasts: the **Northeast** (the old industrial and financial core), the **South** (the former slave-holding states, the *Bible Belt*), the **Midwest** (the agricultural and industrial *Rust Belt*), the **West** (California, *Silicon Valley*, Hollywood).
- Recurrent oppositions: **coasts vs heartland**, **urban vs rural**, **"blue states" (Democratic) vs "red states" (Republican)**.

> 💡 **Colle angle:** the US is less a uniform nation than a **patchwork of regions** with diverging economies, values and politics — a key to understanding its divisions.

---

## 3. THE CONSTITUTION AND THE SEPARATION OF POWERS
- **The Constitution of 1787** (the oldest written constitution still in force), completed by the **Bill of Rights** (the first 10 amendments, 1791).
- A **strict separation of powers** and a system of **checks and balances** so that no branch dominates:
  - **Executive:** the **President**, elected for four years through the **Electoral College**.
  - **Legislative:** the **Congress**, made up of the **House of Representatives** (435 members, proportional to population) and the **Senate** (100 senators, two per state).
  - **Judicial:** the **Supreme Court** (9 justices, appointed for life) — a body with major political weight (it can strike down laws).
- **Key amendments:** 1st (freedom of speech, religion, the press), 2nd (the right to bear arms), 13th (abolition of slavery, 1865), 14th (equal protection and citizenship), 15th and 19th (the vote regardless of race, then for women, 1920).

> 💡 **Colle angle:** the Constitution is both **revered** (almost sacred) and a **battlefield** — its interpretation (especially of the 1st and 2nd Amendments) shapes the fiercest debates.

---

## 4. THE INSTITUTIONS

### 4.1 The presidency (the executive)
- The **President** is both head of state and head of government, and **commander-in-chief** of the armed forces.
- Considerable powers: vetoing laws, issuing *executive orders*, conducting foreign policy, appointing judges and senior officials.
- But **counterbalanced**: Congress votes the budget and laws and can **impeach** the President; the Supreme Court can rule against him.
- The **Vice President** succeeds the President if needed and presides over the Senate.

### 4.2 Congress (the legislature)
- **The House of Representatives:** 435 members, elected every two years, proportional to each state's population.
- **The Senate:** 100 senators (two per state), elected for six years — it gives small states the same weight as large ones.
- Congress **passes laws, votes the budget, declares war** and scrutinises the executive.

### 4.3 The Supreme Court (the judiciary)
- **9 justices appointed for life** by the President and confirmed by the Senate.
- Through *judicial review*, it can declare a law **unconstitutional** — hence its enormous influence (on abortion, guns, civil rights…).
- Its ideological balance (conservative vs liberal justices) is a **major political stake** at every appointment.

---

## 5. THE ELECTORAL SYSTEM AND THE PARTIES ⭐

### 5.1 The Electoral College
- The President is elected **indirectly**: each state has a number of **electors** (in proportion to its population), and in most states the winner **takes all** the state's electors.
- **538 electors in total; 270 are needed to win.**
- **Consequence:** a candidate can **win the presidency without winning the popular vote** (as in 2000 and 2016). The system favours the **swing states** (the few undecided states that decide the election).

> 👉 **Colle tip:** the gap between the *popular vote* and the *Electoral College* is one of the **most testable points**. Be ready to explain, in one sentence, how a candidate can lose the popular vote yet still win the presidency.

### 5.2 The parties and elections
- A **two-party system**: the **Democrats** (centre-left, "blue") vs the **Republicans** (the *GOP*, right, "red").
- **Primaries** select each party's candidate before the general election.
- Other features that fuel debate: ***gerrymandering*** (redrawing districts for partisan gain), questions of **voter access**, and the role of **money** in campaigns.

---

## 6. FEDERALISM AND STATES' RIGHTS
- Power is **shared** between the federal government and the states: each state has its own constitution, laws, governor and courts.
- States decide on many sensitive matters: the **death penalty**, **education**, **gun laws**, and — since 2022 — **abortion**.
- A historic and recurring tension between **federal authority** and **states' rights** (a question at the heart of the Civil War, and still alive today).

> 💡 **Colle angle:** American federalism means the **same country can have radically different laws** from one state to the next — a powerful illustration of its internal divisions.

---

## 7. HISTORY, PERIOD BY PERIOD ⭐

### 7.1 Foundations
- **1607:** Jamestown, the first permanent English colony. **1620:** the *Mayflower* and the Pilgrim Fathers.
- **4 July 1776 — the Declaration of Independence** (Thomas Jefferson): "all men are created equal".
- **1787:** the Constitution; **1791:** the Bill of Rights.
- The conquest of the West (*the Frontier*) and **Manifest Destiny** (the belief in a divine mission to expand).

### 7.2 The Civil War and its legacy
- **The Civil War (1861-1865):** the North (*the Union*) against the South (*the Confederacy*), with **slavery** at its heart.
- **Abraham Lincoln**, the abolition of slavery (13th Amendment, 1865), then a failed **Reconstruction**.
- The legacy: **segregation** (*Jim Crow* laws) in the South until the 1960s.

### 7.3 Rise to world power
- The **Gilded Age** and industrialisation; mass immigration through **Ellis Island**.
- **1929 — the Great Depression** → the **New Deal** (Franklin D. Roosevelt): massive state intervention.
- **The two World Wars**, then the **Cold War** against the USSR (1947-1991): the US as leader of the "free world".

### 7.4 The Civil Rights era and the contemporary US
- **The Civil Rights Movement (1950s-60s):** **Martin Luther King**, Rosa Parks; the **March on Washington** and *"I Have a Dream"* (1963); the **Civil Rights Act (1964)** and the **Voting Rights Act (1965)**.
- The **Vietnam War**, the counter-culture, the **Moon landing (1969)**.
- **11 September 2001:** a turning point, the *War on Terror*.
- **2008:** **Barack Obama**, the first African-American president; the global financial crisis.
- **2020:** the Covid pandemic and the **Black Lives Matter** protests (after the killing of George Floyd).
- **6 January 2021:** the assault on the Capitol — a shock to American democracy.

> 💡 **Colle angle:** American history is a long, unfinished struggle to live up to the promise of 1776 — *"all men are created equal"* — especially on the question of race.

## 8. SOCIETY ⭐

> 👉 **Colle tip:** **race, immigration and guns** are among the **most frequent colle topics** of all. For each, know a few key facts, the central debate, and one ready sentence.

### 8.1 A nation of immigrants
- The **melting pot** (fusion) vs the **salad bowl / mosaic** (diversity preserved): two ways of picturing American society.
- Immigration is **founding and constant**, but a **permanent source of debate** (the Mexican border, *Dreamers* / DACA, national identity).

### 8.2 The American Dream
- The idea that **anyone can succeed through merit and hard work** (*"from rags to riches"*) — a powerful myth, but increasingly **questioned** (inequality, stalled social mobility).

### 8.3 The question of race
- The legacy of **slavery** and **segregation**; the **Civil Rights Movement**; today, **Black Lives Matter**, police violence, and debates over *affirmative action*.

### 8.4 Religion
- A **very religious** society (the weight of evangelicals, the *Bible Belt*), yet with a constitutional **separation of church and state**.

### 8.5 Guns
- The **2nd Amendment**, a powerful lobby (the **NRA**), and recurring **mass shootings** make *gun control* one of the most explosive debates in the country.

## 9. THE MAJOR CONTEMPORARY DEBATES ⭐
1. **Political polarisation:** a deep divide between "red" and "blue" America, and the *culture wars*.
2. **Abortion:** the Supreme Court overturned **Roe v. Wade** in 2022, handing the decision back to the states — a social earthquake.
3. **Immigration:** the wall, the Mexican border, the *Dreamers* — a permanent identity debate.
4. **Inequality:** *the 1%*, access to healthcare, *student debt*, the minimum wage.
5. **Healthcare:** no universal coverage; *Obamacare* (the ACA) remains contested.
6. **The electoral system:** the Electoral College, *gerrymandering*, access to the vote — questions about the health of democracy.
7. **Guns:** the right to bear arms vs public safety.
8. **The US in the world:** isolationism vs interventionism, and rivalry with China.

> 👉 **Colle tip:** the debates that come up most often are **abortion, guns, immigration and polarisation**. Prepare a problématique and two or three facts for each — you will almost always be able to connect one to your document.

## 10. THE ECONOMY
The world's largest economy, built on **capitalism** and **innovation**:
- **Silicon Valley** and Big Tech (the *GAFAM*), **Wall Street**, world-leading universities.
- The **dollar** as the global reserve currency.
- But deep **inequalities**: poverty, the *working poor*, uneven access to healthcare and education, and a shrinking industrial heartland (the *Rust Belt*).

## 11. CULTURE AND NATIONAL IDENTITY
- **Founding values:** liberty, individualism, the pursuit of happiness, patriotism, the flag.
- A colossal **soft power**: Hollywood, music (jazz, rock, hip-hop), fast food, global brands, sport (the NFL, the NBA, baseball).
- Enduring **symbols:** the Statue of Liberty, the American Dream, the *Frontier*, the self-made man.

## 🗣️ LANGUAGE INDICATIONS (how to talk about the US correctly)

The English-usage traps that French students most often fall into when discussing the US:
- **Congress**, not "the Parliament" (the US has a **Congress**, made up of the **House of Representatives** and the **Senate**). A member is **a senator**, **a representative** or **a congressman / congresswoman**.
- **The Democratic Party / a Democrat** (noun) vs **Democratic** (adjective): say *"a Democratic president"*, not *"a Democrat president"*. The Republicans are also called **the GOP** (the "Grand Old Party").
- **The Supreme Court** (not "the High Court"); its members are **justices**, not "judges".
- The President is elected **indirectly** — ⚠️ avoid *"the people elect the President directly"*.
- **gun control** (not "weapon control"); **the right to bear arms**.
- **African Americans / Black Americans** (a capital *B* is now common) — avoid outdated terms.
- Keep the **federal** (national) vs **state** distinction clear: *"a federal law"* vs *"a state law"*.
- **the Civil War** = the American war of 1861-65 (not WWI/WWII); **the Founding Fathers** wrote the Constitution.
- Useful proper nouns: **the White House**, **the Oval Office**, **Capitol Hill**, **Wall Street**.

> 👉 Using these correctly instantly signals that you **know the country**, not just the vocabulary.

## 📅 The US in 10 dates (and why each still matters)
- **1776 — Declaration of Independence** → the founding promise that "all men are created equal", still the yardstick for every debate on rights.
- **1787 — the Constitution** → checks and balances and federalism, the framework of every institutional argument today.
- **1865 — abolition of slavery** → the open wound of race relations runs straight from here.
- **1929 → the New Deal** → the lasting template for debates on the role of the state.
- **1945 — end of WWII** → the US becomes the leader of the "free world"; the root of its global role.
- **1963 — "I Have a Dream"** → the moral high point of the Civil Rights Movement, endlessly quoted.
- **1965 — the Voting Rights Act** → voting rights, still contested today (access, gerrymandering).
- **2001 — 9/11** → reshaped security, foreign policy and civil liberties.
- **2008 — Obama elected** → a symbol of progress on race; also the year of the financial crisis.
- **2022 — Roe v. Wade overturned** → abortion handed back to the states; the culture wars at their sharpest.

## ♻️ America's great paradoxes (where the best questions are born)
- A nation founded on **"all men are created equal"**, yet built in part on **slavery**.
- The **"land of the free"** with one of the world's **highest incarceration rates**.
- A **nation of immigrants** that endlessly debates **immigration**.
- The world's **richest country** without **universal healthcare**.
- A deeply **religious** nation with a strict **separation of church and state**.
- A champion of **democracy abroad** wrestling with strains on its **own democracy**.

> 👉 In the exam, first ask: *which super-theme does my document belong to?* Then pull the relevant topics into your plan.

## 🗂️ Theme-by-theme colle cards

> Each card is built to be **deployed**: a survival sentence, hooks and openings, ten strategic words, how the topic comes up, and a model of good vs weak exploitation.

### 1. The Constitution & checks and balances
- **⏱️ 30-second sentence:** *"The US Constitution divides power between three branches so that none can dominate — a system of checks and balances."* — **Enriched:** *"Revered as almost sacred, the 1787 Constitution is also a constant battlefield, since its interpretation shapes the fiercest debates."*
- **✅ Must know:** Constitution (1787); Bill of Rights (1791); three branches; federalism; judicial review. **❌ Mistakes:** "The President can do anything"; confusing federal and state powers.
- **⚠️ Trap:** *the President is all-powerful* → he is **checked** by Congress (budget, impeachment) and the Supreme Court.
- **📰 How it comes up:** documents on a Supreme Court ruling, a constitutional crisis, an impeachment, gridlock between Congress and the President. The press covers it because of **the balance of powers, the role of unelected judges, democratic legitimacy**.
- **🔄 Use it to talk about:** democracy, the rule of law, the power of judges, institutional reform, federalism.
- **🎣 Hook:** *simple* — *"Few documents are as revered — and as fought over — as the US Constitution."* / *sophisticated* — *"Americans argue about almost everything by arguing about their Constitution."*
- **🚪 Opening:** term limits for justices · constitutional amendments · the politicisation of the Court.
- **🔑 10 words to drop:** checks and balances, the separation of powers, judicial review, an amendment, federalism, the Bill of Rights, to ratify, unconstitutional, gridlock, the rule of law.
- **🎭 Hidden stakes:** the balance of powers, the rule of law, unelected judges shaping society.
- **♻️ Paradox:** a 230-year-old text governing a hyper-modern superpower.
- **🌍 Compare with:** the UK (unwritten constitution), France (codified).
- **📖 Cultural references:** *The West Wing*; *Mr. Smith Goes to Washington*.
- **✍️ Model exploitation:** ❌ *"The US has a Constitution from 1787."* → 🟠 *"The Constitution divides power through checks and balances."* → ✅ *"The reverence for a 1787 text means that, in America, today's fiercest battles — guns, abortion, free speech — are fought as battles over interpretation."*
- **🇫🇷 French-speaker trap:** **Congress**, not "Parliament"; a Supreme Court member is **a justice**, not "a judge".
- **❓ Colle questions:** *Are checks and balances still working? · Should justices serve for life? · Is the Constitution too hard to change?*
- **📝 Ready plan ("Checks and balances under strain"):** I. A system designed to prevent tyranny — II. Growing tensions (executive power, a politicised Court) — III. Can the system renew itself?

### 2. The presidency
- **⏱️ 30-second sentence:** *"The US President is both head of state and head of government, but his power is constantly checked by Congress and the courts."* — **Enriched:** *"The world's most powerful office is also one of the most hemmed in at home."*
- **✅ Must know:** elected for four years via the Electoral College; commander-in-chief; executive orders; veto. **❌ Mistakes:** treating the President like an all-powerful monarch.
- **⚠️ Trap:** *executive orders = absolute power* → they can be **overturned** by courts or reversed by the next president.
- **📰 How it comes up:** documents on a presidential election, an executive order, a veto, an impeachment, the "imperial presidency". The press covers it because of **leadership, accountability, the concentration of power**.
- **🔄 Use it to talk about:** power, leadership, democracy, foreign policy, the media.
- **🎣 Hook:** *simple* — *"The US President is often called the most powerful person on earth."* / *sophisticated* — *"The American presidency is a paradox: immense power abroad, constant constraint at home."*
- **🚪 Opening:** the personalisation of politics · presidential power vs Congress · the role of the media.
- **🔑 10 words to drop:** the executive, commander-in-chief, an executive order, a veto, to impeach, the Cabinet, the Oval Office, term limits, the bully pulpit, accountability.
- **🎭 Hidden stakes:** the concentration of power, the "imperial presidency", accountability.
- **♻️ Paradox:** the world's most powerful office, hemmed in by domestic limits.
- **🌍 Compare with:** France (semi-presidential), the UK (a PM dependent on Parliament).
- **📖 Cultural references:** *House of Cards*; *The West Wing*.
- **✍️ Model exploitation:** ❌ *"The President has a lot of power."* → 🟠 *"The President is checked by Congress and the courts."* → ✅ *"The presidency embodies a tension between the image of an all-powerful leader and a constitutional design built to constrain him."*
- **🇫🇷 French-speaker trap:** in the US, "the government" usually means the whole **federal state**, not "the cabinet".
- **❓ Colle questions:** *Is the US presidency too powerful — or not powerful enough? · Does personality matter more than policy?*
- **📝 Ready plan ("The American presidency"):** I. Immense formal power — II. Real institutional limits — III. The growing weight of personality and media.

### 3. The Electoral College & democracy
- **⏱️ 30-second sentence:** *"Americans do not elect their President directly: the Electoral College can hand victory to the loser of the popular vote."* — **Enriched:** *"The self-proclaimed model of democracy runs on one of the world's most contested electoral systems."*
- **✅ Must know:** 538 electors, 270 to win; winner-takes-all; swing states; happened in 2000 and 2016. **❌ Mistakes:** "The candidate with the most votes wins."
- **⚠️ Trap:** *the popular vote decides* → it is the **Electoral College** that decides; the two can diverge.
- **📰 How it comes up:** documents on a presidential election, gerrymandering, voter ID / access, election denial, polarisation. The press covers it because of **legitimacy, fairness, the health of democracy**.
- **🔄 Use it to talk about:** democracy, elections, populism, polarisation, federalism.
- **🎣 Hook:** *simple* — *"In America, you can win the most votes and still lose the election."* / *sophisticated* — *"The country that exports democracy keeps one of its most disputed voting systems."*
- **🚪 Opening:** abolishing the Electoral College · voter access · threats to democracy.
- **🔑 10 words to drop:** the Electoral College, the popular vote, swing states, winner-takes-all, gerrymandering, voter turnout, voter suppression, legitimacy, polarisation, disenfranchisement.
- **🎭 Hidden stakes:** the legitimacy of elections, the weight of small/rural states, voter access.
- **♻️ Paradox:** the self-proclaimed model of democracy with a contested electoral system.
- **🌍 Compare with:** France (direct, two rounds), most democracies (direct election).
- **📖 Cultural references:** news coverage of Bush v. Gore (2000) and 6 January 2021.
- **✍️ Model exploitation:** ❌ *"Americans elect their President."* → 🟠 *"The Electoral College means the popular-vote winner can lose."* → ✅ *"The Electoral College reveals a deeper American tension: between majority rule and a federal design that deliberately weights states over raw numbers."*
- **🇫🇷 French-speaker trap:** avoid *"the people elect the President directly"* — the election is **indirect**.
- **❓ Colle questions:** *Is the US electoral system truly democratic? · Should the Electoral College be abolished?*
- **📝 Ready plan ("Is American democracy in crisis?"):** I. A flawed electoral system — II. Polarisation and distrust — III. Resilience or decline?

### 4. Race & civil rights
- **⏱️ 30-second sentence:** *"From slavery to segregation to Black Lives Matter, race remains the deepest fault line in American society."* — **Enriched:** *"America's history is a long, unfinished struggle to live up to its founding promise of equality."*
- **✅ Must know:** slavery; abolition (1865); Jim Crow; the Civil Rights Movement (MLK, the 1964-65 Acts); BLM. **❌ Mistakes:** thinking civil rights "solved" racism.
- **⚠️ Trap:** *the Civil Rights Movement ended discrimination* → it ended **legal** segregation, not racial inequality.
- **📰 How it comes up:** documents on police violence, BLM protests, affirmative action (the 2023 ruling), voting rights, the racial wealth gap, Confederate statues. The press covers it because of **justice, equality, the unfinished promise of 1776**.
- **🔄 Use it to talk about:** race, justice, inequality, identity, protest, memory.
- **🎣 Hook:** *simple* — *"The promise that 'all men are created equal' was written by a slaveholder."* / *sophisticated* — *"America's racial history is the gap between its founding ideals and its lived reality."*
- **🚪 Opening:** affirmative action · reparations · the persistent racial wealth gap.
- **🔑 10 words to drop:** slavery, segregation, Jim Crow, civil rights, desegregation, affirmative action, systemic racism, the racial wealth gap, Black Lives Matter, equality.
- **🎭 Hidden stakes:** equality, justice, the unfinished promise of 1776.
- **♻️ Paradox:** a nation founded on equality, built in part on slavery.
- **🌍 Compare with:** South Africa (apartheid), Brazil.
- **📖 Cultural references:** *To Kill a Mockingbird*; *Beloved* (Toni Morrison); *12 Years a Slave*; MLK's *"I Have a Dream"*; *The Hate U Give*.
- **✍️ Model exploitation:** ❌ *"There was slavery and then civil rights."* → 🟠 *"The Civil Rights Movement ended legal segregation."* → ✅ *"From abolition to Black Lives Matter, the story of race in America is the recurring gap between formal equality and persistent injustice."*
- **🇫🇷 French-speaker trap:** say **African Americans / Black Americans** (a capital *B* is now common); avoid outdated terms.
- **❓ Colle questions:** *Has America lived up to its founding promise of equality? · Is BLM a turning point or a moment?*
- **📝 Ready plan ("Race in America"):** I. A history of oppression — II. Hard-won legal victories — III. Persistent inequality and renewed protest.

### 5. Immigration & the American Dream
- **⏱️ 30-second sentence:** *"The US is a nation of immigrants, and the American Dream promises that anyone can rise through hard work — a powerful myth now under question."* — **Enriched:** *"Social mobility has stalled, turning the American Dream into one of the country's most contested ideas."*
- **✅ Must know:** Ellis Island; the melting pot vs the salad bowl; *Dreamers* / DACA; the border debate. **❌ Mistakes:** "All Americans oppose immigration."
- **⚠️ Trap:** *the American Dream guarantees success* → **social mobility** has stalled; the Dream is increasingly **contested**.
- **📰 How it comes up:** documents on the Mexican border / the wall, DACA, asylum, deportations, "the land of opportunity", inequality. The press covers it because of **identity, opportunity, the labour market**.
- **🔄 Use it to talk about:** immigration, identity, inequality, opportunity, the labour market.
- **🎣 Hook:** *simple* — *"America has always called itself a nation of immigrants."* / *sophisticated* — *"The American Dream promises that origins need not be destiny — a promise increasingly hard to keep."*
- **🚪 Opening:** immigration reform · the future of the American Dream · rising inequality.
- **🔑 10 words to drop:** a nation of immigrants, the melting pot, the salad bowl, the American Dream, social mobility, the land of opportunity, undocumented immigrants, Dreamers, assimilation, the border.
- **🎭 Hidden stakes:** national identity, opportunity, inequality, the labour market.
- **♻️ Paradox:** a nation of immigrants endlessly debating immigration.
- **🌍 Compare with:** France (assimilation), Canada (the mosaic), the UK (multiculturalism).
- **📖 Cultural references:** *The Great Gatsby*; *Of Mice and Men*; *The Godfather*; *Hamilton*.
- **✍️ Model exploitation:** ❌ *"Many immigrants live in the US."* → 🟠 *"The American Dream says anyone can succeed."* → ✅ *"The American Dream functions as a national myth of opportunity — one increasingly belied by stalled mobility and widening inequality."*
- **🇫🇷 French-speaker trap:** *"an immigrant"* (noun), not *"immigrated"*; *to demand* = exiger, not "demander".
- **❓ Colle questions:** *Is the American Dream still alive? · Is the US a land of opportunity or a fortress?*
- **📝 Ready plan ("The American Dream today"):** I. A founding and unifying myth — II. A promise belied by inequality — III. What the Dream means for new generations.

### 6. Guns (the 2nd Amendment)
- **⏱️ 30-second sentence:** *"The right to bear arms is written into the Constitution, which makes gun control one of America's most explosive debates."* — **Enriched:** *"Guns in America are less about safety than about identity, freedom and the Constitution."*
- **✅ Must know:** the 2nd Amendment; the NRA; recurring mass shootings; a deep partisan divide. **❌ Mistakes:** "Most Americans want to ban guns."
- **⚠️ Trap:** *it's only about safety* → it is also about **identity, freedom and the Constitution**.
- **📰 How it comes up:** documents on a mass shooting, gun-control debates, the NRA, a Supreme Court ruling, school safety. The press covers it because of **violence, freedom vs security, polarisation**.
- **🔄 Use it to talk about:** freedom vs security, the Constitution, violence, polarisation, individualism.
- **🎣 Hook:** *simple* — *"No debate divides America quite like guns."* / *sophisticated* — *"In America, the right to bear arms collides with the right to feel safe."*
- **🚪 Opening:** gun-control reform · the limits of the 2nd Amendment · the role of the courts.
- **🔑 10 words to drop:** the Second Amendment, the right to bear arms, gun control, a mass shooting, the NRA, a background check, an assault weapon, public safety, individual freedom, regulation.
- **🎭 Hidden stakes:** freedom vs security, the individual vs the community, constitutional interpretation.
- **♻️ Paradox:** the "land of the free" paralysed by gun violence.
- **🌍 Compare with:** the UK, Japan, Australia (strict control after mass shootings).
- **📖 Cultural references:** *Bowling for Columbine* (documentary).
- **✍️ Model exploitation:** ❌ *"Americans have a lot of guns."* → 🟠 *"The 2nd Amendment makes gun control hard."* → ✅ *"The gun debate shows how a constitutional right can freeze policy: for many Americans, guns are not a safety issue but an identity and a freedom."*
- **🇫🇷 French-speaker trap:** ❌ *"weapon control"* → ✔️ **gun control**; here *arms* = firearms.
- **❓ Colle questions:** *Is the right to bear arms a fundamental freedom or a public-health crisis? · Why is gun reform so hard?*
- **📝 Ready plan ("Guns in America"):** I. A constitutional and cultural right — II. A public-health crisis — III. Why reform is blocked.

### 7. Religion & the culture wars
- **⏱️ 30-second sentence:** *"The US is at once deeply religious and constitutionally secular, which fuels intense 'culture wars'."* — **Enriched:** *"Faith shapes American elections in a country whose Constitution separates church and state."*
- **✅ Must know:** the separation of church and state; evangelicals; the Bible Belt; abortion after *Roe v. Wade* (2022). **❌ Mistakes:** assuming the US is secular like France.
- **⚠️ Trap:** *church and state are separate, so religion is marginal* → religion is **central** to public life and politics.
- **📰 How it comes up:** documents on abortion (post-Roe), school prayer / curricula, LGBTQ+ rights, evangelicals and elections, the Supreme Court. The press covers it because of **values, identity, the role of religion in law**.
- **🔄 Use it to talk about:** religion, values, abortion, women's rights, the culture wars, identity.
- **🎣 Hook:** *simple* — *"America is at once one of the most religious and most modern of Western nations."* / *sophisticated* — *"In America, the wall between church and state has never stopped faith from shaping the ballot."*
- **🚪 Opening:** abortion after Roe · religion in schools · the Supreme Court and moral issues.
- **🔑 10 words to drop:** secularism, the separation of church and state, evangelicals, the Bible Belt, the culture wars, abortion, pro-life / pro-choice, Roe v. Wade, values, polarisation.
- **🎭 Hidden stakes:** values, identity, the role of religion in law (abortion, education).
- **♻️ Paradox:** a secular state where faith shapes elections.
- **🌍 Compare with:** France (laïcité), the UK (an established but secularised church).
- **📖 Cultural references:** *The Handmaid's Tale* (Margaret Atwood).
- **✍️ Model exploitation:** ❌ *"Americans are religious."* → 🟠 *"Religion plays a big role despite the separation of church and state."* → ✅ *"The overturning of Roe v. Wade shows how, in a constitutionally secular republic, religious values can still reshape the law."*
- **🇫🇷 French-speaker trap:** *secularism* is not an exact equivalent of *laïcité*; say *evangelical*, not "evangelist".
- **❓ Colle questions:** *Why does religion play such a role in US politics? · Should the Supreme Court decide moral issues?*
- **📝 Ready plan ("The culture wars"):** I. A religious and divided nation — II. Battlegrounds (abortion, education, identity) — III. Can common ground be found?

### 8. Healthcare & inequality
- **⏱️ 30-second sentence:** *"The world's richest country has no universal healthcare, which makes inequality a defining American issue."* — **Enriched:** *"Immense wealth sits alongside deep insecurity in American life."*
- **✅ Must know:** no universal coverage; *Obamacare* (the ACA), contested; medical debt; *the 1%*. **❌ Mistakes:** assuming a European-style public system.
- **⚠️ Trap:** *the US is rich, so people are well cared for* → access depends on **private insurance**; millions remain uninsured.
- **📰 How it comes up:** documents on medical debt, the uninsured, Obamacare, drug prices, the opioid crisis, the 1% / inequality. The press covers it because of **the role of the state, social solidarity, the limits of the market**.
- **🔄 Use it to talk about:** healthcare, inequality, the welfare state, the role of the market, social mobility.
- **🎣 Hook:** *simple* — *"In the world's richest country, getting sick can mean going bankrupt."* / *sophisticated* — *"American healthcare poses a stark question: is health a right or a product?"*
- **🚪 Opening:** universal-healthcare debates · drug prices · the future of Obamacare.
- **🔑 10 words to drop:** healthcare, health insurance, the uninsured, Obamacare (the ACA), medical debt, Medicare / Medicaid, inequality, the welfare state, social solidarity, the working poor.
- **🎭 Hidden stakes:** the role of the state, social solidarity, the limits of the market.
- **♻️ Paradox:** immense wealth alongside deep insecurity.
- **🌍 Compare with:** the UK (NHS), France, Canada (universal systems).
- **📖 Cultural references:** *Nickel and Dimed* (Barbara Ehrenreich); *Sicko* (documentary).
- **✍️ Model exploitation:** ❌ *"The US is rich but has inequality."* → 🟠 *"The US has no universal healthcare, unlike Europe."* → ✅ *"The absence of universal healthcare in the richest nation on earth reveals an American conviction that health is a personal responsibility rather than a collective right."*
- **🇫🇷 French-speaker trap:** say **health insurance**, not "mutuelle"; *to assist* = aider, not "assister à".
- **❓ Colle questions:** *Is healthcare a right or a commodity? · Why does the US resist universal coverage?*
- **📝 Ready plan ("Healthcare and inequality"):** I. A market-based model — II. Its human costs — III. Reform and its political obstacles.

### 9. America in the world
- **⏱️ 30-second sentence:** *"Since 1945 the US has been the world's leading power, torn between the temptations of isolationism and of intervention."* — **Enriched:** *"A superpower that is also, at times, war-weary and inward-looking."*
- **✅ Must know:** Cold War leadership; the *War on Terror*; rivalry with China; soft power. **❌ Mistakes:** seeing US foreign policy as one constant line.
- **⚠️ Trap:** *the US always intervenes* → it swings between **interventionism** and **isolationism** ("America First").
- **📰 How it comes up:** documents on a foreign conflict, US-China rivalry, NATO, "America First", trade wars, soft power. The press covers it because of **the global order, the China rivalry, the limits of American power**.
- **🔄 Use it to talk about:** geopolitics, globalisation, power, alliances, China.
- **🎣 Hook:** *simple* — *"Since 1945, the US has shaped the global order."* / *sophisticated* — *"America oscillates endlessly between leading the world and turning away from it."*
- **🚪 Opening:** the rise of China · the future of NATO · American decline or renewal.
- **🔑 10 words to drop:** a superpower, hegemony, isolationism, interventionism, soft power, hard power, an ally, multilateralism, "America First", the global order.
- **🎭 Hidden stakes:** the global order, the rivalry with China, the limits of American power.
- **♻️ Paradox:** a superpower that is also war-weary and inward-looking.
- **🌍 Compare with:** China (a rising rival), the EU (a partner-competitor).
- **📖 Cultural references:** news coverage of Iraq and Afghanistan; the many Hollywood war films.
- **✍️ Model exploitation:** ❌ *"The US is very powerful."* → 🟠 *"The US swings between intervention and isolationism."* → ✅ *"American foreign policy oscillates between the impulse to lead the world and the temptation to retreat from it — a tension now sharpened by the rise of China."*
- **🇫🇷 French-speaker trap:** *eventually* = finalement, not "éventuellement"; *to control* = maîtriser.
- **❓ Colle questions:** *Is the US still the world's policeman? · Is American power in decline? · Leadership or retreat?*
- **📝 Ready plan ("America's place in the world"):** I. The leader of the post-war order — II. Doubts and overreach — III. A contested superpower facing China.

# 🏆 IF YOU ONLY LEARN 20 THINGS

Short on time? Learn these twenty ideas cold — they are the highest-scoring points on almost any American document.
1. **Checks and balances** split power across three branches.
2. The **Constitution (1787)** is revered and constantly reinterpreted.
3. The **Bill of Rights** = the first 10 amendments (1791).
4. The **1st Amendment** = free speech; the **2nd** = the right to bear arms.
5. The President is elected **indirectly**, via the **Electoral College**.
6. You can **win the popular vote and still lose** the presidency (2000, 2016).
7. The **Supreme Court** (9 justices, for life) can strike down laws.
8. **Federalism:** states set their own laws (abortion, guns, the death penalty).
9. Slavery was abolished in **1865**; segregation lasted until the **1960s**.
10. The **Civil Rights Act (1964)** and **Voting Rights Act (1965)** ended legal segregation.
11. The US is a **"nation of immigrants"**: melting pot vs salad bowl.
12. The **American Dream** = success through merit — increasingly questioned.
13. There is **no universal healthcare**; Obamacare (the ACA) remains contested.
14. The US is **deeply religious yet constitutionally secular**.
15. **Roe v. Wade was overturned in 2022** — abortion returned to the states.
16. **Gun control** is blocked by the 2nd Amendment and deep division.
17. **"Congress"** = the House (435) + the Senate (100), not "Parliament".
18. Since **1945** the US has led the global order, between intervention and isolation.
19. Its **rivalry with China** defines 21st-century geopolitics.
20. **Soft power** (Hollywood, tech, the dollar, universities) underpins US influence.

> 👉 Learn these twenty cold and you can hold your own on almost any American document.
