Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse gender issues: feminism, gender equality, sexism, gender-based violence, consent, the pay gap, care work, reproductive rights, body image, transgender rights, representation, workplace culture and the political backlash around gender debates.

A. Core gender concepts, feminism and discrimination

gender

IPA: /ˈdʒendə/
Pronunciation: JEN-duh
French: genre

Definition: The social and cultural roles, expectations and identities associated with being male, female or non-binary.

Example: Gender shapes how people are treated in families, schools, workplaces and politics.

sex

IPA: /seks/
Pronunciation: seks
French: sexe biologique

Definition: Biological characteristics often classified as male or female.

Example: Sex and gender are related but not identical concepts.

gender identity

IPA: /ˌdʒendər aɪˈdentəti/
Pronunciation: JEN-duh eye-DEN-tuh-tee
French: identité de genre

Definition: A person’s internal sense of their gender.

Example: Gender identity may or may not match the sex assigned at birth.

gender role

IPA: /ˈdʒendə rəʊl/
Pronunciation: JEN-duh rohl
French: rôle de genre

Definition: A social expectation about how men or women should behave.

Example: Traditional gender roles can limit both women and men.

gender norm

IPA: /ˈdʒendə nɔːm/
Pronunciation: JEN-duh norm
French: norme de genre

Definition: A social rule about expected behaviour based on gender.

Example: Gender norms influence clothing, careers and family life.

gender stereotype

IPA: /ˈdʒendə ˌsteriətaɪp/
Pronunciation: JEN-duh STERR-ee-uh-type
French: stéréotype de genre

Definition: A fixed and oversimplified idea about men or women.

Example: Gender stereotypes discourage girls from STEM and boys from care work.

femininity

IPA: /ˌfeməˈnɪnəti/
Pronunciation: fem-uh-NIN-uh-tee
French: féminité

Definition: Qualities or behaviours socially associated with women or girls.

Example: Femininity is defined differently across cultures and periods.

masculinity

IPA: /ˌmæskjʊˈlɪnəti/
Pronunciation: mas-kyuh-LIN-uh-tee
French: masculinité

Definition: Qualities or behaviours socially associated with men or boys.

Example: Masculinity can be positive, but rigid masculinity may harm men and women.

toxic masculinity

IPA: /ˌtɒksɪk ˌmæskjʊˈlɪnəti/
Pronunciation: TOK-sik mas-kyuh-LIN-uh-tee
French: masculinité toxique

Definition: Harmful expectations of masculinity, such as aggression or emotional repression.

Example: Toxic masculinity can contribute to violence and poor mental health.

patriarchy

IPA: /ˈpeɪtriɑːki/
Pronunciation: PAY-tree-ar-kee
French: patriarcat

Definition: A social system in which men hold more power than women.

Example: Patriarchy affects laws, culture, work and family structures.

male-dominated

IPA: /ˌmeɪl ˈdɒmɪneɪtɪd/
Pronunciation: male DOM-i-nay-tid
French: dominé par les hommes

Definition: Controlled or mostly occupied by men.

Example: Politics and finance remain male-dominated fields.

female-dominated

IPA: /ˌfiːmeɪl ˈdɒmɪneɪtɪd/
Pronunciation: FEE-male DOM-i-nay-tid
French: dominé par les femmes

Definition: Mostly occupied by women.

Example: Care work is often female-dominated and undervalued.

gender equality

IPA: /ˌdʒendər iˈkwɒləti/
Pronunciation: JEN-duh i-KWOL-uh-tee
French: égalité des genres ; égalité femmes-hommes

Definition: Equal rights, opportunities and treatment regardless of gender.

Example: Gender equality is a core democratic and human-rights issue.

gender equity

IPA: /ˌdʒendər ˈekwəti/
Pronunciation: JEN-duh EK-wuh-tee
French: équité de genre

Definition: Fairness that takes gender-based barriers into account.

Example: Gender equity may require targeted policies.

women’s rights

IPA: /ˈwɪmɪnz raɪts/
Pronunciation: WIM-inz rites
French: droits des femmes

Definition: Rights protecting women’s equality, freedom and dignity.

Example: Women’s rights have been won through long political struggles.

feminism

IPA: /ˈfemɪnɪzəm/
Pronunciation: FEM-i-niz-uhm
French: féminisme

Definition: The belief and movement supporting gender equality and women’s rights.

Example: Feminism challenges social, political and economic inequality.

feminist

IPA: /ˈfemɪnɪst/
Pronunciation: FEM-i-nist
French: féministe

Definition: A person or idea supporting feminism.

Example: Feminist movements are diverse and sometimes disagree with each other.

liberal feminism

IPA: /ˌlɪbərəl ˈfemɪnɪzəm/
Pronunciation: LIB-uh-ruhl FEM-i-niz-uhm
French: féminisme libéral

Definition: A form of feminism focused on equal rights and opportunities within existing institutions.

Example: Liberal feminism often emphasises law, education and workplace equality.

radical feminism

IPA: /ˌrædɪkəl ˈfemɪnɪzəm/
Pronunciation: RAD-i-kuhl FEM-i-niz-uhm
French: féminisme radical

Definition: A form of feminism focusing on patriarchy as a deep social structure.

Example: Radical feminism argues that gender inequality is rooted in patriarchal power.

intersectional feminism

IPA: /ˌɪntəsekʃənəl ˈfemɪnɪzəm/
Pronunciation: IN-tuh-sek-shuh-nuhl FEM-i-niz-uhm
French: féminisme intersectionnel

Definition: Feminism that considers how gender combines with race, class, sexuality and disability.

Example: Intersectional feminism highlights differences between women’s experiences.

intersectionality

IPA: /ˌɪntəsekʃəˈnæləti/
Pronunciation: IN-tuh-sek-shuh-NAL-uh-tee
French: intersectionnalité

Definition: The way different forms of inequality combine and shape people’s lives.

Example: Intersectionality shows that sexism is experienced differently depending on race or class.

sexism

IPA: /ˈseksɪzəm/
Pronunciation: SEK-siz-uhm
French: sexisme

Definition: Prejudice or discrimination based on sex or gender.

Example: Sexism can be open, subtle or institutional.

misogyny

IPA: /mɪˈsɒdʒəni/
Pronunciation: mi-SOJ-uh-nee
French: misogynie

Definition: Hatred, contempt or deep prejudice against women.

Example: Online misogyny can silence women in public life.

misandry

IPA: /mɪˈsændri/
Pronunciation: mi-SAN-dree
French: misandrie

Definition: Hatred or prejudice against men.

Example: Misandry is sometimes invoked in debates about feminism.

gender discrimination

IPA: /ˈdʒendə dɪˌskrɪmɪˈneɪʃən/
Pronunciation: JEN-duh dis-krim-i-NAY-shuhn
French: discrimination de genre

Definition: Unfair treatment based on gender.

Example: Gender discrimination remains common in hiring and promotion.

sexual harassment

IPA: /ˌsekʃuəl ˈhærəsmənt/
Pronunciation: SEK-shoo-uhl HARR-uhs-muhnt
French: harcèlement sexuel

Definition: Unwanted sexual behaviour that intimidates, humiliates or violates someone.

Example: Sexual harassment can occur in workplaces, schools and public spaces.

harassment

IPA: /ˈhærəsmənt/
Pronunciation: HARR-uhs-muhnt
French: harcèlement

Definition: Repeated behaviour that intimidates or humiliates someone.

Example: Harassment often goes unreported because victims fear retaliation.

catcalling

IPA: /ˈkætkɔːlɪŋ/
Pronunciation: KAT-kaw-ling
French: harcèlement de rue ; interpellation sexiste

Definition: Unwanted comments or whistles directed at someone in public, usually women.

Example: Catcalling is often dismissed as harmless, but it restricts women’s freedom.

street harassment

IPA: /ˈstriːt ˈhærəsmənt/
Pronunciation: street HARR-uhs-muhnt
French: harcèlement de rue

Definition: Unwanted comments, gestures or behaviour in public spaces.

Example: Street harassment affects how women move through cities.

gender-based violence

IPA: /ˈdʒendə beɪst ˈvaɪələns/
Pronunciation: JEN-duh-bayst VY-uh-luhns
French: violence fondée sur le genre

Definition: Violence directed at someone because of their gender.

Example: Gender-based violence is both a private and public issue.

violence against women

IPA: /ˈvaɪələns əˌɡenst ˈwɪmɪn/
Pronunciation: VY-uh-luhns uh-GENST WIM-in
French: violence contre les femmes

Definition: Physical, sexual or psychological violence targeting women.

Example: Violence against women is a global human-rights concern.

domestic violence

IPA: /dəˌmestɪk ˈvaɪələns/
Pronunciation: duh-MES-tik VY-uh-luhns
French: violence domestique ; violences conjugales

Definition: Violence or abuse within a family or intimate relationship.

Example: Domestic violence often remains hidden behind closed doors.

intimate partner violence

IPA: /ˌɪntɪmət ˈpɑːtnə ˈvaɪələns/
Pronunciation: IN-ti-muht PART-nuh VY-uh-luhns
French: violence conjugale

Definition: Violence by a current or former partner.

Example: Intimate partner violence affects people across social classes.

coercive control

IPA: /kəʊˌɜːsɪv kənˈtrəʊl/
Pronunciation: koh-UR-siv kuhn-TROHL
French: contrôle coercitif

Definition: A pattern of controlling behaviour that isolates, frightens or dominates a partner.

Example: Coercive control can exist without visible physical violence.

femicide

IPA: /ˈfemɪsaɪd/
Pronunciation: FEM-i-side
French: féminicide

Definition: The killing of a woman because she is a woman, often by a partner or ex-partner.

Example: Femicide reveals the extreme end of gender-based violence.

rape

IPA: /reɪp/
Pronunciation: rayp
French: viol

Definition: Forced sexual intercourse or penetration without consent.

Example: Rape cases often expose myths about victims and consent.

sexual assault

IPA: /ˌsekʃuəl əˈsɔːlt/
Pronunciation: SEK-shoo-uhl uh-SAWLT
French: agression sexuelle

Definition: A sexual act committed without consent.

Example: Sexual assault survivors often face shame and disbelief.

consent

IPA: /kənˈsent/
Pronunciation: kuhn-SENT
French: consentement

Definition: Clear and voluntary agreement to something, especially sexual activity.

Example: Consent must be freely given and can be withdrawn.

rape culture

IPA: /ˈreɪp ˌkʌltʃə/
Pronunciation: rayp KUL-chuh
French: culture du viol

Definition: Social attitudes that normalise or excuse sexual violence.

Example: Rape culture shifts blame from perpetrators to victims.

victim-blaming

IPA: /ˈvɪktɪm ˌbleɪmɪŋ/
Pronunciation: VIK-tim BLAY-ming
French: culpabilisation de la victime

Definition: Blaming victims for harm done to them.

Example: Victim-blaming discourages survivors from reporting abuse.

survivor

IPA: /səˈvaɪvə/
Pronunciation: suh-VY-vuh
French: survivant ; survivante

Definition: A person who has lived through violence, abuse or trauma.

Example: Many activists use the word survivor rather than victim.

perpetrator

IPA: /ˈpɜːpətreɪtə/
Pronunciation: PUR-puh-tray-tuh
French: auteur de violences ; agresseur

Definition: A person who commits a crime or harmful act.

Example: Perpetrators are often known to their victims.

restraining order

IPA: /rɪˈstreɪnɪŋ ˌɔːdə/
Pronunciation: ri-STRAY-ning OR-duh
French: ordonnance d’éloignement

Definition: A court order preventing someone from contacting or approaching another person.

Example: A restraining order can protect victims from further abuse.

safe house

IPA: /ˈseɪf haʊs/
Pronunciation: sayf howss
French: refuge ; hébergement sécurisé

Definition: A place where victims of violence can stay safely.

Example: Safe houses are essential for women fleeing domestic violence.

gender pay gap

IPA: /ˌdʒendə ˈpeɪ ɡæp/
Pronunciation: JEN-duh pay gap
French: écart salarial entre hommes et femmes

Definition: The difference between men’s and women’s average earnings.

Example: The gender pay gap reflects unequal work, promotion and care responsibilities.

B. Violence, consent, work and care inequalities

equal pay

IPA: /ˌiːkwəl ˈpeɪ/
Pronunciation: EE-kwuhl pay
French: égalité salariale

Definition: The principle of paying people equally for equal work.

Example: Equal pay laws do not always eliminate pay gaps.

pay transparency

IPA: /ˈpeɪ trænsˌpærənsi/
Pronunciation: pay trans-PARR-uhn-see
French: transparence salariale

Definition: Openness about salaries or pay structures.

Example: Pay transparency can expose discrimination.

glass ceiling

IPA: /ˌɡlɑːs ˈsiːlɪŋ/
Pronunciation: glass SEE-ling
French: plafond de verre

Definition: An invisible barrier preventing women or minorities from reaching top positions.

Example: The glass ceiling remains strong in boardrooms and politics.

glass cliff

IPA: /ˌɡlɑːs ˈklɪf/
Pronunciation: glass klif
French: falaise de verre

Definition: A situation where women are promoted to leadership during crisis, when failure is more likely.

Example: The glass cliff can set women leaders up to fail.

sticky floor

IPA: /ˌstɪki ˈflɔː/
Pronunciation: STIK-ee floor
French: plancher collant

Definition: A situation where workers, often women, remain trapped in low-paid jobs.

Example: The sticky floor keeps many women in poorly paid care work.

motherhood penalty

IPA: /ˈmʌðəhʊd ˌpenəlti/
Pronunciation: MUTH-uh-hood PEN-uhl-tee
French: pénalité de maternité

Definition: The career and pay disadvantage experienced by mothers.

Example: The motherhood penalty widens gender inequality over a lifetime.

fatherhood premium

IPA: /ˈfɑːðəhʊd ˌpriːmiəm/
Pronunciation: FAH-thuh-hood PREE-mee-uhm
French: prime à la paternité

Definition: The tendency for fathers to gain status or earnings at work.

Example: The fatherhood premium contrasts sharply with the motherhood penalty.

care work

IPA: /ˈkeə wɜːk/
Pronunciation: care work
French: travail de soin ; care

Definition: Work involving caring for children, elderly people, sick people or disabled people.

Example: Care work is essential but often undervalued.

unpaid care work

IPA: /ʌnˌpeɪd ˈkeə wɜːk/
Pronunciation: un-PAYD care work
French: travail domestique et de soin non rémunéré

Definition: Care work done without pay, often within families.

Example: Women still perform most unpaid care work.

emotional labour

IPA: /ɪˌməʊʃənəl ˈleɪbə/
Pronunciation: i-MOH-shuh-nuhl LAY-buh
French: travail émotionnel

Definition: Managing emotions and relationships as part of work or family life.

Example: Emotional labour is often expected from women.

domestic labour

IPA: /dəˌmestɪk ˈleɪbə/
Pronunciation: duh-MES-tik LAY-buh
French: travail domestique

Definition: Household work such as cleaning, cooking and organising family life.

Example: Domestic labour remains unevenly shared.

double burden

IPA: /ˌdʌbəl ˈbɜːdən/
Pronunciation: DUB-uhl BUR-duhn
French: double journée

Definition: The burden of paid work plus unpaid domestic or care work.

Example: The double burden affects many working mothers.

mental load

IPA: /ˈmentəl ləʊd/
Pronunciation: MEN-tuhl lohd
French: charge mentale

Definition: The invisible planning and organising work required to manage family life.

Example: The mental load is often carried by women.

work-life balance

IPA: /ˌwɜːk laɪf ˈbæləns/
Pronunciation: work-life BAL-uhns
French: équilibre vie professionnelle-vie personnelle

Example: Work-life balance is harder when care duties are unequal.

flexible working

French: travail flexible

part-time work

French: travail à temps partiel

Definition: Work done for fewer hours than a full-time job.

career break

IPA: /kəˈrɪə breɪk/
Pronunciation: kuh-REER brayk
French: interruption de carrière

Definition: A period away from work, often for childcare or caring responsibilities.

Example: Career breaks can reduce long-term earnings.

parental leave

IPA: /pəˌrentəl ˈliːv/
Pronunciation: puh-REN-tuhl leev
French: congé parental

Definition: Time off work to care for a new child.

Example: Parental leave policy shapes gender equality at home and work.

maternity leave

IPA: /məˈtɜːnəti liːv/
Pronunciation: muh-TUR-nuh-tee leev
French: congé maternité

Definition: Time off work for a mother before or after childbirth.

Example: Paid maternity leave protects mothers’ health and employment.

paternity leave

IPA: /pəˈtɜːnəti liːv/
Pronunciation: puh-TUR-nuh-tee leev
French: congé paternité

Definition: Time off work for a father after a child is born or adopted.

childcare

IPA: /ˈtʃaɪldkeə/

French: garde d’enfants

affordable childcare

Pronunciation: uh-FOR-duh-buhl CHYLD-care
French: garde d’enfants abordable

Definition: Childcare that families can pay for without financial hardship.

Example: Affordable childcare can reduce the gender employment gap.

C. Representation, leadership and workplace culture

role model

IPA: /ˈrəʊl ˌmɒdəl/
Pronunciation: rohl MOD-uhl
French: modèle ; exemple à suivre

Definition: A person whose behaviour or success inspires others.

token woman

IPA: /ˌtəʊkən ˈwʊmən/
Pronunciation: TOH-kuhn WUUM-uhn
French: femme alibi

mansplaining

IPA: /ˈmænˌspleɪnɪŋ/
Pronunciation: MAN-splay-ning
French: mecsplication ; mansplaining

manterrupting

IPA: /ˈmæntəˌrʌptɪŋ/
Pronunciation: MAN-tuh-rup-ting
French: interruption masculine répétée

Definition: Men repeatedly interrupting women in conversation.

Example: Manterrupting can silence women in meetings.

bro culture

IPA: /ˈbrəʊ ˌkʌltʃə/
Pronunciation: bro KUL-chuh
French: culture masculine excluante ; bro culture

Definition: A male-dominated workplace culture associated with exclusion or sexism.
