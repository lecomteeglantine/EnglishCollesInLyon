A. Lesson — Where do adjectives go?

Before the noun

French: avant le nom

Rule / explanation: In English, adjectives usually come before the noun they describe.

Example / answer: The author presents a convincing argument.

After linking verbs

French: après les verbes d’état

Rule / explanation: Adjectives can come after verbs such as be, seem, become, remain and appear.

Example / answer: The argument is convincing. The issue remains controversial.

No plural -s

French: pas de pluriel

Rule / explanation: Adjectives do not take plural -s in English.

Example / answer: serious problems, not seriouses problems.

Several adjectives

French: plusieurs adjectifs

Rule / explanation: More than two adjectives are possible, but too many can sound heavy.

Example / answer: a major political crisis is clearer than a major complex long-term political crisis.

B. Standard adjective order

Opinion

French: opinion / jugement

Rule / explanation: Opinion adjectives usually come before more factual adjectives.

Example / answer: a convincing economic argument.

Size and age

French: taille et âge

Rule / explanation: Size usually comes before age.

Example / answer: a major recent scandal.

Origin

French: origine

Rule / explanation: Origin adjectives often come close to the noun.

Example / answer: a controversial British policy.

Purpose

French: fonction / usage

Rule / explanation: Purpose adjectives come just before the noun.

Example / answer: an ambitious housing policy.

C. Academic adjective combinations

Economic / political / social

French: économique / politique / social

Rule / explanation: These adjectives are common in CPGE analysis and usually come close to the noun.

Example / answer: a serious social problem; a major political issue.

Long-term / short-term

French: à long terme / à court terme

Rule / explanation: Use a hyphen when the compound adjective comes before the noun.

Example / answer: a long-term strategy; short-term consequences.

Highly controversial

French: très controversé

Rule / explanation: Use adverbs such as highly, deeply or increasingly to modify adjectives.

Example / answer: a highly controversial reform.

Increasingly important

French: de plus en plus important

Rule / explanation: Increasingly is very useful for trends and data commentary.

Example / answer: an increasingly important political question.

D. Participial adjectives

-ing adjectives

French: adjectifs en -ing

Rule / explanation: -ing adjectives describe the thing that causes a feeling or effect.

Example / answer: a worrying trend; a convincing argument.

-ed adjectives

French: adjectifs en -ed

Rule / explanation: -ed adjectives often describe the person or group affected.

Example / answer: concerned voters; disappointed citizens.

Divided / polarised

French: divisé / polarisé

Rule / explanation: Past participles often describe social or political states.

Example / answer: a divided society; a polarised electorate.

Rising / growing

French: croissant

Rule / explanation: Present participles often describe trends.

Example / answer: rising inequality; growing distrust.

E. Commas between adjectives

Coordinate adjectives

French: adjectifs coordonnés

Rule / explanation: Use a comma when adjectives are equal and could be linked by and.

Example / answer: a clear, persuasive argument.

Cumulative adjectives

French: adjectifs cumulatifs

Rule / explanation: Do not use a comma when adjectives build a single noun phrase in order.

Example / answer: a major political crisis.

Test with and

French: test avec and

Rule / explanation: If and sounds natural between adjectives, a comma may be possible.

Example / answer: a clear and persuasive argument → a clear, persuasive argument.

Do not over-punctuate

French: ne pas surponctuer

Rule / explanation: Many academic adjective combinations do not need commas.

Example / answer: a long-term economic strategy, not a long-term, economic strategy.

F. Adjectives for essay nuance

Convincing / persuasive

French: convaincant

Rule / explanation: Useful to evaluate arguments.

Example / answer: The article presents a persuasive argument.

Limited / flawed

French: limité / imparfait

Rule / explanation: Useful for careful criticism.

Example / answer: The author’s evidence is limited.

Nuanced / balanced

French: nuancé / équilibré

Rule / explanation: Useful to praise a complex analysis.

Example / answer: The article offers a nuanced analysis.

Simplistic / misleading

French: simpliste / trompeur

Rule / explanation: Useful for stronger criticism.

Example / answer: This is a misleading comparison.

G. Typical mistakes by French speakers

French word order

French: ordre français

Rule / explanation: Do not place the adjective after the noun as in French.

Example / answer: Wrong: a reform important. Correct: an important reform.

Plural adjectives

French: adjectifs au pluriel

Rule / explanation: English adjectives do not become plural.

Example / answer: Wrong: differents arguments. Correct: different arguments.

Missing hyphen

French: trait d’union manquant

Rule / explanation: Hyphenate compound adjectives before nouns.

Example / answer: a long-term solution; a data-driven argument.

Boring vs bored

French: -ing vs -ed

Rule / explanation: -ing describes the cause; -ed describes the affected person.

Example / answer: The debate is worrying. Voters are worried.
