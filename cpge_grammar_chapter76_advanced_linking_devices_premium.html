Chapter 76 — Advanced Linking Devices
B2 → C1 language for exact logical relationships, sophisticated transitions and high-level CPGE synthesis.

Chapter objective

This premium grammar chapter develops advanced control of linking devices: addition, contrast, concession, cause, consequence, purpose, result, reformulation, condition, exception, sequencing and source synthesis.

The golden rule

Choose the relationship first, then choose the linker.
Meaning → grammar pattern → punctuation → position
Clause linkeralthough + subject + verb
Prepositional linkerdespite + noun / gerund
Sentence connectorhowever / therefore / consequently
Main warninga sophisticated linker cannot repair weak logic

A. Lesson — What are advanced linking devices?

Linking device

Rule / explanation: A linking device shows the logical relationship between ideas, clauses, sentences or paragraphs.

Example / answer: The reform reduced costs; however, it also widened regional inequalities.

Advanced use

Rule / explanation: Advanced linking means choosing the exact relationship rather than inserting a sophisticated connector mechanically.

Example / answer: Consequently expresses result, whereas nevertheless expresses concession.

Grammar matters

Rule / explanation: Different linkers require different structures: clause, noun phrase, gerund, punctuation or inversion.

Example / answer: Although the policy was costly... / Despite its cost...

CPGE relevance

Rule / explanation: Accurate linking devices strengthen essays, summaries, syntheses, article commentary and oral argument.

Example / answer: The two sources agree on the diagnosis; they differ, however, on the appropriate response.

B. Addition and reinforcement

Furthermore

Rule / explanation: Add a substantial point that develops the same argument.

Example / answer: Furthermore, the policy failed to improve rural access.

Moreover

Rule / explanation: Add information that reinforces the preceding claim.

Example / answer: Moreover, implementation costs exceeded the original forecast.

In addition

Rule / explanation: Introduce a neutral extra point.

Example / answer: In addition, local authorities received no additional funding.

What is more

Rule / explanation: Add an emphatic point; it is more natural in speech and persuasive prose than in highly formal writing.

Example / answer: What is more, the consultation process excluded rural communities.

Not only...but also

Rule / explanation: Use this correlative structure to add and emphasise two parallel elements.

Example / answer: The reform not only reduced local autonomy but also increased administrative costs.

C. Parallel addition

Likewise

Rule / explanation: Introduce a similar point, action or pattern.

Example / answer: Urban services deteriorated; likewise, rural transport declined.

Similarly

Rule / explanation: Show that a comparable statement applies to another case.

Example / answer: The first survey revealed distrust. Similarly, the second identified declining confidence.

By the same token

Rule / explanation: Extend the same reasoning to a parallel case.

Example / answer: If transparency strengthens legitimacy, by the same token, secrecy is likely to weaken it.

Equally

Rule / explanation: Add a point of comparable importance.

Example / answer: Economic feasibility matters; equally important is public legitimacy.

Correspondingly

Rule / explanation: Show that one change is matched by another.

Example / answer: Funding increased, and waiting times correspondingly declined.

D. Contrast refined

However

Rule / explanation: Introduce a direct contrast or limitation.

Example / answer: The reform reduced costs. However, access remained unequal.

Nevertheless

Rule / explanation: Maintain a conclusion despite an obstacle or contrary fact.

Example / answer: The sample is narrow. Nevertheless, the findings remain significant.

Nonetheless

Rule / explanation: A close formal equivalent of nevertheless.

Example / answer: Implementation was delayed; nonetheless, the programme eventually met its target.

By contrast

Rule / explanation: Compare clearly opposed situations, sources or trends.

Example / answer: Urban access improved. By contrast, rural provision deteriorated.

Conversely

Rule / explanation: Introduce an opposite relationship in which the direction is reversed.

Example / answer: Greater transparency may increase trust; conversely, secrecy may intensify suspicion.

E. Whereas, while and whilst

Whereas

Rule / explanation: Contrast two facts, groups or positions within one sentence.

Example / answer: Urban areas benefited, whereas rural regions lost services.

While as contrast

Rule / explanation: Use while to contrast two simultaneous or comparable facts.

Example / answer: While the first article is optimistic, the second predicts prolonged instability.

Whilst

Rule / explanation: Whilst is a more formal British alternative to while, but should not be overused.

Example / answer: Whilst costs declined, regional inequality increased.

No comma splice

Rule / explanation: Whereas and while introduce dependent clauses and require correct sentence structure.

Example / answer: Correct: Costs fell, whereas access remained unequal.

Potential ambiguity

Rule / explanation: While can mean during the time that or although; use whereas when contrast must be unmistakable.

Example / answer: Whereas the first source focuses on cost, the second examines legitimacy.

F. Concession with although and even though

Although

Rule / explanation: Introduce a concession that does not cancel the main clause.

Example / answer: Although the reform reduced costs, it remained unpopular.

Though

Rule / explanation: Though is slightly less formal and may appear at the end in speech.

Example / answer: The evidence is limited. The argument is persuasive, though.

Even though

Rule / explanation: Express a stronger or more surprising concession.

Example / answer: Even though the economy recovered, public trust continued to fall.

Clause required

Rule / explanation: These conjunctions are followed by a subject and a finite verb.

Example / answer: Although the policy was expensive...

No but

Rule / explanation: Do not use but in the main clause after although.

Example / answer: Wrong: Although costs fell, but access remained unequal.

G. Despite and in spite of

Despite + noun

Rule / explanation: Use despite before a noun phrase.

Example / answer: Despite the reform’s high cost, its impact remained limited.

Despite + gerund

Rule / explanation: Use despite before an -ing form.

Example / answer: Despite reducing costs, the policy failed to improve access.

In spite of

Rule / explanation: A close equivalent followed by a noun phrase or gerund.

Example / answer: In spite of strong opposition, the bill was adopted.

The fact that

Rule / explanation: Use despite/in spite of the fact that before a complete clause.

Example / answer: Despite the fact that costs fell, public support declined.

Common error

Rule / explanation: Do not write despite of.

Example / answer: Correct: despite the evidence / in spite of the evidence.

H. Concession through sentence connectors

Admittedly

Rule / explanation: Acknowledge a valid opposing point before maintaining your argument.

Example / answer: Admittedly, the reform improved short-term efficiency.

Granted

Rule / explanation: Accept a point provisionally, often before qualification.

Example / answer: Granted, the proposal is ambitious; however, its funding remains uncertain.

To be sure

Rule / explanation: Acknowledge a fact while preparing a limitation or counterpoint.

Example / answer: To be sure, inflation has fallen, but household costs remain high.

That said

Rule / explanation: Qualify or counter the previous statement.

Example / answer: The policy is innovative. That said, it may be difficult to implement.

All the same

Rule / explanation: Maintain a point despite a concession; more natural in speech than formal essays.

Example / answer: The data are incomplete. All the same, the trend deserves attention.

I. Cause with conjunctions

Because

Rule / explanation: Introduce a direct and explicit cause.

Example / answer: The programme failed because funding was insufficient.

Since

Rule / explanation: Introduce a cause presented as known or less controversial.

Example / answer: Since regional data are incomplete, the conclusion must remain cautious.

As

Rule / explanation: Introduce background cause, often with less emphasis than because.

Example / answer: As implementation was delayed, the expected savings did not materialise.

Given that

Rule / explanation: Introduce a cause or premise already established.

Example / answer: Given that public support is weak, rapid implementation would be risky.

Now that

Rule / explanation: Introduce a cause resulting from a changed present situation.

Example / answer: Now that the report has been published, Parliament can examine the evidence.

J. Cause with prepositional phrases

Because of

Rule / explanation: Use before a noun phrase or gerund.

Example / answer: The vote was delayed because of legal uncertainty.

Due to

Rule / explanation: Use primarily after be or to modify a noun in careful formal English.

Example / answer: The delay was due to legal uncertainty.

Owing to

Rule / explanation: A formal equivalent of because of.

Example / answer: Owing to insufficient funding, several clinics closed.

As a result of

Rule / explanation: Emphasise a cause that produced a consequence.

Example / answer: As a result of the cuts, waiting times increased.

On account of

Rule / explanation: A formal or administrative phrase for cause.

Example / answer: The hearing was postponed on account of new evidence.

K. Result and consequence

Therefore

Rule / explanation: Introduce a logical consequence or conclusion.

Example / answer: Funding was reduced; therefore, waiting times increased.

Consequently

Rule / explanation: Express a formal resulting effect.

Example / answer: The programme lost staff. Consequently, access deteriorated.

As a result

Rule / explanation: Introduce an observable consequence.

Example / answer: Several clinics closed. As a result, patients travelled farther.

Thus

Rule / explanation: Express result, inference or manner concisely.

Example / answer: The reform excluded rural areas, thus weakening its legitimacy.

Hence

Rule / explanation: Introduce a formal conclusion or consequence, often in compact prose.

Example / answer: The evidence remains incomplete, hence the need for further research.

L. So, therefore and punctuation

So

Rule / explanation: So is common in speech and less formal writing.

Example / answer: Funding was cut, so waiting times increased.

Therefore

Rule / explanation: Therefore is a sentence adverb and requires appropriate punctuation.

Example / answer: Funding was cut; therefore, waiting times increased.

Comma splice warning

Rule / explanation: Do not join two independent clauses with only a comma before therefore.

Example / answer: Wrong: Funding was cut, therefore waiting times increased.

Initial position

Rule / explanation: Therefore may begin a sentence.

Example / answer: Therefore, a revised funding model is required.

Mid-position

Rule / explanation: It may also appear after the subject or auxiliary.

Example / answer: The government must therefore revise the timetable.

M. Purpose

To / in order to

Rule / explanation: Use an infinitive when the subject of both actions is the same.

Example / answer: The government increased funding to reduce waiting times.

So as to

Rule / explanation: A formal alternative to in order to.

Example / answer: The agency revised the rules so as to improve transparency.

So that

Rule / explanation: Use before a clause, especially when the subjects differ.

Example / answer: The rules were simplified so that local authorities could respond more quickly.

In order that

Rule / explanation: A highly formal clause linker, less common in modern English.

Example / answer: Safeguards were introduced in order that citizens might retain control over their data.

With a view to

Rule / explanation: Use before a noun or gerund to express formal long-term purpose.

Example / answer: The system was redesigned with a view to improving access.

N. Result clauses

So...that

Rule / explanation: Link a degree to its consequence.

Example / answer: The timetable was so ambitious that implementation failed.

Such...that

Rule / explanation: Use before a noun phrase followed by a result clause.

Example / answer: It was such a controversial measure that several ministers resigned.

Too...to

Rule / explanation: Express excess that prevents a result.

Example / answer: The proposal was too costly to implement nationally.

Enough to

Rule / explanation: Express sufficient degree for a result.

Example / answer: The evidence was strong enough to justify an inquiry.

Thereby

Rule / explanation: Express the means by which the previous action causes a result.

Example / answer: The reform centralised decision-making, thereby reducing local autonomy.

O. Exemplification and illustration

For example

Rule / explanation: Introduce one illustration of a general claim.

Example / answer: Several regions lost services. For example, three rural clinics closed.

For instance

Rule / explanation: A close formal alternative.

Example / answer: Some digital reforms improved access; for instance, remote consultations reduced travel.

Such as

Rule / explanation: Introduce examples inside a noun phrase.

Example / answer: Public services such as healthcare and transport were affected.

Namely

Rule / explanation: Specify the exact item or items previously announced.

Example / answer: One issue remains, namely the lack of long-term funding.

A case in point

Rule / explanation: Introduce a particularly relevant example.

Example / answer: Scotland is a case in point.

P. Reformulation

In other words

Rule / explanation: Restate an idea more clearly.

Example / answer: The policy lacked legitimacy; in other words, citizens did not regard it as justified.

That is to say

Rule / explanation: Provide an explicit formal reformulation.

Example / answer: The measure was regressive, that is to say, it affected poorer households more severely.

More precisely

Rule / explanation: Narrow or correct the previous formulation.

Example / answer: The problem concerns access, or more precisely, unequal regional access.

To put it differently

Rule / explanation: Offer another perspective on the same point.

Example / answer: To put it differently, efficiency was achieved at the expense of fairness.

Rather

Rule / explanation: Correct or refine the previous expression.

Example / answer: The reform did not reduce inequality; rather, it redistributed it.

Q. Condition and limitation

Provided that

Rule / explanation: Introduce a necessary condition for an outcome.

Example / answer: The programme may succeed provided that funding remains stable.

Providing that

Rule / explanation: A slightly less formal equivalent.

Example / answer: The coalition may survive, providing that its members remain united.

As long as

Rule / explanation: Express a continuing condition.

Example / answer: The system can function as long as local authorities cooperate.

Only if

Rule / explanation: Introduce a necessary condition.

Example / answer: The reform can succeed only if implementation improves.

Unless

Rule / explanation: Introduce a negatively framed condition.

Example / answer: The policy will fail unless regional disparities are addressed.

R. Exception and reservation

Except that

Rule / explanation: Introduce an exception in clause form.

Example / answer: The two proposals are similar, except that the second provides stronger safeguards.

Apart from

Rule / explanation: Exclude or add one element depending on context.

Example / answer: Apart from its high cost, the programme has few major weaknesses.

With the exception of

Rule / explanation: A formal phrase identifying an excluded case.

Example / answer: All regions improved, with the exception of the north-east.

Save for

Rule / explanation: A formal or literary equivalent of except for.

Example / answer: The report is comprehensive, save for its treatment of rural access.

Subject to

Rule / explanation: Express a condition, limitation or formal reservation.

Example / answer: The agreement will take effect subject to parliamentary approval.

S. Comparison and correspondence

Compared with

Rule / explanation: Introduce the reference point for comparison.

Example / answer: Compared with 2020, turnout was significantly lower.

In comparison with

Rule / explanation: A more explicit comparative phrase.

Example / answer: In comparison with the national programme, the local scheme was more effective.

In the same way

Rule / explanation: Show similarity in process or consequence.

Example / answer: Public trust shapes compliance; in the same way, perceived unfairness encourages resistance.

Just as...so

Rule / explanation: Create a balanced formal analogy.

Example / answer: Just as transparency can strengthen trust, so secrecy can weaken legitimacy.

The more...the more

Rule / explanation: Express a proportional relationship.

Example / answer: The longer the delay continues, the more expensive implementation becomes.

T. Sequencing and transitions

Initially

Rule / explanation: Introduce the first stage of a process or argument.

Example / answer: Initially, the reform attracted broad support.

Subsequently

Rule / explanation: Introduce a later development.

Example / answer: The proposal was subsequently amended.

Meanwhile

Rule / explanation: Introduce a simultaneous development.

Example / answer: Parliament debated the bill. Meanwhile, public opposition intensified.

Thereafter

Rule / explanation: A formal word meaning after that.

Example / answer: The committee published its report and thereafter met only twice.

Ultimately

Rule / explanation: Introduce the final outcome or deepest conclusion.

Example / answer: Ultimately, the policy’s success depends on public legitimacy.

U. Linkers in source comparison and synthesis

Both sources

Rule / explanation: Introduce shared content without repeating each source separately.

Example / answer: Both sources identify declining trust as a central concern.

While X, Y

Rule / explanation: Compare different emphases efficiently.

Example / answer: While the first article focuses on economic causes, the second emphasises political responsibility.

Similarly

Rule / explanation: Identify a meaningful point of convergence.

Example / answer: Similarly, both writers question the official forecast.

By contrast

Rule / explanation: Identify a clear divergence.

Example / answer: By contrast, they disagree on whether regulation is desirable.

Taken together

Rule / explanation: Synthesize the combined significance of several documents.

Example / answer: Taken together, the sources suggest that the crisis is both economic and institutional.

V. Common mistakes and exercises

Connector accumulation

Rule / explanation: Do not place several equivalent connectors together.

Example / answer: Wrong: However, nevertheless, the policy failed.

Wrong grammatical pattern

Rule / explanation: Check whether the linker needs a clause, noun phrase or gerund.

Example / answer: Although it was costly / despite its cost.

False relationship

Rule / explanation: A sophisticated connector cannot compensate for incorrect logic.

Example / answer: Use therefore only when the second idea follows from the first.

Over-formality

Rule / explanation: Do not force rare linkers into every paragraph.

Example / answer: Choose the clearest accurate linker.

Exercise 1

Rule / explanation: Combine with although: The reform reduced costs. It remained unpopular.

Example / answer: Although the reform reduced costs, it remained unpopular.

Exercise 2

Rule / explanation: Combine with despite: The evidence was limited. The report was influential.

Example / answer: Despite the limited evidence, the report was influential.

Exercise 3

Rule / explanation: Correct the punctuation: Funding was cut, therefore waiting times rose.

Example / answer: Funding was cut; therefore, waiting times rose.

Exercise 4

Rule / explanation: Express purpose: The government simplified the form. Citizens could apply more easily.

Example / answer: The government simplified the form so that citizens could apply more easily.

Exercise 5

Rule / explanation: Translate: Les deux articles s’accordent sur le diagnostic ; en revanche, ils proposent des solutions radicalement différentes.

Example / answer: The two articles agree on the diagnosis; by contrast, they propose radically different solutions.

Mini concours challenge

Rule / explanation: Write a synthesis paragraph using concession, comparison, cause, result and a final synthesis connector.

Example / answer: Although both sources identify a decline in public trust, they explain it differently. The first attributes the trend to economic insecurity, whereas the second emphasises institutional opacity. Because these factors may reinforce one another, a purely economic response is unlikely to succeed. Consequently, reform must address both material conditions and democratic accountability. Taken together, the documents present the crisis as multidimensional.

Mini recap
