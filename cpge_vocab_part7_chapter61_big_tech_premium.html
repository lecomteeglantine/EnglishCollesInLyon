This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse Big Tech: platform power, monopoly, network effects, antitrust, digital regulation, data privacy, surveillance capitalism, cloud infrastructure, AI competition, platform labour, digital rights, online harms, sustainability and the challenge of controlling private companies that now shape public life.

A. Core Big Tech, platforms and innovation vocabulary

Big Tech

IPA: /ˌbɪɡ ˈtek/
Pronunciation: big tek
French: grandes entreprises technologiques ; Big Tech

Definition: The largest and most powerful technology companies, especially those dominating digital markets.

Example: Big Tech has become a central political and economic issue.

technology giant

IPA: /tekˈnɒlədʒi ˈdʒaɪənt/
Pronunciation: tek-NOL-uh-jee JY-uhnt
French: géant technologique

Definition: A very large and influential technology company.

Example: Technology giants control key digital infrastructure.

tech company

IPA: /ˈtek ˌkʌmpəni/
Pronunciation: tek KUM-puh-nee
French: entreprise technologique

Definition: A company producing or selling technology products or services.

Example: Tech companies increasingly shape everyday life.

platform company

IPA: /ˈplætfɔːm ˌkʌmpəni/
Pronunciation: PLAT-form KUM-puh-nee
French: entreprise de plateforme

Definition: A company whose business relies on connecting users, sellers, advertisers or developers through a platform.

Example: Platform companies benefit from network effects.

digital platform

IPA: /ˌdɪdʒɪtəl ˈplætfɔːm/
Pronunciation: DIJ-i-tuhl PLAT-form
French: plateforme numérique

Definition: An online system enabling communication, commerce, services or content distribution.

Example: Digital platforms organise markets and attention.

online platform

IPA: /ˌɒnlaɪn ˈplætfɔːm/
Pronunciation: ON-line PLAT-form
French: plateforme en ligne

Definition: A digital platform accessed through the internet.

Example: Online platforms have become unavoidable intermediaries.

platform economy

IPA: /ˈplætfɔːm iˌkɒnəmi/
Pronunciation: PLAT-form i-KON-uh-mee
French: économie de plateforme

Definition: An economy organised through digital platforms that connect users, workers or businesses.

Example: The platform economy changes work, trade and regulation.

digital economy

IPA: /ˌdɪdʒɪtəl iˈkɒnəmi/
Pronunciation: DIJ-i-tuhl i-KON-uh-mee
French: économie numérique

Definition: Economic activity based on digital technologies, data and online services.

Example: The digital economy depends on data and connectivity.

tech ecosystem

IPA: /ˈtek ˌiːkəʊˌsɪstəm/
Pronunciation: tek EE-koh-SIS-tuhm
French: écosystème technologique

Definition: A network of companies, investors, developers and users around technology.

Example: Silicon Valley is a famous tech ecosystem.

Silicon Valley

IPA: /ˌsɪlɪkən ˈvæli/
Pronunciation: SIL-i-kuhn VAL-ee
French: Silicon Valley

Definition: The California region associated with major technology firms and start-ups.

Example: Silicon Valley symbolises innovation and venture capital.

start-up

IPA: /ˈstɑːt ʌp/
Pronunciation: START-up
French: start-up ; jeune pousse

Definition: A young company designed for rapid growth, often in technology.

Example: Many Big Tech companies began as start-ups.

scale-up

IPA: /ˈskeɪl ʌp/
Pronunciation: SKAYL-up
French: entreprise en forte croissance

Definition: A company growing rapidly after the start-up stage.

Example: A successful start-up can become a scale-up.

unicorn

IPA: /ˈjuːnɪkɔːn/
Pronunciation: YOO-ni-korn
French: licorne

Definition: A privately held start-up valued at over one billion dollars.

Example: Unicorns attract major venture capital.

venture capital

IPA: /ˈventʃə ˌkæpɪtəl/
Pronunciation: VEN-chuh KAP-i-tuhl
French: capital-risque

Definition: Investment in high-growth start-ups in exchange for equity.

Example: Venture capital fuels the tech industry.

VC

IPA: /ˌviː ˈsiː/
Pronunciation: vee-see
French: capital-risque ; investisseur en capital-risque

Definition: Abbreviation for venture capital or venture capitalist.

Example: VC funding can help a platform grow quickly.

founder

IPA: /ˈfaʊndə/
Pronunciation: FOWN-duh
French: fondateur ; fondatrice

Definition: A person who creates a company or organisation.

Example: Tech founders often become public figures.

entrepreneur

IPA: /ˌɒntrəprəˈnɜː/
Pronunciation: ON-truh-pruh-NUR
French: entrepreneur

Definition: A person who creates and develops a business.

Example: Entrepreneurs are celebrated in start-up culture.

innovation

IPA: /ˌɪnəˈveɪʃən/
Pronunciation: in-uh-VAY-shuhn
French: innovation

Definition: The creation or use of new ideas, technologies or methods.

Example: Big Tech presents itself as a driver of innovation.

disruption

IPA: /dɪsˈrʌpʃən/
Pronunciation: dis-RUP-shuhn
French: disruption ; bouleversement

Definition: Radical change to an existing market, industry or practice.

Example: Tech companies often promise disruption.

disruptive innovation

IPA: /dɪsˌrʌptɪv ˌɪnəˈveɪʃən/
Pronunciation: dis-RUP-tiv in-uh-VAY-shuhn
French: innovation disruptive

Definition: Innovation that transforms or replaces existing markets or models.

Example: Disruptive innovation can create winners and losers.

creative destruction

IPA: /kriˌeɪtɪv dɪˈstrʌkʃən/
Pronunciation: kree-AY-tiv di-STRUK-shuhn
French: destruction créatrice

Definition: Economic change where innovation destroys older industries while creating new ones.

Example: Creative destruction is often used to describe technological change.

network effect

IPA: /ˈnetwɜːk ɪˌfekt/
Pronunciation: NET-work i-FEKT
French: effet de réseau

Definition: A situation where a service becomes more valuable as more people use it.

Example: Network effects make it hard to compete with dominant platforms.

winner-takes-all market

IPA: /ˌwɪnə teɪks ˈɔːl ˌmɑːkɪt/
Pronunciation: WIN-uh-tayks-all MAR-kit
French: marché où le gagnant rafle tout

Definition: A market where one or a few firms capture most users and profits.

Example: Digital markets often become winner-takes-all markets.

market dominance

IPA: /ˌmɑːkɪt ˈdɒmɪnəns/
Pronunciation: MAR-kit DOM-i-nuhns
French: domination du marché

Definition: A company’s strong control over a market.

Example: Market dominance gives platforms bargaining power.

monopoly

IPA: /məˈnɒpəli/
Pronunciation: muh-NOP-uh-lee
French: monopole

Definition: A market controlled by one seller or company.

Example: A monopoly can reduce competition and innovation.

B. Market power, competition and regulation

monopoly power

IPA: /məˈnɒpəli ˈpaʊə/
Pronunciation: muh-NOP-uh-lee POW-uh
French: pouvoir monopolistique

Definition: The ability of a dominant firm to control prices, access or conditions.

Example: Regulators worry about Big Tech’s monopoly power.

oligopoly

IPA: /ˌɒlɪˈɡɒpəli/
Pronunciation: ol-i-GOP-uh-lee
French: oligopole

Definition: A market dominated by a small number of large firms.

Example: The tech sector is often an oligopoly.

market concentration

IPA: /ˌmɑːkɪt ˌkɒnsənˈtreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: MAR-kit kon-suhn-TRAY-shuhn
French: concentration du marché

Definition: A situation where a few firms control a large share of a market.

Example: Market concentration raises antitrust concerns.

gatekeeper

IPA: /ˈɡeɪtˌkiːpə/
Pronunciation: GATE-kee-puh
French: contrôleur d’accès

Definition: A powerful platform controlling access to users, data or markets.

Example: Gatekeepers can favour their own services.

digital gatekeeper

IPA: /ˌdɪdʒɪtəl ˈɡeɪtˌkiːpə/
Pronunciation: DIJ-i-tuhl GATE-kee-puh
French: contrôleur d’accès numérique

Definition: A large digital platform controlling access in online markets.

Example: The EU Digital Markets Act targets digital gatekeepers.

intermediary

IPA: /ˌɪntəˈmiːdiəri/
Pronunciation: IN-tuh-MEE-dee-uh-ree
French: intermédiaire

Definition: An actor that connects two or more sides of a market or exchange.

Example: Platforms act as intermediaries between users and advertisers.

two-sided market

IPA: /ˌtuː saɪdɪd ˈmɑːkɪt/
Pronunciation: two-SY-did MAR-kit
French: marché biface

Definition: A market where a platform connects two groups, such as users and advertisers.

Example: Social networks operate as two-sided markets.

multi-sided platform

IPA: /ˌmʌlti saɪdɪd ˈplætfɔːm/
Pronunciation: MUL-tee-SY-did PLAT-form
French: plateforme multiface

Definition: A platform connecting several groups of users or businesses.

Example: App stores are multi-sided platforms.

ecosystem lock-in

IPA: /ˈiːkəʊˌsɪstəm ˈlɒk ɪn/
Pronunciation: EE-koh-SIS-tuhm LOK-in
French: enfermement dans un écosystème

Definition: A situation where users are trapped within a company’s products and services.

Example: Ecosystem lock-in makes switching difficult.

lock-in effect

IPA: /ˈlɒk ɪn ɪˌfekt/
Pronunciation: LOK-in i-FEKT
French: effet de verrouillage

Definition: A situation where users cannot easily leave a service or supplier.

Example: The lock-in effect protects dominant platforms.

switching cost

IPA: /ˈswɪtʃɪŋ kɒst/
Pronunciation: SWITCH-ing kost
French: coût de changement

Definition: The cost or difficulty of moving from one service to another.

Example: High switching costs reduce competition.

data portability

IPA: /ˈdeɪtə ˌpɔːtəˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: DAY-tuh por-tuh-BIL-uh-tee
French: portabilité des données

Definition: The ability to move personal data from one service to another.

Example: Data portability can help users switch platforms.

interoperability

IPA: /ˌɪntərɒpərəˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: in-ter-op-uh-ruh-BIL-uh-tee
French: interopérabilité

Definition: The ability of systems or services to work together.

Example: Interoperability can reduce platform lock-in.

self-preferencing

IPA: /ˌself ˈprefərənsɪŋ/
Pronunciation: self-PREF-uh-ruhn-sing
French: auto-préférence

Definition: A platform favouring its own services over competitors.

Example: Self-preferencing is a major antitrust concern.

preferential treatment

IPA: /ˌprefəˈrenʃəl ˈtriːtmənt/
Pronunciation: pref-uh-REN-shuhl TREET-muhnt
French: traitement préférentiel

Definition: More favourable treatment given to one actor or product.

Example: Dominant platforms may give preferential treatment to their own products.

acquisition

Pronunciation: ak-wi-ZISH-uhn
French: acquisition

merger

IPA: /ˈmɜːdʒə/
Pronunciation: MUR-juh
French: fusion

Definition: The combination of two companies into one.

killer acquisition

IPA: /ˈkɪlər ˌækwɪˈzɪʃən/

Example: Regulators worry about killer acquisitions in tech.
