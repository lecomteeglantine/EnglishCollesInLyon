Chapter 10 — Questions in English
A2 → B1 grammar for khôlles, debates, interviews, article analysis and essay problematics.

Chapter objective

This premium grammar chapter helps CPGE students build accurate and useful questions in English. It covers questions with be, do/does/did, Wh- questions, subject and object questions, indirect questions, question tags, debate questions and common mistakes made by French speakers.

The golden rule

Auxiliary + Subject + Verb
Does the article criticise the reform?
Why are voters angry?
Main useQuestions help students interact in khôlles, challenge arguments, ask for evidence and build essay problematics.
CPGE habitPrepare flexible question patterns: What evidence supports this claim?, Could you explain why...?, To what extent...?
Key trapDo not copy French word order: Why are people angry?, not Why people are angry?

A. Lesson — What is a question?

Question formation

French: formation des questions

Rule / explanation: English questions usually require inversion: auxiliary verb before subject.

Example / answer: Do voters trust institutions?

Yes/No question

French: question fermée

Rule / explanation: A question answered with yes or no.

Example / answer: Is the article convincing?

Wh- question

French: question en wh-

Rule / explanation: A question beginning with who, what, why, where, when, which or how.

Example / answer: Why does the author criticise the reform?

Subject question

French: question sujet

Rule / explanation: A question where the question word is the subject.

Example / answer: Who supports the policy?

Object question

French: question objet

Rule / explanation: A question where the question word is the object; it usually needs an auxiliary.

Example / answer: Who did the journalist interview?

Direct question

French: question directe

Rule / explanation: A normal question with question word order.

Example / answer: Why are voters angry?

Indirect question

French: question indirecte

Rule / explanation: A polite or formal question embedded in a statement or another question.

Example / answer: Could you explain why voters are angry?

CPGE use

French: usage CPGE

Rule / explanation: Questions are essential for khôlles, debates, interviews, oral analysis and essay problematics.

Example / answer: To what extent can the state regulate speech?

B. Questions with BE

Present BE question

French: question avec be au présent

Rule / explanation: Put am/is/are before the subject.

Example / answer: Is the article balanced?

Past BE question

French: question avec be au passé

Rule / explanation: Put was/were before the subject.

Example / answer: Were the results surprising?

Be + adjective

French: be + adjectif

Rule / explanation: Use be questions with adjectives.

Example / answer: Is the argument convincing?

Be + noun phrase

French: be + groupe nominal

Rule / explanation: Use be questions with identity, role or category.

Example / answer: Is this a social crisis?

Be + place

French: be + lieu

Rule / explanation: Use be questions to ask about location.

Example / answer: Where are the protests taking place?

Be + continuous

French: be + présent continu

Rule / explanation: Use be before the subject in continuous tenses.

Example / answer: Are rents rising in major cities?

Negative BE question

French: question négative avec be

Rule / explanation: Use isn’t/aren’t/wasn’t/weren’t before the subject.

Example / answer: Isn’t this argument too simplistic?

Short answers with BE

French: réponses courtes avec be

Rule / explanation: Use yes/no + subject + be.

Example / answer: Yes, it is. / No, they aren’t.

C. Questions with DO / DOES / DID

Present Simple with do

French: do au présent simple

Rule / explanation: Use do + subject + base verb for I/you/we/they.

Example / answer: Do young people trust the media?

Present Simple with does

French: does au présent simple

Rule / explanation: Use does + subject + base verb for he/she/it.

Example / answer: Does the article mention inequality?

Past Simple with did

French: did au prétérit

Rule / explanation: Use did + subject + base verb for past questions.

Example / answer: Did the government address the crisis?

No -s after does

French: pas de -s après does

Rule / explanation: After does, use the base verb.

Example / answer: Wrong: Does the author argues? Correct: Does the author argue?

No past after did

French: base verbale après did

Rule / explanation: After did, use the base verb.

Example / answer: Wrong: Did voters rejected it? Correct: Did voters reject it?

Question word + do

French: wh + do

Rule / explanation: Wh-word + do/does/did + subject + base verb.

Example / answer: Why do voters distrust institutions?

Negative questions with do

French: questions négatives avec do

Rule / explanation: Use don’t/doesn’t/didn’t before the subject.

Example / answer: Didn’t the reform increase inequality?

Short answers with do

French: réponses courtes avec do

Rule / explanation: Use yes/no + subject + do/does/did.

Example / answer: Yes, it does. / No, they didn’t.

D. Wh- questions

Who

French: qui

Rule / explanation: Asks about a person or people.

Example / answer: Who wrote the article?

What

French: que / quoi

Rule / explanation: Asks about a thing, idea, action or definition.

Example / answer: What does the article criticise?

Which

French: lequel / laquelle

Rule / explanation: Asks about a choice among a limited set.

Example / answer: Which argument is the most convincing?

Why

French: pourquoi

Rule / explanation: Asks about reason or cause.

Example / answer: Why did voters reject the proposal?

When

French: quand

Rule / explanation: Asks about time.

Example / answer: When did the crisis begin?

Where

French: où

Rule / explanation: Asks about place.

Example / answer: Where did the protests take place?

How

French: comment

Rule / explanation: Asks about manner, method or degree.

Example / answer: How does social media influence public debate?

How much / how many

French: combien

Rule / explanation: How much with uncountable nouns; how many with countable plural nouns.

Example / answer: How much evidence does the article provide? / How many voters abstained?

How long

French: combien de temps

Rule / explanation: Asks about duration.

Example / answer: How long has the crisis lasted?

How often

French: à quelle fréquence

Rule / explanation: Asks about frequency.

Example / answer: How often do young people read newspapers?

How far

French: jusqu’où / à quel point

Rule / explanation: Asks about distance or extent.

Example / answer: How far should the state regulate speech?

To what extent

French: dans quelle mesure

Rule / explanation: Formal way to ask about degree; very useful for essay questions.

Example / answer: To what extent is the reform likely to reduce inequality?

E. Subject vs object questions

Subject question rule

French: règle question sujet

Rule / explanation: When who/what/which is the subject, do not use do/does/did.

Example / answer: Who supports the reform?

Object question rule

French: règle question objet

Rule / explanation: When who/what/which is the object, use do/does/did if needed.

Example / answer: Who does the reform affect?

Subject question with who

French: who sujet

Rule / explanation: Who + verb + object.

Example / answer: Who criticises the government?

Object question with who

French: who objet

Rule / explanation: Who + auxiliary + subject + verb.

Example / answer: Who did the journalist interview?

Subject question with what

French: what sujet

Rule / explanation: What + verb + object/complement.

Example / answer: What caused the crisis?

Object question with what

French: what objet

Rule / explanation: What + auxiliary + subject + verb.

Example / answer: What did the crisis reveal?

Which + noun as subject

French: which sujet

Rule / explanation: Which argument convinced voters? No auxiliary needed.

Example / answer: Which argument convinced voters?

Which + noun as object

French: which objet

Rule / explanation: Which argument did the author reject? Auxiliary needed.

Example / answer: Which argument did the author reject?

F. Indirect questions

Indirect question order

French: ordre des questions indirectes

Rule / explanation: In indirect questions, use statement word order: subject before verb.

Example / answer: Could you explain why voters are angry?

Could you explain why...

French: formule polie

Rule / explanation: Polite and useful in khôlles.

Example / answer: Could you explain why the author rejects the reform?

Do you know whether...

French: whether / if

Rule / explanation: Use whether/if for yes/no indirect questions.

Example / answer: Do you know whether the bill passed?

I wonder why...

French: je me demande pourquoi

Rule / explanation: Formal and reflective.

Example / answer: I wonder why the article ignores the economic context.

The question is whether...

French: la question est de savoir si

Rule / explanation: Very useful for essay problematics.

Example / answer: The question is whether democracy can survive distrust.

Would you mind explaining...

French: formule très polie

Rule / explanation: Very polite request.

Example / answer: Would you mind explaining how the graph supports your point?

No auxiliary inversion

French: pas d’inversion

Rule / explanation: Do not invert in indirect questions.

Example / answer: Wrong: I wonder why are voters angry. Correct: I wonder why voters are angry.

No do in indirect questions if not needed

French: pas de do inutile

Rule / explanation: Do not keep do/does/did in indirect questions unless grammatically necessary.

Example / answer: Wrong: I wonder why do voters abstain. Correct: I wonder why voters abstain.

G. Question tags

Question tag

French: question tag

Rule / explanation: A short question added at the end of a sentence.

Example / answer: The article is convincing, isn’t it?

Positive sentence + negative tag

French: phrase positive + tag négatif

Rule / explanation: Use a negative tag after a positive sentence.

Example / answer: The policy is controversial, isn’t it?

Negative sentence + positive tag

French: phrase négative + tag positif

Rule / explanation: Use a positive tag after a negative sentence.

Example / answer: The reform is not popular, is it?

With do

French: tag avec do

Rule / explanation: Use do/does/did if there is no auxiliary.

Example / answer: The author criticises the reform, doesn’t he?

With modal

French: tag avec modal

Rule / explanation: Repeat the modal in the tag.

Example / answer: The state should act, shouldn’t it?

With will

French: tag avec will

Rule / explanation: Use won’t/will in the tag.

Example / answer: The issue will remain important, won’t it?

Rising intonation

French: intonation montante

Rule / explanation: Rising tone means real question or uncertainty.

Example / answer: The policy is fair, isn’t it?

Falling intonation

French: intonation descendante

Rule / explanation: Falling tone means confirmation or rhetorical effect.

Example / answer: This is a serious crisis, isn’t it?

H. CPGE debate and khôlle questions

Asking for clarification

French: demander une clarification

Rule / explanation: Use clarification questions when an argument is unclear.

Example / answer: Could you clarify what you mean by freedom?

Asking for evidence

French: demander des preuves

Rule / explanation: Ask what supports a claim.

Example / answer: What evidence supports this claim?

Asking for examples

French: demander un exemple

Rule / explanation: Ask for concrete illustration.

Example / answer: Could you give an example of this trend?

Challenging an argument

French: contester un argument

Rule / explanation: Question the logic or assumption.

Example / answer: Isn’t this argument based on a false assumption?

Asking about consequences

French: demander les conséquences

Rule / explanation: Explore possible outcomes.

Example / answer: What would happen if the reform failed?

Asking about alternatives

French: demander des alternatives

Rule / explanation: Explore other possibilities.

Example / answer: What alternative policy could the government adopt?

Asking for nuance

French: demander de nuancer

Rule / explanation: Push beyond a simplistic answer.

Example / answer: Wouldn’t it be more accurate to distinguish between legal and social freedom?

Opening a debate

French: ouvrir un débat

Rule / explanation: Use broad questions to frame discussion.

Example / answer: Should individual freedom ever be limited for collective security?

I. Typical mistakes by French speakers

Why people are angry?

French: ordre français

Rule / explanation: English needs auxiliary inversion in object questions.

Example / answer: Wrong: Why people are angry? Correct: Why are people angry?

What means this word?

French: what means

Rule / explanation: Use what does... mean?

Example / answer: Wrong: What means this word? Correct: What does this word mean?

You agree?

French: question sans auxiliaire

Rule / explanation: Use do in Present Simple questions.

Example / answer: Wrong: You agree? Correct: Do you agree?

Why did he went?

French: did + base verbale

Rule / explanation: After did, use the base verb.

Example / answer: Wrong: Why did he went? Correct: Why did he go?

Does the article shows?

French: does + base verbale

Rule / explanation: After does, use the base verb.

Example / answer: Wrong: Does the article shows? Correct: Does the article show?

I wonder why are voters angry

French: question indirecte

Rule / explanation: Indirect questions use statement order.

Example / answer: Correct: I wonder why voters are angry.

How many informations?

French: information indénombrable

Rule / explanation: Information is uncountable.

Example / answer: Correct: How much information? / How many pieces of information?

Is the government has...?

French: be + have incorrect

Rule / explanation: Do not mix be and have like this.

Example / answer: Correct: Does the government have...?
