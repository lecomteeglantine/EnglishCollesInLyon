Chapter objective

This premium chapter helps CPGE students transform verbs and adjectives into precise academic noun phrases while avoiding heavy, unnatural prose.

The golden rule

Verb / adjective → noun phrase
The economy grew rapidly. → The rapid growth of the economy...
The system is unstable. → The instability of the system...
Main useTurn actions, processes and qualities into abstract nouns.
CPGE habitUse phrases such as the decline of trust, government intervention and policy implementation.
Key trapDo not nominalise every verb: clarity comes first.

A. What is nominalisation?

Verb → noun

Rule / explanation: A verb becomes a noun expressing an action or process.

Example / answer: The government decided to act. → The government’s decision to act...

Adjective → noun

Rule / explanation: An adjective becomes a noun expressing a quality or state.

Example / answer: The economy is stable. → Economic stability...

Academic effect

Rule / explanation: Nominalisation creates a denser, more impersonal and analytical style.

Example / answer: People oppose the reform. → Public opposition to the reform...

CPGE use

Rule / explanation: It is common in essays, article summaries, introductions and conclusions.

Example / answer: The expansion of digital platforms has transformed public debate.

B. Common verb-to-noun forms

decide → decision

Rule / explanation: Use decision for the act or result of deciding.

Example / answer: The government’s decision to raise taxes caused controversy.

develop → development

Rule / explanation: Development refers to growth, change or progress.

Example / answer: The development of renewable energy has accelerated.

grow → growth

Rule / explanation: Growth is common in economic and demographic analysis.

Example / answer: Population growth is putting pressure on housing.

fail → failure

Rule / explanation: Failure refers to an unsuccessful action, policy or system.

Example / answer: The failure of the reform damaged public trust.

respond → response

Rule / explanation: Response is normally followed by to.

Example / answer: The government’s response to the crisis was criticised.

regulate → regulation

Rule / explanation: Regulation is useful in policy and technology essays.

Example / answer: The regulation of digital platforms remains controversial.

C. Common adjective-to-noun forms

important → importance

Rule / explanation: Use importance to discuss significance.

Example / answer: The article underestimates the importance of local institutions.

equal → equality

Rule / explanation: Equality is central to social and political analysis.

Example / answer: Equality of opportunity remains difficult to achieve.

stable → stability

Rule / explanation: Stability expresses a secure or lasting condition.

Example / answer: Political stability depends on public confidence.

weak → weakness

Rule / explanation: Weakness is useful for evaluating arguments.

Example / answer: The main weakness of the article is its lack of evidence.

legitimate → legitimacy

Rule / explanation: Legitimacy is a high-value political noun.

Example / answer: The crisis weakened the government’s democratic legitimacy.

efficient → efficiency

Rule / explanation: Efficiency expresses how well something works.

Example / answer: The reform’s efficiency remains difficult to assess.

D. Main suffixes

-tion / -sion

Rule / explanation: Common with regulate, decide, expand and conclude.

Example / answer: regulation, decision, expansion, conclusion

-ment

Rule / explanation: Common with develop, improve and achieve.

Example / answer: development, improvement, achievement

-ity

Rule / explanation: Common for abstract qualities.

Example / answer: equality, stability, legitimacy, possibility

-ness

Rule / explanation: Forms nouns from many adjectives.

Example / answer: weakness, awareness, effectiveness, fairness

-ance / -ence

Rule / explanation: Common in formal and academic vocabulary.

Example / answer: importance, independence, difference

-al

Rule / explanation: Often expresses an action or result.

Example / answer: refusal, arrival, approval

E. Building noun phrases

Noun + of

Rule / explanation: Use of to link the process to what is affected.

Example / answer: The rapid growth of the population...

Noun + in

Rule / explanation: Use in for changes in quantity, level or condition.

Example / answer: The increase in unemployment...

Noun + to

Rule / explanation: Some nouns take to for reactions, access or solutions.

Example / answer: Public opposition to the reform...

Possessive + noun

Rule / explanation: Use a possessive when the agent is important.

Example / answer: The government’s failure to act...

Noun compound

Rule / explanation: English often prefers compact noun compounds.

Example / answer: housing policy reform / climate policy implementation

To-infinitive complement

Rule / explanation: Some nouns are followed by to + verb.

Example / answer: The decision to intervene caused controversy.

F. High-value CPGE patterns

The emergence of...

Rule / explanation: Use for new phenomena.

Example / answer: The emergence of artificial intelligence is reshaping labour markets.

The decline of...

Rule / explanation: Use for institutions, industries and trust.

Example / answer: The decline of traditional industries transformed many regions.

The implementation of...

Rule / explanation: Use when analysing how a policy works in practice.

Example / answer: The implementation of the reform remains uneven.

Public distrust

Rule / explanation: A concise alternative to saying that the public does not trust.

Example / answer: Public distrust of political institutions has increased.

Government intervention

Rule / explanation: Useful in economic and social-policy essays.

Example / answer: Government intervention may be necessary to reduce inequality.

Democratic legitimacy

Rule / explanation: Useful for advanced political analysis.

Example / answer: The scandal damaged the institution’s democratic legitimacy.

G. Verbal style vs nominal style

Verbal style

Rule / explanation: Verbs make writing direct and dynamic.

Example / answer: The government reduced spending.

Nominal style

Rule / explanation: Nouns make writing denser and more analytical.

Example / answer: The reduction in public spending caused controversy.

Balanced style

Rule / explanation: Strong writing combines clear verbs with precise noun phrases.

Example / answer: The government reduced spending, a decision that triggered opposition.

Avoid overload

Rule / explanation: Long strings of abstract nouns make writing heavy.

Example / answer: Better: The government regulated development. Heavy: the implementation of the regulation of development.

H. Typical French-speaker mistakes

Wrong preposition

Rule / explanation: Learn nominalised nouns with their usual preposition.

Example / answer: opposition to / increase in / response to / decline in

Countability

Rule / explanation: Some academic nouns are uncountable.

Example / answer: progress, information, evidence, research

Too many of-phrases

Rule / explanation: English may prefer a noun compound.

Example / answer: housing policy reform, not reform of the policy of housing

Overnominalisation

Rule / explanation: Do not replace every verb with an abstract noun.

Example / answer: Clear writing is more important than artificial sophistication.

Wrong word family

Rule / explanation: Check spelling changes carefully.

Example / answer: decide → decision, strong → strength, grow → growth

Missing determiner

Rule / explanation: Singular countable nouns still need a determiner.

Example / answer: The decision was controversial, not Decision was controversial.
