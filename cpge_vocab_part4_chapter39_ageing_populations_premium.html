Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse ageing populations: demographic transition, fertility decline, dependency ratios, pensions, healthcare and long-term care, labour shortages, immigration, the silver economy, ageism, intergenerational fairness and the need to adapt welfare states to longer lives.

How to use this chapter

For press articlesFocus on terms such as ageing population, dependency ratio, pension reform, long-term care, labour shortage, silver economy, ageism and intergenerational fairness.
For oral examsUse this vocabulary to explain how longer life is a social achievement but also creates pressure on pensions, healthcare, public finances, families, migration policy and the labour market.

A. Demography and population trends

ageing population

IPA: /ˌeɪdʒɪŋ ˌpɒpjʊˈleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: AY-jing pop-yuh-LAY-shuhn
French: vieillissement de la population

Definition: A population in which the proportion of older people is increasing.

Example: An ageing population puts pressure on pensions, healthcare and public finances.

ageing society

IPA: /ˌeɪdʒɪŋ səˈsaɪəti/
Pronunciation: AY-jing suh-SY-uh-tee
French: société vieillissante

Definition: A society where older adults represent a growing share of the population.

Example: Japan is often cited as an example of an ageing society.

demographic ageing

IPA: /ˌdeməɡræfɪk ˈeɪdʒɪŋ/
Pronunciation: dem-uh-GRAF-ik AY-jing
French: vieillissement démographique

Definition: The process by which the average age of a population rises.

Example: Demographic ageing is caused by low fertility and longer life expectancy.

population ageing

IPA: /ˌpɒpjʊˈleɪʃən ˈeɪdʒɪŋ/
Pronunciation: pop-yuh-LAY-shuhn AY-jing
French: vieillissement de la population

Definition: The increase in the number and share of older people in a population.

Example: Population ageing forces governments to rethink healthcare and work.

demographic transition

IPA: /ˌdeməɡræfɪk trænˈzɪʃən/
Pronunciation: dem-uh-GRAF-ik tran-ZISH-uhn
French: transition démographique

Definition: The shift from high birth and death rates to low birth and death rates as societies develop.

Example: The demographic transition eventually leads to slower population growth.

demographic shift

IPA: /ˌdeməɡræfɪk ˈʃɪft/
Pronunciation: dem-uh-GRAF-ik shift
French: changement démographique

Definition: A significant change in the structure of a population.

Example: The demographic shift towards older citizens affects voting patterns.

demographic change

IPA: /ˌdeməɡræfɪk ˈtʃeɪndʒ/
Pronunciation: dem-uh-GRAF-ik chaynj
French: évolution démographique

Definition: Changes in population size, age, fertility, mortality or migration.

Example: Demographic change is reshaping labour markets.

population growth

IPA: /ˌpɒpjʊˈleɪʃən ɡrəʊθ/
Pronunciation: pop-yuh-LAY-shuhn growth
French: croissance démographique

Definition: An increase in the number of people in a population.

Example: Some countries still experience rapid population growth.

population decline

IPA: /ˌpɒpjʊˈleɪʃən dɪˈklaɪn/
Pronunciation: pop-yuh-LAY-shuhn di-KLYNE
French: déclin démographique

Definition: A decrease in the number of people in a population.

Example: Population decline can weaken rural areas and local economies.

population stagnation

IPA: /ˌpɒpjʊˈleɪʃən stæɡˈneɪʃən/
Pronunciation: pop-yuh-LAY-shuhn stag-NAY-shuhn
French: stagnation démographique

Definition: A situation where the population stops growing significantly.

Example: Population stagnation can reduce economic dynamism.

shrinking population

IPA: /ˌʃrɪŋkɪŋ ˌpɒpjʊˈleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: SHRINK-ing pop-yuh-LAY-shuhn
French: population en diminution

Definition: A population that is becoming smaller over time.

Example: A shrinking population may lead to school closures and labour shortages.

fertility rate

IPA: /fəˈtɪləti reɪt/
Pronunciation: fuh-TIL-uh-tee rayt
French: taux de fécondité

Definition: The average number of children born to a woman during her lifetime.

Example: A low fertility rate accelerates population ageing.

birth rate

IPA: /ˈbɜːθ reɪt/
Pronunciation: birth rayt
French: taux de natalité

Definition: The number of births in a population during a given period.

Example: Falling birth rates worry policymakers in ageing countries.

death rate

IPA: /ˈdeθ reɪt/
Pronunciation: death rayt
French: taux de mortalité

Definition: The number of deaths in a population during a given period.

Example: Death rates rise when a population becomes older.

life expectancy

IPA: /ˈlaɪf ɪkˌspektənsi/
Pronunciation: life ik-SPEK-tuhn-see
French: espérance de vie

Definition: The average number of years a person is expected to live.

Example: Rising life expectancy is one of the great achievements of modern societies.

healthy life expectancy

IPA: /ˌhelθi ˈlaɪf ɪkˌspektənsi/
Pronunciation: HEL-thee life ik-SPEK-tuhn-see
French: espérance de vie en bonne santé

Definition: The average number of years a person can expect to live in good health.

Example: Healthy life expectancy matters as much as total life expectancy.

longevity

IPA: /lɒnˈdʒevəti/
Pronunciation: lon-JEV-uh-tee
French: longévité

Definition: Long life or the tendency to live longer.

Example: Longevity creates opportunities as well as fiscal challenges.

centenarian

IPA: /ˌsentɪˈneəriən/
Pronunciation: sen-ti-NAIR-ee-uhn
French: centenaire

Definition: A person who is at least 100 years old.

Example: The number of centenarians is rising in many countries.

supercentenarian

IPA: /ˌsuːpəˌsentɪˈneəriən/
Pronunciation: SOO-puh-sen-ti-NAIR-ee-uhn
French: supercentenaire

Definition: A person who is at least 110 years old.

Example: Supercentenarians remain rare, but they fascinate demographers.

population pyramid

IPA: /ˌpɒpjʊˈleɪʃən ˈpɪrəmɪd/
Pronunciation: pop-yuh-LAY-shuhn PIR-uh-mid
French: pyramide des âges

Definition: A chart showing the age and sex structure of a population.

Example: A narrow-based population pyramid reveals low fertility.

age structure

IPA: /ˈeɪdʒ ˌstrʌktʃə/
Pronunciation: ayj STRUK-chuh
French: structure par âge

Definition: The distribution of people across age groups in a population.

Example: A country’s age structure affects schools, pensions and healthcare.

median age

IPA: /ˌmiːdiən ˈeɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: MEE-dee-uhn ayj
French: âge médian

Definition: The age that divides a population into two equal halves.

Example: The median age has risen sharply in many developed countries.

replacement level fertility

IPA: /rɪˈpleɪsmənt ˌlevəl fəˈtɪləti/
Pronunciation: ri-PLAYS-muhnt LEV-uhl fuh-TIL-uh-tee
French: seuil de renouvellement des générations

Definition: The fertility rate needed to keep a population stable without migration.

Example: Many countries are below replacement level fertility.

below-replacement fertility

IPA: /bɪˌləʊ rɪˈpleɪsmənt fəˈtɪləti/
Pronunciation: bi-LOH ri-PLAYS-muhnt fuh-TIL-uh-tee
French: fécondité sous le seuil de renouvellement

Definition: A fertility rate too low to replace the population over time without migration.

Example: Below-replacement fertility is common in Europe and East Asia.

declining fertility

IPA: /dɪˌklaɪnɪŋ fəˈtɪləti/
Pronunciation: di-KLY-ning fuh-TIL-uh-tee
French: baisse de la fécondité

Definition: A decrease in the average number of children people have.

Example: Declining fertility is linked to education, urbanisation and economic pressure.

B. Fertility, family policy and births

low fertility

IPA: /ˌləʊ fəˈtɪləti/
Pronunciation: loh fuh-TIL-uh-tee
French: faible fécondité

Definition: A fertility rate that is too low to maintain population size without migration.

Example: Low fertility creates long-term pension challenges.

childlessness

IPA: /ˈtʃaɪldləsnəs/
Pronunciation: CHYLD-luhs-nuhs
French: absence d’enfants

Definition: The state of not having children, whether by choice or circumstance.

Example: Rising childlessness reflects changing values and economic constraints.

delayed parenthood

IPA: /dɪˌleɪd ˈpeərənthʊd/
Pronunciation: di-LAYD PAIR-uhnt-hood
French: parentalité tardive

Definition: Having children later in life.

Example: Delayed parenthood can reduce the total number of children people have.

late motherhood

IPA: /ˌleɪt ˈmʌðəhʊd/
Pronunciation: late MUTH-uh-hood
French: maternité tardive

Definition: Becoming a mother later than previous generations typically did.

Example: Late motherhood is increasingly common in developed economies.

family planning

IPA: /ˌfæməli ˈplænɪŋ/
Pronunciation: FAM-uh-lee PLAN-ing
French: planification familiale

Definition: Decisions and services related to when and how many children to have.

Example: Family planning has contributed to lower fertility rates.

reproductive choices

IPA: /ˌriːprəˈdʌktɪv ˈtʃɔɪsɪz/
Pronunciation: ree-pruh-DUK-tiv CHOY-siz
French: choix reproductifs

Definition: Choices concerning contraception, pregnancy and childbirth.

Example: Reproductive choices are shaped by work, housing and gender equality.

pro-natalist policy

IPA: /ˌprəʊ ˈneɪtəlɪst ˈpɒləsi/
Pronunciation: proh-NAY-tuh-list POL-uh-see
French: politique nataliste

Definition: A policy designed to encourage people to have more children.

Example: Pro-natalist policies may include childcare support and baby bonuses.

baby bonus

IPA: /ˈbeɪbi ˌbəʊnəs/
Pronunciation: BAY-bee BOH-nuhs
French: prime à la naissance

Definition: A payment offered by the state to encourage childbirth.

Example: A baby bonus alone rarely reverses low fertility.

childcare support

IPA: /ˈtʃaɪldkeə səˌpɔːt/
Pronunciation: CHYLD-care suh-PORT
French: aide à la garde d’enfants

Definition: Financial or practical help with looking after children.

Example: Childcare support can make it easier to combine work and family.

parental leave

IPA: /pəˌrentəl ˈliːv/
Pronunciation: puh-REN-tuhl leev
French: congé parental

Definition: Time off work to care for a new child.

Example: Generous parental leave can support fertility and gender equality.

work-life balance

IPA: /ˌwɜːk laɪf ˈbæləns/
Pronunciation: work-life BAL-uhns
French: équilibre vie professionnelle-vie personnelle

Definition: The balance between employment and personal or family life.

Example: Poor work-life balance discourages some people from having children.

cost of raising children

IPA: /ˌkɒst əv ˈreɪzɪŋ ˈtʃɪldrən/
Pronunciation: kost ov RAY-zing CHIL-druhn
French: coût de l’éducation des enfants

Definition: The financial cost of bringing up children.

Example: The high cost of raising children contributes to low fertility.

housing affordability

IPA: /ˈhaʊzɪŋ əˌfɔːdəˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: HOW-zing uh-FOR-duh-BIL-uh-tee
French: accessibilité financière du logement

Definition: The extent to which people can afford decent housing.

Example: Housing affordability strongly affects family formation.

fertility crisis

IPA: /fəˈtɪləti ˈkraɪsɪs/
Pronunciation: fuh-TIL-uh-tee KRY-sis
French: crise de la fécondité

Definition: A situation where fertility falls so low that it threatens demographic stability.

Example: Some governments describe low birth rates as a fertility crisis.

demographic winter

IPA: /ˌdeməɡræfɪk ˈwɪntə/
Pronunciation: dem-uh-GRAF-ik WIN-tuh
French: hiver démographique

Definition: A dramatic term for very low fertility and population ageing.

Example: Demographic winter is often used in conservative or alarmist rhetoric.

dependency ratio

IPA: /dɪˈpendənsi ˌreɪʃiəʊ/
Pronunciation: di-PEN-duhn-see RAY-shee-oh
French: taux de dépendance

Definition: The ratio of dependants to working-age people.

Example: A rising dependency ratio puts pressure on workers and taxpayers.

old-age dependency ratio

IPA: /ˌəʊld eɪdʒ dɪˈpendənsi ˌreɪʃiəʊ/
Pronunciation: ohld-ayj di-PEN-duhn-see RAY-shee-oh
French: taux de dépendance des personnes âgées

Definition: The ratio of older people to working-age people.

Example: The old-age dependency ratio is rising across Europe.

dependant

IPA: /dɪˈpendənt/
Pronunciation: di-PEN-duhnt
French: personne dépendante

Definition: A person who relies on others for support, such as a child or elderly person.

Example: An ageing society has more elderly dependants.

C. Dependency, pensions and the welfare state

pensioner

IPA: /ˈpenʃənə/
Pronunciation: PEN-shuhn-uh
French: retraité ; pensionné

Definition: A person receiving a pension, especially after retirement.

Example: Pensioners are an influential voting group.

retiree

IPA: /rɪˌtaɪəˈriː/
Pronunciation: ri-ty-uh-REE
French: retraité

Definition: A person who has stopped working because of age.

Example: Retirees may still contribute through volunteering or family support.

retirement

IPA: /rɪˈtaɪəmənt/
Pronunciation: ri-TY-uh-muhnt
French: retraite

Definition: The period after someone stops working, usually in later life.

Example: Longer retirement requires more savings and public funding.

retirement age

IPA: /rɪˈtaɪəmənt eɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: ri-TY-uh-muhnt ayj
French: âge de départ à la retraite

Definition: The age at which people normally stop working and receive a pension.

Example: Raising the retirement age is politically sensitive.

state pension

IPA: /ˌsteɪt ˈpenʃən/
Pronunciation: state PEN-shuhn
French: pension de retraite publique

Definition: A pension paid by the government.

Example: The state pension is a pillar of social protection.

private pension

IPA: /ˌpraɪvət ˈpenʃən/
Pronunciation: PRY-vuht PEN-shuhn
French: retraite privée

Definition: A pension funded privately by individuals or employers.

Example: Private pensions depend on savings and investment returns.

pension system

IPA: /ˈpenʃən ˌsɪstəm/
Pronunciation: PEN-shuhn SIS-tuhm
French: système de retraite

Definition: The rules and institutions that provide retirement income.

Example: Ageing populations threaten the sustainability of pension systems.

pension reform

IPA: /ˈpenʃən rɪˌfɔːm/
Pronunciation: PEN-shuhn ri-FORM
French: réforme des retraites

Definition: Changes to pension rules, funding or retirement age.

Example: Pension reform often triggers protests.

pension fund

IPA: /ˈpenʃən fʌnd/
Pronunciation: PEN-shuhn fund
French: fonds de pension

Definition: A fund that collects and invests money to pay pensions.

Example: Pension funds are major investors in financial markets.

pay-as-you-go pension

IPA: /ˌpeɪ əz juː ˈɡəʊ ˈpenʃən/
Pronunciation: pay-az-yoo-go PEN-shuhn
French: retraite par répartition

Definition: A pension system where current workers fund current retirees.

Example: Pay-as-you-go pensions struggle when there are fewer workers per retiree.

funded pension scheme

IPA: /ˌfʌndɪd ˈpenʃən skiːm/
Pronunciation: FUN-did PEN-shuhn skeem
French: retraite par capitalisation

Definition: A pension system based on accumulated savings and investment.

Example: Funded pension schemes are exposed to market risks.

welfare state

IPA: /ˈwelfeə steɪt/
Pronunciation: WEL-fair state
French: État-providence

Definition: A system in which the state provides social protection such as healthcare, pensions and benefits.

Example: The welfare state must adapt to demographic ageing.

social security

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl sɪˈkjʊərəti/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl si-KYOOR-uh-tee
French: sécurité sociale

Definition: Public systems providing income support, pensions and protection.

Example: Social security spending rises as populations age.

social protection

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl prəˈtekʃən/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl pruh-TEK-shuhn
French: protection sociale

Definition: Policies protecting people from poverty, illness, unemployment or old age.

Example: Social protection is crucial in later life.

healthcare spending

IPA: /ˈhelθkeə ˈspendɪŋ/
Pronunciation: HELTH-care SPEN-ding
French: dépenses de santé

Definition: Money spent on healthcare services.

Example: Healthcare spending tends to increase with age.

public expenditure

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk ɪkˈspendɪtʃə/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik ik-SPEN-di-chuh
French: dépenses publiques

Definition: Government spending on services, benefits and infrastructure.

Example: Ageing populations increase public expenditure.

fiscal burden

IPA: /ˌfɪskəl ˈbɜːdən/
Pronunciation: FIS-kuhl BUR-duhn
French: charge budgétaire

Definition: Pressure on public finances caused by spending or debt.

Example: Pensions and healthcare can become a fiscal burden.

tax base

IPA: /ˈtæks beɪs/
Pronunciation: taks bays
French: assiette fiscale

Definition: The total income, wealth or activity that can be taxed.

Example: A shrinking workforce can weaken the tax base.

intergenerational contract

IPA: /ˌɪntədʒenəˈreɪʃənəl ˈkɒntrækt/
Pronunciation: IN-tuh-jen-uh-RAY-shuh-nuhl KON-trakt
French: contrat intergénérationnel

Definition: The implicit agreement that working generations support older generations.

Example: The intergenerational contract is strained when the population ages.

generational solidarity

IPA: /ˌdʒenəˈreɪʃənəl ˌsɒlɪˈdærəti/
Pronunciation: jen-uh-RAY-shuh-nuhl sol-i-DARR-uh-tee
French: solidarité intergénérationnelle

Definition: Mutual support between generations.

Example: Generational solidarity is essential to sustain pensions.

public finances

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk ˈfaɪnænsɪz/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik FY-nan-siz
French: finances publiques

Definition: Government revenue, spending and debt.

Example: Ageing societies create long-term pressure on public finances.

long-term care

IPA: /ˌlɒŋ tɜːm ˈkeə/
Pronunciation: long-term care
French: soins de longue durée ; dépendance

Definition: Ongoing support for people who cannot fully care for themselves.

Example: Long-term care is one of the biggest challenges of ageing.

nursing home

IPA: /ˈnɜːsɪŋ həʊm/
Pronunciation: NUR-sing hohm
French: maison de retraite médicalisée

Definition: A residential facility providing medical and personal care.

Example: Nursing homes need trained staff and stable funding.

care home

IPA: /ˈkeə həʊm/
Pronunciation: care hohm
French: maison de retraite ; établissement de soins

Definition: A residential facility where older or vulnerable people receive care.

Example: Care homes were heavily affected during the pandemic.

retirement home

IPA: /rɪˈtaɪəmənt həʊm/
Pronunciation: ri-TY-uh-muhnt hohm
French: résidence pour personnes âgées

Definition: A residential setting for older people, often with support services.

Example: Some retirees choose retirement homes for security and community.

assisted living

IPA: /əˌsɪstɪd ˈlɪvɪŋ/
Pronunciation: uh-SIS-tid LIV-ing
French: résidence avec services ; logement assisté

Definition: Housing with support for older or disabled people who need some help.

Example: Assisted living allows more independence than a nursing home.

home care

IPA: /ˈhəʊm keə/
Pronunciation: hohm care
French: soins à domicile ; aide à domicile

Definition: Care provided in a person’s own home.

Example: Home care can help older people remain independent.

D. Healthcare, long-term care and later life

caregiver

IPA: /ˈkeəˌɡɪvə/
Pronunciation: CARE-giv-uh
French: aidant ; soignant

Definition: A person who provides care to someone who is ill, disabled or elderly.

Example: Caregivers often face emotional and financial strain.

informal caregiver

IPA: /ɪnˌfɔːməl ˈkeəˌɡɪvə/
Pronunciation: in-FOR-muhl CARE-giv-uh
French: aidant informel

Definition: An unpaid caregiver, often a family member or friend.

Example: Informal caregivers save public systems large amounts of money.

family caregiver

IPA: /ˌfæməli ˈkeəˌɡɪvə/
Pronunciation: FAM-uh-lee CARE-giv-uh
French: aidant familial

Definition: A relative who provides care to an older, ill or disabled person.

Example: Family caregivers need respite and financial recognition.

respite care

IPA: /ˈrespaɪt keə/
Pronunciation: RESS-pyte care
French: répit pour aidants

Definition: Temporary care that gives regular caregivers a break.

Example: Respite care helps prevent caregiver burnout.

caregiver burden

IPA: /ˈkeəˌɡɪvə ˈbɜːdən/
Pronunciation: CARE-giv-uh BUR-duhn
French: charge de l’aidant

Definition: The physical, emotional or financial strain of caring for someone.

Example: Caregiver burden is often invisible.

dementia

IPA: /dɪˈmenʃə/
Pronunciation: di-MEN-shuh
French: démence

Definition: A group of conditions involving decline in memory and thinking.

Example: Dementia cases increase as populations age.

Alzheimer’s disease

IPA: /ˈæltsˌhaɪməz dɪˌziːz/
Pronunciation: ALTS-hy-muhz di-ZEEZ
French: maladie d’Alzheimer

Definition: A common form of dementia affecting memory and cognition.

Example: Alzheimer’s disease places great pressure on families.

frailty

IPA: /ˈfreɪlti/
Pronunciation: FRAYL-tee
French: fragilité

Definition: Physical vulnerability and reduced resilience in older age.

Example: Frailty increases the risk of falls and hospitalisation.

chronic disease

IPA: /ˌkrɒnɪk dɪˈziːz/
Pronunciation: KRON-ik di-ZEEZ
French: maladie chronique

Definition: A long-lasting disease that requires ongoing management.

Example: Chronic disease becomes more common with age.

age-related disease

IPA: /ˈeɪdʒ rɪˌleɪtɪd dɪˈziːz/
Pronunciation: ayj ri-LAY-tid di-ZEEZ
French: maladie liée à l’âge

Definition: A disease more common in older age.

Example: Cancer and dementia are examples of age-related diseases.

healthy ageing

IPA: /ˌhelθi ˈeɪdʒɪŋ/
Pronunciation: HEL-thee AY-jing
French: vieillissement en bonne santé

Definition: Ageing while maintaining physical, mental and social well-being.

Example: Healthy ageing should be a policy goal, not just longer life.

active ageing

IPA: /ˌæktɪv ˈeɪdʒɪŋ/
Pronunciation: AK-tiv AY-jing
French: vieillissement actif

Definition: Older people remaining active, independent and socially engaged.

Example: Active ageing can reduce isolation and healthcare costs.

geriatric care

IPA: /ˌdʒeriˈætrɪk keə/
Pronunciation: jerr-ee-AT-rik care
French: soins gériatriques

Definition: Medical care specialised for older adults.

Example: Geriatric care requires specific expertise.

elderly care

IPA: /ˈeldəli keə/
Pronunciation: EL-duh-lee care
French: soins aux personnes âgées

Definition: Care and support for older people.

Example: Elderly care is often undervalued and understaffed.

palliative care

IPA: /ˈpæliətɪv keə/
Pronunciation: PAL-ee-uh-tiv care
French: soins palliatifs

Definition: Care focused on comfort and quality of life for serious illness.

Example: Palliative care supports dignity at the end of life.

end-of-life care

IPA: /ˌend əv ˈlaɪf keə/
Pronunciation: end-ov-life care
French: soins de fin de vie

Definition: Care provided to people near the end of life.

Example: End-of-life care raises ethical and emotional questions.

labour shortage

IPA: /ˈleɪbə ˌʃɔːtɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: LAY-buh SHOR-tij
French: pénurie de main-d’œuvre

Definition: A lack of enough workers to fill available jobs.

Example: Ageing populations can create labour shortages.

workforce

IPA: /ˈwɜːkfɔːs/
Pronunciation: WURK-force
French: population active ; main-d’œuvre

Definition: All people available for work in an economy or organisation.

Example: A shrinking workforce can slow economic growth.

labour force participation

IPA: /ˈleɪbə fɔːs pɑːˌtɪsɪˈpeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: LAY-buh force par-tis-i-PAY-shuhn
