Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse inflation: price rises, purchasing power, CPI, cost-of-living crises, demand-pull and cost-push inflation, supply shocks, central banks, interest rates, monetary policy, fiscal responses, wages, strikes, corporate pricing and the social impact of rising prices.

A. Core inflation vocabulary and price measures

inflation

IPA: /ɪnˈfleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: in-FLAY-shuhn
French: inflation

Definition: A general increase in prices across an economy over time.

Example: Inflation reduces purchasing power when wages do not keep up.

inflation rate

IPA: /ɪnˈfleɪʃən reɪt/
Pronunciation: in-FLAY-shuhn rayt
French: taux d’inflation

Definition: The percentage rate at which prices rise over a given period.

Example: The inflation rate became a major political issue during the cost-of-living crisis.

price level

IPA: /ˈpraɪs ˌlevəl/
Pronunciation: price LEV-uhl
French: niveau général des prix

Definition: The average level of prices in an economy.

Example: Inflation means the general price level is rising.

rising prices

IPA: /ˌraɪzɪŋ ˈpraɪsɪz/
Pronunciation: RY-zing PRY-siz
French: hausse des prix

Definition: Prices that are increasing over time.

Example: Rising prices hit low-income households hardest.

price increase

IPA: /ˈpraɪs ˌɪnkriːs/
Pronunciation: price IN-kreess
French: augmentation des prix

Definition: A rise in the price of a good or service.

Example: Food price increases can quickly become politically sensitive.

cost of living

IPA: /ˌkɒst əv ˈlɪvɪŋ/
Pronunciation: kost ov LIV-ing
French: coût de la vie

Definition: The amount of money needed to pay for basic goods and services.

Example: The cost of living has risen faster than many wages.

cost-of-living crisis

IPA: /ˌkɒst əv ˈlɪvɪŋ ˈkraɪsɪs/
Pronunciation: kost-ov-LIV-ing KRY-sis
French: crise du coût de la vie

Definition: A situation where essential costs rise faster than incomes.

Example: The cost-of-living crisis has reshaped political debate.

purchasing power

IPA: /ˈpɜːtʃəsɪŋ ˌpaʊə/
Pronunciation: PUR-chuh-sing POW-uh
French: pouvoir d’achat

Definition: The amount of goods and services money can buy.

Example: Inflation erodes purchasing power.

real income

IPA: /ˌrɪəl ˈɪnkʌm/
Pronunciation: REE-uhl IN-kum
French: revenu réel

Definition: Income adjusted for inflation.

Example: Real income falls when wages rise more slowly than prices.

real wages

IPA: /ˌrɪəl ˈweɪdʒɪz/
Pronunciation: REE-uhl WAY-jiz
French: salaires réels

Definition: Wages adjusted for inflation.

Example: Real wages declined during the inflation shock.

nominal wages

IPA: /ˌnɒmɪnəl ˈweɪdʒɪz/
Pronunciation: NOM-i-nuhl WAY-jiz
French: salaires nominaux

Definition: Wages measured in current money, not adjusted for inflation.

Example: Nominal wages can rise while real wages fall.

consumer price index

IPA: /kənˌsjuːmə ˈpraɪs ˌɪndeks/
Pronunciation: kuhn-SYOO-muh price IN-deks
French: indice des prix à la consommation

Definition: An index measuring changes in prices paid by consumers.

Example: The consumer price index is used to track inflation.

CPI

IPA: /ˌsiː piː ˈaɪ/
Pronunciation: see-pee-eye
French: IPC ; indice des prix à la consommation

Definition: Abbreviation for consumer price index.

Example: CPI is one of the most common inflation measures.

retail price index

IPA: /ˈriːteɪl ˈpraɪs ˌɪndeks/
Pronunciation: REE-tayl price IN-deks
French: indice des prix de détail

Definition: A price index historically used in the UK, often including housing costs.

Example: The retail price index is still used in some contracts.

RPI

IPA: /ˌɑː piː ˈaɪ/
Pronunciation: ar-pee-eye
French: RPI ; indice britannique des prix de détail

Definition: Abbreviation for retail price index.

Example: RPI often gives a different figure from CPI.

core inflation

IPA: /ˌkɔːr ɪnˈfleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: kor in-FLAY-shuhn
French: inflation sous-jacente

Definition: Inflation excluding volatile items such as food and energy.

Example: Central banks watch core inflation closely.

headline inflation

IPA: /ˌhedlaɪn ɪnˈfleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: HED-lyne in-FLAY-shuhn
French: inflation globale

Definition: The overall inflation rate including all items.

Example: Headline inflation rose sharply when energy prices surged.

food inflation

IPA: /ˈfuːd ɪnˌfleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: food in-FLAY-shuhn
French: inflation alimentaire

Definition: Inflation affecting food prices.

Example: Food inflation hurts poorer households disproportionately.

energy inflation

IPA: /ˈenədʒi ɪnˌfleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: EN-uh-jee in-FLAY-shuhn
French: inflation énergétique

Definition: Inflation caused by rising energy prices.

Example: Energy inflation pushed up bills and production costs.

services inflation

IPA: /ˈsɜːvɪsɪz ɪnˌfleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: SUR-vis-iz in-FLAY-shuhn
French: inflation des services

Definition: Inflation in services such as hospitality, transport or healthcare.

Example: Services inflation can be persistent because it is linked to wages.

goods inflation

IPA: /ˈɡʊdz ɪnˌfleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: goods in-FLAY-shuhn
French: inflation des biens

Definition: Inflation in physical goods.

Example: Goods inflation rose when supply chains were disrupted.

housing costs

IPA: /ˈhaʊzɪŋ kɒsts/
Pronunciation: HOW-zing kosts
French: coûts du logement

Definition: Costs related to rent, mortgage payments, maintenance and housing.

Example: Housing costs are central to the cost-of-living debate.

rent inflation

IPA: /ˈrent ɪnˌfleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: rent in-FLAY-shuhn
French: inflation des loyers

Definition: Rising rents over time.

Example: Rent inflation has increased pressure on young people.

wage inflation

IPA: /ˈweɪdʒ ɪnˌfleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: wayj in-FLAY-shuhn
French: inflation salariale

Definition: Rising wages that can contribute to rising costs and prices.

Example: Employers sometimes blame wage inflation for higher prices.

asset price inflation

IPA: /ˈæset praɪs ɪnˌfleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: AS-et price in-FLAY-shuhn
French: inflation des prix des actifs

Definition: Rising prices of assets such as housing, stocks or land.

Example: Asset price inflation can worsen wealth inequality.

B. Causes of inflation and inflation dynamics

demand-pull inflation

IPA: /dɪˈmɑːnd pʊl ɪnˌfleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: di-MAND-puul in-FLAY-shuhn
French: inflation par la demande

Definition: Inflation caused by demand rising faster than supply.

Example: Demand-pull inflation can occur when the economy overheats.

cost-push inflation

IPA: /ˈkɒst pʊʃ ɪnˌfleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: kost-push in-FLAY-shuhn
French: inflation par les coûts

Definition: Inflation caused by rising production costs.

Example: Cost-push inflation can be triggered by energy price shocks.

imported inflation

IPA: /ɪmˈpɔːtɪd ɪnˌfleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: im-POR-tid in-FLAY-shuhn
French: inflation importée

Definition: Inflation caused by rising prices of imported goods or a weaker currency.

Example: Imported inflation increased when the currency depreciated.

supply shock

IPA: /səˈplaɪ ʃɒk/
Pronunciation: suh-PLY shok
French: choc d’offre

Definition: A sudden disruption to supply that affects prices or output.

Example: The war created a supply shock in energy markets.

demand shock

IPA: /dɪˈmɑːnd ʃɒk/
Pronunciation: di-MAND shok
French: choc de demande

Definition: A sudden change in demand affecting the economy.

Example: A demand shock can trigger either recession or inflation.

energy price shock

IPA: /ˈenədʒi praɪs ʃɒk/
Pronunciation: EN-uh-jee price shok
French: choc des prix de l’énergie

Definition: A sudden large rise in energy prices.

Example: The energy price shock pushed inflation higher.

oil shock

IPA: /ˈɔɪl ʃɒk/
Pronunciation: oil shok
French: choc pétrolier

Definition: A sudden disruption or rise in oil prices affecting the economy.

Example: The 1970s oil shocks led to high inflation.

supply-chain disruption

IPA: /səˈplaɪ tʃeɪn dɪsˌrʌpʃən/
Pronunciation: suh-PLY chayn dis-RUP-shuhn
French: perturbation de la chaîne d’approvisionnement

Definition: An interruption in the flow of goods, parts or materials.

Example: Supply-chain disruption contributed to goods inflation.

bottleneck

IPA: /ˈbɒtəlnek/
Pronunciation: BOT-uhl-nek
French: goulot d’étranglement

Definition: A point of congestion or shortage that slows production or supply.

Example: Shipping bottlenecks increased prices.

shortage

IPA: /ˈʃɔːtɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: SHOR-tij
French: pénurie

Definition: A lack of enough goods, workers or resources.

Example: Shortages can push prices upward.

scarcity

IPA: /ˈskeəsəti/
Pronunciation: SKAIR-suh-tee
French: rareté

Definition: A situation where resources or goods are limited.

Example: Scarcity gives suppliers more power to raise prices.

price pressure

IPA: /ˈpraɪs ˌpreʃə/
Pronunciation: price PRESH-uh
French: pression sur les prix

Definition: Forces causing prices to rise or fall.

Example: Energy costs created strong price pressure.

inflationary pressure

IPA: /ɪnˌfleɪʃənəri ˈpreʃə/
Pronunciation: in-FLAY-shuh-nuh-ree PRESH-uh
French: pression inflationniste

Definition: Forces pushing inflation upward.

Example: Tight labour markets can create inflationary pressure.

inflation expectations

IPA: /ɪnˈfleɪʃən ˌekspekˈteɪʃənz/
Pronunciation: in-FLAY-shuhn ek-spek-TAY-shuhnz
French: anticipations d’inflation

Definition: What people and firms expect future inflation to be.

Example: Inflation expectations can influence wage demands and pricing.

anchored expectations

IPA: /ˌæŋkəd ˌekspekˈteɪʃənz/
Pronunciation: ANG-kuhd ek-spek-TAY-shuhnz
French: anticipations ancrées

Definition: Expectations that remain stable despite short-term shocks.

Example: Central banks try to keep inflation expectations anchored.

wage-price spiral

IPA: /ˈweɪdʒ praɪs ˌspaɪrəl/
Pronunciation: wayj-price SPY-ruhl
French: spirale prix-salaires

Definition: A cycle where wages and prices push each other higher.

Example: A wage-price spiral was feared after inflation surged.

price-wage spiral

IPA: /ˈpraɪs weɪdʒ ˌspaɪrəl/
Pronunciation: price-wayj SPY-ruhl
French: spirale prix-salaires

Definition: A self-reinforcing loop of rising prices and wages.

Example: The government warned against a price-wage spiral.

indexation

IPA: /ˌɪndekˈseɪʃən/
Pronunciation: in-dek-SAY-shuhn
French: indexation

Definition: Automatic adjustment of payments or wages to inflation.

Example: Indexation protects incomes but can make inflation persistent.

cost-of-living adjustment

IPA: /ˌkɒst əv ˈlɪvɪŋ əˈdʒʌstmənt/
Pronunciation: kost-ov-LIV-ing uh-JUST-muhnt
French: ajustement au coût de la vie

Definition: An increase in wages or benefits to match inflation.

Example: Cost-of-living adjustments protect retirees from inflation.

COLA

IPA: /ˈkəʊlə/
Pronunciation: KOH-luh
French: ajustement automatique au coût de la vie

Definition: Abbreviation for cost-of-living adjustment.

Example: COLA increases are common in US Social Security.

price stability

IPA: /ˈpraɪs stəˌbɪləti/
Pronunciation: price stuh-BIL-uh-tee
French: stabilité des prix

Definition: A situation where prices rise slowly and predictably.

Example: Price stability is a central bank objective.

central bank

IPA: /ˌsentrəl ˈbæŋk/
Pronunciation: SEN-truhl bank
French: banque centrale

Definition: An institution managing monetary policy and currency stability.

Example: The central bank raised interest rates to fight inflation.

C. Central banks, interest rates and monetary policy

Bank of England

IPA: /ˌbæŋk əv ˈɪŋɡlənd/
Pronunciation: bank ov ING-gluhnd
French: Banque d’Angleterre

Definition: The central bank of the United Kingdom.

Example: The Bank of England targets inflation.

Federal Reserve

IPA: /ˌfedərəl rɪˈzɜːv/
Pronunciation: FED-uh-ruhl ri-ZURV
French: Réserve fédérale américaine

Definition: The central bank of the United States.

Example: The Federal Reserve tightened monetary policy.

Fed

IPA: /fed/
Pronunciation: fed
French: Fed ; Réserve fédérale

Definition: Short form for the US Federal Reserve.

Example: The Fed raised rates to cool demand.

European Central Bank

IPA: /ˌjʊərəpiːən ˈsentrəl bæŋk/
Pronunciation: YOOR-uh-pee-uhn SEN-truhl bank
French: Banque centrale européenne

Definition: The central bank of the eurozone.

Example: The European Central Bank aims to keep inflation under control.

ECB

IPA: /ˌiː siː ˈbiː/
Pronunciation: ee-see-bee
French: BCE

Definition: Abbreviation for European Central Bank.

Example: The ECB increased rates after inflation rose.

monetary policy

IPA: /ˈmʌnɪtəri ˈpɒləsi/
Pronunciation: MUN-i-tuh-ree POL-uh-see
French: politique monétaire

Definition: Central bank policy affecting interest rates and money supply.

Example: Monetary policy is the main tool used to fight inflation.

interest rate

IPA: /ˈɪntrəst reɪt/
Pronunciation: IN-truhst rayt
French: taux d’intérêt

Definition: The cost of borrowing money, expressed as a percentage.

Example: Higher interest rates reduce borrowing and spending.

base rate

IPA: /ˈbeɪs reɪt/
Pronunciation: base rayt
French: taux directeur

Definition: The main interest rate set by a central bank.

Example: The base rate affects mortgages and loans.

policy rate

IPA: /ˈpɒləsi reɪt/
Pronunciation: POL-uh-see rayt
French: taux directeur

Definition: An interest rate set by a central bank to guide monetary conditions.

Example: A higher policy rate is meant to slow inflation.

rate hike

IPA: /ˈreɪt haɪk/
Pronunciation: rayt hyke
French: hausse des taux

Definition: An increase in interest rates.

Example: Rate hikes make borrowing more expensive.

rate cut

IPA: /ˈreɪt kʌt/
Pronunciation: rayt kut
French: baisse des taux

Definition: A decrease in interest rates.

Example: Rate cuts can stimulate growth after inflation falls.

tight monetary policy

IPA: /ˌtaɪt ˈmʌnɪtəri ˈpɒləsi/
Pronunciation: tyte MUN-i-tuh-ree POL-uh-see
French: politique monétaire restrictive

Definition: Monetary policy designed to slow inflation by restricting credit and demand.

Example: Tight monetary policy can reduce inflation but risk recession.

loose monetary policy

IPA: /ˌluːs ˈmʌnɪtəri ˈpɒləsi/
Pronunciation: looss MUN-i-tuh-ree POL-uh-see
French: politique monétaire accommodante

Definition: Monetary policy designed to stimulate borrowing and demand.

Example: Loose monetary policy can support growth but fuel inflation.

quantitative easing

IPA: /ˌkwɒntɪtətɪv ˈiːzɪŋ/
Pronunciation: KWON-ti-tuh-tiv EE-zing
French: assouplissement quantitatif

Definition: Central bank asset purchases used to inject money into the economy.

Example: Quantitative easing was used after the financial crisis.

QE

IPA: /ˌkjuː ˈiː/
Pronunciation: kyoo-ee
French: QE ; assouplissement quantitatif

Definition: Abbreviation for quantitative easing.

Example: QE can support demand but may raise asset prices.

quantitative tightening

IPA: /ˌkwɒntɪtətɪv ˈtaɪtənɪŋ/
Pronunciation: KWON-ti-tuh-tiv TY-tuhn-ing
French: resserrement quantitatif

Definition: Central bank policy reducing its balance sheet or liquidity.

Example: Quantitative tightening can make financial conditions tighter.

QT

IPA: /ˌkjuː ˈtiː/
Pronunciation: kyoo-tee
French: QT ; resserrement quantitatif

Definition: Abbreviation for quantitative tightening.

Example: QT is the opposite of QE.

money supply

IPA: /ˈmʌni səˌplaɪ/
Pronunciation: MUN-ee suh-PLY
French: masse monétaire

Definition: The amount of money circulating in an economy.

Example: Rapid growth in the money supply can contribute to inflation.

liquidity

IPA: /lɪˈkwɪdəti/
Pronunciation: li-KWID-uh-tee
French: liquidité

Definition: Availability of cash or easily usable funds in an economy or market.

Example: Central banks can inject liquidity during a crisis.

credit conditions

IPA: /ˈkredɪt kənˌdɪʃənz/
Pronunciation: KRED-it kuhn-DISH-uhnz
French: conditions de crédit

Definition: The ease or difficulty of borrowing money.

Example: Tighter credit conditions slow business investment.

borrowing costs

IPA: /ˈbɒrəʊɪŋ kɒsts/
Pronunciation: BOR-oh-ing kosts
French: coût de l’emprunt

Definition: The cost of taking out loans.

Example: Higher borrowing costs affect households and firms.

mortgage rates

IPA: /ˈmɔːɡɪdʒ reɪts/
Pronunciation: MOR-gij rayts
French: taux des prêts immobiliers

Definition: Interest rates on home loans.

Example: Mortgage rates rose after central banks tightened policy.

inflation target

IPA: /ɪnˈfleɪʃən ˌtɑːɡɪt/
Pronunciation: in-FLAY-shuhn TAR-git
French: objectif d’inflation

Definition: The inflation rate a central bank aims to achieve.

Example: Many central banks have a 2% inflation target.

target rate

IPA: /ˈtɑːɡɪt reɪt/
Pronunciation: TAR-git rayt
French: taux cible

Definition: The rate a central bank or policy aims to reach.

Example: The target rate guides central bank decisions.

credibility

IPA: /ˌkredəˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: kred-uh-BIL-uh-tee
French: crédibilité

Definition: Trust that an institution will do what it promises.

Example: Central bank credibility helps anchor inflation expectations.

central bank independence

IPA: /ˌsentrəl bæŋk ˌɪndɪˈpendəns/
Pronunciation: SEN-truhl bank in-di-PEN-duhns
French: indépendance de la banque centrale

Definition: The ability of a central bank to make decisions without direct political pressure.

Example: Central bank independence is meant to prevent political inflationary pressure.

hawkish

IPA: /ˈhɔːkɪʃ/
Pronunciation: HAW-kish
French: restrictif ; faucon

Definition: Favouring higher interest rates to fight inflation.

Example: A hawkish central banker prioritises inflation control.

dovish

IPA: /ˈdʌvɪʃ/
Pronunciation: DUV-ish
French: accommodant ; colombe

Definition: Favouring lower interest rates to support growth and employment.

Example: A dovish stance may worry inflation hawks.

fiscal policy

IPA: /ˈfɪskəl ˈpɒləsi/
Pronunciation: FIS-kuhl POL-uh-see
French: politique budgétaire

Definition: Government policy on taxation and spending.

Example: Fiscal policy can either ease or worsen inflation.

government spending

IPA: /ˈɡʌvənmənt ˈspendɪŋ/
Pronunciation: GUV-uhn-muhnt SPEN-ding
French: dépenses publiques

Definition: Money spent by the state.

Example: Government spending can support households during inflation shocks.

tax cuts

IPA: /ˈtæks kʌts/
Pronunciation: taks kuts
French: baisses d’impôts

Definition: Reductions in taxes.

Example: Tax cuts can stimulate demand and fuel inflation.

subsidy

IPA: /ˈsʌbsɪdi/
Pronunciation: SUB-si-dee
French: subvention

Definition: Government financial support to reduce costs or support firms and households.

Example: Energy subsidies can protect households but cost public money.

price cap

IPA: /ˈpraɪs kæp/
Pronunciation: price kap
French: plafonnement des prix

Definition: A legal or policy limit on the price of a good or service.

Example: A price cap can protect consumers but distort markets.

energy price cap

IPA: /ˈenədʒi praɪs kæp/
Pronunciation: EN-uh-jee price kap
French: plafonnement des prix de l’énergie

Definition: A limit on what energy suppliers can charge customers.

Example: The energy price cap became central to UK politics.

price control

IPA: /ˈpraɪs kənˌtrəʊl/
Pronunciation: price kuhn-TROHL
French: contrôle des prix

Definition: Government regulation of prices.

Example: Price controls can reduce immediate pain but create shortages.

D. Fiscal policy, company behaviour and inflation types

windfall tax

IPA: /ˈwɪndfɔːl tæks/
Pronunciation: WIND-fawl taks
French: taxe sur les superprofits

Definition: A tax on unexpectedly high profits.

Example: A windfall tax on energy firms was proposed during the crisis.

excess profits

IPA: /ˌekses ˈprɒfɪts/
Pronunciation: EK-sess PROF-its
French: surprofits

Definition: Profits above normal levels, often due to unusual conditions.

Example: Energy companies were accused of making excess profits.

profit margins

IPA: /ˈprɒfɪt ˌmɑːdʒɪnz/
Pronunciation: PROF-it MAR-jinz
French: marges bénéficiaires

Definition: The share of revenue kept as profit after costs.

Example: Rising profit margins can contribute to inflation.

greedflation

IPA: /ˌɡriːdˈfleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: greed-FLAY-shuhn
French: inflation par avidité ; greedflation

Definition: A critical term claiming firms raise prices excessively to increase profits during inflation.

Example: Greedflation became a popular explanation for rising prices.

shrinkflation

IPA: /ʃrɪŋkˈfleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: shrink-FLAY-shuhn
French: réduflation ; shrinkflation

Definition: Reducing product size while keeping the price the same.

Example: Shrinkflation makes inflation less visible to consumers.

skimpflation

IPA: /skɪmpˈfleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: skimp-FLAY-shuhn
French: baisse de qualité masquée ; skimpflation

Definition: Reducing product or service quality while keeping prices similar.

Example: Skimpflation can make customers feel cheated.

stagflation

IPA: /stæɡˈfleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: stag-FLAY-shuhn
French: stagflation

Definition: A situation with high inflation and weak growth or high unemployment.

Example: Stagflation is difficult because fighting inflation can worsen unemployment.

hyperinflation

IPA: /ˌhaɪpərɪnˈfleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: hy-puh-rin-FLAY-shuhn
French: hyperinflation

Definition: Extremely rapid and uncontrolled inflation.

Example: Hyperinflation destroys trust in money.

deflation

IPA: /diːˈfleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: dee-FLAY-shuhn
French: déflation

Definition: A general fall in prices across the economy.

Example: Deflation can be dangerous if consumers postpone spending.

disinflation

IPA: /ˌdɪsɪnˈfleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: dis-in-FLAY-shuhn
French: désinflation

Definition: A fall in the inflation rate, while prices still rise.

Example: Disinflation means inflation is slowing, not that prices are falling.

reflation

IPA: /ˌriːˈfleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: ree-FLAY-shuhn
French: reflation

Definition: Policies or recovery that push prices and demand up after weak growth.

Example: Reflation can help escape deflation but may overshoot.

devaluation

IPA: /ˌdiːvæljuˈeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: dee-val-yoo-AY-shuhn
French: dévaluation

Definition: A deliberate reduction in the value of a currency under a fixed exchange-rate system.

Example: Devaluation can make imports more expensive.

depreciation

IPA: /dɪˌpriːʃiˈeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: di-pree-shee-AY-shuhn
French: dépréciation

Definition: A fall in the value of a currency in the market.

Example: Currency depreciation can cause imported inflation.

exchange rate

weak currency

Pronunciation: week KUR-uhn-see

strong currency

savers
