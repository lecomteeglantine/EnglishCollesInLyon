Chapter 16 — Passive Voice
B1 → B2 grammar for press articles, essays, policy analysis, history and formal style.

Chapter objective

This premium grammar chapter helps CPGE students master the passive voice. It covers be + past participle, passive forms in several tenses, modal passives, active-to-passive transformation, common article structures and the classic mistakes made by French speakers.

The golden rule

Subject + be + past participle
The law was passed in 2019.
The policy should be revised.
Main useUse the passive when the action, result or affected object matters more than the agent.
CPGE habitUse passive structures for formal analysis: has been criticised, is widely regarded as, should be taken into account.
Key trapThe passive needs be + past participle: The law was adopted, not The law adopted if someone adopted it.

A. Lesson — What is the passive voice?

Passive voice

French: voix passive

Rule / explanation: The passive voice focuses on the action, result or object rather than the person who does the action.

Example / answer: The law was passed in 2019.

Active voice

French: voix active

Rule / explanation: The active voice focuses on the subject doing the action.

Example / answer: Parliament passed the law in 2019.

Passive form

French: forme passive

Rule / explanation: Use be + past participle.

Example / answer: The policy is criticised by many experts.

Agent

French: agent

Rule / explanation: The person or thing doing the action can be introduced with by.

Example / answer: The report was written by an independent committee.

Unknown agent

French: agent inconnu

Rule / explanation: Use the passive when the agent is unknown.

Example / answer: The documents were leaked last night.

Unimportant agent

French: agent peu important

Rule / explanation: Use the passive when the agent is not important.

Example / answer: The data were collected over ten years.

Formal style

French: style formel

Rule / explanation: The passive is common in formal, academic and journalistic English.

Example / answer: The results were published in a national newspaper.

CPGE use

French: usage CPGE

Rule / explanation: The passive is essential for press articles, policy analysis, history, scientific topics and formal essays.

Example / answer: The reform has been criticised for ignoring low-income families.

B. Passive form by tense

Present Simple passive

French: passif présent simple

Rule / explanation: am/is/are + past participle.

Example / answer: The issue is discussed in the article.

Past Simple passive

French: passif prétérit

Rule / explanation: was/were + past participle.

Example / answer: The bill was rejected by Parliament.

Present Continuous passive

French: passif présent continu

Rule / explanation: am/is/are being + past participle.

Example / answer: The policy is being debated this week.

Past Continuous passive

French: passif prétérit continu

Rule / explanation: was/were being + past participle.

Example / answer: The proposal was being discussed when the scandal broke.

Present Perfect passive

French: passif present perfect

Rule / explanation: has/have been + past participle.

Example / answer: The reform has been criticised by experts.

Past Perfect passive

French: passif past perfect

Rule / explanation: had been + past participle.

Example / answer: The report had been published before the election.

Future passive

French: passif futur

Rule / explanation: will be + past participle.

Example / answer: The law will be reviewed next year.

Modal passive

French: passif avec modal

Rule / explanation: modal + be + past participle.

Example / answer: The policy should be revised.

C. Passive negatives and questions

Present negative passive

French: négation au présent passif

Rule / explanation: am/is/are not + past participle.

Example / answer: The issue is not mentioned in the article.

Past negative passive

French: négation au passé passif

Rule / explanation: was/were not + past participle.

Example / answer: The warnings were not taken seriously.

Perfect negative passive

French: négation au perfect passif

Rule / explanation: has/have not been + past participle.

Example / answer: The problem has not been solved.

Modal negative passive

French: négation avec modal passif

Rule / explanation: modal + not + be + past participle.

Example / answer: The evidence should not be ignored.

Present passive question

French: question passive présent

Rule / explanation: am/is/are + subject + past participle.

Example / answer: Is the policy supported by voters?

Past passive question

French: question passive passé

Rule / explanation: was/were + subject + past participle.

Example / answer: Was the report published yesterday?

Perfect passive question

French: question passive perfect

Rule / explanation: has/have + subject + been + past participle.

Example / answer: Has the reform been implemented?

Modal passive question

French: question avec modal passif

Rule / explanation: modal + subject + be + past participle.

Example / answer: Should the law be changed?

D. When should students use the passive?

Focus on the action

French: focalisation sur l’action

Rule / explanation: Use the passive when the action matters more than the agent.

Example / answer: The bill was adopted after weeks of debate.

Focus on the victim or object

French: focalisation sur l’objet

Rule / explanation: Use the passive when the affected person or thing matters most.

Example / answer: Low-income families were affected by the reform.

Unknown responsibility

French: responsabilité floue

Rule / explanation: Use the passive when responsibility is unclear or hidden.

Example / answer: Mistakes were made during the crisis.

Formal reporting

French: information formelle

Rule / explanation: Use it for formal, institutional or journalistic reporting.

Example / answer: The figures were released by the government.

Scientific or academic style

French: style scientifique

Rule / explanation: Use it to describe methods and findings objectively.

Example / answer: The data were analysed by researchers.

Policy analysis

French: analyse politique

Rule / explanation: Use it to discuss laws, reforms and measures.

Example / answer: The proposal was welcomed by business leaders.

Historical writing

French: écriture historique

Rule / explanation: Use it to describe events and decisions.

Example / answer: The treaty was signed in 1998.

Cautious argumentation

French: prudence argumentative

Rule / explanation: Use it to avoid over-personalising blame.

Example / answer: The issue has been underestimated for too long.

E. Active to passive transformation

Basic transformation

French: transformation de base

Rule / explanation: Object of active sentence becomes subject of passive sentence.

Example / answer: Active: Parliament passed the law. Passive: The law was passed by Parliament.

Keep the tense

French: garder le temps

Rule / explanation: The verb be carries the tense of the active verb.

Example / answer: Active: Experts criticise the reform. Passive: The reform is criticised by experts.

Past participle

French: participe passé

Rule / explanation: The main verb becomes a past participle.

Example / answer: Active: Journalists exposed the scandal. Passive: The scandal was exposed by journalists.

By + agent

French: by + agent

Rule / explanation: Use by only if the agent is useful or important.

Example / answer: The report was written by a respected economist.

Omit obvious agents

French: agent inutile

Rule / explanation: Do not add by people, by someone, by them if unnecessary.

Example / answer: Better: The documents were leaked.

Two-object verbs

French: verbes à deux compléments

Rule / explanation: Some verbs can create two passive forms.

Example / answer: Active: They gave citizens new rights. Passive: Citizens were given new rights.

Prepositional verbs

French: verbes prépositionnels

Rule / explanation: Keep the preposition after the verb.

Example / answer: The issue was dealt with too late.

Phrasal verbs

French: phrasal verbs

Rule / explanation: Keep the particle in the passive.

Example / answer: The proposal was turned down.

F. Common passive structures in CPGE English

is often described as

French: est souvent décrit comme

Rule / explanation: Useful for definitions and article summaries.

Example / answer: Populism is often described as a reaction against elites.

is widely regarded as

French: est largement considéré comme

Rule / explanation: Formal expression for public or expert opinion.

Example / answer: The NHS is widely regarded as a pillar of British society.

is seen as

French: est perçu comme

Rule / explanation: Common journalistic structure.

Example / answer: The reform is seen as unfair by many voters.

is considered to be

French: est considéré comme

Rule / explanation: Formal evaluative structure.

Example / answer: The policy is considered to be too expensive.

has been criticised for

French: a été critiqué pour

Rule / explanation: Useful for analysing controversy.

Example / answer: The government has been criticised for its slow response.

has been accused of

French: a été accusé de

Rule / explanation: Useful for reporting accusations.

Example / answer: The minister has been accused of misleading Parliament.

should be taken into account

French: devrait être pris en compte

Rule / explanation: Useful for nuanced essays.

Example / answer: The historical context should be taken into account.

must be distinguished from

French: doit être distingué de

Rule / explanation: Useful for precise argumentation.

Example / answer: Criticism must be distinguished from hate speech.

G. Passive voice in articles and essays

Article summary

French: résumé d’article

Rule / explanation: Use passive forms to report what is said, shown or criticised.

Example / answer: The housing crisis is presented as a generational issue.

Press reporting

French: presse

Rule / explanation: Use passive forms for official actions and reported events.

Example / answer: The measure was announced after months of pressure.

Essay argument

French: argumentation

Rule / explanation: Use passive forms to sound formal and analytical.

Example / answer: This problem cannot be understood without its economic context.

Graph commentary

French: commentaire graphique

Rule / explanation: Use passive forms for data collection and publication.

Example / answer: The data were collected between 2010 and 2024.

Policy recommendation

French: recommandation

Rule / explanation: Use modal passive for proposals.

Example / answer: The law should be amended.

Criticism

French: critique

Rule / explanation: Use passive structures to report criticism without becoming too personal.

Example / answer: The government has been criticised for ignoring expert advice.

Historical context

French: contexte historique

Rule / explanation: Use passive forms to describe institutional decisions.

Example / answer: The agreement was signed after years of negotiation.

Balanced conclusion

French: conclusion nuancée

Rule / explanation: Use passive to focus on solutions or principles.

Example / answer: Fundamental rights must be protected.

H. Typical mistakes by French speakers

Forgetting be

French: oubli de be

Rule / explanation: Passive voice needs be + past participle.

Example / answer: Wrong: The law passed in 2019 if it means someone passed it. Correct: The law was passed in 2019.

Using the wrong participle

French: mauvais participe

Rule / explanation: Use the past participle, not the base verb or past simple form.

Example / answer: Wrong: The report was write. Correct: The report was written.

Confusing active and passive meaning

French: sens actif/passif

Rule / explanation: The law passed can mean the law succeeded; the law was passed means people adopted it.

Example / answer: The bill passed. / The bill was passed by Parliament.

Overusing by

French: by abusif

Rule / explanation: Only use by when the agent matters.

Example / answer: Better: The documents were leaked, not The documents were leaked by someone.

Wrong modal passive

French: modal passif

Rule / explanation: Use modal + be + past participle.

Example / answer: Wrong: The law should changed. Correct: The law should be changed.

Wrong perfect passive

French: perfect passif

Rule / explanation: Use has/have been + past participle.

Example / answer: Wrong: The reform has criticised. Correct: The reform has been criticised.

Wrong continuous passive

French: continu passif

Rule / explanation: Use is/are being + past participle.

Example / answer: Wrong: The policy is debated now. Better: The policy is being debated now.

Translating French on mechanically

French: traduction de on

Rule / explanation: French on often becomes passive in English, not they or we.

Example / answer: On a adopté la loi = The law was adopted.
