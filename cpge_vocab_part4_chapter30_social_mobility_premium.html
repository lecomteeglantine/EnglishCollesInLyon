Essential vocabulary for khôlles, essays, press reviews and competitive exams.

Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse social mobility: class, meritocracy, equal opportunity, inherited privilege, cultural and social capital, education, work, housing, regional inequality, poverty traps, labour-market barriers and policies designed to make mobility possible.

How to use this chapter

For press articlesFocus on terms such as upward mobility, birth lottery, cultural capital, myth of meritocracy, elite reproduction, opportunity gap, property ladder and levelling up.
For oral examsUse this vocabulary to explain how individual effort matters, but is strongly shaped by family wealth, education, housing, networks, geography and discrimination.

A. Core concepts: types of social mobility

social mobility

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl məʊˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl moh-BIL-uh-tee
French: mobilité sociale

Definition: Movement between social classes or positions in society.

Example: Social mobility is often used to measure whether a society offers real equal opportunity.

upward mobility

IPA: /ˌʌpwəd məʊˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: UP-wuhd moh-BIL-uh-tee
French: ascension sociale

Definition: Movement to a higher social or economic position.

Example: Education is often presented as the main path to upward mobility.

downward mobility

IPA: /ˌdaʊnwəd məʊˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: DOWN-wuhd moh-BIL-uh-tee
French: déclassement ; mobilité descendante

Definition: Movement to a lower social or economic position.

Example: Economic crises can increase fears of downward mobility.

intergenerational mobility

IPA: /ˌɪntədʒenəˈreɪʃənəl məʊˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: IN-tuh-jen-uh-RAY-shuh-nuhl moh-BIL-uh-tee
French: mobilité intergénérationnelle

Definition: The extent to which children reach a different social or economic position from their parents.

Example: Intergenerational mobility is lower when family background strongly determines success.

intragenerational mobility

IPA: /ˌɪntrədʒenəˈreɪʃənəl məʊˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: IN-truh-jen-uh-RAY-shuh-nuhl moh-BIL-uh-tee
French: mobilité intragénérationnelle

Definition: Social or economic movement within one person’s lifetime.

Example: Intragenerational mobility can happen through career progression.

absolute mobility

IPA: /ˌæbsəluːt məʊˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: AB-suh-loot moh-BIL-uh-tee
French: mobilité absolue

Definition: Whether people are better off than their parents in real living standards.

Example: Absolute mobility was easier during periods of strong economic growth.

relative mobility

IPA: /ˌrelətɪv məʊˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: REL-uh-tiv moh-BIL-uh-tee
French: mobilité relative

Definition: Whether people change position compared with others in society.

Example: Relative mobility remains limited when privilege is strongly inherited.

class mobility

IPA: /ˌklɑːs məʊˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: klass moh-BIL-uh-tee
French: mobilité de classe

Definition: Movement between social classes.

Example: Class mobility is closely linked to education, wealth and social networks.

economic mobility

IPA: /ˌiːkəˈnɒmɪk məʊˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: ee-kuh-NOM-ik moh-BIL-uh-tee
French: mobilité économique

Definition: The ability to improve one’s income, wealth or economic position.

Example: Economic mobility is harder when wages stagnate and housing costs rise.

occupational mobility

IPA: /ˌɒkjʊˈpeɪʃənəl məʊˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: ok-yuh-PAY-shuh-nuhl moh-BIL-uh-tee
French: mobilité professionnelle

Definition: Movement between jobs or occupational categories.

Example: Occupational mobility may require training and qualifications.

income mobility

IPA: /ˈɪnkʌm məʊˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: IN-kum moh-BIL-uh-tee
French: mobilité des revenus

Definition: Movement up or down the income distribution.

Example: Income mobility is limited when low-paid workers remain trapped in insecure jobs.

wealth mobility

IPA: /ˈwelθ məʊˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: welth moh-BIL-uh-tee
French: mobilité patrimoniale

Definition: Movement up or down the wealth distribution.

Example: Wealth mobility is strongly affected by inheritance and property ownership.

geographical mobility

IPA: /ˌdʒiːəˈɡræfɪkəl məʊˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: jee-uh-GRAF-i-kuhl moh-BIL-uh-tee
French: mobilité géographique

Definition: The ability or willingness to move to another place for better opportunities.

Example: High housing costs can limit geographical mobility.

social ladder

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl ˈlædə/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl LAD-uh
French: échelle sociale

Definition: A metaphor for the hierarchy of social positions.

Example: Many people feel that the social ladder is harder to climb.

ladder of opportunity

IPA: /ˌlædər əv ˌɒpəˈtjuːnəti/
Pronunciation: LAD-uh ov op-uh-TYOO-nuh-tee
French: échelle des opportunités

Definition: A metaphor for routes that allow people to improve their lives.

Example: Good schools are supposed to provide a ladder of opportunity.

glass ceiling

IPA: /ˌɡlɑːs ˈsiːlɪŋ/
Pronunciation: glass SEE-ling
French: plafond de verre

Definition: An invisible barrier preventing certain groups from reaching top positions.

Example: The glass ceiling limits women and minorities in elite professions.

sticky floor

IPA: /ˌstɪki ˈflɔː/
Pronunciation: STIK-ee floor
French: plancher collant ; blocage dans les emplois précaires

Definition: A situation where people remain trapped in low-paid or low-status jobs.

Example: The sticky floor prevents many workers from moving up.

poverty trap

IPA: /ˈpɒvəti træp/
Pronunciation: POV-uh-tee trap
French: piège de la pauvreté

Definition: A situation in which poverty itself makes it difficult to escape poverty.

Example: Low wages, debt and poor housing can create a poverty trap.

cycle of poverty

IPA: /ˌsaɪkəl əv ˈpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: SY-kuhl ov POV-uh-tee
French: cycle de la pauvreté

Definition: The transmission of poverty from one generation to the next.

Example: Early intervention can help break the cycle of poverty.

social background

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl ˈbækɡraʊnd/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl BAK-ground
French: origine sociale

Definition: A person’s family class, education, income and social environment.

Example: Social background still shapes educational achievement.

B. Background, privilege, capital and meritocracy

family background

IPA: /ˌfæməli ˈbækɡraʊnd/
Pronunciation: FAM-uh-lee BAK-ground
French: milieu familial

Definition: The social, economic and educational circumstances of a person’s family.

Example: Family background often affects children’s life chances.

class background

IPA: /ˌklɑːs ˈbækɡraʊnd/
Pronunciation: klass BAK-ground
French: origine de classe

Definition: The social class into which a person is born or raised.

Example: Class background can influence confidence and cultural codes.

socio-economic status

IPA: /ˌsəʊsiəʊ iːkəˈnɒmɪk ˈsteɪtəs/
Pronunciation: SOH-see-oh ee-kuh-NOM-ik STAY-tuhs
French: statut socio-économique

Definition: A person’s position based on income, education and occupation.

Example: Socio-economic status is a strong predictor of educational outcomes.

life chances

IPA: /ˈlaɪf ˌtʃɑːnsɪz/
Pronunciation: life CHAN-siz
French: chances dans la vie

Definition: The opportunities a person has for success, health and security.

Example: Life chances are shaped long before adulthood.

starting point

IPA: /ˈstɑːtɪŋ pɔɪnt/
Pronunciation: STAR-ting point
French: point de départ

Definition: The social or economic position from which someone begins life.

Example: Not everyone starts from the same starting point.

birth lottery

IPA: /ˈbɜːθ ˌlɒtəri/
Pronunciation: birth LOT-uh-ree
French: loterie de la naissance

Definition: The idea that life outcomes are heavily shaped by circumstances of birth.

Example: The birth lottery contradicts the ideal of pure meritocracy.

inherited advantage

IPA: /ɪnˌherɪtɪd ədˈvɑːntɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: in-HER-i-tid ad-VAN-tij
French: avantage hérité

Definition: Benefits received from family background, wealth or networks.

Example: Inherited advantage gives some children a head start.

inherited privilege

IPA: /ɪnˌherɪtɪd ˈprɪvəlɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: in-HER-i-tid PRIV-uh-lij
French: privilège hérité

Definition: Social advantages passed down through family status or wealth.

Example: Inherited privilege undermines the idea of equal opportunity.

inherited wealth

IPA: /ɪnˌherɪtɪd ˈwelθ/
Pronunciation: in-HER-i-tid welth
French: patrimoine hérité

Definition: Money, property or assets passed from one generation to another.

Example: Inherited wealth gives families long-term security.

family wealth

IPA: /ˌfæməli ˈwelθ/
Pronunciation: FAM-uh-lee welth
French: patrimoine familial

Definition: Assets and resources owned by a family.

Example: Family wealth often matters more than income for life chances.

cultural capital

IPA: /ˌkʌltʃərəl ˈkæpɪtəl/
Pronunciation: KUL-chuh-ruhl KAP-i-tuhl
French: capital culturel

Definition: Knowledge, habits, language and tastes that help people succeed in institutions.

Example: Middle-class pupils often arrive at school with more cultural capital.

social capital

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl ˈkæpɪtəl/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl KAP-i-tuhl
French: capital social

Definition: Useful relationships, networks and connections.

Example: Social capital can open doors to internships and jobs.

economic capital

IPA: /ˌiːkəˈnɒmɪk ˈkæpɪtəl/
Pronunciation: ee-kuh-NOM-ik KAP-i-tuhl
French: capital économique

Definition: Money, property and financial resources.

Example: Economic capital allows families to buy better housing and education.

network

IPA: /ˈnetwɜːk/
Pronunciation: NET-wurk
French: réseau

Definition: A group of contacts that can provide information or opportunities.

Example: Professional networks often reproduce privilege.

connections

IPA: /kəˈnekʃənz/
Pronunciation: kuh-NEK-shuhnz
French: relations ; réseau

Definition: Useful personal or professional relationships.

Example: Connections can matter as much as qualifications.

old boys’ network

IPA: /ˌəʊld ˈbɔɪz ˌnetwɜːk/
Pronunciation: ohld boyz NET-wurk
French: réseau des anciens ; entre-soi masculin élitiste

Definition: Informal elite connections, often among men educated at prestigious institutions.

Example: The old boys’ network is often criticised in Britain.

meritocracy

IPA: /ˌmerɪˈtɒkrəsi/
Pronunciation: mer-i-TOK-ruh-see
French: méritocratie

Definition: A system where success is supposed to depend on talent and effort.

Example: Meritocracy is powerful as an ideal but contested in practice.

meritocratic ideal

IPA: /ˌmerɪtəˈkrætɪk aɪˈdɪəl/
Pronunciation: mer-i-tuh-KRAT-ik eye-DEE-uhl
French: idéal méritocratique

Definition: The belief that people should succeed according to talent and hard work.

Example: The meritocratic ideal can hide structural inequalities.

myth of meritocracy

IPA: /ˌmɪθ əv ˌmerɪˈtɒkrəsi/
Pronunciation: mith ov mer-i-TOK-ruh-see
French: mythe de la méritocratie

Definition: The argument that meritocracy is more imagined than real because privilege shapes outcomes.

Example: The myth of meritocracy can blame individuals for structural barriers.

equal opportunity

IPA: /ˌiːkwəl ˌɒpəˈtjuːnəti/
Pronunciation: EE-kwuhl op-uh-TYOO-nuh-tee
French: égalité des chances

Definition: The principle that everyone should have the same chance to succeed.

Example: Equal opportunity requires more than formal rights.

equality of opportunity

IPA: /iˌkwɒləti əv ˌɒpəˈtjuːnəti/
Pronunciation: i-KWOL-uh-tee ov op-uh-TYOO-nuh-tee
French: égalité des chances

Definition: A fair distribution of chances, regardless of background.

Example: Education policy often claims to promote equality of opportunity.

equality of outcome

IPA: /iˌkwɒləti əv ˈaʊtkʌm/
Pronunciation: i-KWOL-uh-tee ov OWT-kum
French: égalité des résultats

Definition: The idea that people should end up with similar results or living standards.

Example: Critics of redistribution often reject equality of outcome.

level playing field

IPA: /ˌlevəl ˈpleɪɪŋ fiːld/
Pronunciation: LEV-uhl PLAY-ing field
French: conditions équitables ; terrain de jeu égal

Definition: A fair situation in which everyone has comparable chances.

Example: Public education should help level the playing field.

fair shot

IPA: /ˌfeə ˈʃɒt/
Pronunciation: fair shot
French: chance équitable

Definition: A fair chance to succeed.

Example: Politicians often promise that every child deserves a fair shot.

fairness

IPA: /ˈfeənəs/
Pronunciation: FAIR-nuhs
French: équité ; justice

Definition: Justice and equal treatment.

Example: Fairness is central to debates about mobility.

structural inequality

IPA: /ˌstrʌktʃərəl ˌɪnɪˈkwɒləti/
Pronunciation: STRUK-chuh-ruhl in-i-KWOL-uh-tee
French: inégalité structurelle

Definition: Inequality produced by institutions, systems and social structures.

Example: Structural inequality limits individual effort.

systemic barrier

IPA: /sɪˌstemɪk ˈbæriə/
Pronunciation: si-STEM-ik BARR-ee-uh
French: obstacle systémique

Definition: A barrier created by a system rather than one individual.

Example: Systemic barriers prevent many talented students from progressing.

social barrier

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl ˈbæriə/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl BARR-ee-uh
French: barrière sociale

Definition: A social obstacle that limits opportunity.

Example: Accent and class codes can act as social barriers.

glass escalator

IPA: /ˌɡlɑːs ˈeskəleɪtə/
Pronunciation: glass ES-kuh-lay-tuh
French: ascenseur de verre

Definition: An advantage that helps some men rise quickly in female-dominated professions.

Example: The glass escalator shows that gender can shape mobility.

elite reproduction

IPA: /eɪˌliːt ˌriːprəˈdʌkʃən/
Pronunciation: ay-LEET ree-pruh-DUK-shuhn
French: reproduction des élites

Definition: The process by which privileged groups maintain their status across generations.

Example: Elite reproduction can occur through schools, universities and networks.

C. Class, elites, education and access

class reproduction

IPA: /ˌklɑːs ˌriːprəˈdʌkʃən/
Pronunciation: klass ree-pruh-DUK-shuhn
French: reproduction sociale

Definition: The transmission of class position from parents to children.

Example: Class reproduction makes mobility more difficult.

social reproduction

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl ˌriːprəˈdʌkʃən/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl ree-pruh-DUK-shuhn
French: reproduction sociale

Definition: The way social structures and inequalities are passed across generations.

Example: Social reproduction can happen through education and housing.

class system

IPA: /ˈklɑːs ˌsɪstəm/
Pronunciation: klass SIS-tuhm
French: système de classes

Definition: A social hierarchy based on wealth, education, occupation and culture.

Example: The British class system remains deeply influential.

working class

IPA: /ˌwɜːkɪŋ ˈklɑːs/
Pronunciation: WUR-king klass
French: classe ouvrière ; classes populaires

Definition: People associated with manual, service or lower-paid work.

Example: Working-class students are underrepresented in elite universities.

middle class

IPA: /ˌmɪdəl ˈklɑːs/
Pronunciation: MID-uhl klass
French: classe moyenne

Definition: People associated with professional work, education and relative security.

Example: Middle-class parents often know how to navigate school systems.

upper class

IPA: /ˌʌpə ˈklɑːs/
Pronunciation: UP-uh klass
French: classe supérieure

Definition: The wealthiest and most socially privileged class.

Example: The upper class benefits from inherited wealth and elite networks.

underclass

IPA: /ˈʌndəklɑːs/
Pronunciation: UN-duh-klass
French: sous-classe ; terme controversé

Definition: A controversial term for a highly disadvantaged group seen as excluded from mainstream society.

Example: The word underclass can stigmatise poverty.

working poor

IPA: /ˌwɜːkɪŋ ˈpɔː/
Pronunciation: WUR-king poor
French: travailleurs pauvres

Definition: People who work but remain poor because wages are low or costs are high.

Example: The working poor show that employment does not always guarantee mobility.

precariat

IPA: /prɪˈkeəriət/
Pronunciation: pri-KAIR-ee-uht
French: précariat

Definition: People living with insecure jobs, low wages and unstable conditions.

Example: The precariat faces limited prospects of upward mobility.

elite

IPA: /ɪˈliːt/
Pronunciation: i-LEET
French: élite

Definition: A small group with significant power, wealth or prestige.

Example: Elite recruitment often favours privileged backgrounds.

establishment

IPA: /ɪˈstæblɪʃmənt/
Pronunciation: i-STAB-lish-muhnt
French: establishment ; élite dirigeante

Definition: Powerful institutions and people who dominate public life.

Example: The establishment is often accused of being closed to outsiders.

social closure

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl ˈkləʊʒə/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl KLOH-zhuh
French: fermeture sociale

Definition: The process by which groups protect privileges by limiting access.

Example: Credential requirements can create social closure.

credentialism

IPA: /krɪˈdenʃəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: kri-DEN-shuhl-iz-uhm
French: diplômisme ; culte du diplôme

Definition: Overreliance on formal qualifications as proof of ability.

Example: Credentialism can exclude capable people without elite degrees.

elite university

IPA: /eɪˌliːt ˌjuːnɪˈvɜːsəti/
Pronunciation: ay-LEET yoo-ni-VUR-suh-tee
French: université d’élite

Definition: A prestigious and highly selective university.

Example: Elite universities can function as gateways to top careers.

Oxbridge

IPA: /ˈɒksbrɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: OKS-brij
French: Oxbridge ; Oxford et Cambridge

Definition: Oxford and Cambridge universities considered together.

Example: Oxbridge is strongly associated with Britain’s elite.

Ivy League

IPA: /ˈaɪvi liːɡ/
Pronunciation: EYE-vee leeg
French: Ivy League

Definition: A group of prestigious universities in the United States.

Example: The Ivy League is often seen as a route to elite careers.

legacy admission

Pronunciation: LEG-uh-see ad-MISH-uhn
French: admission préférentielle des enfants d’anciens élèves

Definition: Preferential admission for applicants whose relatives attended the institution.

Example: Legacy admission is criticised for privileging the already privileged.

affirmative action

IPA: /əˌfɜːmətɪv ˈækʃən/
Pronunciation: uh-FUR-muh-tiv AK-shuhn
French: discrimination positive

Definition: Policies designed to improve access for historically disadvantaged groups.

Example: Affirmative action aims to make elite education more representative.

widening participation

IPA: /ˌwaɪdənɪŋ pɑːˌtɪsɪˈpeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: WY-duh-ning par-tis-i-PAY-shuhn
French: élargissement de la participation

Definition: Policies encouraging underrepresented groups to enter higher education.

Example: Widening participation programmes target disadvantaged students.

access programme

IPA: /ˈækses ˌprəʊɡræm/
Pronunciation: AK-sess PROH-gram
French: programme d’accès ; dispositif d’ouverture sociale

Definition: A scheme helping disadvantaged students access education or careers.

Example: Access programmes can demystify elite universities.

scholarship

IPA: /ˈskɒləʃɪp/
Pronunciation: SKOL-uh-ship
French: bourse d’études

Definition: Financial support awarded to a student, often based on need or merit.

Example: Scholarships can reduce financial barriers.

bursary

French: bourse d’aide sociale

Definition: Need-based financial support for students.

Example: Bursaries help low-income students afford study.

private tutoring

IPA: /ˌpraɪvət ˈtjuːtərɪŋ/

French: cours particuliers

Example: Private tutoring gives wealthy families an advantage.

shadow education

French: éducation parallèle ; soutien privé

school funding

French: financement des écoles

funding gap

French: écart de financement

Example: The funding gap often reflects local inequality.

underfunded school

IPA: /ˌʌndəˈfʌndɪd skuːl/
Pronunciation: UN-duh-FUN-did school
French: école sous-financée

catchment area

IPA: /ˈkætʃmənt ˌeəriə/
Pronunciation: KATCH-muhnt AIR-ee-uh
French: secteur scolaire

child poverty
