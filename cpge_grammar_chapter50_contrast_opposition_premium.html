A. Lesson — What are contrast and opposition?

Contrast

Rule / explanation: Contrast highlights a difference between two ideas, situations, groups or trends.

Example / answer: The first article focuses on economic growth, whereas the second examines social inequality.

Opposition

Rule / explanation: Opposition presents two ideas as conflicting or incompatible.

Example / answer: The reform promises efficiency, but critics fear greater inequality.

Concession vs contrast

Rule / explanation: Concession acknowledges an unexpected fact; contrast simply sets two different ideas against each other.

Example / answer: Although the policy is popular, it is ineffective. / The policy is popular, whereas its predecessor was not.

CPGE relevance

Rule / explanation: Contrast is essential for comparing sources, structuring essays and building nuanced problematics.

Example / answer: The debate is not between progress and decline, but between competing visions of progress.

B. But, yet and still

But

Rule / explanation: But is the most common coordinating conjunction for direct contrast.

Example / answer: The reform is ambitious, but its funding remains uncertain.

Yet

Rule / explanation: Yet often expresses a stronger or more surprising contrast than but.

Example / answer: The policy is widely criticised, yet it remains popular.

Still

Rule / explanation: Still introduces a contrasting point after acknowledging what came before.

Example / answer: The evidence is incomplete. Still, the conclusion deserves consideration.

Punctuation

Rule / explanation: Use a comma before but or yet when they join two independent clauses.

Example / answer: The government promised reform, but Parliament rejected the proposal.

Avoid repetition

Rule / explanation: Do not begin every contrast with but; vary the structure.

Example / answer: Use whereas, however, by contrast and unlike where appropriate.

C. While and whereas

While

Rule / explanation: While can contrast two simultaneous facts or two different viewpoints.

Example / answer: While the government stresses growth, the opposition focuses on inequality.

Whereas

Rule / explanation: Whereas creates a clear and formal contrast between parallel facts.

Example / answer: Urban areas benefited, whereas rural communities were neglected.

Parallel structure

Rule / explanation: The two clauses should be grammatically balanced.

Example / answer: The first text examines causes, whereas the second analyses consequences.

Position

Rule / explanation: While and whereas may begin or connect clauses.

Example / answer: Whereas older voters support the reform, younger voters remain sceptical.

Not always concession

Rule / explanation: In comparative writing, while often means whereas rather than although.

Example / answer: While one author favours regulation, the other defends market freedom.

D. However, nevertheless and nonetheless

However

Rule / explanation: However introduces a contrasting statement and is common in formal writing.

Example / answer: The proposal is costly. However, it may be necessary.

Nevertheless

Rule / explanation: Nevertheless means despite the previous point and often sounds more formal.

Example / answer: The evidence is limited. Nevertheless, the argument is plausible.

Nonetheless

Rule / explanation: Nonetheless is close in meaning to nevertheless.

Example / answer: The article contains factual gaps. Nonetheless, its conclusion remains persuasive.

Correct punctuation

Rule / explanation: Do not join two independent clauses with only a comma before however.

Example / answer: Correct: The policy is flawed; however, it remains popular.

Flexible position

Rule / explanation: These adverbs can appear at the beginning, middle or end of a clause.

Example / answer: The government nevertheless continued with the reform.

E. By contrast, in contrast and conversely

By contrast

Rule / explanation: Use by contrast to introduce a clearly different case.

Example / answer: The first article is optimistic. By contrast, the second predicts a prolonged crisis.

In contrast

Rule / explanation: In contrast has a similar meaning and may be followed by to or with.

Example / answer: In contrast to urban areas, rural regions received little investment.

Conversely

Rule / explanation: Conversely introduces the reverse situation or logical opposite.

Example / answer: Higher wages may increase demand; conversely, wage cuts may reduce consumption.

On the other hand

Rule / explanation: Use this to present another side of an issue, especially in discussion.

Example / answer: Regulation may limit innovation. On the other hand, it can protect consumers.

On the contrary

Rule / explanation: Use on the contrary to reject a previous statement, not merely to add a different point.

Example / answer: The reform did not reduce inequality. On the contrary, it widened the gap.

F. Unlike, unlike that of and compared with

Unlike + noun

Rule / explanation: Use unlike before a noun or pronoun to express difference.

Example / answer: Unlike the previous government, the current administration supports regulation.

Unlike + clause error

Rule / explanation: Unlike is not normally followed directly by a full finite clause.

Example / answer: Better: Unlike France, Britain... / Whereas France..., Britain...

Unlike that of

Rule / explanation: Use that of or those of to avoid comparing unlike things.

Example / answer: Britain’s healthcare system differs from that of the United States.

Compared with

Rule / explanation: Use compared with to examine similarities and differences.

Example / answer: Compared with earlier reforms, the new policy is more ambitious.

Compared to

Rule / explanation: Compared to is often acceptable, especially for broader analogy, but compared with is traditional for direct comparison.

Example / answer: Compared with the first article, the second is more critical.

G. Rather than, instead of and as opposed to

Rather than

Rule / explanation: Use rather than to reject one option in favour of another.

Example / answer: The government chose delay rather than reform.

Parallel forms

Rule / explanation: Keep the grammatical forms on both sides balanced.

Example / answer: The article seeks to explain rather than condemn.

Instead of

Rule / explanation: Use instead of before a noun or gerund.

Example / answer: Instead of reducing inequality, the policy widened the gap.

As opposed to

Rule / explanation: Use as opposed to for a formal contrast between alternatives.

Example / answer: The debate concerns regulation as opposed to prohibition.

Not X but Y

Rule / explanation: Use this pattern to correct or redefine an interpretation.

Example / answer: The issue is not a lack of ambition but a failure of implementation.

H. Antithesis and balanced opposition

Antithesis

Rule / explanation: Antithesis places opposing ideas in parallel structures for rhetorical force.

Example / answer: The reform promised stability but produced uncertainty.

Not only contrast, but tension

Rule / explanation: A strong antithesis reveals a meaningful conflict, not just a difference.

Example / answer: The government demands patience; voters demand results.

From X to Y

Rule / explanation: Use from...to... to express movement between contrasting states.

Example / answer: The debate has shifted from economic efficiency to democratic legitimacy.

The more...the less

Rule / explanation: Use this paired comparative to show inverse development.

Example / answer: The more centralised the system becomes, the less responsive it appears.

Whether...or

Rule / explanation: Use whether...or to frame competing alternatives.

Example / answer: The question is whether regulation protects innovation or restricts it.

I. Contrast in article comparison

The first text..., while the second...

Rule / explanation: Use this pattern to compare two sources efficiently.

Example / answer: The first text emphasises economic benefits, while the second highlights social costs.

Unlike the first author...

Rule / explanation: Use unlike to contrast authors, arguments or methods.

Example / answer: Unlike the first author, the second questions the reliability of the data.

Both texts agree on X, but differ on Y

Rule / explanation: This structure combines common ground and opposition.

Example / answer: Both texts agree on the urgency of reform but differ on the role of the state.

A more critical perspective

Rule / explanation: Use comparative language to distinguish tone or position.

Example / answer: The second article adopts a markedly more critical perspective.

Contrasting evidence

Rule / explanation: Use contrast markers to organise conflicting data.

Example / answer: Official figures suggest recovery; by contrast, household surveys reveal continuing hardship.

J. Contrast in CPGE argumentation

Balanced thesis

Rule / explanation: A strong thesis can place two legitimate values in tension.

Example / answer: The challenge is to reconcile economic efficiency with social justice.

False opposition

Rule / explanation: Use contrast structures to challenge an oversimplified binary.

Example / answer: The debate should not oppose growth to sustainability, but examine how the two can be combined.

Qualification

Rule / explanation: Contrast helps refine an argument instead of choosing an absolute side.

Example / answer: Regulation may slow innovation in the short term, yet strengthen trust in the long term.

Counter-position

Rule / explanation: Introduce an opposing viewpoint before responding.

Example / answer: Some argue that markets regulate themselves. By contrast, recent crises suggest that oversight remains necessary.

Conclusion

Rule / explanation: End by resolving or reframing the central opposition.

Example / answer: Ultimately, the choice is not between freedom and regulation, but between responsible freedom and uncontrolled power.

K. Typical mistakes by French speakers

Comma splice with however

Rule / explanation: However cannot join two independent clauses with only a comma.

Example / answer: Wrong: The policy failed, however it remained popular. Correct: The policy failed; however, it remained popular.

On the contrary misuse

Rule / explanation: On the contrary rejects a previous statement; it does not simply mean on the other hand.

Example / answer: Use on the other hand for another perspective.

Unlike + full clause

Rule / explanation: Use whereas or while before a full clause.

Example / answer: Wrong: Unlike France invests more, Britain cuts spending. Correct: Whereas France invests more, Britain cuts spending.

Illogical comparison

Rule / explanation: Compare equivalent elements.

Example / answer: Wrong: Britain’s economy is larger than Ireland. Correct: Britain’s economy is larger than Ireland’s.

Rather than mismatch

Rule / explanation: Keep both sides grammatically parallel.

Example / answer: Wrong: The policy aims to reduce costs rather than improving access.

Overusing but

Rule / explanation: Too many simple but-links make writing repetitive.

Example / answer: Vary with whereas, however, by contrast, unlike and rather than.

L. Exercises

1. Combine with whereas

Rule / explanation: The first article focuses on causes. The second focuses on consequences.

Example / answer: The first article focuses on causes, whereas the second focuses on consequences.

2. Correct the punctuation

Rule / explanation: The reform is popular, however its results are disappointing.

Example / answer: The reform is popular; however, its results are disappointing.

3. Choose the correct connector

Rule / explanation: The policy did not reduce inequality. ___, it widened the gap. (On the other hand / On the contrary)

Example / answer: The policy did not reduce inequality. On the contrary, it widened the gap.

4. Rewrite with unlike

Rule / explanation: The previous government opposed regulation. The current government supports it.

Example / answer: Unlike the previous government, the current government supports regulation.

5. Correct the comparison

Rule / explanation: Britain’s healthcare system differs from the United States.

Example / answer: Britain’s healthcare system differs from that of the United States.

6. Rewrite with rather than

Rule / explanation: The article explains the crisis. It does not condemn individuals.

Example / answer: The article seeks to explain the crisis rather than condemn individuals.

7. Translate

Rule / explanation: Les deux textes s’accordent sur l’urgence de la réforme, mais divergent sur le rôle de l’État.

Example / answer: Both texts agree on the urgency of reform but differ on the role of the state.

8. Mini concours challenge

Rule / explanation: Write a short paragraph using whereas, however, by contrast, unlike and not X but Y.

Example / answer: Example: Whereas the first article stresses economic recovery, the second focuses on persistent inequality. Official figures appear encouraging; however, household surveys remain pessimistic. By contrast with the government’s optimism, public confidence is weak. Unlike the minister, the journalist argues that the problem is not growth but distribution.
