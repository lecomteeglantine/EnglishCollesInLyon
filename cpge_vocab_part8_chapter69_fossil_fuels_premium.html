Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse fossil fuels: coal, oil, natural gas, extraction, drilling, fracking, pipelines, refining, emissions, methane leaks, air pollution, energy security, petrostates, subsidies, stranded assets, greenwashing, carbon capture and the political difficulty of phasing out fossil fuels while protecting societies and economies.

How to use this chapter

For press articlesFocus on terms such as fossil fuel dependency, fracking, LNG, methane leak, energy geopolitics, fossil fuel subsidy, carbon lock-in, stranded asset, greenwashing and carbon capture and storage.
For oral examsUse this vocabulary to explain fossil fuels as both the foundation of industrial modernity and the main driver of climate change, geopolitical dependency and air pollution.

A. Core fossil fuel vocabulary

fossil fuels

IPA: /ˈfɒsəl ˌfjuːəlz/
Pronunciation: FOS-uhl FYOO-uhlz
French: combustibles fossiles ; énergies fossiles

Definition: Energy sources formed from ancient organic matter, mainly coal, oil and natural gas.

Example: Fossil fuels still dominate the global energy system.

coal

IPA: /kəʊl/
Pronunciation: kohl
French: charbon

Definition: A black or brown fossil fuel burned for electricity, heat and industry.

Example: Coal is the most carbon-intensive major fossil fuel.

oil

IPA: /ɔɪl/
Pronunciation: oil
French: pétrole

Definition: A liquid fossil fuel refined into petrol, diesel, jet fuel and petrochemical products.

Example: Oil remains central to transport and global trade.

crude oil

IPA: /ˌkruːd ˈɔɪl/
Pronunciation: krood oil
French: pétrole brut

Definition: Unrefined oil extracted from underground reservoirs.

Example: Crude oil prices influence the global economy.

natural gas

IPA: /ˌnætʃərəl ˈɡæs/
Pronunciation: NAT-chuh-ruhl gas
French: gaz naturel

Definition: A fossil fuel made mostly of methane, used for heating, electricity and industry.

Example: Natural gas emits CO2 and can leak methane.

methane

IPA: /ˈmiːθeɪn/
Pronunciation: MEE-thayn
French: méthane

Definition: A powerful greenhouse gas and the main component of natural gas.

Example: Methane leaks make gas more climate-damaging.

hydrocarbon

IPA: /ˌhaɪdrəʊˈkɑːbən/
Pronunciation: HY-droh-KAR-buhn
French: hydrocarbure

Definition: A chemical compound made of hydrogen and carbon, found in fossil fuels.

Example: Oil and gas are hydrocarbons.

petroleum

IPA: /pəˈtrəʊliəm/
Pronunciation: puh-TROH-lee-uhm
French: pétrole

Definition: A broad term for crude oil and products made from it.

Example: Petroleum products include petrol, diesel and plastics.

gasoline

IPA: /ˈɡæsəliːn/
Pronunciation: GAS-uh-leen
French: essence

Definition: US term for petrol, a fuel used in cars.

Example: Gasoline demand remains high in car-dependent societies.

petrol

IPA: /ˈpetrəl/
Pronunciation: PET-ruhl
French: essence

Definition: UK term for gasoline, a fuel used in cars.

Example: Petrol cars are being phased out in some countries.

diesel

IPA: /ˈdiːzəl/
Pronunciation: DEE-zuhl
French: diesel ; gazole

Definition: A fuel used in many cars, trucks, ships and generators.

Example: Diesel engines emit pollutants harmful to health.

jet fuel

IPA: /ˈdʒet ˌfjuːəl/
Pronunciation: jet FYOO-uhl
French: kérosène ; carburant aviation

Definition: Fuel used by aircraft.

Example: Jet fuel is difficult to replace at scale.

liquefied natural gas

IPA: /ˈlɪkwɪfaɪd ˌnætʃərəl ˈɡæs/
Pronunciation: LIK-wi-fyd NAT-chuh-ruhl gas
French: gaz naturel liquéfié

Definition: Natural gas cooled into liquid form for transport by ship.

Example: Liquefied natural gas has reshaped energy geopolitics.

LNG

IPA: /ˌel en ˈdʒiː/
Pronunciation: el-en-jee
French: GNL

Definition: Abbreviation for liquefied natural gas.

Example: LNG imports can reduce dependence on pipelines.

shale gas

IPA: /ˈʃeɪl ɡæs/
Pronunciation: shayl gas
French: gaz de schiste

Definition: Natural gas extracted from shale rock, often using fracking.

Example: Shale gas transformed the US energy market.

shale oil

IPA: /ˈʃeɪl ɔɪl/
Pronunciation: shayl oil
French: pétrole de schiste

Definition: Oil extracted from shale formations, often through hydraulic fracturing.

Example: Shale oil increased US oil production.

tar sands

IPA: /ˈtɑː sændz/
Pronunciation: tar sandz
French: sables bitumineux

Definition: Deposits of sand mixed with heavy oil, also called oil sands.

Example: Tar sands extraction has a high environmental impact.

oil sands

IPA: /ˈɔɪl sændz/
Pronunciation: oil sandz
French: sables bitumineux

Definition: Deposits of sand, clay and bitumen used to produce oil.

Example: Oil sands are carbon-intensive to exploit.

B. Extraction, transport, refining and combustion

fossil fuel extraction

IPA: /ˈfɒsəl ˌfjuːəl ɪkˈstrækʃən/
Pronunciation: FOS-uhl FYOO-uhl ik-STRAK-shuhn
French: extraction des combustibles fossiles

Definition: The process of removing coal, oil or gas from the ground.

Example: Fossil fuel extraction damages landscapes and ecosystems.

mining

IPA: /ˈmaɪnɪŋ/
Pronunciation: MY-ning
French: exploitation minière

Definition: Removing minerals, coal or other resources from the ground.

Example: Coal mining can be dangerous for workers.

coal mining

IPA: /ˈkəʊl ˌmaɪnɪŋ/
Pronunciation: kohl MY-ning
French: extraction du charbon

Definition: The extraction of coal from underground or surface mines.

Example: Coal mining shaped many industrial regions.

open-cast mining

IPA: /ˌəʊpən kɑːst ˈmaɪnɪŋ/
Pronunciation: OH-puhn-kast MY-ning
French: exploitation à ciel ouvert

Definition: Mining from a large open pit at the surface.

Example: Open-cast mining scars landscapes.

underground mining

IPA: /ˌʌndəɡraʊnd ˈmaɪnɪŋ/
Pronunciation: UN-duh-ground MY-ning
French: exploitation souterraine

Definition: Mining carried out below the surface.

Example: Underground mining creates serious safety risks.

drilling

IPA: /ˈdrɪlɪŋ/
Pronunciation: DRIL-ing
French: forage

Definition: Making wells to extract oil or gas.

Example: Offshore drilling is technically complex and risky.

oil rig

IPA: /ˈɔɪl rɪɡ/
Pronunciation: oil rig
French: plateforme pétrolière ; appareil de forage

Definition: A structure used to drill for oil or gas.

Example: An oil rig can operate offshore or on land.

offshore drilling

IPA: /ˌɒfʃɔː ˈdrɪlɪŋ/
Pronunciation: OFF-shor DRIL-ing
French: forage offshore

Definition: Drilling for oil or gas beneath the seabed.

Example: Offshore drilling can cause major oil spills.

fracking

IPA: /ˈfrækɪŋ/
Pronunciation: FRAK-ing
French: fracturation hydraulique

Definition: Hydraulic fracturing, a method of extracting oil or gas by cracking rock with pressurised fluid.

Example: Fracking is controversial because of water use, earthquakes and pollution.

hydraulic fracturing

IPA: /haɪˌdrɔːlɪk ˈfræktʃərɪŋ/
Pronunciation: hy-DRAW-lik FRAK-chuh-ring
French: fracturation hydraulique

Definition: The full term for fracking.

Example: Hydraulic fracturing opened new fossil fuel reserves.

pipeline

IPA: /ˈpaɪplaɪn/
Pronunciation: PYPE-line
French: oléoduc ; gazoduc

Definition: A long pipe used to transport oil or gas.

Example: Pipelines create strategic dependencies.

refinery

IPA: /rɪˈfaɪnəri/
Pronunciation: ri-FY-nuh-ree
French: raffinerie

Definition: An industrial facility that turns crude oil into usable products.

Example: Refineries produce petrol, diesel and jet fuel.

petrochemical industry

IPA: /ˌpetrəʊˈkemɪkəl ˈɪndəstri/
Pronunciation: PET-roh-KEM-i-kuhl IN-duh-stree
French: industrie pétrochimique

Definition: The industry producing chemicals from fossil fuels.

Example: The petrochemical industry links fossil fuels to consumer goods.

combustion

IPA: /kəmˈbʌstʃən/
Pronunciation: kuhm-BUS-chuhn
French: combustion

Definition: Burning fuel to release energy.

Example: Combustion of fossil fuels releases CO2.

coal-fired power plant

IPA: /ˈkəʊl faɪəd ˈpaʊə plɑːnt/
Pronunciation: kohl-fy-uhrd POW-uh plant
French: centrale à charbon

Definition: A power plant that burns coal to generate electricity.

Example: Coal-fired power plants are being closed in some countries.

gas-fired power plant

IPA: /ˈɡæs faɪəd ˈpaʊə plɑːnt/
Pronunciation: gas-fy-uhrd POW-uh plant
French: centrale à gaz

Definition: A power plant that burns natural gas.

Example: Gas-fired power plants can respond quickly to demand.

C. Climate, pollution and environmental damage

carbon emissions

IPA: /ˈkɑːbən ɪˌmɪʃənz/
Pronunciation: KAR-buhn i-MISH-uhnz
French: émissions de carbone

Definition: Emissions of carbon dioxide or carbon-based greenhouse gases.

Example: Carbon emissions must fall quickly.

greenhouse gas emissions

IPA: /ˈɡriːnhaʊs ɡæs ɪˌmɪʃənz/
Pronunciation: GREEN-house gas i-MISH-uhnz
French: émissions de gaz à effet de serre

Definition: Emissions of gases that trap heat in the atmosphere.

Example: Fossil fuels are the main source of greenhouse gas emissions.

carbon intensity

IPA: /ˈkɑːbən ɪnˌtensəti/
Pronunciation: KAR-buhn in-TEN-suh-tee
French: intensité carbone

Definition: The amount of carbon emitted per unit of energy or output.

Example: Coal has a high carbon intensity.

air pollution

IPA: /ˈeə pəˌluːʃən/
Pronunciation: air puh-LOO-shuhn
French: pollution de l’air

Definition: Harmful substances in the air.

Example: Air pollution from fossil fuels harms health.

particulate matter

IPA: /pɑːˌtɪkjʊlət ˈmætə/
Pronunciation: par-TIK-yuh-luht MAT-uh
French: particules fines

Definition: Tiny particles in the air that can damage lungs and hearts.

Example: Particulate matter is linked to premature deaths.

PM2.5

IPA: /ˌpiː em tuː pɔɪnt ˈfaɪv/
Pronunciation: pee-em two-point-five
French: PM2,5 ; particules fines

Definition: Fine particulate matter smaller than 2.5 micrometres.

Example: PM2.5 can penetrate deep into the lungs.

sulphur dioxide

IPA: /ˌsʌlfə daɪˈɒksaɪd/
Pronunciation: SUL-fuh dy-OK-syde
French: dioxyde de soufre

Definition: A pollutant produced by burning sulphur-containing fuels.

Example: Sulphur dioxide contributes to acid rain.

nitrogen oxides

IPA: /ˌnaɪtrədʒən ˈɒksaɪdz/
Pronunciation: NY-truh-juhn OK-sydz
French: oxydes d’azote

Definition: Air pollutants produced by combustion.

Example: Nitrogen oxides worsen urban air pollution.

acid rain

IPA: /ˌæsɪd ˈreɪn/
Pronunciation: ASS-id rayn
French: pluie acide

Definition: Rain made acidic by pollutants such as sulphur dioxide and nitrogen oxides.

Example: Acid rain damages forests, lakes and buildings.

oil spill

IPA: /ˈɔɪl spɪl/
Pronunciation: oil spill
French: marée noire ; déversement de pétrole

Definition: A release of oil into the environment, especially water.

Example: Oil spills can devastate marine ecosystems.

methane leak

IPA: /ˈmiːθeɪn liːk/
Pronunciation: MEE-thayn leek
French: fuite de méthane

Definition: An unintended release of methane into the atmosphere.

Example: Methane leaks undermine the climate case for gas.

flaring

IPA: /ˈfleərɪŋ/
Pronunciation: FLAIR-ing
French: torchage

Definition: Burning unwanted gas during oil or gas extraction.

Example: Flaring wastes energy and emits CO2.

venting

IPA: /ˈventɪŋ/
Pronunciation: VEN-ting
French: rejet direct ; dégazage

Definition: Releasing gas directly into the atmosphere.

Example: Venting methane is highly damaging for the climate.

environmental degradation

IPA: /ɪnˌvaɪrənˈmentəl ˌdeɡrəˈdeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: in-vy-ruhn-MEN-tuhl deg-ruh-DAY-shuhn
French: dégradation environnementale

Definition: Damage to ecosystems, land, water or air.

Example: Fossil fuel extraction causes environmental degradation.

water contamination

IPA: /ˈwɔːtə kənˌtæmɪˈneɪʃən/
Pronunciation: WOR-tuh kuhn-tam-i-NAY-shuhn
French: contamination de l’eau

Definition: Pollution of water by harmful substances.

Example: Fracking can raise concerns about water contamination.

D. Energy security, geopolitics and fossil fuel economics

fossil fuel dependency

IPA: /ˈfɒsəl ˌfjuːəl dɪˌpendənsi/
Pronunciation: FOS-uhl FYOO-uhl di-PEN-duhn-see
French: dépendance aux énergies fossiles

Definition: Reliance on coal, oil and gas for energy or revenue.

Example: Fossil fuel dependency slows the energy transition.

energy security

IPA: /ˈenədʒi sɪˌkjʊərəti/
Pronunciation: EN-uh-jee si-KYOOR-uh-tee
French: sécurité énergétique

Definition: Reliable and affordable access to energy.

Example: Energy security became urgent after gas supply shocks.

energy crisis

IPA: /ˈenədʒi ˌkraɪsɪs/
Pronunciation: EN-uh-jee KRY-sis
French: crise énergétique

Definition: A severe disruption in energy supply, affordability or stability.

Example: An energy crisis can trigger inflation and social unrest.

price volatility

IPA: /ˈpraɪs ˌvɒləˈtɪləti/
Pronunciation: pryce vol-uh-TIL-uh-tee
French: volatilité des prix

Definition: Frequent and unpredictable changes in prices.

Example: Oil and gas markets are prone to price volatility.

oil shock

IPA: /ˈɔɪl ʃɒk/
Pronunciation: oil shok
French: choc pétrolier

Definition: A sudden disruption or surge in oil prices.

Example: Oil shocks can damage economic growth.

global energy demand

IPA: /ˌɡləʊbəl ˈenədʒi dɪˌmɑːnd/
Pronunciation: GLOH-buhl EN-uh-jee di-MAHND
French: demande énergétique mondiale

Definition: Worldwide demand for energy.

Example: Global energy demand continues to rise.

energy-intensive industry

IPA: /ˈenədʒi ɪnˌtensɪv ˈɪndəstri/
Pronunciation: EN-uh-jee in-TEN-siv IN-duh-stree
French: industrie énergivore

Definition: An industry requiring large amounts of energy.

Example: Steel and cement are energy-intensive industries.

hard-to-abate sector

IPA: /ˌhɑːd tuː əˈbeɪt ˈsektə/
Pronunciation: hard-to-uh-BAYT SEK-tuh
French: secteur difficile à décarboner

Definition: A sector where emissions are difficult to reduce.

Example: Aviation and cement are hard-to-abate sectors.

petrostate

IPA: /ˈpetrəʊsteɪt/
Pronunciation: PET-roh-state
French: État pétrolier

Definition: A state whose economy and politics depend heavily on oil or gas revenues.

Example: Petrostates face transition risks.

resource curse

IPA: /rɪˈsɔːs kɜːs/
Pronunciation: ri-SORSS curse
French: malédiction des ressources

Definition: The paradox that resource-rich countries may suffer corruption, conflict or weak development.

Example: Oil wealth can create a resource curse.

rentier state

IPA: /ˈrentieɪ steɪt/
Pronunciation: REN-tee-ay state
French: État rentier

Definition: A state relying on external rents such as oil revenues rather than broad taxation.

Example: Rentier states may be less accountable to citizens.

OPEC

IPA: /ˈəʊpek/
Pronunciation: OH-pek
French: OPEP

Definition: Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries.

Example: OPEC influences global oil supply and prices.

energy geopolitics

IPA: /ˈenədʒi ˌdʒiːəʊˈpɒlətɪks/
Pronunciation: EN-uh-jee jee-oh-POL-uh-tiks
French: géopolitique de l’énergie

Definition: The geopolitical power relations linked to energy resources and supply.

Example: Fossil fuels have long shaped energy geopolitics.

pipeline politics

IPA: /ˈpaɪplaɪn ˌpɒlətɪks/
Pronunciation: PYPE-line POL-uh-tiks
French: politique des pipelines

Definition: Political conflicts and power relations linked to pipelines.

Example: Pipeline politics can divide allies.

energy weapon

IPA: /ˈenədʒi ˌwepən/
Pronunciation: EN-uh-jee WEP-uhn
French: arme énergétique

Definition: Energy supplies used as political pressure.

Example: Gas exports can become an energy weapon.

supply disruption

IPA: /səˈplaɪ dɪsˌrʌpʃən/
Pronunciation: suh-PLY dis-RUP-shuhn
French: rupture d’approvisionnement

Definition: An interruption in the availability of goods or energy.

Example: Supply disruption can push energy prices up.

fossil fuel subsidy

IPA: /ˈfɒsəl ˌfjuːəl ˈsʌbsɪdi/
Pronunciation: FOS-uhl FYOO-uhl SUB-si-dee
French: subvention aux énergies fossiles

Definition: Government support that lowers fossil fuel production or consumption costs.

Example: Fossil fuel subsidies make clean energy less competitive.

carbon lock-in

IPA: /ˈkɑːbən ˌlɒk ɪn/
Pronunciation: KAR-buhn LOK-in
French: verrouillage carbone

Definition: Dependence on high-carbon infrastructure that is difficult to change.

Example: New gas infrastructure can create carbon lock-in.

stranded asset

IPA: /ˌstrændɪd ˈæset/
Pronunciation: STRAN-did AS-et
French: actif échoué

Definition: An asset that loses value because of policy, market or environmental change.

Example: Coal plants may become stranded assets.

transition risk

IPA: /trænˈzɪʃən rɪsk/
Pronunciation: tran-ZISH-uhn risk
French: risque de transition

Definition: Risk created by the shift to a low-carbon economy.

Example: Fossil fuel firms face transition risk.

divestment

IPA: /daɪˈvestmənt/
Pronunciation: dy-VEST-muhnt
French: désinvestissement

Definition: Selling investments for ethical, political or financial reasons.

Example: Universities have faced pressure for fossil fuel divestment.

fossil fuel divestment

IPA: /ˈfɒsəl ˌfjuːəl daɪˈvestmənt/
Pronunciation: FOS-uhl FYOO-uhl dy-VEST-muhnt
French: désinvestissement des énergies fossiles

Definition: Selling investments in fossil fuel companies.

Example: Fossil fuel divestment is a climate activism strategy.

polluter pays principle

IPA: /pəˈluːtə peɪz ˈprɪnsəpəl/
Pronunciation: puh-LOO-tuh payz PRIN-suh-puhl
French: principe pollueur-payeur

Definition: The principle that those causing pollution should pay for the damage.

Example: The polluter pays principle supports climate accountability.

climate liability

IPA: /ˈklaɪmət ˌlaɪəˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: KLY-muht ly-uh-BIL-uh-tee
French: responsabilité climatique

Definition: Legal responsibility for climate-related harm.

Example: Climate liability could reshape corporate behaviour.

greenwashing

IPA: /ˈɡriːnˌwɒʃɪŋ/
Pronunciation: GREEN-wosh-ing
French: greenwashing ; écoblanchiment

Definition: Misleading environmental claims made to improve reputation.

Example: Oil companies are often accused of greenwashing.

carbon capture and storage

IPA: /ˌkɑːbən ˈkæptʃər ənd ˈstɔːrɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: KAR-buhn KAP-chuh and STOR-ij
French: captage et stockage du carbone

Definition: Capturing CO2 and storing it underground or elsewhere.

Example: Carbon capture and storage is promoted by some fossil fuel companies.

CCS

IPA: /ˌsiː siː ˈes/
Pronunciation: see-see-ess
French: CSC

Definition: Abbreviation for carbon capture and storage.

Example: CCS may help some industries but cannot justify unlimited fossil fuel use.

blue hydrogen

IPA: /ˌbluː ˈhaɪdrədʒən/
Pronunciation: blue HY-druh-juhn
French: hydrogène bleu

Definition: Hydrogen produced from natural gas with carbon capture.

Example: Blue hydrogen depends on gas and effective carbon capture.

French: diversifier le mix énergétique

Example: Countries diversify the energy mix for security and climate reasons.

to create stranded assets

IPA: /kriˈeɪt ˌstrændɪd ˈæsets/
Pronunciation: kree-AYT STRAN-did AS-ets
French: créer des actifs échoués

Definition: To make assets lose value because of the transition or regulation.

Example: Strict climate policy may create stranded assets.

to hold polluters accountable

IPA: /həʊld pəˈluːtəz əˈkaʊntəbəl/
Pronunciation: hohld puh-LOO-tuhz uh-KOWN-tuh-buhl
French: tenir les pollueurs responsables

Definition: To make polluters responsible for environmental harm.

Example: Climate litigation seeks to hold polluters accountable.

to invest in clean energy

IPA: /ɪnˈvest ɪn ˌkliːn ˈenədʒi/
Pronunciation: in-VEST in kleen EN-uh-jee
French: investir dans l’énergie propre

Definition: To put money into low-pollution energy systems.

Example: Oil companies claim to invest in clean energy.

to manage the transition

IPA: /ˈmænɪdʒ ðə trænˈzɪʃən/
Pronunciation: MAN-ij thuh tran-ZISH-uhn
French: gérer la transition

Definition: To organise the shift to a new energy system.

Example: Governments must manage the transition fairly.

to balance energy security and climate goals

IPA: /ˈbæləns ˈenədʒi sɪˌkjʊərəti ənd ˈklaɪmət ɡəʊlz/
Pronunciation: BAL-uhns EN-uh-jee si-KYOOR-uh-tee and KLY-muht gohlz
French: équilibrer sécurité énergétique et objectifs climatiques

Definition: To protect energy supply while reducing emissions.

Example: Policymakers must balance energy security and climate goals.
