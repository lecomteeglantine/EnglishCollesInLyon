Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse taxation: tax revenue, income tax, VAT, corporation tax, wealth tax, progressivity, fairness, redistribution, tax evasion, tax avoidance, tax havens, corporate profit shifting, environmental taxes and the tension between funding public services and preserving incentives.

A. Core taxation, revenue and tax-base vocabulary

taxation

IPA: /tækˈseɪʃən/
Pronunciation: tak-SAY-shuhn
French: fiscalité ; imposition

Definition: The system or process by which a government collects money from people and organisations.

Example: Taxation funds public services, welfare and infrastructure.

tax

IPA: /tæks/
Pronunciation: taks
French: impôt ; taxe

Definition: Money that individuals or businesses must pay to the government.

Example: Taxes are used to finance public spending.

tax system

IPA: /ˈtæks ˌsɪstəm/
Pronunciation: TAKS SIS-tuhm
French: système fiscal

Definition: The set of rules and institutions used to collect taxes.

Example: A fair tax system should be efficient and progressive.

tax policy

IPA: /ˈtæks ˌpɒləsi/
Pronunciation: TAKS POL-uh-see
French: politique fiscale

Definition: Government policy about which taxes to collect and how.

Example: Tax policy can reduce inequality or encourage investment.

fiscal policy

IPA: /ˈfɪskəl ˈpɒləsi/
Pronunciation: FIS-kuhl POL-uh-see
French: politique budgétaire

Definition: Government policy on taxation, spending and borrowing.

Example: Taxation is a key part of fiscal policy.

public revenue

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk ˈrevənjuː/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik REV-uhn-yoo
French: recettes publiques

Definition: Money received by the government, mainly through taxes.

Example: Public revenue must cover essential services.

tax revenue

IPA: /ˈtæks ˌrevənjuː/
Pronunciation: TAKS REV-uhn-yoo
French: recettes fiscales

Definition: Money collected by the government through taxes.

Example: Tax revenue rises when employment and profits increase.

government revenue

IPA: /ˈɡʌvənmənt ˈrevənjuː/
Pronunciation: GUV-uhn-muhnt REV-uhn-yoo
French: recettes de l’État

Definition: All income received by the state.

Example: Government revenue fell during the recession.

tax base

IPA: /ˈtæks beɪs/
Pronunciation: TAKS bays
French: assiette fiscale

Definition: The income, wealth, property or activity that can be taxed.

Example: A broad tax base can finance public services with lower rates.

tax rate

IPA: /ˈtæks reɪt/
Pronunciation: TAKS rayt
French: taux d’imposition

Definition: The percentage or amount at which something is taxed.

Example: High tax rates can be politically controversial.

tax bracket

IPA: /ˈtæks ˌbrækɪt/
Pronunciation: TAKS BRAK-it
French: tranche d’imposition

Definition: A range of income taxed at a particular rate.

Example: Higher earners may fall into a higher tax bracket.

tax threshold

IPA: /ˈtæks ˌθreʃhəʊld/
Pronunciation: TAKS THRESH-hohld
French: seuil d’imposition

Definition: The income level at which a tax begins to apply.

Example: Raising the tax threshold can help low-income workers.

tax allowance

IPA: /ˈtæks əˌlaʊəns/
Pronunciation: TAKS uh-LOW-uhns
French: abattement fiscal ; déduction

Definition: An amount of income that is not taxed.

Example: A tax allowance reduces taxable income.

tax deduction

IPA: /ˈtæks dɪˌdʌkʃən/
Pronunciation: TAKS di-DUK-shuhn
French: déduction fiscale

Definition: An amount subtracted from taxable income.

Example: Tax deductions can encourage donations or investment.

tax credit

IPA: /ˈtæks ˌkredɪt/
Pronunciation: TAKS KRED-it
French: crédit d’impôt

Definition: An amount subtracted from tax owed, or a payment to eligible households.

Example: Tax credits can support low-income families.

tax relief

IPA: /ˈtæks rɪˌliːf/
Pronunciation: TAKS ri-LEEF
French: allègement fiscal

Definition: A reduction in tax owed.

Example: Tax relief can encourage saving or business investment.

tax exemption

IPA: /ˈtæks ɪɡˌzempʃən/
Pronunciation: TAKS ig-ZEMP-shuhn
French: exonération fiscale

Definition: A situation where someone or something is not subject to a tax.

Example: Charities often receive tax exemptions.

taxable income

IPA: /ˌtæksəbəl ˈɪnkʌm/
Pronunciation: TAK-suh-buhl IN-kum
French: revenu imposable

Definition: Income on which tax must be paid.

Example: Taxable income is calculated after allowances and deductions.

gross income

IPA: /ˌɡrəʊs ˈɪnkʌm/
Pronunciation: grohss IN-kum
French: revenu brut

Definition: Income before taxes and deductions.

Example: Gross income is higher than take-home pay.

net income

IPA: /ˌnet ˈɪnkʌm/
Pronunciation: net IN-kum
French: revenu net

Definition: Income after taxes and deductions.

Example: Net income determines actual purchasing power.

take-home pay

IPA: /ˌteɪk həʊm ˈpeɪ/
Pronunciation: take-home pay
French: salaire net

Definition: Money received by a worker after tax and deductions.

Example: Tax rises reduce take-home pay.

direct tax

IPA: /dəˌrekt ˈtæks/
Pronunciation: di-REKT taks
French: impôt direct

Definition: A tax paid directly by individuals or organisations to the government.

Example: Income tax is a direct tax.

indirect tax

IPA: /ˌɪndəˈrekt ˈtæks/
Pronunciation: in-di-REKT taks
French: impôt indirect

Definition: A tax paid through the purchase of goods or services.

Example: VAT is an indirect tax.

B. Main types of taxes

income tax

IPA: /ˈɪnkʌm tæks/
Pronunciation: IN-kum taks
French: impôt sur le revenu

Definition: A tax on personal income.

Example: Income tax is often progressive.

personal income tax

IPA: /ˈpɜːsənəl ˈɪnkʌm tæks/
Pronunciation: PUR-suh-nuhl IN-kum taks
French: impôt sur le revenu des personnes physiques

Definition: A tax paid by individuals on their income.

Example: Personal income tax is a major source of public revenue.

corporation tax

IPA: /ˌkɔːpəˈreɪʃən tæks/
Pronunciation: kor-puh-RAY-shuhn taks
French: impôt sur les sociétés

Definition: A tax on company profits.

Example: Corporation tax affects business investment decisions.

corporate tax

IPA: /ˈkɔːpərət tæks/
Pronunciation: KOR-puh-ruht taks
French: impôt sur les sociétés

Definition: Tax paid by companies on profits.

Example: Corporate tax competition can reduce public revenue.

capital gains tax

IPA: /ˌkæpɪtəl ˈɡeɪnz tæks/
Pronunciation: KAP-i-tuhl gaynz taks
French: impôt sur les plus-values

Definition: A tax on profit made from selling assets.

Example: Capital gains tax affects investors and homeowners.

wealth tax

IPA: /ˈwelθ tæks/
Pronunciation: welth taks
French: impôt sur la fortune

Definition: A tax on a person’s total assets or net wealth.

Example: A wealth tax is often proposed to reduce inequality.

property tax

IPA: /ˈprɒpəti tæks/
Pronunciation: PROP-uh-tee taks
French: taxe foncière ; impôt immobilier

Definition: A tax on land or buildings.

Example: Property tax can fund local government.

inheritance tax

IPA: /ɪnˈherɪtəns tæks/
Pronunciation: in-HERR-i-tuhns taks
French: droits de succession

Definition: A tax on money or property inherited after someone dies.

Example: Inheritance tax is controversial because it affects family wealth.

estate tax

IPA: /ɪˈsteɪt tæks/
Pronunciation: i-STATE taks
French: impôt successoral

Definition: US term for a tax on a deceased person’s estate.

Example: The estate tax is often called a tax on inherited wealth.

value-added tax

IPA: /ˌvæljuː ˈædɪd tæks/
Pronunciation: VAL-yoo AD-id taks
French: TVA

Definition: A consumption tax added at each stage of production and sale.

Example: Value-added tax is paid by consumers through prices.

VAT

IPA: /ˌviː eɪ ˈtiː/
Pronunciation: vee-ay-tee
French: TVA

Definition: Abbreviation for value-added tax.

Example: VAT is usually regressive unless essentials are exempt.

sales tax

IPA: /ˈseɪlz tæks/
Pronunciation: sales taks
French: taxe sur les ventes

Definition: A tax added to the sale of goods and services.

Example: Sales tax is common in the United States.

excise duty

IPA: /ˈeksaɪz ˌdjuːti/
Pronunciation: EK-syze DYOO-tee
French: droit d’accise

Definition: A tax on specific goods such as alcohol, tobacco or fuel.

Example: Excise duty can discourage harmful consumption.

fuel duty

IPA: /ˈfjuːəl ˌdjuːti/
Pronunciation: FYOO-uhl DYOO-tee
French: taxe sur les carburants

Definition: A tax on petrol, diesel or other fuels.

Example: Fuel duty is politically sensitive in rural areas.

carbon tax

IPA: /ˈkɑːbən tæks/
Pronunciation: KAR-buhn taks
French: taxe carbone

Definition: A tax on carbon emissions or fossil fuels.

Example: A carbon tax aims to make polluters pay.

green tax

IPA: /ˈɡriːn tæks/
Pronunciation: green taks
French: taxe écologique

Definition: A tax designed to discourage environmentally harmful behaviour.

Example: Green taxes can support the transition to a low-carbon economy.

sin tax

IPA: /ˈsɪn tæks/
Pronunciation: sin taks
French: taxe comportementale ; taxe sur les produits nocifs

Definition: A tax on goods considered harmful, such as tobacco or alcohol.

Example: Sin taxes can raise revenue and improve public health.

sugar tax

IPA: /ˈʃʊɡə tæks/
Pronunciation: SHUUG-uh taks
French: taxe sur le sucre

Definition: A tax on sugary drinks or products.

Example: A sugar tax can reduce consumption of unhealthy drinks.

tariff

IPA: /ˈtærɪf/
Pronunciation: TAR-if
French: droit de douane

Definition: A tax on imported goods.

Example: Tariffs protect domestic industries but raise consumer prices.

customs duty

IPA: /ˈkʌstəmz ˌdjuːti/
Pronunciation: KUS-tuhmz DYOO-tee
French: droit de douane

Definition: A tax paid on goods imported into a country.

Example: Customs duties affect international trade.

payroll tax

IPA: /ˈpeɪrəʊl tæks/
Pronunciation: PAY-rohl taks
French: cotisation sociale ; taxe sur les salaires

Definition: A tax linked to wages, often used to finance social security.

Example: Payroll taxes can make hiring more expensive.

social security contributions

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl sɪˈkjʊərəti ˌkɒntrɪˈbjuːʃənz/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl si-KYOOR-uh-tee kon-tri-BYOO-shuhnz
French: cotisations sociales

Definition: Payments by workers and employers to finance social protection.

Example: Social security contributions fund pensions and healthcare.

national insurance

IPA: /ˌnæʃənəl ɪnˈʃʊərəns/
Pronunciation: NASH-uh-nuhl in-SHOOR-uhns
French: cotisations sociales britanniques

Definition: UK system of contributions financing benefits, pensions and healthcare.

Example: National Insurance affects workers and employers.

local tax

IPA: /ˌləʊkəl ˈtæks/
Pronunciation: LOH-kuhl taks
French: impôt local

Definition: A tax collected by local government.

Example: Local taxes finance schools, roads and local services.

council tax

IPA: /ˈkaʊnsəl tæks/
Pronunciation: KOWN-suhl taks
French: taxe d’habitation britannique

Definition: A UK local tax on residential property.

Example: Council tax is often criticised as unfair.

progressive tax

IPA: /prəˌɡresɪv ˈtæks/
Pronunciation: pruh-GRES-iv taks
French: impôt progressif

Definition: A tax where richer people pay a higher share of income.

Example: Progressive taxation can reduce inequality.

regressive tax

IPA: /rɪˌɡresɪv ˈtæks/
Pronunciation: ri-GRES-iv taks
French: impôt régressif

Definition: A tax that takes a larger share of income from poorer people.

Example: VAT can be regressive because poorer households spend more of their income.

proportional tax

IPA: /prəˌpɔːʃənəl ˈtæks/
Pronunciation: pruh-POR-shuh-nuhl taks
French: impôt proportionnel

Definition: A tax taking the same percentage from everyone.

Example: A flat tax is a type of proportional tax.

flat tax

IPA: /ˌflæt ˈtæks/
Pronunciation: flat taks
French: impôt à taux unique

Definition: A single tax rate applied to all taxpayers or income levels.

Example: Flat tax supporters claim it is simple.

C. Progressivity, fairness and redistribution

marginal tax rate

IPA: /ˌmɑːdʒɪnəl ˈtæks reɪt/
Pronunciation: MAR-ji-nuhl taks rayt
French: taux marginal d’imposition

Definition: The tax rate paid on the next unit of income.

Example: High marginal tax rates may affect incentives.

average tax rate

IPA: /ˌævərɪdʒ ˈtæks reɪt/
Pronunciation: AV-uh-rij taks rayt
French: taux moyen d’imposition

Definition: Total tax paid divided by total income.

Example: The average tax rate is usually lower than the top marginal rate.

effective tax rate

IPA: /ɪˌfektɪv ˈtæks reɪt/
Pronunciation: i-FEK-tiv taks rayt
French: taux effectif d’imposition

Definition: The actual rate paid after deductions, exemptions and credits.

Example: Some corporations pay a low effective tax rate.

top tax rate

IPA: /ˌtɒp ˈtæks reɪt/
Pronunciation: top taks rayt
French: taux marginal supérieur

Definition: The highest marginal tax rate in a tax system.

Example: Raising the top tax rate is often controversial.

tax progressivity

IPA: /ˈtæks prəʊˌɡresɪvəti/
Pronunciation: TAKS proh-gres-IV-uh-tee
French: progressivité de l’impôt

Definition: The degree to which taxes rise with income or wealth.

Example: Tax progressivity can reduce income inequality.

tax burden

IPA: /ˈtæks ˌbɜːdən/
Pronunciation: TAKS BUR-duhn
French: charge fiscale

Definition: The amount of tax paid by a person, group or economy.

Example: The tax burden is often debated during elections.

tax incidence

IPA: /ˈtæks ˌɪnsɪdəns/
Pronunciation: TAKS IN-si-duhns
French: incidence fiscale

Definition: Who ultimately bears the cost of a tax.

Example: Tax incidence can differ from who legally pays the tax.

redistribution

IPA: /ˌriːdɪstrɪˈbjuːʃən/
Pronunciation: ree-dis-tri-BYOO-shuhn
French: redistribution

Definition: The transfer of income or wealth through taxes and public spending.

Example: Redistribution reduces inequality when taxes fund public services.

income redistribution

IPA: /ˈɪnkʌm ˌriːdɪstrɪˈbjuːʃən/
Pronunciation: IN-kum ree-dis-tri-BYOO-shuhn
French: redistribution des revenus

Definition: The redistribution of income between groups.

Example: Income redistribution is a central goal of progressive taxation.

wealth redistribution

IPA: /ˈwelθ ˌriːdɪstrɪˈbjuːʃən/
Pronunciation: welth ree-dis-tri-BYOO-shuhn
French: redistribution des richesses

Definition: The redistribution of assets or wealth between groups.

Example: Wealth redistribution is politically sensitive.

inequality

IPA: /ˌɪnɪˈkwɒləti/
Pronunciation: in-i-KWOL-uh-tee
French: inégalité

Definition: Unequal distribution of income, wealth or opportunities.

Example: Taxation can either reduce or worsen inequality.

income inequality

IPA: /ˈɪnkʌm ˌɪnɪˈkwɒləti/
Pronunciation: IN-kum in-i-KWOL-uh-tee
French: inégalité des revenus

Definition: Unequal distribution of income.

Example: Progressive income tax can reduce income inequality.

wealth inequality

IPA: /ˈwelθ ˌɪnɪˈkwɒləti/
Pronunciation: welth in-i-KWOL-uh-tee
French: inégalité patrimoniale

Definition: Unequal distribution of assets and wealth.

Example: Inheritance tax targets wealth inequality.

tax fairness

IPA: /ˈtæks ˌfeənəs/
Pronunciation: TAKS FAIR-nuhs
French: justice fiscale

Definition: The idea that taxes should be distributed justly.

Example: Tax fairness is central to debates about the wealthy.

fiscal justice

IPA: /ˈfɪskəl ˌdʒʌstɪs/
Pronunciation: FIS-kuhl JUS-tis
French: justice fiscale

Definition: Fairness in taxation and public finance.

Example: Campaigners demand fiscal justice from multinationals.

ability to pay

IPA: /əˌbɪləti tə ˈpeɪ/
Pronunciation: uh-BIL-uh-tee tuh pay
French: capacité contributive

Definition: The principle that people with more resources should pay more tax.

Example: Progressive taxation is based on ability to pay.

benefit principle

IPA: /ˈbenɪfɪt ˌprɪnsəpəl/
Pronunciation: BEN-i-fit PRIN-suh-puhl
French: principe du bénéfice reçu

Definition: The idea that people should pay taxes according to the benefits they receive.

Example: Road tolls reflect the benefit principle.

tax morale

IPA: /ˈtæks məˌrɑːl/
Pronunciation: TAKS muh-RAHL
French: civisme fiscal

Definition: Citizens’ willingness to pay taxes voluntarily.

Example: Tax morale declines when people see corruption or unfairness.

tax compliance

IPA: /ˈtæks kəmˌplaɪəns/
Pronunciation: TAKS kuhm-PLY-uhns
French: conformité fiscale

Definition: The extent to which taxpayers obey tax rules.

Example: Tax compliance improves when rules are simple and trusted.

taxpayer

IPA: /ˈtæksˌpeɪə/
Pronunciation: TAKS-pay-uh
French: contribuable

Definition: A person or organisation that pays taxes.

Example: Taxpayers expect public money to be used responsibly.

tax return

IPA: /ˈtæks rɪˌtɜːn/
Pronunciation: TAKS ri-TURN
French: déclaration d’impôts

Definition: A form reporting income and tax owed.

Example: Taxpayers file a tax return each year.

tax audit

IPA: /ˈtæks ˌɔːdɪt/
Pronunciation: TAKS AW-dit
French: contrôle fiscal

Definition: An official examination of someone’s tax records.

Example: A tax audit can reveal fraud or errors.

tax collection

IPA: /ˈtæks kəˌlekʃən/
Pronunciation: TAKS kuh-LEK-shuhn
French: recouvrement de l’impôt

Definition: The process of collecting taxes.

Example: Efficient tax collection strengthens public finances.

tax administration

IPA: /ˈtæks ədˌmɪnɪˈstreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: TAKS ad-min-i-STRAY-shuhn
French: administration fiscale

Definition: The institutions and procedures used to manage taxation.

Example: Good tax administration reduces evasion.

D. Tax compliance, evasion, avoidance and havens

tax authority

IPA: /ˈtæks ɔːˌθɒrəti/
Pronunciation: TAKS aw-THOR-uh-tee
French: administration fiscale

Definition: A public body responsible for collecting and enforcing taxes.

Example: Tax authorities investigate suspicious declarations.

HMRC

IPA: /ˌeɪtʃ em ɑː ˈsiː/
Pronunciation: aych-em-ar-see
French: administration fiscale britannique

Definition: HM Revenue and Customs, the UK tax authority.

Example: HMRC collects taxes in the UK.

IRS

IPA: /ˌaɪ ɑːr ˈes/
Pronunciation: eye-ar-ess
French: administration fiscale américaine

Definition: Internal Revenue Service, the US tax authority.

Example: The IRS processes federal tax returns.

tax enforcement

IPA: /ˈtæks ɪnˌfɔːsmənt/
Pronunciation: TAKS in-FORSS-muhnt
French: application de la loi fiscale

Definition: Action to ensure taxes are paid correctly.

Example: Tax enforcement deters evasion.

tax evasion

IPA: /ˈtæks ɪˌveɪʒən/
Pronunciation: TAKS i-VAY-zhuhn
French: fraude fiscale

Definition: Illegal non-payment or underpayment of taxes.

Example: Tax evasion deprives the state of revenue.

tax avoidance

IPA: /ˈtæks əˌvɔɪdəns/
Pronunciation: TAKS uh-VOY-duhns
French: optimisation fiscale

Definition: Legal but often controversial reduction of tax liabilities.

Example: Tax avoidance by large companies causes public anger.

tax fraud

IPA: /ˈtæks frɔːd/
Pronunciation: TAKS fraud
French: fraude fiscale

Definition: Deliberate deception to pay less tax illegally.

Example: Tax fraud can lead to fines or prison.

tax loophole

IPA: /ˈtæks ˌluːphəʊl/
Pronunciation: TAKS LOOP-hohl
French: niche fiscale ; faille fiscale

Definition: A gap in tax law that allows people to reduce tax.

Example: Wealthy taxpayers often exploit tax loopholes.

loophole

IPA: /ˈluːphəʊl/
Pronunciation: LOOP-hohl
French: faille juridique

Definition: An ambiguity or gap in rules that can be exploited.

Example: Closing loopholes can raise revenue.

tax shelter

IPA: /ˈtæks ˌʃeltə/
Pronunciation: TAKS SHEL-tuh
French: abri fiscal

Definition: A legal arrangement used to reduce tax owed.

Example: Some tax shelters are legal but controversial.

tax haven

IPA: /ˈtæks ˌheɪvən/
Pronunciation: TAKS HAY-vuhn
French: paradis fiscal

Definition: A country or territory with low taxes and secrecy.

Example: Tax havens help individuals and firms hide wealth.

offshore account

IPA: /ˌɒfʃɔːr əˈkaʊnt/
Pronunciation: OFF-shor uh-KOWNT
French: compte offshore

Definition: A bank account held in another country, often for tax reasons.

Example: Offshore accounts can be used for legal or illegal purposes.

offshore finance

IPA: /ˌɒfʃɔː ˈfaɪnæns/
Pronunciation: OFF-shor FY-nans
French: finance offshore

Definition: Financial activity located in low-tax jurisdictions.

Example: Offshore finance reduces transparency.

shell company

IPA: /ˈʃel ˌkʌmpəni/
Pronunciation: shel KUM-puh-nee
French: société écran

Definition: A company with no real operations, often used to hold assets or hide ownership.

Example: Shell companies can conceal tax evasion.

beneficial owner

IPA: /ˌbenɪfɪʃəl ˈəʊnə/
Pronunciation: ben-i-FISH-uhl OH-nuh
French: bénéficiaire effectif

Definition: The real person who ultimately owns or controls an asset or company.

Example: Transparency rules aim to identify beneficial owners.

profit shifting

IPA: /ˈprɒfɪt ˌʃɪftɪŋ/
Pronunciation: PROF-it SHIFT-ing
French: transfert de bénéfices

Definition: Moving profits to low-tax jurisdictions to reduce taxes.

Example: Profit shifting reduces corporate tax revenue.

base erosion

IPA: /ˈbeɪs ɪˌrəʊʒən/
Pronunciation: bays i-ROH-zhuhn
French: érosion de la base fiscale

Definition: The shrinking of a tax base through avoidance or deductions.

Example: Base erosion is a major concern in international taxation.

BEPS

IPA: /beps/
Pronunciation: beps
French: BEPS ; érosion de la base d’imposition et transfert de bénéfices

Definition: Base Erosion and Profit Shifting, a term used in international tax reform.

Example: BEPS reforms aim to limit corporate tax avoidance.

transfer pricing

IPA: /ˈtrænsfɜː ˌpraɪsɪŋ/
Pronunciation: TRANS-fur PRY-sing
French: prix de transfert

Definition: Pricing of transactions between related companies in different countries.

Example: Transfer pricing can be used to shift profits.

tax competition

IPA: /ˈtæks ˌkɒmpəˈtɪʃən/
Pronunciation: TAKS kom-puh-TISH-uhn
French: concurrence fiscale

Definition: Competition between countries to attract business through lower taxes.

Example: Tax competition can create a race to the bottom.

race to the bottom

IPA: /ˌreɪs tə ðə ˈbɒtəm/
Pronunciation: race tuh thuh BOT-uhm
French: course vers le bas

Definition: Competition that lowers taxes, wages or standards.

Example: Corporate tax cuts can create a race to the bottom.

global minimum tax

IPA: /ˌɡləʊbəl ˈmɪnɪməm tæks/
Pronunciation: GLOH-buhl MIN-i-muhm taks
French: impôt minimum mondial

Definition: An international minimum tax rate on multinational profits.

Example: A global minimum tax aims to curb tax competition.
