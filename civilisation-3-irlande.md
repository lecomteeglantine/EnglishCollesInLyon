## 1. ESSENTIAL FACTS
- **Official name:** Ireland (*Éire*); often called the **Republic of Ireland** to distinguish it from Northern Ireland.
- **Capital:** Dublin. **Population:** ~5.3 million (Republic only).
- **Currency:** the **euro** (Ireland joined the eurozone — unlike the UK).
- **System:** a parliamentary republic.
- **Head of state:** the **President** (*Uachtarán*), a largely **ceremonial** role — office-holder as of early 2026 **to be checked** (Michael D. Higgins's second term ran until late 2025).
- **Head of government:** the **Taoiseach** (Prime Minister) — **Micheál Martin** as of early 2026, to be checked.
- **Languages:** **Irish** (*Gaeilge*) and **English**, both official; Irish is constitutionally the *first* official language, but English is dominant in everyday life.
- **International standing:** member of the **European Union** since 1973; uses the euro; **militarily neutral** (not a member of NATO); not part of the UK or the Commonwealth.

> ⚠️ **Key distinction:** **the Republic of Ireland** (independent, in the EU) ≠ **Northern Ireland** (part of the **UK**). Confusing the two is the classic mistake.

---

## 2. GEOGRAPHY AND THE TWO PARTS OF THE ISLAND
- An island in the North Atlantic, west of Great Britain, famous for its green landscapes (*"the Emerald Isle"*).
- Historically divided into **four provinces**: Leinster, Munster, Connacht and **Ulster**.
- **A partitioned island:** the **Republic** (26 counties, independent) and **Northern Ireland** (6 counties of Ulster, part of the UK).
- Mostly rural and agricultural, but heavily **urbanised around Dublin**, where much of the population and economy is concentrated.

> 💡 **Colle angle:** the **border** between the Republic and Northern Ireland — almost invisible for years — became a central issue again with **Brexit**.

---

## 3. THE POLITICAL SYSTEM AND THE CONSTITUTION (1937)
- A **parliamentary republic** governed by a **written constitution**, *Bunreacht na hÉireann* (1937), which replaced the Free State constitution.
- The Constitution originally reflected a **Catholic and nationalist** vision (a "special position" of the Catholic Church, a ban on divorce, a claim over Northern Ireland).
- It has since been **deeply transformed by referendums** (divorce in 1995, same-sex marriage in 2015, abortion in 2018), reflecting a profound social change.
- **Any constitutional change requires a referendum** — which has made the Irish people direct authors of their country's modernisation.

> 💡 **Colle angle:** Ireland is a striking case of a country that **modernised its fundamental law through popular votes**, turning referendums into milestones of social change.

---

## 4. THE INSTITUTIONS

### 4.1 The President (*Uachtarán*)
- **Head of state**, elected for seven years, with a mainly **ceremonial and symbolic** role (a guardian of the Constitution).
- Real political power lies with the government, not the President.

### 4.2 The Taoiseach and the government
- The **Taoiseach** (pronounced *"TEE-shuck"*) is the **Prime Minister** and head of government; the **Tánaiste** is the deputy.
- The government is accountable to the lower house.

### 4.3 The Oireachtas (Parliament)
- The **Oireachtas** is the national parliament, made up of the President and **two houses**:
  - **Dáil Éireann** (the *Dáil*, pronounced *"DOIL"*): the **lower house**, directly elected, the centre of power; its members are **TDs** (*Teachtaí Dála*).
  - **Seanad Éireann** (the *Seanad*): the **upper house** (Senate), partly appointed and indirectly elected, with limited powers.

### 4.4 Justice
- An independent judiciary; a **Supreme Court** as the highest court. The national police force is **An Garda Síochána** (the *Gardaí*).

---

## 5. THE ELECTORAL SYSTEM AND THE PARTIES ⭐

### 5.1 Proportional representation (PR-STV)
- Ireland uses **proportional representation by the single transferable vote (PR-STV)**: voters **rank** candidates (1, 2, 3…) in multi-seat constituencies.
- **Consequences:** it produces a **multi-party system** and almost always **coalition governments**; it is far more representative than the UK's First Past The Post.

> 👉 **Colle tip:** PR-STV is a perfect **contrast** with the UK (FPTP) and the US (the Electoral College) — a ready-made comparison on "what makes an electoral system fair".

### 5.2 The parties
- **Fianna Fáil** and **Fine Gael:** the two traditional centre / centre-right parties, born of the **opposing sides in the Civil War** (1922-23) rather than of a clear left-right divide.
- **Sinn Féin:** a left-wing, nationalist party (historically linked to the IRA), now a major force, campaigning for **Irish reunification** and on **housing**.
- Others: **Labour**, the **Green Party**, the **Social Democrats**.

---

## 6. THE IRISH LANGUAGE AND CULTURAL IDENTITY
- **Irish (*Gaeilge*)** is a Celtic language, the **first official language** and a powerful **symbol of national identity**.
- Yet it is spoken daily by a **minority**, mainly in the **Gaeltacht** (Irish-speaking regions in the west). It is **compulsory in school**, but English dominates public life.
- A long **revival effort** (road signs, Irish-language media such as TG4, the language in the Constitution) keeps it alive as a marker of identity rather than as the everyday tongue.

> 💡 **Colle angle:** Irish is a textbook case of a **minority/heritage language**: cherished as identity, but struggling against the dominance of a global language (English).

## 7. HISTORY, PERIOD BY PERIOD ⭐

### 7.1 From Gaelic Ireland to British rule
- Early Christian Ireland (St Patrick, 5th c.): a *"land of saints and scholars"*.
- From the **12th century**, gradual **English then British control**; the **Plantations** (esp. of **Ulster** with Protestant settlers) sowed the seeds of later division.
- **Penal Laws** discriminated against the Catholic majority; a **Protestant Ascendancy** ruled.
- **1801 — the Act of Union:** Ireland was merged into the **United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland**.

### 7.2 The Great Famine (1845-1852)
- A catastrophic potato blight: ~**1 million died** and ~**1 million emigrated** (*An Gorta Mór*, "the Great Hunger").
- Resentment at the British response deepened **nationalism** and triggered a massive, lasting **emigration** that built the global **Irish diaspora**.

### 7.3 The struggle for independence
- **1916 — the Easter Rising:** a failed rebellion in Dublin, but its executed leaders became martyrs and galvanised the independence movement.
- **1919-1921 — the War of Independence** (the IRA against British forces).
- **1921 — partition and the Anglo-Irish Treaty:** the island was divided; the south became the **Irish Free State** (a dominion), the north remained in the UK.
- **1922-1923 — the Civil War:** between supporters and opponents of the Treaty — the origin of the Fianna Fáil / Fine Gael divide.

### 7.4 Building the modern state
- **1937 — the Constitution** (*Bunreacht na hÉireann*).
- **1949 — Ireland is declared a Republic** and leaves the Commonwealth.
- Decades of economic difficulty, conservatism and **emigration**; the powerful influence of the **Catholic Church**.
- **1973 — Ireland joins the EEC** (now the EU): a turning point that brought funds, openness and modernisation.

### 7.5 The Celtic Tiger and contemporary Ireland
- **1990s-2000s — the Celtic Tiger:** rapid economic growth, foreign investment, a shift from emigration to **immigration**.
- **1998 — the Good Friday Agreement:** Ireland helped end the **Troubles** in Northern Ireland (and dropped its constitutional claim to the North).
- **2008 — the crash:** the property bubble burst; a severe crisis and an EU/IMF bailout.
- **A social revolution:** **same-sex marriage (2015)** and the **repeal of the abortion ban (2018)**, both by referendum; the **decline of the Catholic Church** (accelerated by abuse scandals).
- **2016-2020 — Brexit:** put the Irish **border** and the Good Friday Agreement back at the centre of European politics.

> 💡 **Colle angle:** in two generations, Ireland moved from a poor, emigrant, Church-dominated country to a wealthy, multicultural, socially liberal EU member — a spectacular **transformation**.

## 8. SOCIETY

### 8.1 The Catholic Church and secularisation ⭐
- For most of the 20th century, the **Catholic Church** dominated education, healthcare and morality.
- Its authority **collapsed** under the weight of **abuse scandals** (the **Magdalene Laundries**, mother-and-baby homes) and rapid **secularisation** — symbolised by the 2015 and 2018 referendums.

### 8.2 Emigration and the diaspora
- Mass **emigration** (after the Famine, and again in the 1980s) shaped the nation; the **Irish diaspora** numbers tens of millions worldwide (especially in the **US**), a huge source of cultural and political influence (e.g. St Patrick's Day).

### 8.3 From emigration to immigration
- With the Celtic Tiger, Ireland became, for the first time, a **country of immigration** (EU citizens, then global migration).
- This has brought new **diversity** but also recent **tensions** over housing and asylum.

## 9. THE MAJOR CONTEMPORARY DEBATES ⭐
1. **Irish reunification:** increasingly discussed since Brexit and shifting demographics in Northern Ireland — could there be a *"border poll"*?
2. **The housing crisis:** soaring rents and prices, a shortage of homes — arguably *the* domestic issue.
3. **Immigration and integration:** a new and sensitive subject for a country long defined by emigration.
4. **Military neutrality:** should Ireland keep its traditional neutrality, given the war in Ukraine and European defence?
5. **The economic model:** heavy reliance on **US multinationals** and low corporate tax — a strength and a vulnerability.
6. **The legacy of the Catholic Church:** redress for abuse, the role of religion in schools.
7. **The Irish language:** how to keep it alive.

---

## 10. THE ECONOMY
- The **Celtic Tiger** transformed a poor economy into a wealthy, globalised one.
- A magnet for **US multinationals** (tech and pharma — Google, Apple, Meta, Pfizer…), attracted by a **low corporate tax rate (12.5%)**, EU access and an English-speaking, educated workforce.
- This makes Ireland **prosperous but exposed**: GDP figures are distorted by multinationals, and the model depends on global tax rules (e.g. the EU's Apple tax case).
- Persistent challenges: **housing**, the **cost of living**, and **regional inequality** (Dublin vs the rest).

---

## 11. CULTURE AND NATIONAL IDENTITY
- A **literary giant** for its size: **W.B. Yeats**, **James Joyce**, **Samuel Beckett**, **Oscar Wilde**, **George Bernard Shaw**, **Seamus Heaney** (four Nobel laureates in literature); today, writers such as **Sally Rooney**.
- **Music and dance:** traditional music, **U2**, **Riverdance**; the global **St Patrick's Day**.
- **Sport:** the **GAA** and its Gaelic games (**hurling**, **Gaelic football**) — deeply tied to identity — and rugby.
- **Symbols:** the shamrock, the harp, Guinness, the colour green; a strong sense of **community** and storytelling (*craic*).

## 📅 Ireland in 10 dates (and why each still matters)
- **1801 — the Act of Union** → Ireland is absorbed into the UK; the start of the modern independence struggle.
- **1845-1852 — the Great Famine** → trauma, mass emigration and lasting resentment of British rule.
- **1916 — the Easter Rising** → the symbolic spark of independence; its leaders became martyrs.
- **1921 — partition & the Anglo-Irish Treaty** → the island is split; the root of today's "border question".
- **1922-23 — the Civil War** → it still structures party politics (Fianna Fáil vs Fine Gael).
- **1937 — the Constitution** → the legal backbone, modernised ever since by referendum.
- **1973 — joining the EEC** → openness, funds and modernisation; Europe as a counterweight to Britain.
- **1998 — the Good Friday Agreement** → peace in the North; Ireland drops its claim to the territory.
- **2008 — the crash** → the end of the Celtic Tiger; austerity and a bailout.
- **2015 / 2018 — marriage equality & abortion referendums** → the social revolution of a once-Catholic country.

## ♻️ Ireland's great paradoxes (where the best questions are born)
- A once deeply **Catholic** country that became one of Europe's most **socially liberal** — by popular vote.
- A **small island** whose **diaspora** numbers tens of millions worldwide.
- A nation forged **against British rule**, now bound to global (especially **US**) capital.
- An officially **Irish-speaking** country that lives in **English**.
- A **militarily neutral** country at the **heart of the EU**.
- Once the land of **emigrants**, now a land of **immigrants**.

## 🗂️ Theme-by-theme colle cards

### 1. Independence & partition
- **⏱️ 30-second sentence:** *"Ireland won its independence from Britain in 1921, but at the price of a partition that still shapes the island today."* — **Enriched:** *"The struggle for independence ended in both a free state and a divided island — and a civil war over that very compromise."*
- **✅ Must know:** the 1916 Easter Rising; the War of Independence; the 1921 Treaty & partition; the 1922-23 Civil War; Republic declared 1949. **❌ Mistakes:** thinking all of Ireland left the UK in 1921.
- **⚠️ Trap:** *Ireland became independent and united* → it became independent but **partitioned** (the North stayed in the UK).
- **📰 How it comes up:** documents on 1916 commemorations, the Civil War's legacy, Anglo-Irish relations, reunification. The press covers it because of **identity, memory, the future of the island**.
- **🔄 Use it to talk about:** nationalism, decolonisation, identity, memory, Anglo-Irish relations.
- **🎣 Hook:** *simple* — *"Ireland was Britain's first and oldest colony."* / *sophisticated* — *"Irish independence came wrapped in a paradox: a free state, but a divided island."*
- **🚪 Opening:** Irish reunification · the memory of 1916 · other independence struggles.
- **🔑 10 words to drop:** independence, the Easter Rising, partition, the Anglo-Irish Treaty, the Irish Free State, a republic, nationalism, self-determination, the Civil War, reunification.
- **🎭 Hidden stakes:** national identity, the legitimacy of the border, historical memory.
- **♻️ Paradox:** a victory for independence that left the nation divided.
- **🌍 Compare with:** India (decolonisation & partition), the US (a revolution against Britain).
- **📖 Cultural references:** *Michael Collins*; *The Wind That Shakes the Barley*.
- **✍️ Model exploitation:** ❌ *"Ireland became independent in 1921."* → 🟠 *"Independence came with the partition of the island."* → ✅ *"Irish independence is best understood as an unfinished settlement: it created a state, but also a border and a civil war whose divisions still structure Irish politics."*
- **❓ Colle questions:** *Was partition inevitable? · How does 1916 still shape Irish identity?*
- **📝 Ready plan ("Independence and its legacy"):** I. A long struggle against British rule — II. A divided and contested settlement — III. A memory that still shapes politics.

### 2. The Great Famine & the diaspora
- **⏱️ 30-second sentence:** *"The Great Famine of the 1840s killed about a million people and drove a million more abroad, creating a global Irish diaspora."* — **Enriched:** *"The Famine was both a demographic catastrophe and a political turning point that hardened Irish nationalism."*

- **⚠️ Trap:** *the Famine was just a natural event* → many see it as worsened by **British policy** (food was exported as people starved).
- **📰 How it comes up:** documents on emigration, the diaspora, Irish-America, St Patrick's Day, historical memory, reparation/apology debates. The press covers it because of **memory, identity, Anglo-Irish relations**.
- **🔄 Use it to talk about:** emigration, identity, memory, globalisation, Anglo-Irish relations.
- **🎣 Hook:** *simple* — *"There are far more people of Irish descent abroad than there are in Ireland."* / *sophisticated* — *"The Famine emptied Ireland — and, in doing so, scattered it across the world."*
- **🚪 Opening:** the political weight of the diaspora · migration today · the memory of the Famine.
- **🔑 10 words to drop:** the Great Famine, the Great Hunger, blight, emigration, the diaspora, exile, Irish-America, memory, trauma, mass migration.
- **🎭 Hidden stakes:** historical memory, blame and responsibility, the power of the diaspora.
- **♻️ Paradox:** a tragedy that became a source of global influence.
- **🌍 Compare with:** other emigration nations (Italy); other famines and their politics.
- **📖 Cultural references:** *Angela's Ashes* (Frank McCourt); *Star of the Sea* (Joseph O'Connor).
- **✍️ Model exploitation:** ❌ *"There was a famine in Ireland."* → 🟠 *"The Famine caused mass emigration."* → ✅ *"The Famine is remembered less as a natural disaster than as a failure of empire — and as the origin of a diaspora that still gives Ireland global reach."*
- **❓ Colle questions:** *Was the Famine a natural disaster or a political failure? · Why does the diaspora still matter?*
- **📝 Ready plan ("The Famine and its legacy"):** I. A demographic catastrophe — II. A political turning point — III. The birth of a global Irish nation.

- **📝 Ready plan ("Towards a united Ireland?"):** I. A divided island and a long conflict — II. The fragile peace of the GFA — III. Brexit, demographics and the case for reunification.

### 6. Brexit & the border
- **⏱️ 30-second sentence:** *"Brexit put the invisible Irish border back at the centre of European politics, because a hard border could threaten the peace."* — **Enriched:** *"A small island became the hardest problem of the entire Brexit negotiation."*
- **✅ Must know:** the need to avoid a hard border; the Northern Ireland Protocol, then the Windsor Framework; the GFA at stake. **❌ Mistakes:** thinking Brexit was only a British issue.
- **⚠️ Trap:** *Brexit didn't really affect Ireland* → it made the **border** the single thorniest issue.
- **📰 How it comes up:** documents on the Protocol, trade frictions, the GFA, North-South relations, Ireland-EU solidarity. The press covers it because of **peace, trade, sovereignty**.
- **🔄 Use it to talk about:** Brexit, the EU, sovereignty, borders, peace.
- **🎣 Hook:** *simple* — *"Brexit's hardest problem was a line on a map across Ireland."* / *sophisticated* — *"Leaving the EU forced Britain to confront a border it had long tried to forget."*
- **🚪 Opening:** the future EU-UK relationship · the impact on reunification · borders in a globalised world.
- **🔑 10 words to drop:** the border, the backstop, the Northern Ireland Protocol, the Windsor Framework, the single market, the customs union, the Good Friday Agreement, sovereignty, frictionless trade, alignment.
- **🎭 Hidden stakes:** peace, the integrity of the EU single market, North-South relations.
- **♻️ Paradox:** an "invisible" border that became the biggest obstacle to Brexit.
- **🌍 Compare with:** other contested borders; the EU's external frontier elsewhere.
- **📖 Cultural references:** news coverage; *Derry Girls* (set on the border).
- **✍️ Model exploitation:** ❌ *"Brexit affected Ireland too."* → 🟠 *"Brexit raised the problem of the Irish border."* → ✅ *"The Irish border exposed the core contradiction of Brexit: you cannot leave the EU's single market and keep a frictionless border without undermining either trade or peace."*
- **❓ Colle questions:** *Why was the Irish border so central to Brexit? · Could Brexit hasten reunification?*
- **📝 Ready plan ("Brexit and Ireland"):** I. An almost invisible border — II. The Brexit dilemma — III. Long-term effects on the island.

### 7. The Celtic Tiger & the economy
- **⏱️ 30-second sentence:** *"The 'Celtic Tiger' turned a poor, emigrant nation into a wealthy, globalised economy built on US multinationals and low taxes."* — **Enriched:** *"Ireland's prosperity is real but exposed, resting on a model that global tax reform could shake."*
- **✅ Must know:** rapid growth (1990s-2000s); the 2008 crash & bailout; FDI; 12.5% corporate tax; tech & pharma multinationals. **❌ Mistakes:** taking headline GDP at face value (it is distorted by multinationals).
- **⚠️ Trap:** *Ireland is simply rich* → it is prosperous but **dependent** on foreign multinationals and global tax rules.
- **📰 How it comes up:** documents on tech/pharma investment, the Apple tax case, corporate tax, inequality, the cost of living. The press covers it because of **globalisation, tax, inequality**.
- **🔄 Use it to talk about:** globalisation, multinationals, tax, inequality, economic models.
- **🎣 Hook:** *simple* — *"In one generation, Ireland went from one of Europe's poorest countries to one of its richest."* / *sophisticated* — *"Ireland's boom is a bet: that the world's biggest companies will keep choosing a small island."*
- **🚪 Opening:** global tax reform · the dependence on multinationals · the housing fallout of growth.
- **🔑 10 words to drop:** the Celtic Tiger, foreign direct investment, a multinational, corporate tax, globalisation, a tax haven (debated), the bailout, austerity, inequality, the cost of living.
- **🎭 Hidden stakes:** dependence vs prosperity, tax justice, inequality.
- **♻️ Paradox:** a nation built on anti-colonial struggle, now reliant on global (US) capital.
- **🌍 Compare with:** Luxembourg, Singapore (small, low-tax, FDI-driven economies).
- **📖 Cultural references:** *The Guarantee* (on the 2008 bank bailout); Sally Rooney's novels (boom-era youth).
- **✍️ Model exploitation:** ❌ *"Ireland is a rich country now."* → 🟠 *"The Celtic Tiger made Ireland wealthy via multinationals."* → ✅ *"Ireland's economic model is a high-reward, high-risk bet on foreign capital — spectacularly successful, but hostage to global tax rules and to the choices of a handful of multinationals."*
- **❓ Colle questions:** *Is Ireland's economic model sustainable? · Is Ireland a tax haven?*
- **📝 Ready plan ("The Irish economic model"):** I. From poverty to the Celtic Tiger — II. A model built on multinationals — III. Prosperity, dependence and inequality.

### 8. Neutrality & Ireland in the world
- **⏱️ 30-second sentence:** *"Ireland is militarily neutral and not a member of NATO, but the war in Ukraine has reopened the debate about its place in European defence."* — **Enriched:** *"Neutrality is a cherished part of Irish identity, increasingly tested by a more dangerous world."*
- **✅ Must know:** military neutrality; not in NATO; a committed EU member; an active UN peacekeeper. **❌ Mistakes:** assuming Ireland is in NATO because it is Western and pro-EU.
- **⚠️ Trap:** *Ireland is a normal Western ally* → it is **militarily neutral** and outside NATO.
- **📰 How it comes up:** documents on neutrality, the Ukraine war, EU defence, defence spending, peacekeeping. The press covers it because of **security, identity, Europe**.
- **🔄 Use it to talk about:** neutrality, the EU, security, identity, globalisation.
- **🎣 Hook:** *simple* — *"Ireland sits at the heart of the EU yet stays out of NATO."* / *sophisticated* — *"For Ireland, neutrality is less a strategy than a part of who it is."*
- **🚪 Opening:** the future of European defence · neutrality after Ukraine · small states in a hard world.
- **🔑 10 words to drop:** neutrality, non-alignment, NATO, peacekeeping, the EU, defence spending, sovereignty, security, multilateralism, soft power.
- **🎭 Hidden stakes:** security, identity, Ireland's role in Europe.
- **♻️ Paradox:** a militarily neutral country at the heart of an increasingly defence-minded EU.
- **🌍 Compare with:** Switzerland, Austria; Sweden & Finland (which recently abandoned neutrality).
- **📖 Cultural references:** Ireland's long record of UN peacekeeping.
- **✍️ Model exploitation:** ❌ *"Ireland is neutral."* → 🟠 *"Ireland is neutral and not in NATO."* → ✅ *"Irish neutrality, born of its anti-imperial history, is now caught between a treasured identity and the pressures of a Europe rearming after 2022."*
- **❓ Colle questions:** *Should Ireland abandon its neutrality? · Can a small state stay neutral today?*
- **📝 Ready plan ("Irish neutrality"):** I. Neutrality as identity — II. A committed European and UN actor — III. Neutrality tested by a dangerous world.

### 9. Modern Ireland: housing, immigration & inequality
- **⏱️ 30-second sentence:** *"Behind Ireland's prosperity lie a severe housing crisis and the new challenge of immigration in a country long defined by emigration."* — **Enriched:** *"Success has brought problems of its own: who can afford to live in the country its boom created?"*
- **✅ Must know:** soaring rents/prices and a housing shortage; the shift from emigration to immigration; recent tensions (e.g. asylum). **❌ Mistakes:** assuming wealth means there are no social problems.
- **⚠️ Trap:** *Ireland is rich, so life is easy* → growth has created a deep **housing** and **cost-of-living** crisis.
- **📰 How it comes up:** documents on housing, rents, homelessness, immigration, asylum, the cost of living. The press covers it because of **inequality, identity, social cohesion**.
- **🔄 Use it to talk about:** inequality, housing, immigration, identity, the cost of living.
- **🎣 Hook:** *simple* — *"Ireland's boom created a country many young people can no longer afford to live in."* / *sophisticated* — *"Prosperity gave Ireland a new question: not how to keep its people, but how to house them."*
- **🚪 Opening:** the housing crisis across Europe · integration and identity · the limits of growth.
- **🔑 10 words to drop:** the housing crisis, rents, homelessness, the cost of living, immigration, integration, social cohesion, inequality, asylum, generational divide.
- **🎭 Hidden stakes:** inequality, social cohesion, the meaning of Irish identity today.
- **♻️ Paradox:** a country that long lost its young to emigration now struggles to house them.
- **🌍 Compare with:** other housing crises (the UK, Canada); other new immigration countries.
- **📖 Cultural references:** *Normal People* (Sally Rooney) — youth in modern Ireland.
- **✍️ Model exploitation:** ❌ *"Ireland has a housing problem."* → 🟠 *"Growth has caused a housing crisis."* → ✅ *"Modern Ireland's central tension is that the very boom which ended emigration now prices its young people out — while immigration reshapes a long-homogeneous society."*
- **❓ Colle questions:** *Has prosperity made Ireland more unequal? · How is immigration changing Irish identity?*
- **📝 Ready plan ("Modern Ireland's challenges"):** I. The shadow side of prosperity (housing) — II. From emigration to immigration — III. Identity and cohesion in a wealthier Ireland.

3. Ireland is **militarily neutral** (not in NATO).
4. **PR-STV** produces coalitions — unlike the UK's FPTP.

6. The **1937 Constitution** can only be changed by **referendum**.

8. The **1916 Easter Rising** sparked the independence movement.
9. **1921 partition** + the Treaty led to the **Civil War** (1922-23).
10. **Fianna Fáil vs Fine Gael** = the Civil War divide, not left vs right.
11. **Sinn Féin:** nationalist, left-wing, pro-reunification, now a major force.
12. **1949:** Ireland becomes a Republic and leaves the Commonwealth.

17. The **Celtic Tiger** turned Ireland from emigration to **immigration**.
18. **US multinationals + a 12.5% corporate tax** drive the economy.
