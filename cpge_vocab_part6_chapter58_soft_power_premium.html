Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse soft power: attraction, legitimacy, values, culture, language, education, science diplomacy, sport, tourism, diaspora, global media, digital diplomacy, propaganda, sharp power, reputation laundering and the fragile link between image, credibility and actual policy.

How to use this chapter

For press articlesFocus on terms such as soft power, sharp power, public diplomacy, nation branding, cultural diplomacy, sportswashing, digital diplomacy, disinformation, credibility gap and double standards.
For oral examsUse this vocabulary to explain how states influence foreign publics through attraction and reputation, but also how image-building can become manipulation when it hides coercion or abuses.

A. Core concepts: soft, hard, smart and sharp power

soft power

IPA: /ˌsɒft ˈpaʊə/
Pronunciation: soft POW-uh
French: soft power ; puissance douce

Definition: The ability to influence others through attraction, culture, values and legitimacy rather than coercion.

Example: Soft power allows a country to shape preferences without using force.

hard power

IPA: /ˌhɑːd ˈpaʊə/
Pronunciation: hard POW-uh
French: hard power ; puissance coercitive

Definition: Influence based on military force, economic pressure or coercion.

Example: Hard power can impose decisions but may create resentment.

smart power

IPA: /ˌsmɑːt ˈpaʊə/
Pronunciation: smart POW-uh
French: smart power ; puissance intelligente

Definition: A strategy combining soft power and hard power effectively.

Example: Smart power blends diplomacy, culture, trade and military credibility.

sharp power

IPA: /ˌʃɑːp ˈpaʊə/
Pronunciation: sharp POW-uh
French: sharp power ; pouvoir d’influence coercitif

Definition: Influence that uses manipulation, censorship, pressure or covert interference.

Example: Sharp power blurs the line between attraction and manipulation.

normative power

IPA: /ˈnɔːmətɪv ˈpaʊə/
Pronunciation: NOR-muh-tiv POW-uh
French: puissance normative

Definition: Influence based on shaping norms, rules and values.

Example: The European Union is often described as a normative power.

civilian power

IPA: /səˈvɪliən ˈpaʊə/
Pronunciation: suh-VIL-yuhn POW-uh
French: puissance civile

Definition: Influence based on diplomacy, trade, aid and law rather than military force.

Example: The EU has traditionally relied on civilian power.

cultural diplomacy

IPA: /ˌkʌltʃərəl dɪˈpləʊməsi/
Pronunciation: KUL-chuh-ruhl di-PLOH-muh-see
French: diplomatie culturelle

Definition: The use of culture to build relationships and influence abroad.

Example: Cultural diplomacy includes language, cinema, museums and festivals.

public diplomacy

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk dɪˈpləʊməsi/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik di-PLOH-muh-see
French: diplomatie publique

Definition: Efforts by a state to communicate with foreign publics and shape its image.

Example: Public diplomacy targets societies, not only governments.

nation branding

IPA: /ˌneɪʃən ˈbrændɪŋ/
Pronunciation: NAY-shuhn BRAN-ding
French: image de marque nationale

Definition: The strategy of shaping a country’s image abroad like a brand.

Example: Nation branding can attract tourists, investors and students.

country image

IPA: /ˌkʌntri ˈɪmɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: KUN-tree IM-ij
French: image d’un pays

Definition: How a country is perceived by foreign publics.

Example: A positive country image strengthens soft power.

national image

IPA: /ˌnæʃənəl ˈɪmɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: NASH-uh-nuhl IM-ij
French: image nationale

Definition: The reputation or perception of a nation.

Example: A national image can be damaged by war or human-rights abuses.

international image

IPA: /ˌɪntəˈnæʃənəl ˈɪmɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: IN-tuh-NASH-uh-nuhl IM-ij
French: image internationale

Definition: The way a state is seen abroad.

Example: International image matters for diplomacy and trade.

IPA: /ˌɡləʊbəl ˌrepjʊˈteɪʃən/
Pronunciation: GLOH-buhl rep-yuh-TAY-shuhn
French: réputation mondiale

Example: A global reputation can take decades to build and years to lose.
