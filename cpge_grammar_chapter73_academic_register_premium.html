Chapter 73 — Academic Register
B2 → C1 language for precise, structured, evidence-based and appropriately formal CPGE communication.

Chapter objective

This premium grammar chapter develops advanced control of academic register: formal vocabulary, precise verbs, controlled impersonality, academic cohesion, reporting language, evidence presentation, problematics and natural formal speech.

The golden rule

Academic register = clarity + precision + evidence + controlled formality.
Do not try to sound complicated.
Try to make complex ideas easy to follow.
Avoidvague words, unsupported judgement, conversational fillers, emotional wording
Preferprecise verbs, clear criteria, accurate connectors, source attribution
Controlcertainty, sentence length, nominalisation, impersonality
Goalformal, natural, readable and intellectually precise English

A. Lesson — What is academic register?

Register

Rule / explanation: Register is the level and style of language chosen for a particular audience, purpose and context.

Example / answer: Academic writing requires precise, structured and appropriately formal language.

Academic register

Rule / explanation: Academic register favours clarity, evidence, logical organisation and controlled evaluation.

Example / answer: The findings suggest that regional inequality remains a significant concern.

Not artificial complexity

Rule / explanation: Formal writing should not be made difficult through unnecessary jargon or excessively long sentences.

Example / answer: Clear: The policy failed because implementation was poorly coordinated.

CPGE relevance

Rule / explanation: Students need academic register for essays, summaries, syntheses, article commentary and structured oral argument.

Example / answer: The author’s claim is persuasive, although the evidence remains incomplete.

B. Formal vs conversational language

Conversational

Rule / explanation: Everyday English often uses contractions, vague words, phrasal verbs and direct personal reactions.

Example / answer: The government didn’t really think the plan through.

Academic

Rule / explanation: Academic English uses more precise vocabulary and less conversational phrasing.

Example / answer: The government did not assess the plan adequately.

Appropriate distance

Rule / explanation: Avoid sounding either too casual or excessively pompous.

Example / answer: The argument is unconvincing because it overlooks several key variables.

Audience awareness

Rule / explanation: Choose language that suits an informed reader rather than a friend or social-media audience.

Example / answer: The issue requires careful examination.

Consistency

Rule / explanation: Do not switch randomly between highly formal and very informal phrasing.

Example / answer: Maintain the same level of register throughout the paragraph.

C. Avoiding contractions

Full forms

Rule / explanation: In formal writing, prefer full forms such as do not, cannot and it is.

Example / answer: The evidence does not support the conclusion.

Oral exception

Rule / explanation: Contractions are natural in khôlles and formal speech when fluency matters.

Example / answer: I don’t entirely agree with the author.

Cannot

Rule / explanation: Cannot is usually written as one word.

Example / answer: The trend cannot be explained by one factor alone.

Possessives

Rule / explanation: Do not confuse contractions with possessive forms.

Example / answer: The government’s policy = possessive; it’s = it is.

Controlled flexibility

Rule / explanation: Modern academic prose may use occasional contractions, but CPGE students are safer using full forms in essays.

Example / answer: It is unlikely that the reform will succeed.

D. Precise verbs instead of vague verbs

Show

Rule / explanation: Replace show with demonstrate, indicate, reveal or illustrate according to strength.

Example / answer: The figures indicate a gradual decline.

Say

Rule / explanation: Replace say with argue, claim, state, suggest, observe or acknowledge.

Example / answer: The author argues that regulation is necessary.

Think

Rule / explanation: Replace think with believe, contend, maintain, assume or consider.

Example / answer: Many analysts contend that the reform is unsustainable.

Get

Rule / explanation: Replace get with obtain, become, receive, achieve or experience.

Example / answer: Rural regions received less funding.

Make

Rule / explanation: Replace make with create, produce, establish, cause or formulate.

Example / answer: The policy created additional administrative costs.

E. Formal alternatives to phrasal verbs

Find out

Rule / explanation: Use determine, discover or establish.

Example / answer: The inquiry sought to determine why the system failed.

Look into

Rule / explanation: Use investigate or examine.

Example / answer: The committee will investigate the allegations.

Bring about

Rule / explanation: Use cause, produce or generate.

Example / answer: The reform produced significant changes.

Carry out

Rule / explanation: Use conduct or undertake.

Example / answer: Researchers conducted a nationwide survey.

Point out

Rule / explanation: Use note, emphasise, observe or highlight.

Example / answer: The article highlights regional disparities.

F. Avoiding vague language

Things

Rule / explanation: Replace things with precise nouns such as factors, measures, consequences or developments.

Example / answer: Several factors contributed to the decline.

A lot of

Rule / explanation: Use many, much, considerable, substantial or numerous.

Example / answer: The reform generated considerable opposition.

Good / bad

Rule / explanation: Use effective, beneficial, convincing, harmful, flawed or inadequate.

Example / answer: The policy was ineffective.

Big

Rule / explanation: Use major, substantial, significant, considerable or extensive.

Example / answer: The scandal had significant political consequences.

Nice / interesting

Rule / explanation: Use relevant, insightful, compelling, noteworthy or valuable.

Example / answer: The comparison is particularly insightful.

G. Formal noun phrases

Nominalisation

Rule / explanation: Academic English often turns verbs or adjectives into nouns to package complex ideas.

Example / answer: The implementation of the reform was delayed.

Dense information

Rule / explanation: Noun phrases can condense arguments, but excessive nominalisation makes prose heavy.

Example / answer: Heavy: The implementation of the reduction of expenditure...

Balanced style

Rule / explanation: Combine clear verbs with useful noun phrases.

Example / answer: The government implemented the reform without adequate consultation.

Pre-modification

Rule / explanation: Academic noun phrases often place adjectives or nouns before the head noun.

Example / answer: Long-term regional development policy.

Post-modification

Rule / explanation: Use of-phrases, relative clauses and participles after the noun.

Example / answer: The measures introduced by Parliament.

H. Impersonal structures

It is argued that

Rule / explanation: Use this to present a recognised position without foregrounding the writer.

Example / answer: It is often argued that regulation restricts innovation.

It appears that

Rule / explanation: Use this for cautious interpretation.

Example / answer: It appears that the policy has had uneven effects.

There is evidence that

Rule / explanation: Use this to connect a claim explicitly to support.

Example / answer: There is evidence that trust has declined.

It remains unclear whether

Rule / explanation: Use this to identify an unresolved question.

Example / answer: It remains unclear whether the reform will be sustainable.

Caution

Rule / explanation: Impersonal structures can hide responsibility or source.

Example / answer: Identify the author or evidence when attribution matters.

I. Personal voice in academic English

I would argue

Rule / explanation: First person can be appropriate when presenting a reasoned position.

Example / answer: I would argue that public trust is the central issue.

In my view

Rule / explanation: Use this sparingly to mark evaluation.

Example / answer: In my view, the article underestimates regional inequality.

Avoid repetition

Rule / explanation: Do not begin every sentence with I think.

Example / answer: Use evidence and analytical structures instead.

We

Rule / explanation: Avoid vague inclusive we unless the audience is clearly defined.

Example / answer: Weak: We all know that inequality is rising.

Authorial responsibility

Rule / explanation: A controlled first person can be clearer than an anonymous passive.

Example / answer: I will first examine the economic causes.

J. Hedging and boosters

Hedging

Rule / explanation: Use may, might, appear, suggest and arguably when certainty is limited.

Example / answer: The reform may have contributed to the decline.

Boosters

Rule / explanation: Use clearly, strongly, undoubtedly and demonstrate when evidence is strong.

Example / answer: The results strongly suggest a structural problem.

Calibration

Rule / explanation: Match the force of the language to the quality of the evidence.

Example / answer: The survey suggests, rather than proves, that trust is weakening.

Avoid stacking

Rule / explanation: Do not combine many hedge markers unnecessarily.

Example / answer: Weak: It might perhaps possibly indicate...

Balanced authority

Rule / explanation: Academic register is confident where evidence is clear and cautious where uncertainty remains.

Example / answer: The policy reduced costs, although its long-term effects remain uncertain.

K. Formal connectors

Addition

Rule / explanation: Use furthermore, moreover, in addition and likewise.

Example / answer: Moreover, the policy failed to address regional disparities.

Contrast

Rule / explanation: Use however, nevertheless, by contrast and whereas.

Example / answer: The measure reduced costs; however, access remained unequal.

Cause

Rule / explanation: Use therefore, consequently, thus and as a result.

Example / answer: Funding was reduced; consequently, waiting times increased.

Concession

Rule / explanation: Use although, while, admittedly and despite.

Example / answer: Although the reform was ambitious, implementation was weak.

Sequence

Rule / explanation: Use first, subsequently, finally and in turn.

Example / answer: The report first identifies the causes and subsequently examines the consequences.

L. Cohesion and reference

Pronoun reference

Rule / explanation: Make sure pronouns clearly refer to a specific noun.

Example / answer: The policy was revised after the committee rejected it.

This + noun

Rule / explanation: Use this decision, this trend or this evidence rather than vague this.

Example / answer: This decline reflects a broader loss of trust.

Repetition with control

Rule / explanation: Repeat key terms when necessary for clarity rather than forcing awkward synonyms.

Example / answer: The reform affected access. This reform also changed funding rules.

Lexical chains

Rule / explanation: Use related vocabulary to maintain topic continuity.

Example / answer: Regulation, safeguards, oversight and accountability.

Paragraph unity

Rule / explanation: Each paragraph should develop one central analytical function.

Example / answer: Do not combine unrelated examples merely because they concern the same country.

M. Sentence structure

Controlled complexity

Rule / explanation: Use complex sentences to express relationships, not to display grammar.

Example / answer: Although costs fell, regional inequality increased.

One main idea

Rule / explanation: A sentence should normally have one clear main proposition.

Example / answer: Avoid joining several arguments with repeated and.

Subordination

Rule / explanation: Use because, although, whereas, while and if to show logical hierarchy.

Example / answer: While the reform improved efficiency, it weakened local autonomy.

Variation

Rule / explanation: Combine shorter analytical sentences with longer developed ones.

Example / answer: The policy failed. More precisely, it failed because implementation was rushed.

Punctuation

Rule / explanation: Use semicolons, colons and dashes only when their logical function is clear.

Example / answer: One issue remains: the lack of long-term funding.

N. Formal definitions and explanations

X refers to

Rule / explanation: Use this to define a term precisely.

Example / answer: Institutional trust refers to citizens’ confidence in public bodies.

X can be defined as

Rule / explanation: Use this for explicit definitions.

Example / answer: Political legitimacy can be defined as the recognised right to govern.

In other words

Rule / explanation: Use this to reformulate a complex point clearly.

Example / answer: The policy lacked legitimacy; in other words, citizens did not view it as justified.

More specifically

Rule / explanation: Use this to narrow a broad statement.

Example / answer: The reform affected access, more specifically in rural areas.

That is

Rule / explanation: Use this for concise clarification.

Example / answer: The measure was regressive; that is, it placed a greater burden on poorer households.

O. Presenting evidence formally

According to

Rule / explanation: Use this to attribute information to a source.

Example / answer: According to the report, waiting times increased by 12 per cent.

The data indicate

Rule / explanation: Use indicate for cautious evidence-based interpretation.

Example / answer: The data indicate a widening regional gap.

The findings demonstrate

Rule / explanation: Use demonstrate for stronger evidential support.

Example / answer: The findings demonstrate that access remains unequal.

As illustrated by

Rule / explanation: Use this to introduce an example that supports a claim.

Example / answer: As illustrated by the Scottish case, local autonomy can improve implementation.

Evidence limitation

Rule / explanation: Mention limits such as sample size, period or methodology.

Example / answer: The survey is informative, although its sample is relatively narrow.

P. Referring to authors and sources

The author argues

Rule / explanation: Use reporting verbs that accurately reflect stance.

Example / answer: The author argues that regulation is necessary.

The journalist claims

Rule / explanation: Claim may suggest that the proposition is disputed or unverified.

Example / answer: The journalist claims that ministers ignored several warnings.

The report acknowledges

Rule / explanation: Acknowledge introduces a concession or recognised fact.

Example / answer: The report acknowledges that costs increased initially.

The article emphasises

Rule / explanation: Use emphasise for an idea given particular prominence.

Example / answer: The article emphasises the importance of public trust.

Avoid repetition

Rule / explanation: Vary reporting verbs according to meaning, not merely for style.

Example / answer: Do not replace argues with proves unless proof is genuinely established.

Q. Formal comparison and contrast

Whereas

Rule / explanation: Use whereas to contrast two facts or positions in one sentence.

Example / answer: Urban access improved, whereas rural access declined.

By contrast

Rule / explanation: Use this as a sentence connector.

Example / answer: The first source is optimistic. By contrast, the second predicts prolonged instability.

Compared with

Rule / explanation: Use this to introduce the reference point.

Example / answer: Compared with 2020, turnout was significantly lower.

In comparison

Rule / explanation: Use this to create an explicit comparative transition.

Example / answer: In comparison, the regional programme achieved more consistent results.

Respectively

Rule / explanation: Use this when matching two ordered sets clearly.

Example / answer: Support rose to 45 and 52 per cent in Wales and Scotland, respectively.

R. Avoiding emotional and biased wording

Loaded language

Rule / explanation: Avoid words that condemn or praise without analysis.

Example / answer: Weak: The government’s disgusting betrayal.

Analytical alternative

Rule / explanation: Describe the action and evaluate it through criteria.

Example / answer: The decision was politically controversial and procedurally opaque.

Absolute labels

Rule / explanation: Avoid calling a source ridiculous, stupid or obviously wrong.

Example / answer: The argument is flawed because it relies on an unrepresentative sample.

Emotional intensifiers

Rule / explanation: Avoid really, totally, incredibly and absolutely unless analysing rhetoric.

Example / answer: The policy was highly ineffective.

Controlled criticism

Rule / explanation: Formal register can be firm without becoming insulting.

Example / answer: The author’s interpretation is difficult to sustain.

S. Formal questions and problematics

Analytical question

Rule / explanation: A problématique should identify a tension, mechanism or condition.

Example / answer: How can governments restore trust without creating unrealistic expectations?

Avoid to what extent by habit

Rule / explanation: Use a structure that reflects the specific issue rather than a generic formula.

Example / answer: Can regulation protect consumers while preserving innovation?

Embedded question

Rule / explanation: Formal writing often turns a question into a noun clause.

Example / answer: The central issue is whether the reform can remain socially sustainable.

No decorative question

Rule / explanation: Every question should guide the analysis and receive an answer.

Example / answer: Do not begin with What is democracy? unless definition is central.

Contextualised framing

Rule / explanation: Introduce the situation before stating the key question.

Example / answer: Recent scandals have weakened confidence. The question is how legitimacy can be restored.

T. Academic register in khôlles

Formal but natural

Rule / explanation: Oral academic English should sound structured without becoming read aloud or artificial.

Example / answer: I would first like to summarise the author’s main argument.

Signposting

Rule / explanation: Use first, turning now to, this leads to and finally.

Example / answer: This leads to the broader question of democratic legitimacy.

Measured reaction

Rule / explanation: Replace I like this article with an analytical evaluation.

Example / answer: The article is persuasive, although its evidence is limited.

Clarification

Rule / explanation: Use in other words, more precisely and what the author means is.

Example / answer: More precisely, the problem concerns unequal access rather than total absence of services.

Fluency

Rule / explanation: Use familiar formal phrases accurately rather than rare expressions hesitantly.

Example / answer: On balance, the argument remains convincing.

U. Common mistakes by French speakers

Actually

Rule / explanation: Actually means in fact, not actuellement.

Example / answer: Currently means at present.

Eventually

Rule / explanation: Eventually means in the end, not éventuellement.

Example / answer: Possibly or perhaps express possibility.

According to me

Rule / explanation: Use in my view or I would argue.

Example / answer: In my view, the conclusion is too broad.

Assist to

Rule / explanation: Use attend for an event and assist for help.

Example / answer: She attended the conference.

Make a synthesis

Rule / explanation: Use write or produce a synthesis.

Example / answer: The student produced a comparative synthesis.

Pass an exam

Rule / explanation: Pass means succeed; take or sit means participate.

Example / answer: She sat the entrance examination and passed it.

V. Exercises

1. Formalise

Rule / explanation: The government didn’t really think the plan through.

Example / answer: The government did not assess the plan adequately.

2. Replace the vague verb

Rule / explanation: The figures show that trust is falling.

Example / answer: The figures indicate that trust is declining.

3. Replace the phrasal verb

Rule / explanation: The committee looked into the allegations.

Example / answer: The committee investigated the allegations.

4. Improve the evaluation

Rule / explanation: The article is bad.

Example / answer: The article is unconvincing because it relies on limited evidence.

5. Add academic caution

Rule / explanation: The reform caused regional inequality.

Example / answer: The reform may have contributed to regional inequality.

6. Correct the false friend

Rule / explanation: Actually, the government is preparing a new bill.

Example / answer: Currently, the government is preparing a new bill.

7. Translate

Rule / explanation: L’auteur soutient que la réforme est nécessaire, bien que ses effets à long terme restent incertains.

Example / answer: The author argues that the reform is necessary, although its long-term effects remain uncertain.

8. Mini concours challenge

Rule / explanation: Rewrite a conversational paragraph in academic register using one reporting verb, one hedge, one formal connector and one evaluative adjective.

Example / answer: The author argues that the reform will improve efficiency. However, the available evidence suggests that its effects may vary considerably across regions. The proposal is potentially useful, but its long-term feasibility remains uncertain.
