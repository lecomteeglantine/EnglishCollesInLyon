Chapter 83 — Concision and Precision
B2 → C1 techniques for economical, exact and intellectually controlled CPGE English.

Chapter objective

This premium grammar chapter develops advanced control of concision and precision: eliminating redundancy, replacing vague words, controlling nominalisation, clarifying reference, calibrating degree and writing efficient summaries, syntheses and arguments.

Level B2 → C1 Concision Precision Academic Style Editing Skills Exercises + Answers

The golden rule

Say everything necessary—and nothing unnecessary.
MEANING → EXACT WORD → DIRECT STRUCTURE → NECESSARY NUANCE
Removeempty phrases, duplicated meaning, needless repetition
Replacevague nouns, weak verbs, generic adjectives
Preserveconditions, modality, attribution, contrast and uncertainty
Final testCan the sentence be shorter without becoming less accurate?

A. Lesson — What are concision and precision?

Concision

Rule / explanation: Concision means expressing the necessary idea without redundant words, empty phrases or repeated information.

Example / answer: Concise: The reform failed because funding was insufficient.

Precision

Rule / explanation: Precision means choosing words and structures that communicate the exact meaning intended.

Example / answer: Precise: The reform reduced national expenditure but increased rural waiting times.

Not mere brevity

Rule / explanation: A short sentence is not automatically clear, and a detailed sentence is not automatically wordy.

Example / answer: The aim is complete meaning with no unnecessary language.

CPGE relevance

Rule / explanation: Concision and precision strengthen summaries, syntheses, essays, translations, article commentary and oral answers.

Example / answer: A focused argument is easier to understand, remember and evaluate.

B. The economy principle

One clear function

Rule / explanation: Every sentence should perform a clear function: claim, explanation, evidence, qualification or transition.

Example / answer: The statistic supports the paragraph’s claim about regional inequality.

Remove empty wording

Rule / explanation: Delete expressions that do not change the meaning.

Example / answer: Wordy: It is important to note the fact that costs rose. Concise: Costs rose.

Preserve necessary nuance

Rule / explanation: Do not remove modal verbs, conditions or limitations that affect accuracy.

Example / answer: The policy may reduce costs is not equivalent to the policy reduces costs.

Prefer direct structure

Rule / explanation: Place the main subject and verb where the reader can find them easily.

Example / answer: The committee rejected the proposal.

Revision test

Rule / explanation: Ask whether each word adds meaning, grammar, emphasis or coherence.

Example / answer: If removing a phrase changes nothing, it may be unnecessary.

C. Empty introductory phrases

It is important to note that

Rule / explanation: Usually state the important point directly.

Example / answer: Wordy: It is important to note that trust declined. Better: Trust declined.

It should be pointed out that

Rule / explanation: Replace with a direct claim unless attribution or emphasis is needed.

Example / answer: Better: The report excludes rural regions.

It is interesting to observe that

Rule / explanation: Explain why the fact matters rather than calling it interesting.

Example / answer: The contrast reveals a widening generational divide.

The fact that

Rule / explanation: Use a direct clause when the noun phrase adds no function.

Example / answer: Wordy: The fact that costs rose caused concern. Better: Rising costs caused concern.

There is no doubt that

Rule / explanation: Avoid unsupported certainty and unnecessary framing.

Example / answer: The evidence strongly suggests that implementation failed.

D. Redundant pairs

Each and every

Rule / explanation: Use each or every unless deliberate emphasis is required.

Example / answer: Every region submitted a report.

First and foremost

Rule / explanation: Use first or above all according to meaning.

Example / answer: Above all, the reform raises a question of legitimacy.

Basic fundamentals

Rule / explanation: Fundamentals already means basic principles.

Example / answer: The course covers the fundamentals of argumentation.

Future plans

Rule / explanation: Plans normally concern the future.

Example / answer: The government announced its plans.

Past history

Rule / explanation: History already refers to the past.

Example / answer: The institution’s history explains its present structure.

E. Tautologies and duplicated meaning

Completely unanimous

Rule / explanation: Unanimous already means complete agreement.

Example / answer: The committee was unanimous.

Final outcome

Rule / explanation: Outcome already indicates the result.

Example / answer: The outcome remains uncertain.

End result

Rule / explanation: Use result unless contrast with an intermediate stage is necessary.

Example / answer: The result was disappointing.

Unexpected surprise

Rule / explanation: A surprise is normally unexpected.

Example / answer: The decision came as a surprise.

New innovation

Rule / explanation: Innovation already implies novelty.

Example / answer: The policy encourages innovation.

F. Wordy prepositional phrases

In order to

Rule / explanation: Use to unless emphasis or ambiguity requires the longer form.

Example / answer: The government increased funding to reduce waiting times.

Due to the fact that

Rule / explanation: Replace with because.

Example / answer: The programme failed because funding was insufficient.

In the event that

Rule / explanation: Use if in most ordinary contexts.

Example / answer: If negotiations fail, a new vote will be held.

With regard to

Rule / explanation: Use about or a direct topic phrase when formality does not require it.

Example / answer: The article examines regional access.

For the purpose of

Rule / explanation: Use to when the subject is the same.

Example / answer: The survey was conducted to assess public trust.

G. Nominalisation versus direct verbs

Nominalised form

Rule / explanation: Academic nouns can package ideas but often increase length and hide agency.

Example / answer: The implementation of the reform was undertaken by the ministry.

Direct verb

Rule / explanation: Use an active verb when it makes the sentence clearer.

Example / answer: The ministry implemented the reform.

Useful nominalisation

Rule / explanation: Retain nominalisation when discussing the concept itself.

Example / answer: The implementation was uneven across regions.

Hidden action

Rule / explanation: Search for nouns ending in -tion, -ment, -ance or -al that could become verbs.

Example / answer: An evaluation of the programme was conducted becomes the programme was evaluated.

Balance

Rule / explanation: Academic style needs both precise nouns and energetic verbs.

Example / answer: Do not convert every noun into a verb mechanically.

H. Weak verbs and heavy nouns

Make a decision

Rule / explanation: Use decide when the action matters more than the noun.

Example / answer: The committee decided to postpone the vote.

Carry out an analysis

Rule / explanation: Use analyse when possible.

Example / answer: Researchers analysed the regional data.

Give consideration to

Rule / explanation: Use consider.

Example / answer: The government should consider local variation.

Reach an agreement

Rule / explanation: This collocation is natural, but agree may be more concise.

Example / answer: The parties agreed on a revised timetable.

Have an impact on

Rule / explanation: Use affect when the exact meaning is clear.

Example / answer: The cuts affected rural services.

I. Vague nouns

Thing

Rule / explanation: Replace thing with the exact noun: factor, issue, measure, consequence or development.

Example / answer: Several factors contributed to the decline.

Aspect

Rule / explanation: Specify which dimension is meant.

Example / answer: The policy’s financial cost remains uncertain.

Problem

Rule / explanation: Use obstacle, limitation, contradiction, risk or failure where appropriate.

Example / answer: The main limitation is the narrow sample.

Situation

Rule / explanation: Name the event or condition directly.

Example / answer: The funding shortage intensified regional inequality.

Stuff

Rule / explanation: Stuff is unsuitable for formal analytical English.

Example / answer: The report contains extensive evidence.

J. Vague verbs

Do

Rule / explanation: Replace do with conduct, implement, perform, address or complete.

Example / answer: The agency conducted a review.

Make

Rule / explanation: Use create, produce, cause, establish, formulate or enact.

Example / answer: The reform created additional costs.

Get

Rule / explanation: Use obtain, receive, become, achieve or experience.

Example / answer: Rural regions received less funding.

Have

Rule / explanation: Use experience, possess, include, contain or face according to meaning.

Example / answer: The policy faces significant opposition.

Show

Rule / explanation: Use indicate, reveal, demonstrate, illustrate or suggest according to evidential strength.

Example / answer: The figures indicate a gradual decline.

K. Precise reporting verbs

Argue

Rule / explanation: Use when a source presents reasons for a position.

Example / answer: The author argues that stronger safeguards are necessary.

Claim

Rule / explanation: Use for a disputed or insufficiently verified assertion.

Example / answer: The minister claims that the reform has reduced inequality.

Suggest

Rule / explanation: Use for cautious inference rather than proof.

Example / answer: The findings suggest that trust is declining.

Demonstrate

Rule / explanation: Use only when evidence strongly establishes the point.

Example / answer: The experiment demonstrates a causal effect.

Acknowledge

Rule / explanation: Use when a source recognises a limitation or opposing fact.

Example / answer: The report acknowledges that implementation costs rose.

L. Precise evaluative adjectives

Good

Rule / explanation: Replace with effective, convincing, relevant, equitable or beneficial.

Example / answer: The programme was effective in reducing delays.

Bad

Rule / explanation: Replace with flawed, harmful, ineffective, inadequate or disproportionate.

Example / answer: The methodology is flawed.

Important

Rule / explanation: Use significant, crucial, decisive, central or relevant according to degree and function.

Example / answer: Public trust is crucial to legitimacy.

Interesting

Rule / explanation: Use insightful, revealing, noteworthy or relevant and explain why.

Example / answer: The comparison is revealing because it exposes a regional divide.

Big

Rule / explanation: Use major, substantial, considerable, extensive or far-reaching.

Example / answer: The ruling may have far-reaching consequences.

M. Precise degree

Very

Rule / explanation: Replace with a more exact intensifier or stronger adjective when appropriate.

Example / answer: Highly controversial / substantial / crucial.

A lot

Rule / explanation: Use considerably, substantially, frequently, many or much according to grammar.

Example / answer: Waiting times increased considerably.

Some

Rule / explanation: Clarify quantity or degree when evidence allows.

Example / answer: Approximately one third of respondents opposed the proposal.

Many

Rule / explanation: Use a figure, proportion or defined group when available.

Example / answer: Sixty-two per cent of rural respondents expressed concern.

Almost

Rule / explanation: Distinguish almost, nearly, virtually and just under.

Example / answer: Just under half the sample supported the measure.

N. Precise comparison

Better

Rule / explanation: State the criterion of improvement.

Example / answer: The local programme was more effective at reducing waiting times.

Worse

Rule / explanation: Identify the dimension that deteriorated.

Example / answer: Rural access became less equitable.

Similar

Rule / explanation: Explain which features are comparable.

Example / answer: The two reports reach similar conclusions about public trust.

Different

Rule / explanation: Name the exact contrast.

Example / answer: The sources differ in tone, evidence and proposed solutions.

More successful

Rule / explanation: Specify the measure of success.

Example / answer: The programme was more successful financially but less equitable socially.

O. Avoiding overgeneralisation

Everyone

Rule / explanation: Use a defined population or proportion.

Example / answer: Most respondents under thirty supported the proposal.

Always

Rule / explanation: Use often, generally, consistently or in the cases examined.

Example / answer: Economic crises often intensify political distrust.

Never

Rule / explanation: Use rarely, seldom or no documented case where appropriate.

Example / answer: The report provides no evidence that consultation occurred.

Society

Rule / explanation: Identify the country, institution, group or period.

Example / answer: British voters expressed growing concern.

People

Rule / explanation: Use citizens, patients, workers, students, policymakers or respondents.

Example / answer: Rural patients travelled farther for treatment.

P. Avoiding needless repetition

Repeated claim

Rule / explanation: Do not restate the same point in different words without adding analysis.

Example / answer: Each sentence should advance the argument.

Repeated conclusion

Rule / explanation: Do not end every paragraph with the same general judgement.

Example / answer: Derive a new implication from each section.

Repeated source name

Rule / explanation: Use accurate pronouns or noun phrases where reference remains clear.

Example / answer: The report... It... The analysis...

Repeated connector

Rule / explanation: Vary connectors only when the logical function differs.

Example / answer: Do not replace however merely to avoid repetition if contrast remains the same.

Strategic repetition

Rule / explanation: Keep repetition when it provides emphasis or thematic cohesion.

Example / answer: Without trust, reform fails; without trust, legitimacy weakens.

Q. Sentence length and information density

Overlong sentence

Rule / explanation: A sentence containing several independent arguments becomes difficult to process.

Example / answer: Divide it when the logical hierarchy is unclear.

Over-short writing

Rule / explanation: A series of short sentences may sound abrupt and hide relationships.

Example / answer: Combine related ideas through subordination.

One main proposition

Rule / explanation: Keep one central proposition and organise supporting details around it.

Example / answer: Although costs fell, access remained unequal.

Heavy noun phrase

Rule / explanation: Break up dense noun clusters when they obscure meaning.

Example / answer: The long-term regional healthcare funding review becomes the review of long-term regional healthcare funding.

Read-aloud test

Rule / explanation: If a sentence cannot be read naturally in one controlled breath, inspect its structure.

Example / answer: Punctuation cannot rescue excessive conceptual density.

R. Active and passive for precision

Active voice

Rule / explanation: Use active voice when the agent matters and responsibility should be clear.

Example / answer: The ministry ignored three warnings.

Passive voice

Rule / explanation: Use passive voice when the action, result or affected object is central.

Example / answer: Three warnings were ignored.

Hidden responsibility

Rule / explanation: Avoid passive constructions that conceal an important agent.

Example / answer: Mistakes were made is less precise than officials misallocated the funds.

Unknown agent

Rule / explanation: Passive voice is appropriate when the agent is unknown or irrelevant.

Example / answer: The documents were stolen overnight.

Strategic choice

Rule / explanation: Choose voice according to information focus, not a rule that active is always better.

Example / answer: The policy was adopted in 2019; Parliament later revised it.

S. Precise reference

Vague this

Rule / explanation: Add a noun after this when the reference is broad or abstract.

Example / answer: This decline weakened public trust.

Ambiguous it

Rule / explanation: Repeat the noun if more than one antecedent is possible.

Example / answer: The committee rejected the proposal because the proposal lacked funding.

Unclear they

Rule / explanation: Identify the relevant group.

Example / answer: Ministers denied the allegation, while journalists requested evidence.

Such

Rule / explanation: Use such + noun to refer precisely to a previously defined type.

Example / answer: Such measures may discourage small firms.

The former / latter

Rule / explanation: Use only with two clearly ordered items.

Example / answer: Efficiency and fairness were discussed; the latter received less attention.

T. Concision in summaries and syntheses

Preserve core meaning

Rule / explanation: Keep the source’s claim, mechanism and qualification.

Example / answer: The author argues that automation may increase efficiency but weaken accountability.

Remove examples selectively

Rule / explanation: Omit secondary examples unless they are essential to the reasoning.

Example / answer: Summarise the pattern rather than listing every case.

Avoid commentary

Rule / explanation: A summary reports the source rather than adding personal evaluation.

Example / answer: The article claims, not the article correctly proves.

Combine related information

Rule / explanation: Use subordination, participle clauses and precise reporting verbs.

Example / answer: Citing regional data, the report warns that national averages are misleading.

Do not oversimplify

Rule / explanation: Concision must not erase uncertainty or contrast.

Example / answer: May, partly and although may be essential.

U. Common French-to-English verbosity and errors

Concerning

Rule / explanation: Concerning is possible, but about or a direct construction is often clearer.

Example / answer: The article examines climate policy.

At the level of

Rule / explanation: Avoid literal translation of au niveau de when no physical level is meant.

Example / answer: Use in, regarding or from the perspective of.

In a first time

Rule / explanation: Use initially, first or at first according to meaning.

Example / answer: Initially, the proposal attracted broad support.

Make a focus on

Rule / explanation: Use focus on or examine.

Example / answer: The article focuses on rural inequality.

Explain us

Rule / explanation: Explain takes the thing directly and the person with to.

Example / answer: The author explains the mechanism to the reader.

Informations

Rule / explanation: Information is uncountable.

Example / answer: The report provides useful information.

V. Exercises and model corrections

1. Remove empty phrasing

Rule / explanation: It is important to note that the reform was unsuccessful.

Example / answer: The reform was unsuccessful.

2. Replace a vague verb

Rule / explanation: The figures show a change in public opinion.

Example / answer: The figures indicate a decline in public support.

3. Replace a nominalisation

Rule / explanation: The committee carried out an evaluation of the programme.

Example / answer: The committee evaluated the programme.

4. Make the comparison precise

Rule / explanation: The second policy was better.

Example / answer: The second policy was more effective at reducing rural waiting times.

5. Correct overgeneralisation

Rule / explanation: Young people always get their news online.

Example / answer: Young adults increasingly obtain news from online platforms.

6. Improve reference

Rule / explanation: Funding fell. This created problems.

Example / answer: Funding fell. This reduction created staffing shortages.

7. Translate

Rule / explanation: Le rapport est convaincant, mais sa conclusion est plus générale que ne le permettent les données.

Example / answer: The report is convincing, but its conclusion is broader than the evidence allows.

8. Mini concours challenge

Rule / explanation: Rewrite the paragraph concisely while preserving its qualifications.

Example / answer: Wordy: It is important to note the fact that the policy may possibly have had a certain number of negative effects on some rural areas. Model: The policy may have harmed some rural areas.

9. Precision challenge

Rule / explanation: Improve: The article says that a lot of people had a bad reaction to the new thing.

Example / answer: The article reports that many rural residents strongly opposed the new funding measure.

10. Full correction

Rule / explanation: Improve: Due to the fact that the government made the decision to reduce funding, this had a big impact on hospitals.

Example / answer: Because the government reduced funding, hospitals experienced substantial staffing shortages.
