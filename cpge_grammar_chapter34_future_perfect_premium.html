The golden rule

will have + past participle
By 2030, the government will have introduced several reforms.
Main useUse the Future Perfect for an action that will be completed before a specific future time.
CPGE habitUse it for projections, deadlines and policy targets: By the next election, public trust may have declined further.
Key trapAfter time expressions such as by the time, use Present Simple in the time clause: By the time voters go to the polls...

A. Lesson — What is the Future Perfect?

Completed before a future point

French: achevé avant un moment futur

Rule / explanation: The Future Perfect looks forward to a future moment and sees an action as completed before it.

Example / answer: By next year, Parliament will have passed the bill.

Future deadline

French: échéance future

Rule / explanation: It is common with deadlines and targets.

Example / answer: By 2050, the country will have reduced emissions significantly.

Projection

French: projection

Rule / explanation: Use it to describe what may be completed according to forecasts.

Example / answer: By the end of the decade, housing costs will have risen further.

Analytical value

French: valeur analytique

Rule / explanation: It helps discuss long-term consequences and future outcomes.

Example / answer: If no action is taken, inequality will have widened by the next generation.

B. Form and structure

Affirmative

French: forme affirmative

Rule / explanation: Use will have + past participle for all subjects.

Example / answer: The government will have completed the reform by 2028.

Negative

French: forme négative

Rule / explanation: Use will not have / won’t have + past participle.

Example / answer: The policy will not have solved the housing crisis by then.

Question

French: question

Rule / explanation: Put will before the subject.

Example / answer: Will the government have met its targets by 2030?

Wh-question

French: question en wh-

Rule / explanation: Use wh-word + will + subject + have + past participle.

Example / answer: What will the reform have achieved by the next election?

C. Time markers with Future Perfect

By + time

French: d’ici / avant

Rule / explanation: By means not later than a future point.

Example / answer: By 2035, the population will have changed significantly.

By the time + Present Simple

French: d’ici à ce que

Rule / explanation: In future time clauses, use Present Simple, not will.

Example / answer: By the time voters go to the polls, the crisis will have deepened.

By then

French: d’ici là

Rule / explanation: By then refers back to a future point already mentioned.

Example / answer: The next election is in 2029. By then, the party will have changed leaders.

Within + period

French: dans un délai de

Rule / explanation: Within focuses on completion inside a period.

Example / answer: Within five years, the reform will have affected millions of households.

D. Future Perfect vs Future Simple

Future Simple

French: futur simple

Rule / explanation: Use will + verb for a future action or prediction.

Example / answer: The reform will reduce costs.

Future Perfect

French: futur antérieur

Rule / explanation: Use will have + past participle to emphasise completion before a future point.

Example / answer: By 2030, the reform will have reduced costs.

Prediction vs completed projection

French: prédiction vs bilan futur

Rule / explanation: Future Perfect sounds like looking back from a future date.

Example / answer: In ten years, analysts will see that the crisis will have reshaped politics.

Use sparingly

French: usage mesuré

Rule / explanation: Do not use it if ordinary future is clearer.

Example / answer: Simple: The policy will fail. More precise: By 2028, the policy will have failed to meet its targets.

E. Future Perfect with modals

May have + past participle

French: il se peut que... ait

Rule / explanation: Use may have for uncertain future completion.

Example / answer: By then, inequality may have increased further.

Might have + past participle

French: pourrait avoir

Rule / explanation: Might have is more tentative than may have.

Example / answer: By 2030, the policy might have improved access to education.

Could have + past participle

French: pourrait avoir

Rule / explanation: Could have can express possible future completion.

Example / answer: Within a decade, the reform could have transformed public services.

Should have + past participle

French: devrait avoir

Rule / explanation: Should have can express expected completion by a future point.

Example / answer: By next year, the committee should have published its report.

F. CPGE policy, climate and data patterns

Policy target

French: objectif politique

Rule / explanation: Use it to discuss whether goals will be achieved by a deadline.

Example / answer: By 2030, the government will have missed several climate targets.

Long-term consequence

French: conséquence à long terme

Rule / explanation: Use it to imagine the future result of present choices.

Example / answer: By the next generation, housing inequality will have become even more entrenched.

Data projection

French: projection chiffrée

Rule / explanation: Useful for statistics and forecasts.

Example / answer: By 2040, the share of elderly voters will have increased.

Article conclusion

French: conclusion d’article

Rule / explanation: Use it to express what may be clear later.

Example / answer: In a few years, this crisis may have changed the political landscape.

G. Typical mistakes by French speakers

Will in time clauses

French: pas de will après by the time

Rule / explanation: Use Present Simple in future time clauses.

Example / answer: Wrong: By the time voters will decide. Correct: By the time voters decide.

Forgetting have

French: oubli de have

Rule / explanation: Future Perfect needs will have + past participle.

Example / answer: Wrong: will reduced. Correct: will have reduced.

Wrong participle

French: mauvais participe passé

Rule / explanation: Use the past participle after have.

Example / answer: Wrong: will have went. Correct: will have gone.

Overusing Future Perfect

French: emploi excessif

Rule / explanation: Use Future Simple if completion before a future point is not important.

Example / answer: Better: The policy will affect voters. Not always: will have affected voters.
