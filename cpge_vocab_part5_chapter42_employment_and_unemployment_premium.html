Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse employment and unemployment: labour markets, job creation, labour shortages, contracts, precarious work, wages, trade unions, automation, reskilling, youth unemployment, long-term unemployment, workplace discrimination and the future of work.

How to use this chapter

For press articlesFocus on terms such as unemployment rate, labour shortage, precarious work, zero-hours contract, gig worker, minimum wage, strike, automation, reskilling and youth unemployment.

A. Core employment and labour-market vocabulary

employment

IPA: /ɪmˈplɔɪmənt/
Pronunciation: im-PLOY-muhnt
French: emploi

Definition: The state of having paid work.

Example: Employment is central to income, dignity and social inclusion.

unemployment

IPA: /ˌʌnɪmˈplɔɪmənt/
Pronunciation: un-im-PLOY-muhnt
French: chômage

Definition: The state of being without a job while available for work.

Example: Unemployment can damage both economic growth and mental health.

unemployment rate

IPA: /ˌʌnɪmˈplɔɪmənt reɪt/
Pronunciation: un-im-PLOY-muhnt rayt
French: taux de chômage

Definition: The percentage of the labour force that is unemployed.

Example: The unemployment rate is closely watched by governments and economists.

joblessness

IPA: /ˈdʒɒbləsnəs/
Pronunciation: JOB-luhs-nuhs
French: absence d’emploi ; chômage

Definition: The condition of having no paid job.

Example: Long-term joblessness can lead to social exclusion.

labour market

IPA: /ˈleɪbə ˌmɑːkɪt/
Pronunciation: LAY-buh MAR-kit
French: marché du travail

Definition: The system in which workers seek jobs and employers seek workers.

Example: The labour market has been transformed by automation and remote work.

workforce

IPA: /ˈwɜːkfɔːs/
Pronunciation: WURK-force
French: population active ; main-d’œuvre

Definition: All the people available for work in an economy or organisation.

Example: An ageing workforce can create labour shortages.

labour force

IPA: /ˈleɪbə fɔːs/
Pronunciation: LAY-buh force
French: population active

Definition: People who are working or actively looking for work.

Example: Students and retirees are often outside the labour force.

labour force participation

IPA: /ˈleɪbə fɔːs pɑːˌtɪsɪˈpeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: LAY-buh force par-tis-i-PAY-shuhn
French: taux d’activité

Definition: The share of working-age people who are working or looking for work.

Example: Higher labour force participation can support economic growth.

working-age population

IPA: /ˌwɜːkɪŋ eɪdʒ ˌpɒpjʊˈleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: WUR-king-ayj pop-yuh-LAY-shuhn
French: population en âge de travailler

Definition: People old enough and young enough to be considered available for work.

Example: The working-age population is shrinking in many ageing societies.

economically active

IPA: /ˌiːkəˈnɒmɪkli ˈæktɪv/
Pronunciation: ee-kuh-NOM-ik-lee AK-tiv
French: actif économiquement

Definition: Working or actively seeking work.

Example: Economically active people include both employed and unemployed workers.

economically inactive

IPA: /ˌiːkəˈnɒmɪkli ɪnˈæktɪv/
Pronunciation: ee-kuh-NOM-ik-lee in-AK-tiv
French: inactif économiquement

Definition: Not working and not actively seeking work.

Example: Some people are economically inactive because of illness, study or caring duties.

job creation

IPA: /ˈdʒɒb kriˌeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: job kree-AY-shuhn
French: création d’emplois

Definition: The process of creating new jobs.

Example: Job creation is a major political promise.

job destruction

IPA: /ˈdʒɒb dɪˌstrʌkʃən/
Pronunciation: job di-STRUK-shuhn
French: destruction d’emplois

Definition: The disappearance of jobs, often through crisis, automation or restructuring.

Example: Automation can cause job destruction in routine occupations.

net job creation

IPA: /ˌnet ˈdʒɒb kriˌeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: net job kree-AY-shuhn
French: création nette d’emplois

Definition: The number of jobs created minus the number of jobs lost.

Example: Net job creation can hide losses in specific sectors.

full employment

IPA: /ˌfʊl ɪmˈplɔɪmənt/
Pronunciation: full im-PLOY-muhnt
French: plein emploi

Definition: A situation where nearly everyone who wants work can find it.

Example: Full employment does not necessarily mean every job is secure or well-paid.

underemployment

IPA: /ˌʌndərɪmˈplɔɪmənt/
Pronunciation: UN-duh-rim-PLOY-muhnt
French: sous-emploi

Definition: Working fewer hours or in a lower-skilled job than desired.

Example: Underemployment is common among young graduates.

overemployment

IPA: /ˌəʊvərɪmˈplɔɪmənt/
Pronunciation: OH-vuh-rim-PLOY-muhnt
French: suremploi ; surcharge de travail

Definition: Working more hours than one wants or can sustainably manage.

Example: Overemployment can contribute to burnout.

labour shortage

IPA: /ˈleɪbə ˌʃɔːtɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: LAY-buh SHOR-tij
French: pénurie de main-d’œuvre

Definition: A lack of enough workers to fill available jobs.

Example: Healthcare and hospitality have faced labour shortages.

skills shortage

IPA: /ˈskɪlz ˌʃɔːtɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: skilz SHOR-tij
French: pénurie de compétences

Definition: A lack of workers with the skills employers need.

Example: Skills shortages can slow economic growth.

skills gap

IPA: /ˈskɪlz ɡæp/
Pronunciation: skilz gap
French: écart de compétences

Definition: A mismatch between workers’ skills and employers’ needs.

Example: The skills gap is often cited in debates on education and training.

labour supply

IPA: /ˈleɪbə səˌplaɪ/
Pronunciation: LAY-buh suh-PLY
French: offre de travail

Definition: The number of workers available and willing to work.

Example: Immigration can increase labour supply.

labour demand

IPA: /ˈleɪbə dɪˌmɑːnd/
Pronunciation: LAY-buh di-MAND
French: demande de travail

Definition: Employers’ demand for workers.

Example: Labour demand falls during recessions.

vacancy

IPA: /ˈveɪkənsi/
Pronunciation: VAY-kuhn-see
French: poste vacant

Definition: An unfilled job position.

Example: A high number of vacancies may indicate a tight labour market.

job vacancy

IPA: /ˈdʒɒb ˌveɪkənsi/
Pronunciation: job VAY-kuhn-see
French: offre d’emploi ; poste vacant

Definition: An available job that an employer wants to fill.

Example: Job vacancies rose after the pandemic.

tight labour market

IPA: /ˌtaɪt ˈleɪbə ˌmɑːkɪt/
Pronunciation: tyte LAY-buh MAR-kit
French: marché du travail tendu

Definition: A labour market with low unemployment and many vacancies.

Example: A tight labour market can push wages up.

slack labour market

IPA: /ˌslæk ˈleɪbə ˌmɑːkɪt/
Pronunciation: slak LAY-buh MAR-kit
French: marché du travail détendu

Definition: A labour market with high unemployment or weak demand for workers.

Example: A slack labour market weakens workers’ bargaining power.

jobseeker

IPA: /ˈdʒɒbˌsiːkə/
Pronunciation: JOB-see-kuh
French: demandeur d’emploi

Definition: A person looking for a job.

Example: Jobseekers may need training and support.

applicant

IPA: /ˈæplɪkənt/
Pronunciation: AP-li-kuhnt
French: candidat ; candidate

Definition: A person applying for a job.

Example: Employers received hundreds of applicants for the position.

candidate

IPA: /ˈkændɪdət/
Pronunciation: KAN-di-duht
French: candidat ; candidate

Definition: A person being considered for a job or position.

Example: The strongest candidate had both experience and soft skills.

employer

IPA: /ɪmˈplɔɪə/
Pronunciation: im-PLOY-uh
French: employeur

Definition: A person or organisation that gives people work.

Example: Employers complain about skills shortages.

employee

IPA: /ɪmˈplɔɪiː/
Pronunciation: im-PLOY-ee
French: salarié ; employé

Definition: A person who works for an employer.

Example: Employees expect fair pay and safe conditions.

worker

IPA: /ˈwɜːkə/
Pronunciation: WUR-kuh
French: travailleur ; travailleuse

Definition: A person who works, especially for wages.

Example: Workers have demanded better pay during inflation.

labourer

IPA: /ˈleɪbərə/
Pronunciation: LAY-buh-ruh
French: ouvrier ; manœuvre

Definition: A person doing manual work, often physically demanding.

Example: Labourers are often exposed to insecure working conditions.

white-collar worker

IPA: /ˌwaɪt ˈkɒlə ˈwɜːkə/
Pronunciation: white KOL-uh WUR-kuh
French: col blanc

Definition: A worker in office, professional or administrative employment.

Example: White-collar workers increasingly use digital tools.

blue-collar worker

IPA: /ˌbluː ˈkɒlə ˈwɜːkə/
Pronunciation: blue KOL-uh WUR-kuh
French: col bleu ; ouvrier

Definition: A worker doing manual, industrial or trade work.

Example: Blue-collar workers were strongly affected by deindustrialisation.

B. Types of work, contracts and working conditions

pink-collar worker

IPA: /ˌpɪŋk ˈkɒlə ˈwɜːkə/
Pronunciation: pink KOL-uh WUR-kuh
French: emploi féminisé ; travailleur du care

Definition: A worker in care, service or traditionally feminised jobs.

Example: Pink-collar workers are often underpaid despite essential roles.

self-employed

IPA: /ˌself ɪmˈplɔɪd/
Pronunciation: self-im-PLOYD
French: indépendant ; à son compte

Definition: Working for oneself rather than for an employer.

Example: Self-employed workers may lack social protection.

freelancer

IPA: /ˈfriːˌlɑːnsə/
Pronunciation: FREE-lan-suh
French: travailleur indépendant ; freelance

Definition: A self-employed person working for different clients.

Example: Freelancers have flexibility but irregular income.

contractor

IPA: /kənˈtræktə/
Pronunciation: kuhn-TRAK-tuh
French: prestataire ; contractuel

Definition: A person or company hired to perform specific work under contract.

Example: Contractors may not receive the same benefits as employees.

gig worker

IPA: /ˈɡɪɡ ˌwɜːkə/
Pronunciation: gig WUR-kuh
French: travailleur de plateforme ; gig worker

Definition: A worker doing short-term tasks through platforms or temporary arrangements.

Example: Gig workers often lack job security.

platform worker

IPA: /ˈplætfɔːm ˌwɜːkə/
Pronunciation: PLAT-form WUR-kuh
French: travailleur de plateforme

Definition: A worker whose work is organised through a digital platform.

Example: Platform workers have fought for employee rights.

zero-hours contract

IPA: /ˌzɪərəʊ aʊəz ˈkɒntrækt/
Pronunciation: ZEER-oh-hours KON-trakt
French: contrat zéro heure

Definition: A contract with no guaranteed working hours.

Example: Zero-hours contracts offer flexibility but create insecurity.

temporary contract

IPA: /ˈtempərəri ˈkɒntrækt/
Pronunciation: TEM-puh-ruh-ree KON-trakt
French: contrat temporaire

Definition: A work contract for a limited period.

Example: Temporary contracts are common among young workers.

fixed-term contract

IPA: /ˌfɪkst tɜːm ˈkɒntrækt/
Pronunciation: fikst-term KON-trakt
French: contrat à durée déterminée

Definition: A contract ending on a specified date.

Example: Fixed-term contracts may reduce job security.

permanent contract

IPA: /ˈpɜːmənənt ˈkɒntrækt/
Pronunciation: PUR-muh-nuhnt KON-trakt
French: contrat à durée indéterminée

Definition: A contract without a fixed end date.

Example: A permanent contract gives workers more stability.

part-time work

IPA: /ˌpɑːt taɪm ˈwɜːk/
Pronunciation: part-time work
French: travail à temps partiel

Definition: Work for fewer hours than a full-time job.

Example: Part-time work is often linked to care responsibilities.

full-time work

IPA: /ˌfʊl taɪm ˈwɜːk/
Pronunciation: full-time work
French: travail à temps plein

Definition: Work for the standard number of hours in a week.

Example: Full-time work does not always guarantee a living wage.

shift work

IPA: /ˈʃɪft wɜːk/
Pronunciation: shift work
French: travail posté

Definition: Work organised in shifts, often outside regular office hours.

Example: Shift work can disrupt sleep and family life.

night shift

IPA: /ˈnaɪt ʃɪft/
Pronunciation: nyte shift
French: travail de nuit

Definition: A period of work during the night.

Example: Night shifts are common in hospitals and logistics.

remote work

IPA: /rɪˌməʊt ˈwɜːk/
Pronunciation: ri-MOHT work
French: travail à distance

Definition: Work done away from the employer’s premises, often from home.

Example: Remote work expanded rapidly after the pandemic.

working from home

IPA: /ˌwɜːkɪŋ frəm ˈhəʊm/
Pronunciation: WUR-king from hohm
French: télétravail ; travail à domicile

Definition: Doing one’s job from home rather than the workplace.

Example: Working from home can improve flexibility but blur boundaries.

hybrid work

IPA: /ˌhaɪbrɪd ˈwɜːk/
Pronunciation: HY-brid work
French: travail hybride

Definition: A work arrangement combining remote and workplace-based work.

Example: Hybrid work has changed office culture.

flexible working

IPA: /ˌfleksəbəl ˈwɜːkɪŋ/
Pronunciation: FLEK-suh-buhl WUR-king
French: travail flexible

Definition: Work arrangements with flexibility in hours or location.

Example: Flexible working can support parents and carers.

working conditions

IPA: /ˈwɜːkɪŋ kənˌdɪʃənz/
Pronunciation: WUR-king kuhn-DISH-uhnz
French: conditions de travail

Definition: The environment, hours, pay and treatment experienced at work.

Example: Poor working conditions can cause burnout and turnover.

job security

IPA: /ˈdʒɒb sɪˌkjʊərəti/
Pronunciation: job si-KYOOR-uh-tee
French: sécurité de l’emploi

Definition: Confidence that one will keep one’s job.

Example: Job security has declined in many sectors.

job insecurity

IPA: /ˈdʒɒb ˌɪnsɪˈkjʊərəti/
Pronunciation: job in-si-KYOOR-uh-tee
French: insécurité de l’emploi

Definition: Fear or risk of losing one’s job or income.

Example: Job insecurity affects mental health.

precarious work

IPA: /prɪˌkeəriəs ˈwɜːk/
Pronunciation: pri-KAIR-ee-uhs work
French: travail précaire

Definition: Work that is insecure, poorly paid or lacking protection.

Example: Precarious work is common in the gig economy.

precarity

IPA: /prɪˈkeərəti/
Pronunciation: pri-KAIR-uh-tee
French: précarité

Definition: Insecurity and instability in work or life conditions.

Example: Precarity has become a defining issue for younger workers.

informal work

IPA: /ɪnˌfɔːməl ˈwɜːk/
Pronunciation: in-FOR-muhl work
French: travail informel

Definition: Work not fully regulated, taxed or protected by labour law.

Example: Informal work provides income but little legal protection.

informal economy

IPA: /ɪnˌfɔːməl iˈkɒnəmi/
Pronunciation: in-FOR-muhl i-KON-uh-mee
French: économie informelle

Definition: Economic activity outside formal regulation and taxation.

Example: The informal economy is large in many developing countries.

black market work

IPA: /ˌblæk ˈmɑːkɪt wɜːk/
Pronunciation: black MAR-kit work
French: travail au noir

Definition: Illegal or undeclared work.

Example: Black market work deprives the state of tax revenue.

undeclared work

IPA: /ˌʌndɪˈkleəd ˈwɜːk/
Pronunciation: un-di-KLAIRD work
French: travail non déclaré

Definition: Work not declared to tax or social security authorities.

Example: Undeclared work leaves workers vulnerable.

minimum wage

IPA: /ˌmɪnɪməm ˈweɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: MIN-i-muhm wayj
French: salaire minimum

Definition: The lowest legal hourly or monthly wage.

Example: Raising the minimum wage can reduce in-work poverty.

living wage

IPA: /ˈlɪvɪŋ weɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: LIV-ing wayj
French: salaire décent

Definition: A wage high enough to cover basic living costs.

Example: A living wage is higher than some legal minimum wages.

low-paid work

IPA: /ˌləʊ peɪd ˈwɜːk/
Pronunciation: loh-paid work
French: travail mal rémunéré

Definition: Work that pays poorly.

Example: Low-paid work is common in care and hospitality.

in-work poverty

IPA: /ˌɪn wɜːk ˈpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: in-work POV-uh-tee
French: pauvreté laborieuse

Definition: Poverty affecting people who are employed.

Example: In-work poverty proves that employment alone is not enough.

wage stagnation

IPA: /ˈweɪdʒ stæɡˌneɪʃən/
Pronunciation: wayj stag-NAY-shuhn
French: stagnation des salaires

Definition: A period when wages barely increase.

Example: Wage stagnation has fuelled political anger.

real wages

IPA: /ˌrɪəl ˈweɪdʒɪz/
Pronunciation: REE-uhl WAY-jiz
French: salaires réels

Definition: Wages adjusted for inflation.

Example: Real wages fall when inflation exceeds pay rises.

pay rise

IPA: /ˈpeɪ raɪz/
Pronunciation: pay ryze
French: augmentation de salaire

Definition: An increase in wages or salary.

Example: Workers demanded a pay rise during the cost-of-living crisis.

gender pay gap

IPA: /ˌdʒendə ˈpeɪ ɡæp/
Pronunciation: JEN-duh pay gap
French: écart salarial entre femmes et hommes

Definition: The difference between men’s and women’s average earnings.

Example: The gender pay gap reflects both discrimination and unequal care work.

collective bargaining

IPA: /kəˌlektɪv ˈbɑːɡɪnɪŋ/
Pronunciation: kuh-LEK-tiv BAR-gi-ning
French: négociation collective

Definition: Negotiation between employers and workers’ representatives.

Example: Collective bargaining can strengthen workers’ pay and rights.

trade union

IPA: /ˌtreɪd ˈjuːniən/
Pronunciation: trade YOO-nee-uhn
French: syndicat

Definition: An organisation representing workers’ interests.

Example: Trade unions campaign for better pay and working conditions.

unionisation

IPA: /ˌjuːniənaɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: YOO-nee-uh-ny-ZAY-shuhn
French: syndicalisation

Definition: The process or level of workers joining unions.

Example: Unionisation rates have declined in many countries.

C. Pay, rights, unions and workplace protection

strike

IPA: /straɪk/
Pronunciation: stryke
French: grève

Definition: A work stoppage used to demand better pay or conditions.

Example: Strikes increased during the cost-of-living crisis.

industrial action

IPA: /ɪnˌdʌstriəl ˈækʃən/
Pronunciation: in-DUS-tree-uhl AK-shuhn
French: action syndicale ; mouvement social

Definition: Action by workers, such as strikes, to pressure employers.

Example: Industrial action can disrupt transport, schools and hospitals.

workplace rights

IPA: /ˈwɜːkpleɪs raɪts/
Pronunciation: WURK-place rites
French: droits au travail

Definition: Legal and social rights at work.

Example: Workplace rights include safety, pay and protection from discrimination.

labour rights

IPA: /ˈleɪbə raɪts/
Pronunciation: LAY-buh rites
French: droits des travailleurs

Definition: Rights protecting workers’ pay, conditions and freedom to organise.

Example: Labour rights are often weaker in informal work.

employment law

IPA: /ɪmˈplɔɪmənt lɔː/
Pronunciation: im-PLOY-muhnt law
French: droit du travail

Definition: Law governing work, hiring, dismissal and workplace rights.

Example: Employment law protects workers from unfair dismissal.

labour law

IPA: /ˈleɪbə lɔː/
Pronunciation: LAY-buh law
French: droit du travail

Definition: Law concerning workers, unions and employment relations.

Example: Labour law varies sharply between countries.

unfair dismissal

IPA: /ˌʌnfeə dɪsˈmɪsəl/
Pronunciation: un-FAIR dis-MISS-uhl
French: licenciement abusif

Definition: Losing a job without a fair or lawful reason.

Example: Unfair dismissal laws protect employees from arbitrary decisions.

redundancy

IPA: /rɪˈdʌndənsi/
Pronunciation: ri-DUN-duhn-see
French: licenciement économique

Definition: Job loss because a role is no longer needed.

Example: Redundancy can follow restructuring or automation.

layoff

IPA: /ˈleɪɒf/
Pronunciation: LAY-off
French: licenciement

Definition: A job loss, usually because of economic conditions or restructuring.

Example: Tech companies announced large layoffs.

downsizing

IPA: /ˈdaʊnsaɪzɪŋ/
Pronunciation: DOWN-sy-zing
French: réduction d’effectifs

Definition: Reducing the number of workers in a company.

Example: Downsizing can raise profits but damage morale.

outsourcing

IPA: /ˈaʊtsɔːsɪŋ/
Pronunciation: OWT-sor-sing
French: externalisation ; sous-traitance

Definition: Contracting work to another company, sometimes abroad.

Example: Outsourcing can reduce costs but weaken job security.

offshoring

IPA: /ˌɒfˈʃɔːrɪŋ/
Pronunciation: OFF-shor-ing
French: délocalisation

Definition: Moving business activity to another country.

Example: Offshoring has contributed to job losses in manufacturing.

reshoring

IPA: /ˌriːˈʃɔːrɪŋ/
Pronunciation: ree-SHOR-ing
French: relocalisation

Definition: Bringing production back to the home country.

Example: Reshoring is promoted as a way to rebuild industrial jobs.

automation

IPA: /ˌɔːtəˈmeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: aw-tuh-MAY-shuhn
French: automatisation

Definition: Technology performing tasks previously done by humans.

Example: Automation can destroy some jobs while creating others.

robotics

IPA: /rəʊˈbɒtɪks/
Pronunciation: roh-BOT-iks
French: robotique

Definition: The design and use of robots.

Example: Robotics affects manufacturing, logistics and care.

artificial intelligence

IPA: /ˌɑːtɪfɪʃəl ɪnˈtelɪdʒəns/
Pronunciation: ar-ti-FISH-uhl in-TEL-i-juhns
French: intelligence artificielle

Definition: Computer systems able to perform tasks requiring human intelligence.

Example: Artificial intelligence may transform white-collar work.

AI-driven automation

IPA: /ˌeɪ ˈaɪ ˌdrɪvən ˌɔːtəˈmeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: ay-eye DRIV-uhn aw-tuh-MAY-shuhn
French: automatisation par l’IA

Definition: Automation powered by artificial intelligence.

Example: AI-driven automation threatens some routine cognitive jobs.

technological unemployment

French: chômage technologique

Definition: Unemployment caused by technology replacing workers.

job displacement

IPA: /ˈdʒɒb dɪsˌpleɪsmənt/
Pronunciation: job dis-PLAYS-muhnt
French: destruction ou déplacement d’emplois

Definition: Workers losing jobs because of economic or technological change.

Example: Job displacement requires retraining and support.

labour-saving technology

IPA: /ˈleɪbə ˌseɪvɪŋ tekˈnɒlədʒi/
Pronunciation: LAY-buh SAY-ving tek-NOL-uh-jee
French: technologie économisant la main-d’œuvre

digitalisation

IPA: /ˌdɪdʒɪtəlaɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: DIJ-i-tuh-ly-ZAY-shuhn
French: numérisation

Definition: The spread of digital technologies in work and society.

Example: Digitalisation changes the skills workers need.

reskilling

IPA: /ˌriːˈskɪlɪŋ/
Pronunciation: ree-SKIL-ing
