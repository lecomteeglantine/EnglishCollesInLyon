Part 8 — Environment | Chapter 73 — Climate Migration
Essential vocabulary for khôlles, essays, press reviews and competitive exams.

Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse climate migration: displacement, climate migrants, legal gaps, sea-level rise, drought, floods, disaster risk, adaptation, resilience, planned relocation, host communities, climate justice, refugee law, small island states, loss and damage, securitisation and the challenge of protecting people without turning migration into a threat narrative.

How to use this chapter

For press articlesFocus on terms such as climate displacement, planned relocation, slow-onset disaster, trapped population, migration as adaptation, protection gap, small island developing states, loss and damage and securitisation of migration.
For oral examsUse this vocabulary to explain climate migration as a human, legal, geopolitical and moral issue: people move not only because of disasters, but because livelihoods, homes and rights become impossible to protect.
For essaysMemorise the collocations: to flee a climate disaster, to relocate communities, to adapt in place, to create a protection gap, to manage migration humanely.

A. Core climate migration vocabulary

climate migration

IPA: /ˈklaɪmət maɪˌɡreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: KLY-muht my-GRAY-shuhn
French: migration climatique

Definition: The movement of people linked to climate change and environmental pressures.

Example: Climate migration can be temporary, permanent, internal or cross-border.

climate migrant

IPA: /ˈklaɪmət ˌmaɪɡrənt/
Pronunciation: KLY-muht MY-gruhnt
French: migrant climatique

Definition: A person who moves partly or mainly because of climate-related factors.

Example: The term climate migrant covers many different situations.

environmental migration

IPA: /ɪnˌvaɪrənˈmentəl maɪˌɡreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: in-vy-ruhn-MEN-tuhl my-GRAY-shuhn
French: migration environnementale

Definition: Migration caused or influenced by environmental change.

Example: Environmental migration is not always caused by climate change alone.

environmental migrant

IPA: /ɪnˌvaɪrənˈmentəl ˌmaɪɡrənt/
Pronunciation: in-vy-ruhn-MEN-tuhl MY-gruhnt
French: migrant environnemental

Definition: A person who moves because environmental conditions affect life or livelihood.

Example: Environmental migrants may move because of drought, floods or land degradation.

climate refugee

IPA: /ˈklaɪmət ˌrefjʊˈdʒiː/
Pronunciation: KLY-muht REF-yoo-jee
French: réfugié climatique

Definition: A widely used but legally contested term for someone displaced by climate impacts.

Example: Climate refugee is powerful politically, but it is not a formal legal status in most contexts.

environmental refugee

IPA: /ɪnˌvaɪrənˈmentəl ˌrefjʊˈdʒiː/
Pronunciation: in-vy-ruhn-MEN-tuhl REF-yoo-jee
French: réfugié environnemental

Definition: A contested term for a person forced to move because of environmental harm.

Example: Environmental refugee is often used in media debates.

displacement

IPA: /dɪsˈpleɪsmənt/
Pronunciation: dis-PLAYS-muhnt
French: déplacement forcé

Definition: Forced movement from home because of danger, crisis or loss of livelihood.

Example: Displacement can follow floods, storms or conflict.

climate displacement

IPA: /ˈklaɪmət dɪsˌpleɪsmənt/
Pronunciation: KLY-muht dis-PLAYS-muhnt
French: déplacement climatique

Definition: Forced movement caused or worsened by climate impacts.

Example: Climate displacement is expected to increase in vulnerable regions.

internal displacement

IPA: /ɪnˌtɜːnəl dɪsˈpleɪsmənt/
Pronunciation: in-TUR-nuhl dis-PLAYS-muhnt
French: déplacement interne

Definition: Forced movement within a country’s borders.

Example: Most climate-related displacement is internal.

internally displaced person

IPA: /ɪnˌtɜːnəli dɪsˌpleɪst ˈpɜːsən/
Pronunciation: in-TUR-nuh-lee dis-PLAYST PUR-suhn
French: personne déplacée interne

Definition: A person forced to move within their own country.

Example: Internally displaced people may not receive international protection.

IDP

IPA: /ˌaɪ diː ˈpiː/
Pronunciation: eye-dee-pee
French: PDI

Definition: Abbreviation for internally displaced person.

Example: IDPs often need shelter, food and legal assistance.

cross-border migration

IPA: /ˌkrɒs bɔːdə maɪˈɡreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: cross-BOR-duh my-GRAY-shuhn
French: migration transfrontalière

Definition: Movement from one country to another.

Example: Cross-border migration raises legal and diplomatic questions.

forced migration

IPA: /ˌfɔːst maɪˈɡreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: forst my-GRAY-shuhn
French: migration forcée

Definition: Movement caused by coercion, danger or lack of viable alternatives.

Example: Forced migration can result from war, persecution or environmental collapse.

voluntary migration

IPA: /ˈvɒləntəri maɪˌɡreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: VOL-uhn-tuh-ree my-GRAY-shuhn
French: migration volontaire

Definition: Migration chosen by individuals, though often under pressure.

Example: The line between voluntary and forced migration can be blurred.

planned relocation

IPA: /ˌplænd ˌriːləʊˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: pland ree-loh-KAY-shuhn
French: réinstallation planifiée

Definition: Organised movement of communities away from high-risk areas.

Example: Planned relocation can protect lives but may damage identity and livelihoods.

relocation

IPA: /ˌriːləʊˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: ree-loh-KAY-shuhn
French: réinstallation ; relocalisation

Definition: Moving people or communities to another place.

Example: Relocation is difficult when communities have deep ties to land.

resettlement

IPA: /ˌriːˈsetəlmənt/
Pronunciation: ree-SET-uhl-muhnt
French: réinstallation

Definition: The process of settling people in a new place after displacement.

Example: Resettlement requires housing, jobs and social support.

evacuation

IPA: /ɪˌvækjuˈeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: i-vak-yoo-AY-shuhn
French: évacuation

Definition: Moving people away from immediate danger.

Example: Evacuation saves lives during hurricanes and wildfires.

temporary migration

IPA: /ˌtempərəri maɪˈɡreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: TEM-puh-ruh-ree my-GRAY-shuhn
French: migration temporaire

Definition: Migration for a limited period before returning or moving on.

Example: Temporary migration can help families cope with drought.

permanent migration

IPA: /ˌpɜːmənənt maɪˈɡreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: PUR-muh-nuhnt my-GRAY-shuhn
French: migration permanente

Definition: Migration intended to last indefinitely.

Example: Sea-level rise may force permanent migration.

seasonal migration

IPA: /ˌsiːzənəl maɪˈɡreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: SEE-zuh-nuhl my-GRAY-shuhn
French: migration saisonnière

Definition: Migration that happens during particular seasons.

Example: Seasonal migration may increase when farming becomes unreliable.

rural exodus

IPA: /ˌrʊərəl ˈeksədəs/
Pronunciation: ROOR-uhl EK-suh-duhs
French: exode rural

Definition: Large-scale movement from rural areas to cities.

Example: Drought can accelerate rural exodus.

urban migration

IPA: /ˈɜːbən maɪˌɡreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: UR-buhn my-GRAY-shuhn
French: migration urbaine

Definition: Movement of people towards towns and cities.

Example: Climate stress may increase urban migration.

rural-to-urban migration

IPA: /ˌrʊərəl tə ˈɜːbən maɪˌɡreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: ROOR-uhl tuh UR-buhn my-GRAY-shuhn
French: migration rurale-urbaine

Definition: Movement from the countryside to cities.

Example: Rural-to-urban migration can strain housing and infrastructure.

migration pressure

IPA: /maɪˈɡreɪʃən ˌpreʃə/
Pronunciation: my-GRAY-shuhn PRESH-uh
French: pression migratoire

Definition: Factors that increase people’s need or desire to migrate.

Example: Climate impacts can intensify migration pressure.

push factor

IPA: /ˈpʊʃ ˌfæktə/
Pronunciation: push FAK-tuh
French: facteur de répulsion

Definition: A factor that drives people to leave a place.

Example: Drought can be a push factor.

pull factor

IPA: /ˈpʊl ˌfæktə/
Pronunciation: pull FAK-tuh
French: facteur d’attraction

Definition: A factor that attracts people to a new place.

Example: Jobs and safety are pull factors.

livelihood

IPA: /ˈlaɪvlihʊd/
Pronunciation: LYVE-lee-hood
French: moyen de subsistance

Definition: The way people earn a living and meet basic needs.

Example: Climate change threatens rural livelihoods.

loss of livelihood

IPA: /ˌlɒs əv ˈlaɪvlihʊd/
Pronunciation: loss ov LYVE-lee-hood
French: perte de moyens de subsistance

Definition: The loss of income, work or means of survival.

Example: Loss of livelihood can force families to move.

subsistence farming

IPA: /səbˈsɪstəns ˌfɑːmɪŋ/
Pronunciation: suhb-SIS-tuhns FAR-ming
French: agriculture de subsistance

Definition: Farming mainly to feed one’s family rather than sell crops.

Example: Subsistence farming is vulnerable to drought and crop failure.

B. Climate drivers and environmental pressures

crop failure

IPA: /ˈkrɒp ˌfeɪljə/
Pronunciation: crop FAYL-yuh
French: mauvaise récolte ; perte de récolte

Definition: The failure of crops to grow or produce food.

Example: Repeated crop failure can push farmers to migrate.

food insecurity

IPA: /ˈfuːd ˌɪnsɪˈkjʊərəti/
Pronunciation: food in-si-KYOOR-uh-tee
French: insécurité alimentaire

Definition: Lack of reliable access to enough food.

Example: Food insecurity can contribute to displacement.

water scarcity

IPA: /ˈwɔːtə ˌskeəsəti/
Pronunciation: WOR-tuh SKAIR-suh-tee
French: pénurie d’eau

Definition: Lack of sufficient water resources.

Example: Water scarcity can make regions less habitable.

drought

IPA: /draʊt/
Pronunciation: drowt
French: sécheresse

Definition: A long period with too little rainfall.

Example: Drought can destroy crops and force migration.

desertification

IPA: /dɪˌzɜːtɪfɪˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: di-zur-ti-fi-KAY-shuhn
French: désertification

Definition: Land degradation that turns fertile land into desert-like conditions.

Example: Desertification threatens livelihoods in dry regions.

land degradation

IPA: /ˈlænd ˌdeɡrəˈdeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: land deg-ruh-DAY-shuhn
French: dégradation des terres

Definition: Decline in land quality and productivity.

Example: Land degradation can reduce farming income.

soil erosion

IPA: /ˈsɔɪl ɪˌrəʊʒən/
Pronunciation: soil i-ROH-zhuhn
French: érosion des sols

Definition: The loss of fertile soil by wind, water or human activity.

Example: Soil erosion makes agriculture more difficult.

flood

IPA: /flʌd/
Pronunciation: flud
French: inondation

Definition: An overflow of water onto normally dry land.

Example: Floods can displace thousands of people in a few hours.

flash flood

IPA: /ˈflæʃ flʌd/
Pronunciation: flash flud
French: crue soudaine

Definition: A sudden and fast flood.

Example: Flash floods leave little time for evacuation.

coastal flooding

IPA: /ˌkəʊstəl ˈflʌdɪŋ/
Pronunciation: KOH-stuhl FLUD-ing
French: inondation côtière

Definition: Flooding in coastal areas caused by storms or sea-level rise.

Example: Coastal flooding threatens low-lying cities.

sea-level rise

IPA: /ˈsiː levəl raɪz/
Pronunciation: SEE-LEV-uhl ryze
French: élévation du niveau de la mer

Definition: The increase in average ocean level.

Example: Sea-level rise may make some islands uninhabitable.

coastal erosion

IPA: /ˌkəʊstəl ɪˈrəʊʒən/
Pronunciation: KOH-stuhl i-ROH-zhuhn
French: érosion côtière

Definition: The wearing away of land along coasts.

Example: Coastal erosion can force communities to relocate.

storm surge

IPA: /ˈstɔːm sɜːdʒ/
Pronunciation: storm surj
French: onde de tempête

Definition: A rise in sea level caused by a storm pushing water inland.

Example: Storm surges can devastate coastal settlements.

hurricane

IPA: /ˈhʌrɪkən/
Pronunciation: HUR-i-kuhn
French: ouragan

Definition: A powerful tropical storm in the Atlantic or eastern Pacific.

Example: Hurricanes often cause temporary and permanent displacement.

cyclone

IPA: /ˈsaɪkləʊn/
Pronunciation: SY-klohn
French: cyclone

Definition: A powerful rotating storm, especially in the Indian Ocean or South Pacific.

Example: Cyclones can destroy homes and infrastructure.

typhoon

IPA: /taɪˈfuːn/
Pronunciation: ty-FOON
French: typhon

Definition: A powerful tropical storm in the western Pacific.

Example: Typhoons can trigger mass evacuations.

wildfire

IPA: /ˈwaɪldfaɪə/
Pronunciation: WYLD-fire
French: incendie de forêt

Definition: An uncontrolled fire in forests, grasslands or settlements.

Example: Wildfires can force communities to evacuate.

heatwave

IPA: /ˈhiːtweɪv/
Pronunciation: HEET-wave
French: canicule

Definition: A prolonged period of unusually high temperatures.

Example: Extreme heat can make outdoor work dangerous.

extreme weather

IPA: /ɪkˌstriːm ˈweðə/
Pronunciation: ik-STREEM WETH-uh
French: phénomènes météorologiques extrêmes

Definition: Severe weather events such as floods, heatwaves, droughts and storms.

Example: Extreme weather increasingly drives displacement.

slow-onset disaster

IPA: /ˌsləʊ ˈɒnset dɪˌzɑːstə/
Pronunciation: sloh-ON-set di-ZAH-stuh
French: catastrophe à évolution lente

Definition: A gradual crisis such as sea-level rise, desertification or drought.

Example: Slow-onset disasters may push people to migrate over years.

sudden-onset disaster

IPA: /ˌsʌdən ˈɒnset dɪˌzɑːstə/
Pronunciation: SUD-uhn-ON-set di-ZAH-stuh
French: catastrophe soudaine

Definition: A rapid disaster such as a flood, cyclone or wildfire.

Example: Sudden-onset disasters often cause emergency displacement.

natural hazard

IPA: /ˌnætʃərəl ˈhæzəd/
Pronunciation: NAT-chuh-ruhl HAZ-uhd
French: aléa naturel

Definition: A natural process that can threaten people or property.

Example: Climate change can intensify natural hazards.

disaster risk

IPA: /dɪˈzɑːstə rɪsk/
Pronunciation: di-ZAH-stuh risk
French: risque de catastrophe

Definition: The likelihood of harm from hazards combined with vulnerability and exposure.

Example: Disaster risk is higher where infrastructure is weak.

disaster risk reduction

IPA: /dɪˈzɑːstə rɪsk rɪˌdʌkʃən/
Pronunciation: di-ZAH-stuh risk ri-DUK-shuhn
French: réduction des risques de catastrophe

Definition: Measures to reduce harm from disasters.

Example: Disaster risk reduction can prevent displacement.

DRR

IPA: /ˌdiː ɑːr ˈɑː/
Pronunciation: dee-ar-ar
French: RRC

Definition: Abbreviation for disaster risk reduction.

Example: DRR includes early warning systems and safer infrastructure.

early warning system

IPA: /ˌɜːli ˈwɔːnɪŋ ˌsɪstəm/
Pronunciation: UR-lee WOR-ning SIS-tuhm
French: système d’alerte précoce

Definition: A system that warns people before hazards strike.

climate adaptation

IPA: /ˈklaɪmət ˌædæpˈteɪʃən/
Pronunciation: KLY-muht ad-ap-TAY-shuhn
French: adaptation au changement climatique

Definition: Adjusting to climate impacts to reduce harm.

Example: Climate adaptation can reduce forced migration.

adaptation strategy

IPA: /ˌædæpˈteɪʃən ˈstrætədʒi/
Pronunciation: ad-ap-TAY-shuhn STRAT-uh-jee
French: stratégie d’adaptation

Definition: A plan to reduce vulnerability to climate impacts.

Example: Migration can be part of an adaptation strategy.

C. Adaptation, vulnerability and receiving communities

resilience

IPA: /rɪˈzɪliəns/
Pronunciation: ri-ZIL-ee-uhns
French: résilience

Definition: The ability to withstand and recover from shocks.

Example: Resilience reduces the need for emergency displacement.

climate resilience

IPA: /ˈklaɪmət rɪˌzɪliəns/
Pronunciation: KLY-muht ri-ZIL-ee-uhns
French: résilience climatique

Definition: The ability to cope with and recover from climate impacts.

Example: Climate resilience requires infrastructure, finance and social support.

vulnerability

IPA: /ˌvʌlnərəˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: vul-nuh-ruh-BIL-uh-tee
French: vulnérabilité

Definition: Susceptibility to harm because of exposure or limited resources.

Example: Poverty increases vulnerability to climate displacement.

exposure

IPA: /ɪkˈspəʊʒə/
Pronunciation: ik-SPOH-zhuh
French: exposition

Definition: The presence of people or assets in places that could be harmed.

Example: Coastal exposure increases risk from sea-level rise.

adaptive capacity

IPA: /əˌdæptɪv kəˈpæsəti/
Pronunciation: uh-DAP-tiv kuh-PASS-uh-tee
French: capacité d’adaptation

Definition: The ability to adjust to environmental change.

Example: Low adaptive capacity makes migration more likely.

trapped population

IPA: /ˌtræpt pɒpjʊˈleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: trapt pop-yuh-LAY-shuhn
French: population piégée

Definition: People unable to move despite serious risk.

immobility

IPA: /ˌɪməʊˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: im-oh-BIL-uh-tee
French: immobilité

Definition: The condition of not moving, sometimes because movement is impossible.

Pronunciation: my-GRAY-shuhn az ad-ap-TAY-shuhn

managed retreat

IPA: /ˌmænɪdʒd rɪˈtriːt/

French: retrait planifié

Definition: Planned movement away from areas at high environmental risk.
