Part 10 — Concours Toolkit | Chapter 86 — Describing Graphs and Charts
Essential phrases for graph commentary, data description, essays and khôlles.

Chapter objective

This premium toolkit chapter gives CPGE students the language needed to describe graphs, charts and visual data clearly: axes, scales, trends, rises, falls, peaks, fluctuations, comparisons, proportions and cautious interpretation. It is designed for oral graph commentary, press article analysis and essays using figures as evidence.

How to use this chapter

For graph commentaryStart with the overall trend, then mention the most striking change, then compare groups or periods, and finally qualify the interpretation.
For khôllesUse: The graph shows..., What stands out is..., There was a sharp rise..., By contrast..., The data should be treated with caution...
WarningAlways check the scale and the unit. A graph can look dramatic simply because the vertical axis has been cropped.

A. Types of graphs and visual elements

graph

IPA: /ɡrɑːf/
Pronunciation: grahf
French: graphique

Definition: A visual representation of data showing relationships or changes.

Example: The graph shows a steady rise in energy prices.

chart

IPA: /tʃɑːt/
Pronunciation: chart
French: graphique ; diagramme

Definition: A visual display of data using bars, lines or sectors.

Example: The chart compares income levels across regions.

bar chart

IPA: /ˈbɑː tʃɑːt/
Pronunciation: bar chart
French: diagramme en barres

Definition: A chart using bars to compare quantities.

Example: The bar chart compares voting intentions by age.

line graph

IPA: /ˈlaɪn ɡrɑːf/
Pronunciation: line graf
French: graphique en courbe

Definition: A graph using a line to show change over time.

Example: The line graph shows inflation between 2010 and 2025.

pie chart

IPA: /ˈpaɪ tʃɑːt/
Pronunciation: pie chart
French: camembert ; diagramme circulaire

Definition: A circular chart divided into sectors showing proportions.

Example: The pie chart shows the share of household spending.

scatter plot

IPA: /ˈskætə plɒt/
Pronunciation: SKAT-uh plot
French: nuage de points

Definition: A graph using dots to show the relationship between two variables.

Example: The scatter plot suggests a correlation between income and life expectancy.

axis

IPA: /ˈæksɪs/
Pronunciation: AK-sis
French: axe

Definition: A reference line on a graph.

Example: The vertical axis shows the unemployment rate.

x-axis

IPA: /ˈeks ˌæksɪs/
Pronunciation: EX AK-sis
French: axe des abscisses

Definition: The horizontal axis of a graph.

Example: The x-axis represents time.

y-axis

IPA: /ˈwaɪ ˌæksɪs/
Pronunciation: WHY AK-sis
French: axe des ordonnées

Definition: The vertical axis of a graph.

Example: The y-axis shows percentage growth.

scale

IPA: /skeɪl/
Pronunciation: skayl
French: échelle

Definition: The range and intervals used to measure values on a graph.

Example: The scale makes the increase look dramatic.

legend

IPA: /ˈledʒənd/
Pronunciation: LEJ-uhnd
French: légende

Definition: A key explaining symbols, colours or lines on a chart.

Example: The legend identifies each age group.

label

IPA: /ˈleɪbəl/
Pronunciation: LAY-buhl
French: étiquette ; libellé

Definition: A word or phrase identifying part of a graph.

Example: The axis labels are clear.

source

IPA: /sɔːs/
Pronunciation: sorss
French: source

Definition: Where the data come from.

Example: The source of the data is a government survey.

unit

IPA: /ˈjuːnɪt/
Pronunciation: YOO-nit
French: unité

Definition: The measurement used, such as pounds, euros or percentages.

Example: The unit is percentage of total income.

time period

IPA: /ˈtaɪm ˌpɪəriəd/
Pronunciation: time PEER-ee-uhd
French: période

Definition: The dates or years covered by the data.

Example: The time period runs from 1990 to 2024.

data point

IPA: /ˈdeɪtə pɔɪnt/
Pronunciation: DAY-tuh point
French: point de données

Definition: One individual value shown on a graph.

Example: Each data point represents one country.

trend line

IPA: /ˈtrend laɪn/
Pronunciation: trend lyne
French: courbe de tendance

Definition: A line showing the general direction of data.

Example: The trend line points upwards.

baseline

IPA: /ˈbeɪslaɪn/
Pronunciation: BAYSS-lyne
French: niveau de référence

Definition: A starting point used for comparison.

Example: The baseline is the pre-pandemic level.

B. Trends, changes and movement verbs

trend

IPA: /trend/
Pronunciation: trend
French: tendance

Definition: A general direction of change over time.

Example: The trend points to rising political polarisation.

upward trend

IPA: /ˌʌpwəd ˈtrend/
Pronunciation: UP-wuhd trend
French: tendance à la hausse

Definition: A general increase over time.

Example: The graph shows an upward trend in rents.

downward trend

IPA: /ˌdaʊnwəd ˈtrend/
Pronunciation: DOWN-wuhd trend

Example: There is a downward trend in newspaper readership.

stable trend

French: tendance stable

Definition: A pattern with little change over time.

Pronunciation: fluk-choo-AY-shuhn

variation

trough

fall

decline

D. Comparing groups and proportions

compared with

IPA: /kəmˈpeəd wɪð/
Pronunciation: kuhm-PAIRD with
French: comparé à

Definition: Used to compare one value with another.

Example: Compared with 2010, the rate is much higher.

compared to

IPA: /kəmˈpeəd tə/
Pronunciation: kuhm-PAIRD tuh
French: comparé à

Definition: Used to compare one value with another.

Example: Compared to older voters, young voters abstained more.

relative to

IPA: /ˈrelətɪv tə/
Pronunciation: REL-uh-tiv tuh
French: par rapport à

Definition: In relation to another value or standard.

Example: Wages fell relative to housing costs.

whereas

IPA: /ˌweərˈæz/
Pronunciation: wair-AZ
French: alors que ; tandis que

Definition: Used to contrast two values or groups.

Example: Urban support rose, whereas rural support fell.

while

IPA: /waɪl/
Pronunciation: wyle
French: tandis que ; alors que

Definition: Used to compare or contrast.

Example: While rents increased, wages stagnated.

in contrast

IPA: /ɪn ˈkɒntrɑːst/
Pronunciation: in KON-trast
French: en revanche ; par contraste

Definition: Used to introduce a contrasting value or trend.

Example: In contrast, the rate fell among older workers.

by contrast

IPA: /baɪ ˈkɒntrɑːst/
Pronunciation: by KON-trast
French: par contraste

Definition: Used to emphasise difference.

Example: By contrast, rural areas saw little change.

respectively

IPA: /rɪˈspektɪvli/
Pronunciation: ri-SPEK-tiv-lee
French: respectivement

Definition: In the same order as the items already mentioned.

Example: Men and women earned £30,000 and £25,000, respectively.

gap

IPA: /ɡæp/
Pronunciation: gap
French: écart ; fossé

Definition: A difference between groups, levels or outcomes.

Example: The gap widened over the period.

narrowing gap

IPA: /ˈnærəʊɪŋ ɡæp/
Pronunciation: NAR-oh-ing gap
French: écart qui se réduit

Definition: A difference that is getting smaller.

Example: The chart shows a narrowing gap between regions.

widening gap

IPA: /ˈwaɪdənɪŋ ɡæp/
Pronunciation: WY-duh-ning gap
French: écart qui se creuse

Definition: A difference that is getting larger.

Example: There is a widening gap between rich and poor.

to widen

IPA: /ˈwaɪdən/
Pronunciation: WY-duhn
French: s’élargir ; creuser

Definition: To become larger or make a gap larger.

Example: The wealth gap widened.

to narrow

IPA: /ˈnærəʊ/
Pronunciation: NAR-oh
French: se réduire ; réduire

Definition: To become smaller or make a gap smaller.

E. Exam-ready graph commentary phrases

the graph shows

IPA: /ðə ɡrɑːf ʃəʊz/
Pronunciation: thuh graf showz
French: le graphique montre

Definition: Used to introduce the main information in a graph.

Example: The graph shows a steady rise in prices.

the chart illustrates

IPA: /ðə tʃɑːt ˈɪləstreɪts/
Pronunciation: thuh chart IL-uh-strayts
French: le graphique illustre

Definition: Used to describe what a chart visually explains.

Example: The chart illustrates regional disparities.

the table presents

IPA: /ðə ˈteɪbəl prɪˈzents/
Pronunciation: thuh TAY-buhl pri-ZENTS
French: le tableau présente

Definition: Used to introduce data in a table.

Example: The table presents income by age group.

the figures suggest

IPA: /ðə ˈfɪɡəz səˈdʒest/
Pronunciation: thuh FIG-uhz suh-JEST
French: les chiffres suggèrent

Definition: Used to draw a cautious conclusion from data.

Example: The figures suggest growing insecurity.

the data indicate

IPA: /ðə ˈdeɪtə ˈɪndɪkeɪt/
Pronunciation: thuh DAY-tuh IN-di-kayt
French: les données indiquent

Definition: Used to state what data point to.

Example: The data indicate a long-term decline.

the trend points to

IPA: /ðə trend pɔɪnts tə/
Pronunciation: thuh trend points tuh
French: la tendance indique

Definition: Used to interpret the direction of data.

Example: The trend points to rising inequality.

the most striking feature is

IPA: /ðə məʊst ˈstraɪkɪŋ ˈfiːtʃə ɪz/
Pronunciation: thuh most STRY-king FEE-chuh iz
French: l’élément le plus frappant est

Definition: Used to identify the key visual element.

Example: The most striking feature is the sudden fall after 2020.

what stands out is

IPA: /wɒt stændz aʊt ɪz/
Pronunciation: wot standz out iz
French: ce qui ressort, c’est

Definition: Used to identify what is most noticeable.

Example: What stands out is the gap between regions.

overall

IPA: /ˌəʊvərˈɔːl/
Pronunciation: OH-vuh-rawl
French: dans l’ensemble

Definition: Considering the graph as a whole.

Example: Overall, the trend is upward.

broadly speaking

IPA: /ˌbrɔːdli ˈspiːkɪŋ/
Pronunciation: BROD-lee SPEE-king
French: globalement

Definition: Generally speaking.

Example: Broadly speaking, prices increased throughout the period.

at first glance

IPA: /æt ˌfɜːst ˈɡlɑːns/
Pronunciation: at first glahns
French: à première vue

Definition: On initial viewing.

Example: At first glance, the chart suggests progress.

on closer inspection

IPA: /ɒn ˌkləʊsər ɪnˈspekʃən/
Pronunciation: on KLOH-zuh in-SPEK-shuhn
French: à y regarder de plus près

Definition: After looking more carefully.

Example: On closer inspection, the improvement is uneven.

the scale may be misleading

IPA: /ðə skeɪl meɪ bi mɪsˈliːdɪŋ/
Pronunciation: thuh skayl may bee mis-LEE-ding
French: l’échelle peut être trompeuse

Definition: Used when the axis exaggerates or minimises change.

Example: The scale may be misleading because it does not start at zero.

the chart does not show

IPA: /ðə tʃɑːt dʌz nɒt ʃəʊ/
Pronunciation: thuh chart duz not show
French: le graphique ne montre pas

Definition: Used to point out missing information.

Example: The chart does not show regional differences.

the data should be treated with caution

IPA: /ðə ˈdeɪtə ʃʊd bi ˈtriːtɪd wɪð ˈkɔːʃən/
Pronunciation: thuh DAY-tuh should bee TREE-tid with KAW-shuhn
French: les données doivent être interprétées avec prudence

Definition: Used to warn that the data may be limited.

Example: The data should be treated with caution because the sample is small.

the figures do not prove causation

IPA: /ðə ˈfɪɡəz duː nɒt pruːv kɔːˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: thuh FIG-uhz do not proov kaw-ZAY-shuhn
French: les chiffres ne prouvent pas une causalité

Definition: Used to avoid false causal claims.

Example: The figures do not prove causation.
