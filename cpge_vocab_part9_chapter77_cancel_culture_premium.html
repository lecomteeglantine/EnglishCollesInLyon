Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse cancel culture: public shaming, online backlash, call-out culture, social accountability, due process, proportionality, apology, redemption, outrage cycles, context collapse, old posts, identity politics, culture wars, platform bans and the tension between accountability and mob justice.

How to use this chapter

For press articlesFocus on public shaming, trial by social media, call-out culture, accountability, due process, proportionality, context collapse, redemption, culture war and platform governance.
For oral examsUse this vocabulary to explain cancel culture as a clash between accountability and free speech, between social justice and mob punishment, and between changing norms and fear of conformity.

A. Core cancel culture vocabulary

cancel culture

IPA: /ˈkænsəl ˌkʌltʃə/
Pronunciation: KAN-suhl KUL-chuh
French: cancel culture ; culture de l’annulation

Definition: A contested term for public pressure to punish people for offensive, harmful or controversial words or actions.

Example: Cancel culture is often used as a weapon in culture-war debates.

cancellation

IPA: /ˌkænsəˈleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: kan-suh-LAY-shuhn
French: annulation ; mise à l’écart

Definition: The act of withdrawing support, invitations, jobs or platforms from someone after controversy.

Example: The cancellation of the speaker sparked a free speech debate.

to be cancelled

IPA: /bi ˈkænsəld/
Pronunciation: bee KAN-suhld
French: être cancel ; être mis à l’écart

Definition: To face public backlash and loss of reputation, platform or opportunities.

Example: The actor claimed he had been cancelled after his comments.

public backlash

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk ˈbæklæʃ/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik BAK-lash
French: retour de bâton ; réaction hostile du public

Definition: A strong negative public reaction.

Example: A public backlash followed the influencer’s remarks.

online backlash

IPA: /ˌɒnlaɪn ˈbæklæʃ/
Pronunciation: ON-lyne BAK-lash
French: réaction hostile en ligne

Definition: A wave of criticism on the internet.

Example: Online backlash can escalate within hours.

public shaming

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk ˈʃeɪmɪŋ/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik SHAY-ming
French: humiliation publique

Definition: Publicly humiliating or condemning someone for perceived wrongdoing.

Example: Public shaming can feel disproportionate.

naming and shaming

IPA: /ˌneɪmɪŋ ənd ˈʃeɪmɪŋ/
Pronunciation: NAY-ming and SHAY-ming
French: dénonciation publique

Definition: Publicly identifying someone in order to criticise or pressure them.

Example: Naming and shaming can expose abuse but also fuel mob justice.

call-out culture

IPA: /ˈkɔːl aʊt ˌkʌltʃə/
Pronunciation: CALL-out KUL-chuh
French: culture de la dénonciation

Definition: A culture of publicly criticising people for problematic behaviour or speech.

Example: Call-out culture can hold people accountable, but it can also become punitive.

calling out

IPA: /ˌkɔːlɪŋ ˈaʊt/
Pronunciation: CALL-ing out
French: dénonciation publique

Definition: Publicly criticising someone’s behaviour, words or attitudes.

Example: Calling out racism can be necessary.

calling in

IPA: /ˌkɔːlɪŋ ˈɪn/
Pronunciation: CALL-ing in
French: interpellation constructive

Definition: Privately or constructively challenging someone to learn rather than publicly shame them.

Example: Calling in is often presented as an alternative to calling out.

accountability

IPA: /əˌkaʊntəˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: uh-kown-tuh-BIL-uh-tee
French: responsabilité ; redevabilité

Definition: Responsibility for one’s actions and their consequences.

Example: Supporters argue that cancel culture is simply accountability.

social accountability

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl əˌkaʊntəˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl uh-kown-tuh-BIL-uh-tee
French: responsabilité sociale

Definition: Accountability enforced by society or public opinion rather than formal institutions.

Example: Social accountability can fill gaps left by institutions.

consequence culture

IPA: /ˈkɒnsɪkwəns ˌkʌltʃə/
Pronunciation: KON-si-kwuhns KUL-chuh
French: culture des conséquences

Definition: A term used to argue that people are facing consequences, not censorship.

Example: Some activists prefer the term consequence culture.

mob justice

IPA: /ˌmɒb ˈdʒʌstɪs/
Pronunciation: mob JUS-tis
French: justice expéditive de la foule

Definition: Punishment driven by a crowd rather than fair process.

Example: Critics say cancel culture resembles mob justice.

trial by social media

IPA: /ˌtraɪəl baɪ ˌsəʊʃəl ˈmiːdiə/
Pronunciation: TRY-uhl by SOH-shuhl MEE-dee-uh
French: procès sur les réseaux sociaux

Definition: Public judgement of someone through social media before facts are fully established.

Example: Trial by social media can destroy reputations without due process.

trial by media

IPA: /ˌtraɪəl baɪ ˈmiːdiə/
Pronunciation: TRY-uhl by MEE-dee-uh
French: procès médiatique

Definition: Public judgement through media coverage before any formal process.

Example: Trial by media can undermine the presumption of innocence.

online mob

IPA: /ˌɒnlaɪn ˈmɒb/
Pronunciation: ON-lyne mob
French: meute numérique

Definition: A large group attacking someone online.

Example: An online mob can overwhelm an individual.

digital mob

IPA: /ˌdɪdʒɪtəl ˈmɒb/
Pronunciation: DIJ-i-tuhl mob
French: foule numérique

Definition: A crowd of internet users collectively attacking or pressuring someone.

Example: A digital mob can punish faster than institutions.

pile-on

IPA: /ˈpaɪl ɒn/
Pronunciation: PYLE-on
French: déferlement de critiques

Definition: A sudden wave of criticism or abuse directed at someone.

Example: The pile-on began after a screenshot went viral.

dogpiling

IPA: /ˈdɒɡpaɪlɪŋ/
Pronunciation: DOG-py-ling
French: acharnement collectif en ligne

Definition: Mass online criticism or harassment by many users at once.

Example: Dogpiling can turn criticism into harassment.

witch hunt

IPA: /ˈwɪtʃ hʌnt/
Pronunciation: witch hunt
French: chasse aux sorcières

Definition: A campaign against someone seen as unfair, exaggerated or politically motivated.

Example: The politician described the investigation as a witch hunt.

social sanction

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl ˈsæŋkʃən/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl SANK-shuhn
French: sanction sociale

Definition: A social penalty imposed for violating norms.

Example: Social sanctions can enforce norms without courts.

reputational damage

IPA: /ˌrepjʊˈteɪʃənəl ˈdæmɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: rep-yuh-TAY-shuhn-uhl DAM-ij
French: atteinte à la réputation

Definition: Harm to someone’s public image or credibility.

Example: Reputational damage can affect careers.

career consequences

IPA: /kəˈrɪə ˈkɒnsɪkwənsɪz/
Pronunciation: kuh-REER KON-si-kwen-siz
French: conséquences professionnelles

Definition: Professional effects following controversy or misconduct.

Example: Cancel culture debates often focus on career consequences.

reputational risk

IPA: /ˌrepjʊˈteɪʃənəl rɪsk/
Pronunciation: rep-yuh-TAY-shuhn-uhl risk
French: risque réputationnel

Definition: The risk of damage to reputation.

Example: Companies fear reputational risk during controversies.

social media storm

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl ˈmiːdiə stɔːm/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl MEE-dee-uh storm
French: tempête sur les réseaux sociaux

Definition: A sudden intense controversy on social media.

Example: The social media storm forced the brand to apologise.

viral outrage

IPA: /ˈvaɪrəl ˈaʊtreɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: VY-ruhl OWT-rayj
French: indignation virale

Definition: Anger spreading rapidly online.

Example: Viral outrage can be amplified by algorithms.

outrage cycle

IPA: /ˈaʊtreɪdʒ ˌsaɪkəl/
Pronunciation: OWT-rayj SY-kuhl
French: cycle de l’indignation

Definition: The repeated pattern of online scandal, anger and replacement by a new controversy.

Example: The outrage cycle leaves little room for reflection.

outrage economy

IPA: /ˈaʊtreɪdʒ iˌkɒnəmi/
Pronunciation: OWT-rayj i-KON-uh-mee
French: économie de l’indignation

Definition: A media environment where anger attracts attention and profit.

Example: Cancel culture thrives in the outrage economy.

attention economy

IPA: /əˈtenʃən iˌkɒnəmi/
Pronunciation: uh-TEN-shuhn i-KON-uh-mee
French: économie de l’attention

Definition: An economy where attention is captured and monetised.

Example: The attention economy rewards extreme reactions.

algorithmic amplification

IPA: /ˌælɡəˈrɪðmɪk ˌæmplɪfɪˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: al-guh-RITH-mik am-pli-fi-KAY-shuhn
French: amplification algorithmique

Definition: The process by which algorithms make certain content more visible.

Example: Algorithmic amplification can intensify outrage.

context collapse

IPA: /ˈkɒntekst kəˌlæps/
Pronunciation: KON-tekst kuh-LAPS
French: effondrement du contexte

Definition: When content intended for one audience is seen by different audiences lacking the original context.

Example: Context collapse can turn old jokes into public scandals.

out-of-context quote

IPA: /ˌaʊt əv ˈkɒntekst kwəʊt/
Pronunciation: out-ov-KON-tekst kwoht
French: citation sortie de son contexte

Definition: A quotation presented without the context needed to understand it fairly.

Example: An out-of-context quote can trigger backlash.

screenshots

IPA: /ˈskriːnʃɒts/
Pronunciation: SCREEN-shots
French: captures d’écran

Definition: Images capturing what appeared on a screen.

Example: Screenshots can preserve evidence but also remove context.

receipts

IPA: /rɪˈsiːts/
Pronunciation: ri-SEETS
French: preuves ; captures compromettantes

Definition: Informal term for screenshots or evidence used to prove a claim online.

Example: Online users demanded receipts before believing the accusation.

B. Accountability, apology, redemption and due process

receipts culture

IPA: /rɪˈsiːts ˌkʌltʃə/
Pronunciation: ri-SEETS KUL-chuh
French: culture des preuves numériques

Definition: A culture of demanding screenshots or evidence in online disputes.

Example: Receipts culture can support accountability but also surveillance.

digital footprint

IPA: /ˌdɪdʒɪtəl ˈfʊtprɪnt/
Pronunciation: DIJ-i-tuhl FOOT-print
French: empreinte numérique

Definition: The trail of data and content a person leaves online.

Example: A teenager’s digital footprint can resurface years later.

old posts

IPA: /ˌəʊld ˈpəʊsts/
Pronunciation: ohld posts
French: anciens posts

Definition: Past social media posts that may resurface later.

Example: Old posts can be judged by today’s standards.

resurfacing

IPA: /ˌriːˈsɜːfɪsɪŋ/
Pronunciation: ree-SUR-fi-sing
French: réapparition

Definition: The reappearance of old content, often causing controversy.

Example: The resurfacing of old tweets damaged his campaign.

apology

IPA: /əˈpɒlədʒi/
Pronunciation: uh-POL-uh-jee
French: excuses

Definition: A statement expressing regret or responsibility.

Example: A sincere apology can reduce public anger.

public apology

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk əˈpɒlədʒi/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik uh-POL-uh-jee
French: excuses publiques

Definition: An apology made openly to the public.

Example: The CEO issued a public apology.

non-apology apology

IPA: /ˌnɒn əˈpɒlədʒi əˈpɒlədʒi/
Pronunciation: non-uh-POL-uh-jee uh-POL-uh-jee
French: fausses excuses

Definition: A statement that sounds like an apology but avoids responsibility.

Example: A non-apology apology often makes backlash worse.

performative apology

IPA: /pəˌfɔːmətɪv əˈpɒlədʒi/
Pronunciation: puh-FOR-muh-tiv uh-POL-uh-jee
French: excuses performatives

Definition: An apology made mainly to look good rather than to repair harm.

Example: Critics dismissed the statement as a performative apology.

remorse

IPA: /rɪˈmɔːs/
Pronunciation: ri-MORSS
French: remords

Definition: Deep regret for wrongdoing.

Example: People are more willing to forgive when they see remorse.

forgiveness

IPA: /fəˈɡɪvnəs/
Pronunciation: fuh-GIV-nuhs
French: pardon

Definition: The act of no longer blaming or resenting someone.

Example: Cancel culture debates often ask whether forgiveness is possible.

redemption

IPA: /rɪˈdempʃən/
Pronunciation: ri-DEMP-shuhn
French: rédemption

Definition: The possibility of moral recovery after wrongdoing.

Example: A healthy society needs paths to redemption.

second chance

IPA: /ˌsekənd ˈtʃɑːns/
Pronunciation: SEK-uhnd chanss
French: seconde chance

Definition: An opportunity to recover after a mistake.

Example: Critics say cancel culture leaves no room for second chances.

rehabilitation

IPA: /ˌriːhəˌbɪlɪˈteɪʃən/
Pronunciation: ree-huh-bil-i-TAY-shuhn
French: réhabilitation

Definition: The process of restoring someone’s reputation or behaviour after wrongdoing.

Example: Rehabilitation requires time and accountability.

restorative justice

IPA: /rɪˌstɒrətɪv ˈdʒʌstɪs/
Pronunciation: ri-STOR-uh-tiv JUS-tis
French: justice réparatrice

Definition: An approach focused on repairing harm rather than only punishing wrongdoing.

Example: Restorative justice offers an alternative to punitive cancellation.

punitive culture

IPA: /ˈpjuːnɪtɪv ˌkʌltʃə/
Pronunciation: PYOO-ni-tiv KUL-chuh
French: culture punitive

Definition: A culture focused on punishment rather than repair or learning.

Example: Cancel culture is often criticised as punitive culture.

proportionality

IPA: /prəˌpɔːʃəˈnæləti/
Pronunciation: pruh-por-shuh-NAL-uh-tee
French: proportionnalité

Definition: The principle that punishment or response should match the seriousness of the wrongdoing.

Example: Proportionality is central to debates about cancellation.

due process

IPA: /ˌdjuː ˈprəʊses/
Pronunciation: dyoo PROH-sess
French: procédure régulière ; garanties procédurales

Definition: Fair procedures before punishment or judgement.

Example: Critics argue that online cancellation lacks due process.

presumption of innocence

IPA: /prɪˌzʌmpʃən əv ˈɪnəsəns/
Pronunciation: pri-ZUMP-shuhn ov IN-uh-suhns
French: présomption d’innocence

Definition: The principle that someone is considered innocent until proven guilty.

Example: Trial by social media can weaken the presumption of innocence.

evidence

IPA: /ˈevɪdəns/
Pronunciation: EV-i-duhns
French: preuves ; éléments probants

Definition: Information or material supporting a claim.

Example: Serious accusations require evidence.

allegation

IPA: /ˌælɪˈɡeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: al-i-GAY-shuhn
French: allégation

Definition: A claim that someone has done something wrong, not yet proven.

Example: The allegation was investigated by the employer.

accusation

IPA: /ˌækjʊˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: ak-yuh-ZAY-shuhn
French: accusation

Definition: A statement blaming someone for wrongdoing.

Example: Accusations can spread faster than corrections.

misconduct

IPA: /ˌmɪsˈkɒndʌkt/
Pronunciation: mis-KON-dukt
French: faute ; comportement répréhensible

Definition: Unacceptable or improper behaviour.

Example: Workplace misconduct should be investigated fairly.

accountability process

IPA: /əˌkaʊntəˈbɪləti ˈprəʊses/
Pronunciation: uh-kown-tuh-BIL-uh-tee PROH-sess
French: processus de responsabilisation

Definition: A procedure for examining wrongdoing and deciding consequences.

Example: An accountability process should be transparent.

investigation

IPA: /ɪnˌvestɪˈɡeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: in-ves-ti-GAY-shuhn
French: enquête

Definition: A formal examination of facts.

Example: An investigation can distinguish rumours from evidence.

context

IPA: /ˈkɒntekst/
Pronunciation: KON-tekst
French: contexte

Definition: The circumstances needed to understand something properly.

Example: Context matters when judging old statements.

intent

IPA: /ɪnˈtent/
Pronunciation: in-TENT
French: intention

Definition: What someone meant or planned to do.

Example: Intent does not erase harm, but it matters.

impact

IPA: /ˈɪmpækt/
Pronunciation: IM-pakt
French: impact ; effet

Definition: The effect or consequence of words or actions.

Example: Free speech debates often oppose intent and impact.

intent versus impact

IPA: /ɪnˈtent ˈvɜːsəs ˈɪmpækt/
Pronunciation: in-TENT VUR-suhs IM-pakt
French: intention contre impact

Definition: The tension between what someone meant and how it affected others.

Example: Intent versus impact is central to call-out debates.

harm

IPA: /hɑːm/
Pronunciation: harm
French: préjudice ; tort

Definition: Damage or injury caused to a person or group.

Example: Some speech can cause real harm.

offence

IPA: /əˈfens/
Pronunciation: uh-FENS
French: offense

Definition: The feeling of being upset, insulted or outraged.

Example: Offence and harm are not always the same.

microaggression

IPA: /ˌmaɪkrəʊəˈɡreʃən/
Pronunciation: MY-kroh-uh-GRESH-uhn
French: microagression

Definition: A subtle comment or action that may express prejudice towards a marginalised group.

Example: Microaggressions can accumulate over time.

structural inequality

IPA: /ˌstrʌktʃərəl ˌɪnɪˈkwɒləti/
Pronunciation: STRUK-chuh-ruhl in-i-KWOL-uh-tee
French: inégalité structurelle

Definition: Inequality built into institutions and social systems.

Example: Supporters link call-out culture to structural inequality.

marginalised group

IPA: /ˈmɑːdʒɪnəlaɪzd ɡruːp/
Pronunciation: MAR-ji-nuh-lyzd group
French: groupe marginalisé

Definition: A group pushed to the edges of power or recognition.

Example: Cancel culture debates often involve marginalised groups.

power imbalance

IPA: /ˈpaʊər ɪmˌbæləns/
Pronunciation: POW-uh im-BAL-uhns
French: rapport de force inégal

Definition: Unequal power between people or groups.

Example: Calling out can challenge a power imbalance.

speaking truth to power

IPA: /ˌspiːkɪŋ truːθ tə ˈpaʊə/
Pronunciation: SPEE-king trooth tuh POW-uh
French: dire la vérité au pouvoir

Definition: Telling uncomfortable truths to powerful people or institutions.

Example: Activists describe call-outs as speaking truth to power.

punching up

IPA: /ˌpʌntʃɪŋ ˈʌp/
Pronunciation: PUN-ching up
French: critiquer les puissants

Definition: Criticising those with more power.

Example: Satire is often defended when it is punching up.

punching down

IPA: /ˌpʌntʃɪŋ ˈdaʊn/
Pronunciation: PUN-ching down
French: s’en prendre aux plus vulnérables

Definition: Mocking or attacking those with less power.

Example: A joke may be criticised for punching down.

C. Power, identity, culture wars and social norms

culture war

IPA: /ˈkʌltʃə wɔː/
Pronunciation: KUL-chuh war
French: guerre culturelle

Definition: A conflict over values, identity and social norms.

Example: Cancel culture is a major theme in culture-war politics.

moral panic

IPA: /ˌmɒrəl ˈpænɪk/
Pronunciation: MOR-uhl PAN-ik
French: panique morale

Definition: Exaggerated public fear about a perceived social threat.

Example: Some see cancel culture discourse as a moral panic.

political correctness

IPA: /pəˌlɪtɪkəl kəˈrektnəs/
Pronunciation: puh-LIT-i-kuhl kuh-REKT-nuhs
French: politiquement correct

Definition: Language or behaviour intended to avoid offending marginalised groups, often used critically.

Example: Political correctness is often framed as limiting free speech.

wokeness

IPA: /ˈwəʊknəs/
Pronunciation: WOHK-nuhs
French: wokisme ; conscience des injustices

Definition: A contested term for awareness of social injustice, often used critically.

Example: Wokeness is frequently attacked in culture-war rhetoric.

anti-woke backlash

IPA: /ˌænti ˈwəʊk ˈbæklæʃ/
Pronunciation: AN-tee WOHK BAK-lash
French: réaction anti-woke

Definition: A reaction against progressive social justice language or politics.

Example: Anti-woke backlash has shaped electoral politics.

identity politics

IPA: /aɪˌdentəti ˈpɒlətɪks/
Pronunciation: eye-DEN-tuh-tee POL-uh-tiks
French: politique identitaire

Definition: Politics focused on the interests and experiences of identity groups.

Example: Cancel culture is often linked to identity politics.

victimhood

IPA: /ˈvɪktɪmhʊd/
Pronunciation: VIK-tim-hood
French: statut de victime ; victimisation

Definition: The state or identity of being a victim, sometimes used critically.

Example: Critics accuse cancel culture of rewarding victimhood.

moral purity

IPA: /ˌmɒrəl ˈpjʊərəti/
Pronunciation: MOR-uhl PYOO-ruh-tee
French: pureté morale

Definition: The desire to appear morally flawless or ideologically pure.

Example: Moral purity can make compromise difficult.

purity test

IPA: /ˈpjʊərəti test/
Pronunciation: PYOO-ruh-tee test
French: test de pureté idéologique

Definition: A demand that someone prove ideological or moral correctness.

Example: Political movements can become obsessed with purity tests.

groupthink

IPA: /ˈɡruːpθɪŋk/
Pronunciation: GROUP-think
French: pensée de groupe

Definition: Pressure to conform to the opinions of a group.

Example: Groupthink can suppress dissent.

orthodoxy

IPA: /ˈɔːθədɒksi/
Pronunciation: OR-thuh-dok-see
French: orthodoxie

Definition: An accepted or dominant belief system.

Example: Free speech allows people to challenge orthodoxy.

heresy

IPA: /ˈherəsi/
Pronunciation: HERR-uh-see
French: hérésie

Definition: A belief that goes against accepted doctrine or norms.

Example: In culture wars, disagreement can be treated as heresy.

dissenting voice

IPA: /dɪˌsentɪŋ ˈvɔɪs/
Pronunciation: di-SEN-ting voys
French: voix dissidente

Definition: A person or opinion disagreeing with the dominant view.

Example: Dissenting voices may fear cancellation.

chilling climate

IPA: /ˈtʃɪlɪŋ ˌklaɪmət/
Pronunciation: CHIL-ing KLY-muht
French: climat dissuasif

Definition: An atmosphere discouraging people from speaking freely.

Example: A chilling climate can reduce open discussion.

self-policing

IPA: /ˌself pəˈliːsɪŋ/
Pronunciation: self puh-LEE-sing
French: autocontrôle

Definition: Monitoring one’s own words or behaviour to avoid social punishment.

Example: Self-policing can become exhausting.

social conformity

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl kənˈfɔːməti/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl kuhn-FOR-muh-tee
French: conformisme social

Definition: Pressure to fit dominant social expectations.

Example: Cancel culture may increase social conformity.

free speech absolutism

IPA: /ˌfriː ˈspiːtʃ ˈæbsəluːtɪzəm/
Pronunciation: free speech AB-suh-loo-tiz-uhm
French: absolutisme de la liberté d’expression

Definition: The view that speech should almost never be restricted.

Example: Free speech absolutism is difficult to apply online.

harm reduction

IPA: /ˈhɑːm rɪˌdʌkʃən/
Pronunciation: HARM ri-DUK-shuhn
French: réduction des risques

Definition: Policies or actions aimed at reducing harm rather than eliminating all risk.

Example: Moderation can be framed as harm reduction.

speech norms

IPA: /ˈspiːtʃ nɔːmz/
Pronunciation: speech normz
French: normes discursives

Definition: Social expectations about what can be said and how.

Example: Speech norms change over time.

social norms

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl ˈnɔːmz/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl normz
French: normes sociales

Definition: Shared expectations about acceptable behaviour.

Example: Cancel culture reflects changing social norms.

norm enforcement

IPA: /ˈnɔːm ɪnˌfɔːsmənt/
Pronunciation: norm in-FORSS-muhnt
French: application des normes sociales

Definition: The process of making people follow social rules.

Example: Call-outs are a form of norm enforcement.

institutional response

IPA: /ˌɪnstɪˈtjuːʃənəl rɪˈspɒns/
Pronunciation: in-sti-TYOO-shuhn-uhl ri-SPONS
French: réponse institutionnelle
