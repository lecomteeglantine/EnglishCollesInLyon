Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse the American Dream: opportunity, meritocracy, social mobility, class, homeownership, education, student debt, inequality, work, economic insecurity, the safety net and the decline or reinvention of the Dream. Each entry includes the English term, IPA phonetics, a practical pronunciation guide, an English definition, a French translation, and an exam-ready example sentence.

How to use this chapter

For press articles Focus on terms such as upward mobility, equal opportunity, wealth gap, homeownership, student debt, wage stagnation and economic insecurity.
For oral exams Use this vocabulary to analyse the gap between the American promise of opportunity and the realities of inherited privilege, debt, inequality and regional decline.
For essays Memorise the collocations: to achieve the American Dream, to climb the social ladder, to level the playing field, to reproduce inequality, to question the American Dream.

A. Core concepts: dream, merit and opportunity

American Dream

IPA: /əˌmerɪkən ˈdriːm/
Pronunciation: uh-MER-i-kuhn dreem
French: rêve américain

Definition: The belief that anyone in the United States can achieve success, prosperity and upward mobility through hard work.

Example: The American Dream remains powerful, but many argue that it has become harder to achieve.

upward mobility

IPA: /ˌʌpwəd məʊˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: UP-wuhd moh-BIL-uh-tee
French: ascension sociale

Definition: The ability to move to a higher social or economic position.

Example: Upward mobility is often presented as the promise of the American Dream.

social mobility

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl məʊˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl moh-BIL-uh-tee
French: mobilité sociale

Definition: Movement between social classes, either upward or downward.

Example: Declining social mobility challenges the idea of equal opportunity.

economic mobility

IPA: /ˌiːkəˈnɒmɪk məʊˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: ee-kuh-NOM-ik moh-BIL-uh-tee
French: mobilité économique

Definition: The ability to improve one’s economic position over time.

Example: Economic mobility depends heavily on education, housing and family wealth.

intergenerational mobility

IPA: /ˌɪntədʒenəˈreɪʃənəl məʊˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: IN-tuh-jen-uh-RAY-shuh-nuhl moh-BIL-uh-tee
French: mobilité intergénérationnelle

Definition: The extent to which children achieve a different social or economic status from their parents.

Example: Intergenerational mobility has slowed in many parts of the United States.

rags to riches

IPA: /ˌræɡz tə ˈrɪtʃɪz/
Pronunciation: ragz tuh RICH-iz
French: de la misère à la richesse

Definition: A story of rising from poverty to wealth.

Example: The rags-to-riches story is central to American mythology.

self-made man

IPA: /ˌself meɪd ˈmæn/
Pronunciation: self-made man
French: self-made man ; homme qui s’est fait tout seul

Definition: A person who becomes successful through their own effort rather than inherited privilege.

Example: The self-made man is a classic figure of the American Dream.

self-made success

IPA: /ˌself meɪd səkˈses/
Pronunciation: self-made suhk-SESS
French: réussite personnelle acquise par ses propres efforts

Definition: Success achieved through personal effort rather than family privilege.

Example: The billionaire presented himself as a self-made success.

meritocracy

IPA: /ˌmerɪˈtɒkrəsi/
Pronunciation: mer-i-TOK-ruh-see
French: méritocratie

Definition: A system in which success is supposed to depend on talent and effort.

Example: The American Dream is often linked to meritocracy.

meritocratic ideal

IPA: /ˌmerɪtəˈkrætɪk aɪˈdɪəl/
Pronunciation: mer-i-tuh-KRAT-ik eye-DEE-uhl
French: idéal méritocratique

Definition: The belief that people should succeed according to ability and hard work.

Example: The meritocratic ideal can hide structural inequalities.

hard work

IPA: /ˌhɑːd ˈwɜːk/
Pronunciation: hard work
French: travail acharné

Definition: Great effort and dedication.

Example: The American Dream suggests that hard work should be rewarded.

work ethic

IPA: /ˈwɜːk ˌeθɪk/
Pronunciation: work ETH-ik
French: éthique du travail

Definition: A belief in the moral value of hard work and discipline.

Example: The Protestant work ethic influenced American attitudes to success.

individual effort

IPA: /ˌɪndɪˈvɪdʒuəl ˈefət/
Pronunciation: in-di-VIJ-oo-uhl EF-uht
French: effort individuel

Definition: Personal work, determination and responsibility.

Example: Supporters of the American Dream emphasise individual effort.

personal responsibility

IPA: /ˌpɜːsənəl rɪˌspɒnsəˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: PUR-suh-nuhl ri-spon-suh-BIL-uh-tee
French: responsabilité individuelle

Definition: The idea that individuals are responsible for their own choices and outcomes.

Example: Personal responsibility is a key theme in conservative accounts of success.

ambition

IPA: /æmˈbɪʃən/
Pronunciation: am-BISH-uhn
French: ambition

Definition: A strong desire to succeed or achieve something.

Example: Ambition is often celebrated in American culture.

determination

IPA: /dɪˌtɜːmɪˈneɪʃən/
Pronunciation: di-tur-mi-NAY-shuhn
French: détermination

Definition: The quality of continuing to work towards a goal despite difficulty.

Example: Determination is often portrayed as the key to success.

resilience

IPA: /rɪˈzɪliəns/
Pronunciation: ri-ZIL-yuhns
French: résilience

Definition: The ability to recover from difficulties or setbacks.

Example: The American Dream celebrates resilience in the face of hardship.

opportunity

IPA: /ˌɒpəˈtjuːnəti/
Pronunciation: op-uh-TYOO-nuh-tee
French: opportunité ; occasion

Definition: A chance to improve one’s life or achieve success.

Example: America has often been described as a land of opportunity.

land of opportunity

IPA: /ˌlænd əv ˌɒpəˈtjuːnəti/
Pronunciation: land ov op-uh-TYOO-nuh-tee
French: terre d’opportunités

Definition: A phrase describing the United States as a place where people can succeed.

Example: For many immigrants, the US represented a land of opportunity.

equal opportunity

IPA: /ˌiːkwəl ˌɒpəˈtjuːnəti/
Pronunciation: EE-kwuhl op-uh-TYOO-nuh-tee
French: égalité des chances

Definition: The principle that everyone should have the same chances to succeed.

Example: Equal opportunity is central to the promise of the American Dream.

equality of opportunity

IPA: /iˌkwɒləti əv ˌɒpəˈtjuːnəti/
Pronunciation: i-KWOL-uh-tee ov op-uh-TYOO-nuh-tee
French: égalité des chances

Definition: The idea that people should have fair chances, regardless of background.

Example: The American Dream rests on equality of opportunity rather than equality of outcome.

equality of outcome

IPA: /iˌkwɒləti əv ˈaʊtkʌm/
Pronunciation: i-KWOL-uh-tee ov OWT-kum
French: égalité des résultats

Definition: The idea that people should end up with similar results or living standards.

Example: Critics of redistribution often reject equality of outcome.

level playing field

IPA: /ˌlevəl ˈpleɪɪŋ fiːld/
Pronunciation: LEV-uhl PLAY-ing field
French: conditions équitables ; terrain de jeu égal

Definition: A fair situation in which everyone has the same chance to succeed.

Example: Many argue that America no longer offers a level playing field.

fair shot

IPA: /ˌfeə ˈʃɒt/
Pronunciation: fair shot
French: chance équitable

Definition: A fair chance to succeed.

Example: Politicians often promise every child a fair shot.

life chances

IPA: /ˈlaɪf ˌtʃɑːnsɪz/
Pronunciation: life CHAN-siz
French: chances dans la vie

Definition: The opportunities a person has for success, health, education and security.

Example: Life chances are strongly shaped by family background.

starting point

IPA: /ˈstɑːtɪŋ pɔɪnt/
Pronunciation: STAR-ting point
French: point de départ

Definition: A person’s initial social or economic position in life.

Example: Not everyone begins from the same starting point.

birth lottery

IPA: /ˈbɜːθ ˌlɒtəri/
Pronunciation: birth LOT-uh-ree
French: loterie de la naissance

Definition: The idea that life outcomes are heavily shaped by the circumstances into which someone is born.

Example: The birth lottery contradicts the myth of pure meritocracy.

American exceptionalism

IPA: /əˌmerɪkən ɪkˈsepʃənəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: uh-MER-i-kuhn ik-SEP-shuh-nuh-liz-uhm
French: exceptionnalisme américain

Definition: The belief that the United States is uniquely free, successful or morally special.

Example: The American Dream is closely linked to American exceptionalism.

frontier spirit

IPA: /ˈfrʌntɪə ˈspɪrɪt/
Pronunciation: FRUN-teer SPIR-it
French: esprit de la frontière

Definition: The cultural ideal of independence, risk-taking and expansion associated with the frontier.

Example: The frontier spirit shaped myths of self-reliance.

self-reliance

IPA: /ˌself rɪˈlaɪəns/
Pronunciation: self ri-LY-uhns
French: autosuffisance ; autonomie

Definition: The ability or belief in depending on oneself rather than others.

Example: Self-reliance is a major American cultural value.

rugged individualism

IPA: /ˌrʌɡɪd ˌɪndɪˈvɪdʒuəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: RUG-id in-di-VIJ-oo-uh-liz-uhm
French: individualisme farouche

Definition: A strong belief in individual independence and limited reliance on government.

Example: Rugged individualism can make welfare policies politically controversial.

pursuit of happiness

IPA: /pəˌsjuːt əv ˈhæpinəs/
Pronunciation: puh-SYOOT ov HAP-ee-nuhs
French: quête du bonheur

Definition: A phrase from the Declaration of Independence referring to the right to seek fulfilment.

Example: The pursuit of happiness is part of America’s founding language.

founding myth

IPA: /ˈfaʊndɪŋ mɪθ/
Pronunciation: FOWN-ding myth
French: mythe fondateur

Definition: A story that helps define a nation’s identity and values.

Example: The American Dream functions as a founding myth.

national myth

IPA: /ˌnæʃənəl ˈmɪθ/
Pronunciation: NASH-uh-nuhl myth
French: mythe national

Definition: A widely shared story about a nation’s identity, often simplified or idealised.

Example: The national myth of opportunity hides many inequalities.

promise of America

IPA: /ˈprɒmɪs əv əˈmerɪkə/
Pronunciation: PROM-is ov uh-MER-i-kuh
French: promesse américaine

Definition: The ideal that America offers freedom, opportunity and dignity.

Example: Civil rights leaders demanded that America fulfil the promise of America.

upward climb

IPA: /ˌʌpwəd ˈklaɪm/
Pronunciation: UP-wuhd klyme
French: ascension sociale difficile

Definition: The struggle to rise socially or economically.

Example: For many families, the upward climb has become steeper.

B. Class, wealth and economic inequality

middle class

IPA: /ˌmɪdəl ˈklɑːs/
Pronunciation: MID-uhl klass
French: classe moyenne

Definition: The social group between the working class and the wealthy, often associated with stability and comfort.

Example: Homeownership has long been a marker of middle-class success.

working class

IPA: /ˌwɜːkɪŋ ˈklɑːs/
Pronunciation: WUR-king klass
French: classe ouvrière ; classes populaires

Definition: People employed in manual, service or lower-paid work, often with less economic security.

Example: Many working-class Americans feel the Dream has slipped away.

lower class

IPA: /ˌləʊə ˈklɑːs/
Pronunciation: LOH-uh klass
French: classe défavorisée

Definition: A social group with low income, wealth and status.

Example: Class labels are often avoided in American political rhetoric.

upper class

IPA: /ˌʌpə ˈklɑːs/
Pronunciation: UP-uh klass
French: classe supérieure

Definition: The wealthiest and most privileged social group.

Example: The upper class benefits from inherited wealth and elite networks.

elite

IPA: /ɪˈliːt/
Pronunciation: i-LEET
French: élite

Definition: A small group with significant wealth, power or status.

Example: The American Dream is often criticised for favouring the elite.

wealth

IPA: /welθ/
Pronunciation: welth
French: patrimoine ; richesse

Definition: Assets and resources owned by a person or household.

Example: Wealth matters more than income for long-term security.

income

IPA: /ˈɪnkʌm/
Pronunciation: IN-kum
French: revenu

Definition: Money received from work, investments or benefits.

Example: Income inequality has increased in recent decades.

wealth gap

IPA: /ˈwelθ ɡæp/
Pronunciation: welth gap
French: écart de patrimoine

Definition: The difference in wealth between groups.

Example: The wealth gap limits social mobility.

income inequality

IPA: /ˈɪnkʌm ˌɪnɪˈkwɒləti/
Pronunciation: IN-kum in-i-KWOL-uh-tee
French: inégalité de revenus

Definition: Unequal distribution of income across society.

Example: Income inequality undermines belief in the American Dream.

wealth inequality

IPA: /ˈwelθ ˌɪnɪˈkwɒləti/
Pronunciation: welth in-i-KWOL-uh-tee
French: inégalité patrimoniale

Definition: Unequal distribution of assets and wealth.

Example: Wealth inequality can reproduce privilege across generations.

racial wealth gap

IPA: /ˌreɪʃəl ˈwelθ ɡæp/
Pronunciation: RAY-shuhl welth gap
French: écart de patrimoine racial

Definition: The difference in accumulated wealth between racial groups.

Example: The racial wealth gap reveals the limits of equal opportunity.

class divide

IPA: /ˈklɑːs dɪˌvaɪd/
Pronunciation: klass di-VYDE
French: fracture de classe

Definition: A social division between classes.

Example: The class divide has become more visible in debates about education.

social ladder

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl ˈlædə/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl LAD-uh
French: échelle sociale

Definition: A metaphor for moving up or down in social status.

Example: Education is often seen as a way up the social ladder.

glass ceiling

IPA: /ˌɡlɑːs ˈsiːlɪŋ/
Pronunciation: glass SEE-ling
French: plafond de verre

Definition: An invisible barrier preventing certain groups from reaching top positions.

Example: The glass ceiling limits women and minorities in elite careers.

sticky floor

IPA: /ˌstɪki ˈflɔː/
Pronunciation: STIK-ee floor
French: plancher collant ; blocage dans les emplois précaires

Definition: A situation where people remain trapped in low-paid or low-status jobs.

Example: The sticky floor prevents many workers from moving up.

poverty trap

IPA: /ˈpɒvəti træp/
Pronunciation: POV-uh-tee trap
French: piège de la pauvreté

Definition: A situation in which poverty makes it difficult to escape poverty.

Example: Debt and low wages can create a poverty trap.

cycle of poverty

IPA: /ˌsaɪkəl əv ˈpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: SY-kuhl ov POV-uh-tee
French: cycle de la pauvreté

Definition: The repeated transmission of poverty from one generation to the next.

Example: The cycle of poverty challenges the promise of mobility.

working poor

IPA: /ˌwɜːkɪŋ ˈpɔː/
Pronunciation: WUR-king poor
French: travailleurs pauvres

Definition: People who work but remain poor because wages are low or costs are high.

Example: The working poor reveal that hard work does not always guarantee security.

minimum wage

IPA: /ˌmɪnɪməm ˈweɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: MIN-i-muhm wayj
French: salaire minimum

Definition: The lowest legal hourly pay.

Example: A low minimum wage can make the American Dream unreachable.

living wage

IPA: /ˈlɪvɪŋ weɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: LIV-ing wayj
French: salaire décent

Definition: A wage high enough to cover basic living costs.

Example: Campaigners argue that workers deserve a living wage.

precarious work

IPA: /prɪˌkeəriəs ˈwɜːk/
Pronunciation: pri-KAIR-ee-uhs work
French: emploi précaire

Definition: Unstable or insecure work with low protection.

Example: Precarious work makes upward mobility harder.

gig economy

IPA: /ˈɡɪɡ iˌkɒnəmi/
Pronunciation: gig i-KON-uh-mee
French: économie des petits boulots ; gig economy

Definition: A labour market based on short-term, freelance or platform-based work.

Example: The gig economy offers flexibility but little security.

economic insecurity

IPA: /ˌiːkəˈnɒmɪk ˌɪnsɪˈkjʊərəti/
Pronunciation: ee-kuh-NOM-ik in-si-KYOOR-uh-tee
French: insécurité économique

Definition: Uncertainty about income, work, housing or financial stability.

Example: Economic insecurity has weakened faith in the American Dream.

cost of living

IPA: /ˌkɒst əv ˈlɪvɪŋ/
Pronunciation: kost ov LIV-ing
French: coût de la vie

Definition: The amount of money needed to pay for basic expenses.

Example: The rising cost of living makes it harder to save.

homeownership

IPA: /ˈhəʊmˌəʊnəʃɪp/
Pronunciation: HOHM-oh-nuh-ship
French: accession à la propriété ; propriété immobilière

Definition: The state of owning one’s home.

Example: Homeownership has long symbolised the American Dream.

property ownership

IPA: /ˈprɒpəti ˌəʊnəʃɪp/
Pronunciation: PROP-uh-tee OH-nuh-ship
French: propriété immobilière

Definition: The legal ownership of land or housing.

Example: Property ownership is a major source of wealth.

legacy admission

school funding

D. The Dream in crisis: decline, work and insecurity

Medicaid

IPA: /ˈmedɪkeɪd/
Pronunciation: MED-i-kayd
French: Medicaid ; assurance santé pour bas revenus

Definition: A public health insurance programme for low-income people in the US.

Example: Medicaid expansion improved healthcare access in some states.

bootstrap narrative

IPA: /ˈbuːtstræp ˈnærətɪv/
Pronunciation: BOOT-strap NAR-uh-tiv
French: récit de réussite par ses propres moyens

Definition: The story that people can succeed purely by their own effort.

Example: The bootstrap narrative can blame the poor for structural problems.

to pull oneself up by one’s bootstraps

IPA: /pʊl wʌnˈself ʌp baɪ wʌnz ˈbuːtstræps/
Pronunciation: pull wun-SELF up by wunz BOOT-straps
French: s’en sortir par ses propres moyens

Definition: To improve one’s situation through one’s own effort, without help.

Example: Politicians often praise people who pull themselves up by their bootstraps.

myth of meritocracy

IPA: /ˌmɪθ əv ˌmerɪˈtɒkrəsi/
Pronunciation: mith ov mer-i-TOK-ruh-see
French: mythe de la méritocratie

Definition: The idea that meritocracy is more imagined than real because inequality shapes outcomes.

Example: The myth of meritocracy hides inherited privilege.

broken ladder

IPA: /ˌbrəʊkən ˈlædə/
Pronunciation: BROH-kuhn LAD-uh
French: échelle sociale brisée

Definition: A metaphor suggesting that upward mobility has become difficult or impossible.

Example: For many Americans, the social ladder feels broken.

American Dream in decline

IPA: /əˌmerɪkən ˈdriːm ɪn dɪˈklaɪn/
Pronunciation: uh-MER-i-kuhn dreem in di-KLYNE
French: déclin du rêve américain

Definition: The idea that the American Dream is weakening or becoming less achievable.

Example: Many commentators describe the American Dream as in decline.

dream deferred

IPA: /ˌdriːm dɪˈfɜːd/
Pronunciation: dreem di-FURD
French: rêve différé ; espoir repoussé

Definition: A delayed or frustrated hope for equality or success, echoing Langston Hughes.

Example: For many minorities, the American Dream has long been a dream deferred.

disillusionment

IPA: /ˌdɪsɪˈluːʒənmənt/

left behind

economic anxiety

Definition: A region in the northern US affected by industrial decline.

IPA: /ˌwaɪt ˈkɒlə ˈwɜːkə/

middle-class squeeze

Pronunciation: MID-uhl klass skweez
French: pression sur la classe moyenne

Definition: Pressure on middle-class households from stagnant wages and rising costs.

Example: The middle-class squeeze has weakened confidence in the American Dream.

wage stagnation

IPA: /ˈweɪdʒ stæɡˌneɪʃən/

income stagnation

IPA: /ˈɪnkʌm stæɡˌneɪʃən/
Pronunciation: IN-kum stag-NAY-shuhn
French: stagnation des revenus

Definition: A situation where household income stops growing.

Example: Income stagnation has affected many middle-class families.

outsourcing

IPA: /ˈaʊtsɔːsɪŋ/

French: externalisation ; délocalisation

automation

IPA: /ˌɔːtəˈmeɪʃən/

entrepreneurship
