Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse biodiversity: ecosystems, species diversity, habitat loss, extinction, ecosystem services, conservation, protected areas, rewilding, deforestation, wetlands, coral reefs, overfishing, poaching, pesticides, invasive species, climate impacts, biodiversity policy and the tension between conservation and development.

How to use this chapter

For press articlesFocus on terms such as biodiversity loss, ecosystem services, habitat fragmentation, invasive species, protected area, rewilding, deforestation, overfishing, IUCN Red List and 30 by 30.
For oral examsUse this vocabulary to explain biodiversity as a scientific, ecological, economic and ethical issue: protecting nature also means protecting food, water, health and climate stability.
For essaysMemorise the collocations: to protect biodiversity, to restore ecosystems, to halt biodiversity loss, to curb overfishing, to balance conservation and development.

A. Core biodiversity and ecosystem vocabulary

biodiversity

IPA: /ˌbaɪəʊdaɪˈvɜːsəti/
Pronunciation: BY-oh-dy-VUR-suh-tee
French: biodiversité

Definition: The variety of life on Earth, including species, genes and ecosystems.

Example: Biodiversity is essential for stable ecosystems and human survival.

biological diversity

IPA: /ˌbaɪəˈlɒdʒɪkəl daɪˈvɜːsəti/
Pronunciation: BY-uh-LOJ-i-kuhl dy-VUR-suh-tee
French: diversité biologique

Definition: The full term for biodiversity.

Example: Biological diversity is declining at an alarming rate.

species diversity

IPA: /ˈspiːʃiːz daɪˌvɜːsəti/
Pronunciation: SPEE-sheez dy-VUR-suh-tee
French: diversité des espèces

Definition: The variety of species in a habitat or ecosystem.

Example: Tropical rainforests have high species diversity.

genetic diversity

IPA: /dʒəˌnetɪk daɪˈvɜːsəti/
Pronunciation: juh-NET-ik dy-VUR-suh-tee
French: diversité génétique

Definition: The variety of genes within a species or population.

Example: Genetic diversity helps species adapt to change.

ecosystem diversity

IPA: /ˈiːkəʊˌsɪstəm daɪˈvɜːsəti/
Pronunciation: EE-koh-SIS-tuhm dy-VUR-suh-tee
French: diversité des écosystèmes

Definition: The variety of ecosystems in a region or on Earth.

Example: Ecosystem diversity includes forests, wetlands, oceans and grasslands.

ecosystem

IPA: /ˈiːkəʊˌsɪstəm/
Pronunciation: EE-koh-SIS-tuhm
French: écosystème

Definition: A community of living organisms interacting with their physical environment.

Example: An ecosystem can be disrupted by pollution or climate change.

habitat

IPA: /ˈhæbɪtæt/
Pronunciation: HAB-i-tat
French: habitat

Definition: The natural place where a plant, animal or organism lives.

Example: Habitat destruction is a major cause of extinction.

habitat loss

IPA: /ˈhæbɪtæt lɒs/
Pronunciation: HAB-i-tat loss
French: perte d’habitat

Definition: The destruction or degradation of natural habitats.

Example: Habitat loss threatens thousands of species.

habitat fragmentation

IPA: /ˈhæbɪtæt ˌfræɡmənˈteɪʃən/
Pronunciation: HAB-i-tat frag-muhn-TAY-shuhn
French: fragmentation des habitats

Definition: The breaking of habitats into smaller isolated patches.

Example: Roads and urban sprawl cause habitat fragmentation.

ecosystem services

IPA: /ˈiːkəʊˌsɪstəm ˈsɜːvɪsɪz/
Pronunciation: EE-koh-SIS-tuhm SUR-vis-iz
French: services écosystémiques

Definition: Benefits humans obtain from ecosystems, such as pollination, clean water and carbon storage.

Example: Ecosystem services are often invisible until they collapse.

pollination

IPA: /ˌpɒlɪˈneɪʃən/
Pronunciation: pol-i-NAY-shuhn
French: pollinisation

Definition: The transfer of pollen enabling plants to reproduce.

Example: Pollination is vital for many crops.

pollinator

IPA: /ˈpɒlɪneɪtə/
Pronunciation: POL-i-nay-tuh
French: pollinisateur

Definition: An animal that carries pollen, such as a bee, butterfly or bird.

Example: Pollinators are threatened by pesticides and habitat loss.

food chain

IPA: /ˈfuːd tʃeɪn/
Pronunciation: food chayn
French: chaîne alimentaire

Definition: A sequence of organisms where each is eaten by the next.

Example: Removing one species can disrupt the food chain.

food web

IPA: /ˈfuːd web/
Pronunciation: food web
French: réseau trophique

Definition: A network of feeding relationships in an ecosystem.

Example: A food web is more complex than a simple food chain.

keystone species

IPA: /ˈkiːstəʊn ˌspiːʃiːz/
Pronunciation: KEY-stohn SPEE-sheez
French: espèce clé de voûte

Definition: A species with a crucial role in maintaining an ecosystem.

Example: The loss of a keystone species can transform an ecosystem.

indicator species

IPA: /ˈɪndɪkeɪtə ˌspiːʃiːz/
Pronunciation: IN-di-kay-tuh SPEE-sheez
French: espèce indicatrice

Definition: A species that reveals the health of an ecosystem.

Example: Frogs are often indicator species because they are sensitive to pollution.

endemic species

IPA: /enˈdemɪk ˌspiːʃiːz/
Pronunciation: en-DEM-ik SPEE-sheez
French: espèce endémique

Definition: A species found naturally only in one geographic area.

Example: Island ecosystems often contain many endemic species.

native species

IPA: /ˈneɪtɪv ˌspiːʃiːz/
Pronunciation: NAY-tiv SPEE-sheez
French: espèce indigène

Definition: A species that naturally belongs to a region.

Example: Native species can be threatened by invasive species.

invasive species

IPA: /ɪnˈveɪsɪv ˌspiːʃiːz/
Pronunciation: in-VAY-siv SPEE-sheez
French: espèce invasive

Definition: A non-native species that spreads and harms ecosystems, economies or health.

Example: Invasive species can outcompete native plants and animals.

alien species

IPA: /ˈeɪliən ˌspiːʃiːz/
Pronunciation: AY-lee-uhn SPEE-sheez
French: espèce exotique

Definition: A species introduced outside its natural range.

Example: Not every alien species becomes invasive.

threatened species

IPA: /ˈθretənd ˌspiːʃiːz/
Pronunciation: THRET-uhnd SPEE-sheez
French: espèce menacée

Definition: A species likely to become endangered if pressures continue.

Example: Threatened species need protection before it is too late.

endangered species

IPA: /ɪnˈdeɪndʒəd ˌspiːʃiːz/
Pronunciation: in-DAYN-juhd SPEE-sheez
French: espèce en voie de disparition

Definition: A species at serious risk of extinction.

Example: The tiger is an endangered species.

critically endangered

IPA: /ˌkrɪtɪkəli ɪnˈdeɪndʒəd/
Pronunciation: KRIT-i-kuh-lee in-DAYN-juhd
French: en danger critique d’extinction

Definition: Facing an extremely high risk of extinction in the wild.

Example: Critically endangered species require urgent action.

extinction

IPA: /ɪkˈstɪŋkʃən/
Pronunciation: ik-STINK-shuhn
French: extinction

Definition: The permanent disappearance of a species.

Example: Extinction is irreversible.

mass extinction

IPA: /ˌmæs ɪkˈstɪŋkʃən/
Pronunciation: mass ik-STINK-shuhn
French: extinction de masse

Definition: A period when many species disappear in a relatively short time.

Example: Scientists warn that humans may be driving a sixth mass extinction.

biodiversity loss

IPA: /ˌbaɪəʊdaɪˈvɜːsəti lɒs/
Pronunciation: BY-oh-dy-VUR-suh-tee loss
French: perte de biodiversité

Definition: The decline in the variety and abundance of life.

Example: Biodiversity loss undermines food security and ecosystem resilience.

species decline

IPA: /ˈspiːʃiːz dɪˌklaɪn/
Pronunciation: SPEE-sheez di-KLYNE
French: déclin des espèces

Definition: A reduction in the number or range of a species.

Example: Species decline is often caused by habitat destruction.

population decline

IPA: /ˌpɒpjʊˈleɪʃən dɪˌklaɪn/
Pronunciation: pop-yuh-LAY-shuhn di-KLYNE
French: déclin d’une population

Definition: A fall in the number of individuals in a population.

Example: Bird population decline is visible in many farming landscapes.

ecosystem collapse

IPA: /ˈiːkəʊˌsɪstəm kəˌlæps/
Pronunciation: EE-koh-SIS-tuhm kuh-LAPS
French: effondrement d’un écosystème

Definition: A breakdown in the structure and function of an ecosystem.

Example: Ecosystem collapse can affect water, food and livelihoods.

ecological balance

IPA: /ˌiːkəˈlɒdʒɪkəl ˈbæləns/
Pronunciation: ee-kuh-LOJ-i-kuhl BAL-uhns
French: équilibre écologique

Definition: A stable relationship between organisms and their environment.

Example: Human activity can disrupt ecological balance.

ecological resilience

IPA: /ˌiːkəˈlɒdʒɪkəl rɪˈzɪliəns/
Pronunciation: ee-kuh-LOJ-i-kuhl ri-ZIL-ee-uhns
French: résilience écologique

Definition: The ability of an ecosystem to resist and recover from disturbance.

Example: Biodiversity increases ecological resilience.

conservation

IPA: /ˌkɒnsəˈveɪʃən/
Pronunciation: kon-suh-VAY-shuhn
French: conservation ; protection de la nature

Definition: Protection and management of nature, species and ecosystems.

Example: Conservation requires science, funding and local support.

B. Conservation, restoration and protected areas

wildlife conservation

IPA: /ˈwaɪldlaɪf ˌkɒnsəˈveɪʃən/
Pronunciation: WYLD-life kon-suh-VAY-shuhn
French: conservation de la faune sauvage

Definition: Protection of wild animals and their habitats.

Example: Wildlife conservation can conflict with development pressures.

nature conservation

IPA: /ˈneɪtʃə ˌkɒnsəˈveɪʃən/
Pronunciation: NAY-chuh kon-suh-VAY-shuhn
French: conservation de la nature

Definition: Protection of natural environments and species.

Example: Nature conservation is essential for future generations.

protected area

IPA: /prəˌtektɪd ˈeəriə/
Pronunciation: pruh-TEK-tid AIR-ee-uh
French: aire protégée

Definition: A legally protected place for nature conservation.

Example: Protected areas can preserve habitats and species.

national park

IPA: /ˌnæʃənəl ˈpɑːk/
Pronunciation: NASH-uh-nuhl park
French: parc national

Definition: A protected natural area managed for conservation and public enjoyment.

Example: National parks can protect iconic landscapes.

nature reserve

IPA: /ˈneɪtʃə rɪˌzɜːv/
Pronunciation: NAY-chuh ri-ZURV
French: réserve naturelle

Definition: An area protected for wildlife or habitats.

Example: Nature reserves can protect rare species.

marine protected area

IPA: /məˈriːn prəˌtektɪd ˈeəriə/
Pronunciation: muh-REEN pruh-TEK-tid AIR-ee-uh
French: aire marine protégée

Definition: A protected area in the ocean or sea.

Example: Marine protected areas can help fish populations recover.

wildlife corridor

IPA: /ˈwaɪldlaɪf ˌkɒrɪdɔː/
Pronunciation: WYLD-life KOR-i-dor
French: corridor écologique

Definition: A strip of habitat connecting separated wildlife areas.

Example: Wildlife corridors reduce habitat fragmentation.

ecological corridor

IPA: /ˌiːkəˈlɒdʒɪkəl ˈkɒrɪdɔː/
Pronunciation: ee-kuh-LOJ-i-kuhl KOR-i-dor
French: corridor écologique

Definition: A connection allowing species to move between habitats.

Example: Ecological corridors help animals migrate and reproduce.

rewilding

IPA: /ˌriːˈwaɪldɪŋ/
Pronunciation: ree-WYLD-ing
French: réensauvagement

Definition: Restoring natural processes, often by reintroducing species or reducing human control.

Example: Rewilding can restore damaged ecosystems.

reintroduction

IPA: /ˌriːɪntrəˈdʌkʃən/
Pronunciation: ree-in-truh-DUK-shuhn
French: réintroduction

Definition: The return of a species to an area where it once lived.

Example: The reintroduction of predators can rebalance ecosystems.

restoration

IPA: /ˌrestəˈreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: res-tuh-RAY-shuhn
French: restauration écologique

Definition: The process of repairing damaged ecosystems.

Example: Wetland restoration can reduce flood risk.

ecological restoration

IPA: /ˌiːkəˈlɒdʒɪkəl ˌrestəˈreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: ee-kuh-LOJ-i-kuhl res-tuh-RAY-shuhn
French: restauration écologique

Definition: Repairing ecosystems so they regain function and biodiversity.

Example: Ecological restoration can bring back native species.

afforestation

IPA: /əˌfɒrɪˈsteɪʃən/
Pronunciation: uh-for-i-STAY-shuhn
French: boisement

Definition: Planting trees where there was no recent forest.

Example: Afforestation can store carbon but may harm grassland ecosystems.

reforestation

IPA: /ˌriːfɒrɪˈsteɪʃən/
Pronunciation: ree-for-i-STAY-shuhn
French: reboisement

Definition: Replanting trees in a previously forested area.

Example: Reforestation can restore habitats if done carefully.

deforestation

IPA: /diːˌfɒrɪˈsteɪʃən/
Pronunciation: dee-for-i-STAY-shuhn
French: déforestation

Definition: The clearing or destruction of forests.

Example: Deforestation is a major driver of biodiversity loss.

forest degradation

IPA: /ˈfɒrɪst ˌdeɡrəˈdeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: FOR-ist deg-ruh-DAY-shuhn
French: dégradation des forêts

Definition: The decline in forest quality and ecological function.

Example: Forest degradation can occur even when trees remain.

rainforest

IPA: /ˈreɪnˌfɒrɪst/
Pronunciation: RAYN-for-ist
French: forêt tropicale humide

Definition: A dense forest in a wet tropical or temperate region.

Example: Rainforests contain exceptional biodiversity.

tropical forest

IPA: /ˌtrɒpɪkəl ˈfɒrɪst/
Pronunciation: TROP-i-kuhl FOR-ist
French: forêt tropicale

Definition: A forest found in tropical regions.

Example: Tropical forests store carbon and host many species.

wetland

IPA: /ˈwetlənd/
Pronunciation: WET-land
French: zone humide

Definition: Land such as marshes or swamps that is saturated with water.

Example: Wetlands filter water and store carbon.

mangrove

IPA: /ˈmæŋɡrəʊv/
Pronunciation: MAN-grohv
French: mangrove

Definition: A coastal forest growing in salty water in tropical regions.

Example: Mangroves protect coasts and provide fish nurseries.

coral reef

IPA: /ˈkɒrəl riːf/
Pronunciation: KOR-uhl reef
French: récif corallien

Definition: A marine ecosystem built by corals, rich in biodiversity.

Example: Coral reefs are threatened by warming and ocean acidification.

grassland

IPA: /ˈɡrɑːslænd/
Pronunciation: GRASS-land
French: prairie ; savane herbeuse

Definition: An ecosystem dominated by grasses.

Example: Grasslands support many grazing animals and birds.

soil biodiversity

IPA: /ˈsɔɪl ˌbaɪəʊdaɪˈvɜːsəti/
Pronunciation: soil BY-oh-dy-VUR-suh-tee
French: biodiversité des sols

Definition: The variety of organisms living in soil.

Example: Soil biodiversity is essential for fertile land.

microbial diversity

IPA: /maɪˌkrəʊbiəl daɪˈvɜːsəti/
Pronunciation: my-KROH-bee-uhl dy-VUR-suh-tee
French: diversité microbienne

Definition: The variety of microorganisms in an ecosystem.

Example: Microbial diversity is crucial for nutrient cycles.

insect decline

IPA: /ˈɪnsekt dɪˌklaɪn/
Pronunciation: IN-sekt di-KLYNE
French: déclin des insectes

Definition: A reduction in insect populations and diversity.

Example: Insect decline threatens pollination and food webs.

C. Threats to biodiversity

pollinator decline

IPA: /ˈpɒlɪneɪtə dɪˌklaɪn/
Pronunciation: POL-i-nay-tuh di-KLYNE
French: déclin des pollinisateurs

Definition: A reduction in pollinator populations.

Example: Pollinator decline threatens fruit and vegetable production.

overexploitation

IPA: /ˌəʊvərˌeksplɔɪˈteɪʃən/
Pronunciation: oh-vuh-ek-sploy-TAY-shuhn
French: surexploitation

Definition: Unsustainable use of natural resources or species.

Example: Overexploitation can push species towards extinction.

overfishing

IPA: /ˌəʊvəˈfɪʃɪŋ/
Pronunciation: oh-vuh-FISH-ing
French: surpêche

Definition: Catching fish faster than populations can recover.

Example: Overfishing damages marine ecosystems.

poaching

IPA: /ˈpəʊtʃɪŋ/
Pronunciation: POH-ching
French: braconnage

Definition: Illegal hunting, capturing or killing of wildlife.

Example: Poaching threatens elephants and rhinos.

illegal wildlife trade

IPA: /ɪˈliːɡəl ˈwaɪldlaɪf treɪd/
Pronunciation: i-LEE-guhl WYLD-life trade
French: trafic illégal d’espèces sauvages

Definition: The illegal buying and selling of wild animals or plants.

Example: Illegal wildlife trade fuels organised crime.

bushmeat

IPA: /ˈbʊʃmiːt/
Pronunciation: BUUSH-meet
French: viande de brousse

Definition: Meat from wild animals, often hunted in forests.

Example: Unsustainable bushmeat hunting can reduce wildlife populations.

bycatch

IPA: /ˈbaɪkætʃ/
Pronunciation: BY-katch
French: prises accessoires

Definition: Animals unintentionally caught during fishing.

Example: Dolphins and turtles can die as bycatch.

bottom trawling

IPA: /ˌbɒtəm ˈtrɔːlɪŋ/
Pronunciation: BOT-uhm TRAWL-ing
French: chalutage de fond

Definition: Fishing by dragging heavy nets along the seabed.

Example: Bottom trawling destroys seafloor habitats.

pesticide

IPA: /ˈpestɪsaɪd/
Pronunciation: PES-ti-syde
French: pesticide

Definition: A chemical used to kill pests.

Example: Pesticides can harm insects and birds.

herbicide

IPA: /ˈhɜːbɪsaɪd/
Pronunciation: HUR-bi-syde
French: herbicide

Definition: A chemical used to kill unwanted plants.

Example: Herbicides can reduce plant diversity.

fertiliser runoff

IPA: /ˈfɜːtəlaɪzə ˈrʌnɒf/
Pronunciation: FUR-tuh-ly-zuh RUN-off
French: ruissellement d’engrais

Definition: Fertiliser washed from land into rivers or seas.

Example: Fertiliser runoff can cause algal blooms.

eutrophication

IPA: /juːˌtrɒfɪˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: yoo-trof-i-KAY-shuhn
French: eutrophisation

Definition: Excess nutrients in water causing algal growth and oxygen loss.

Example: Eutrophication can create dead zones.

dead zone

IPA: /ˈded zəʊn/
Pronunciation: dead zone
French: zone morte

Definition: A water area with too little oxygen to support most marine life.

Example: Dead zones often result from fertiliser runoff.

plastic pollution

IPA: /ˈplæstɪk pəˌluːʃən/
Pronunciation: PLAS-tik puh-LOO-shuhn
French: pollution plastique

Definition: Pollution caused by plastic waste.

Example: Plastic pollution harms marine animals.

microplastic

IPA: /ˌmaɪkrəʊˈplæstɪk/
Pronunciation: MY-kroh-PLAS-tik
French: microplastique

Definition: Tiny plastic particles that persist in the environment.

Example: Microplastics have been found in oceans, soil and animals.

chemical pollution

IPA: /ˈkemɪkəl pəˌluːʃən/
Pronunciation: KEM-i-kuhl puh-LOO-shuhn
French: pollution chimique

Definition: Pollution caused by harmful chemicals.

Example: Chemical pollution can damage reproduction in wildlife.

light pollution

IPA: /ˈlaɪt pəˌluːʃən/
Pronunciation: light puh-LOO-shuhn
French: pollution lumineuse

Definition: Excess artificial light disrupting ecosystems and night skies.

Example: Light pollution affects insects, birds and bats.

noise pollution

IPA: /ˈnɔɪz pəˌluːʃən/
Pronunciation: noyz puh-LOO-shuhn
French: pollution sonore

Definition: Unwanted or harmful noise in the environment.

Example: Noise pollution can disturb marine mammals.

climate change

IPA: /ˈklaɪmət tʃeɪndʒ/
Pronunciation: KLY-muht chaynj
French: changement climatique

Definition: Long-term changes in climate, largely driven today by greenhouse gas emissions.

Example: Climate change is a major driver of biodiversity loss.

global warming

IPA: /ˌɡləʊbəl ˈwɔːmɪŋ/
Pronunciation: GLOH-buhl WAR-ming
French: réchauffement climatique

Definition: The rise in Earth’s average temperature.

Example: Global warming pushes species out of their usual ranges.

ocean acidification

IPA: /ˈəʊʃən əˌsɪdɪfɪˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: OH-shuhn uh-sid-i-fi-KAY-shuhn
French: acidification des océans

Definition: The decrease in ocean pH caused by absorbed CO2.

Example: Ocean acidification weakens coral reefs and shellfish.

range shift

IPA: /ˈreɪndʒ ʃɪft/
Pronunciation: raynj shift
French: déplacement de l’aire de répartition

Definition: A change in the geographic area where a species lives.

Example: Climate change causes range shifts in many species.

migration pattern

IPA: /maɪˈɡreɪʃən ˌpætən/
Pronunciation: my-GRAY-shuhn PAT-uhn
French: schéma migratoire

Definition: The regular movement pattern of animals between areas.

Example: Climate change can disrupt migration patterns.

phenology

IPA: /fɪˈnɒlədʒi/
Pronunciation: fi-NOL-uh-jee
French: phénologie

Definition: The timing of seasonal biological events, such as flowering or migration.

Example: Climate change alters phenology.

phenological mismatch

IPA: /ˌfiːnəˈlɒdʒɪkəl ˈmɪsmætʃ/
Pronunciation: FEE-noh-LOJ-i-kuhl MIS-match
French: désynchronisation phénologique

Definition: A timing mismatch between interacting species caused by environmental change.

Example: Phenological mismatch can reduce breeding success.

nature-based solution

IPA: /ˈneɪtʃə beɪst səˌluːʃən/
Pronunciation: NAY-chuh-bayst suh-LOO-shuhn
French: solution fondée sur la nature

Definition: Using natural systems to address environmental or social problems.

Example: Mangrove restoration is a nature-based solution.

biodiversity hotspot

IPA: /ˌbaɪəʊdaɪˈvɜːsəti ˈhɒtspɒt/
Pronunciation: BY-oh-dy-VUR-suh-tee HOT-spot
French: point chaud de biodiversité

Definition: A region with exceptional biodiversity under serious threat.

Example: Biodiversity hotspots are conservation priorities.

IUCN Red List

IPA: /ˌaɪ juː siː ˈen red lɪst/
Pronunciation: eye-yoo-see-en red list
French: liste rouge de l’UICN

Definition: A global list assessing species’ extinction risk.

Example: The IUCN Red List tracks endangered species.

IUCN

IPA: /ˌaɪ juː siː ˈen/
Pronunciation: eye-yoo-see-en
French: UICN

Definition: International Union for Conservation of Nature.

Example: The IUCN provides conservation assessments and guidance.

Convention on Biological Diversity

IPA: /kənˌvenʃən ɒn ˌbaɪəˈlɒdʒɪkəl daɪˈvɜːsəti/

French: Convention sur la diversité biologique

Example: The Convention on Biological Diversity guides global biodiversity policy.

CBD

IPA: /ˌsiː biː ˈdiː/
Pronunciation: see-bee-dee
French: CDB

Definition: Abbreviation for Convention on Biological Diversity.

Example: The CBD sets international biodiversity goals.

Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework

IPA: /ˌkʊnmɪŋ ˌmɒntriˈɔːl ˈɡləʊbəl ˌbaɪəʊdaɪˈvɜːsəti ˈfreɪmwɜːk/
Pronunciation: KOON-ming mon-tree-AWL GLOH-buhl BY-oh-dy-VUR-suh-tee FRAYM-work
French: cadre mondial de la biodiversité de Kunming-Montréal

Definition: A global agreement adopted to halt and reverse biodiversity loss.

Example: The Kunming-Montreal Framework includes the 30 by 30 target.

30 by 30

IPA: /ˌθɜːti baɪ ˈθɜːti/

French: objectif 30 x 30

Definition: A target to protect 30% of land and sea by 2030.

natural capital

IPA: /ˌnætʃərəl ˈkæpɪtəl/
Pronunciation: NAT-chuh-ruhl KAP-i-tuhl

ecological footprint

IPA: /ˌiːkəˈlɒdʒɪkəl ˈfʊtprɪnt/
Pronunciation: ee-kuh-LOJ-i-kuhl FOOT-print
French: empreinte écologique

Definition: The environmental impact of human consumption and activity.

IPA: /ˌɜːbən ˈsprɔːl/

monoculture

agroecology

Pronunciation: suh-STAY-nuh-buhl AG-ri-kul-chuh

Definition: Farming that protects resources, ecosystems and future production.

regenerative agriculture

IPA: /rɪˌdʒenərətɪv ˈæɡrɪkʌltʃə/
