Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse political parties, ideological labels, internal divisions, party discipline, polarisation and political strategy. Each entry includes the English term, IPA phonetics, a practical pronunciation guide, an English definition, a French translation, and an exam-ready example sentence.

How to use this chapter

For press articles Focus on terms such as ruling party, opposition party, party platform, grassroots, faction and party line.
For oral exams Use the ideology words carefully. Avoid saying only “good” or “bad”: explain whether a party is centre-left, right-wing, populist, mainstream or fringe.
For essays Memorise the collocations: to shift to the right, to broaden one’s appeal, to alienate voters, to follow the party line.

A. Parties and party organisation

political party

IPA: /pəˈlɪtɪkəl ˈpɑːti/
Pronunciation: puh-LIT-i-kuhl PAR-tee
French: parti politique

Definition: An organised group of people who share political ideas and try to win power through elections.

Example: Political parties compete to win elections and shape public policy.

party system

IPA: /ˈpɑːti ˈsɪstəm/
Pronunciation: PAR-tee SIS-tuhm
French: système partisan

Definition: The way political parties are organised and compete within a country.

Example: Britain’s party system has become more fragmented in recent years.

two-party system

IPA: /ˌtuː ˈpɑːti ˈsɪstəm/
Pronunciation: too PAR-tee SIS-tuhm
French: bipartisme ; système bipartisan

Definition: A political system dominated by two major parties.

Example: The United States is often described as a two-party system.

multi-party system

IPA: /ˌmʌlti ˈpɑːti ˈsɪstəm/
Pronunciation: MUL-tee PAR-tee SIS-tuhm
French: multipartisme

Definition: A political system in which several parties have a realistic chance of influencing government.

Example: A multi-party system can make coalition governments more likely.

ruling party

IPA: /ˈruːlɪŋ ˈpɑːti/
Pronunciation: ROO-ling PAR-tee
French: parti au pouvoir

Definition: The political party currently in power.

Example: The ruling party defended its economic record.

opposition party

IPA: /ˌɒpəˈzɪʃən ˈpɑːti/
Pronunciation: op-uh-ZISH-uhn PAR-tee
French: parti d’opposition

Definition: A political party that is not in government and criticises or challenges the ruling party.

Example: Opposition parties accused the government of ignoring the crisis.

coalition

IPA: /ˌkəʊəˈlɪʃən/
Pronunciation: koh-uh-LISH-uhn
French: coalition

Definition: An alliance between political parties or groups, often formed to govern together.

Example: The election result forced the parties to form a coalition.

grassroots

IPA: /ˈɡrɑːsruːts/
Pronunciation: GRASS-roots
French: base militante ; militants de terrain

Definition: Ordinary members or supporters of a political movement, rather than its leaders.

Example: Grassroots activists helped the campaign gain momentum.

party membership

IPA: /ˈpɑːti ˈmembəʃɪp/
Pronunciation: PAR-tee MEM-buh-ship
French: adhérents d’un parti

Definition: The group of people who officially belong to a political party.

Example: Party membership has declined in many Western democracies.

party leader

IPA: /ˈpɑːti ˈliːdə/
Pronunciation: PAR-tee LEE-duh
French: chef de parti ; dirigeant de parti

Definition: The person who leads a political party.

Example: The party leader promised to rebuild public trust.

rank-and-file members

IPA: /ˌræŋk ən ˈfaɪl ˈmembəz/
Pronunciation: rank uhn FILE MEM-buhz
French: militants de base ; membres ordinaires

Definition: Ordinary members of a party or organisation, not its leaders.

Example: Rank-and-file members criticised the leadership’s decision.

party conference

IPA: /ˈpɑːti ˈkɒnfərəns/
Pronunciation: PAR-tee KON-fuh-ruhns
French: congrès d’un parti

Definition: A large formal meeting where a political party discusses policies and strategy.

Example: The prime minister delivered a major speech at the party conference.

manifesto

IPA: /ˌmænɪˈfestəʊ/
Pronunciation: man-i-FES-toh
French: programme électoral ; manifeste

Definition: A public document in which a political party presents its ideas, promises and programme.

Example: The manifesto included pledges on taxation, housing and climate policy.

party platform

IPA: /ˈpɑːti ˈplætfɔːm/
Pronunciation: PAR-tee PLAT-form
French: programme politique ; plateforme d’un parti

Definition: The official set of policies and principles supported by a party.

Example: The party platform moved further to the right on immigration.

policy

IPA: /ˈpɒləsi/
Pronunciation: POL-uh-see
French: politique ; mesure ; ligne politique

Definition: A plan or course of action adopted by a government, party or institution.

Example: The party’s housing policy was popular among young voters.

ideology

IPA: /ˌaɪdiˈɒlədʒi/
Pronunciation: eye-dee-OL-uh-jee
French: idéologie

Definition: A set of political beliefs or principles about how society should be organised.

Example: Political ideology shapes how parties view the role of the state.

B. Ideologies and the political spectrum

left-wing

IPA: /ˌleft ˈwɪŋ/
Pronunciation: left wing
French: de gauche

Definition: Supporting social equality, public services and a more active role for the state in the economy.

Example: Left-wing parties usually support stronger welfare policies.

right-wing

IPA: /ˌraɪt ˈwɪŋ/
Pronunciation: right wing
French: de droite

Definition: Supporting tradition, free markets, national identity or limited state intervention, depending on context.

Example: Right-wing parties often call for lower taxes and stricter law enforcement.

centre-left

IPA: /ˌsentə ˈleft/
Pronunciation: SEN-tuh left
French: centre gauche

Definition: Moderately left-wing; usually supportive of welfare and equality but within a market economy.

Example: The centre-left party promised to protect public services.

centre-right

IPA: /ˌsentə ˈraɪt/
Pronunciation: SEN-tuh right
French: centre droit

Definition: Moderately right-wing; usually supportive of free markets, fiscal discipline and gradual reform.

Example: The centre-right candidate appealed to business leaders.

the political spectrum

IPA: /ðə pəˈlɪtɪkəl ˈspektrəm/
Pronunciation: thuh puh-LIT-i-kuhl SPEK-truhm
French: échiquier politique ; spectre politique

Definition: The range of political positions from left to right, including moderate and extreme views.

Example: The issue divides voters across the political spectrum.

moderate

IPA: /ˈmɒdərət/
Pronunciation: MOD-uh-ruht
French: modéré

Definition: Having political views that are not extreme.

Example: Moderate voters may reject radical proposals.

centrist

IPA: /ˈsentrɪst/
Pronunciation: SEN-trist
French: centriste

Definition: A person or party with moderate political views between the left and the right.

Example: The centrist party tried to attract voters tired of polarisation.

radical

IPA: /ˈrædɪkəl/
Pronunciation: RAD-i-kuhl
French: radical

Definition: Supporting deep or fundamental political change.

Example: Radical movements often emerge during periods of crisis.

extremist

IPA: /ɪkˈstriːmɪst/
Pronunciation: ik-STREEM-ist
French: extrémiste

Definition: A person or group with political views far outside the mainstream, often rejecting democratic norms.

Example: Extremist groups exploited anger and economic insecurity.

mainstream

IPA: /ˈmeɪnstriːm/
Pronunciation: MAYN-streem
French: dominant ; traditionnel ; grand public

Definition: Considered normal, dominant or widely accepted in society or politics.

Example: The idea has moved from the margins into mainstream politics.

fringe party

IPA: /ˈfrɪndʒ ˈpɑːti/
Pronunciation: frinj PAR-tee
French: parti marginal

Definition: A small party with views outside the political mainstream.

Example: The fringe party gained attention through social media.

populism

IPA: /ˈpɒpjəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: POP-yuh-liz-uhm
French: populisme

Definition: A political style that claims to defend ordinary people against corrupt elites.

Example: Populism often presents politics as a battle between the people and the elite.

populist

IPA: /ˈpɒpjəlɪst/
Pronunciation: POP-yuh-list
French: populiste

Definition: A politician, party or movement that uses populist language or ideas.

Example: The populist leader promised to speak for ordinary citizens.

nationalism

IPA: /ˈnæʃənəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: NASH-uh-nuh-liz-uhm
French: nationalisme

Definition: A political belief that emphasises national identity, sovereignty and loyalty to the nation.

Example: Nationalism can unite people but can also fuel exclusion.

patriotism

IPA: /ˈpætriətɪzəm/
Pronunciation: PAT-ree-uh-tiz-uhm
French: patriotisme

Definition: Love of and loyalty to one’s country, usually without necessarily rejecting others.

Example: The speech appealed to patriotism rather than hostility to foreigners.

conservatism

IPA: /kənˈsɜːvətɪzəm/
Pronunciation: kuhn-SUR-vuh-tiz-uhm
French: conservatisme

Definition: A political ideology that values tradition, stability, gradual change and often limited government.

Example: Conservatism often stresses order, family and national continuity.

liberalism

IPA: /ˈlɪbərəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: LIB-uh-ruh-liz-uhm
French: libéralisme

Definition: A political ideology that emphasises individual rights, freedoms, democracy and the rule of law.

Example: Liberalism places strong emphasis on civil liberties.

socialism

IPA: /ˈsəʊʃəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuh-liz-uhm
French: socialisme

Definition: A political ideology that supports greater economic equality and public or collective control of key resources.

Example: Socialism has historically called for stronger workers’ rights.

C. Major political traditions

social democracy

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl dɪˈmɒkrəsi/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl di-MOK-ruh-see
French: social-démocratie

Definition: A moderate left-wing ideology that supports democracy, welfare policies and a regulated market economy.

Example: Social democracy seeks to combine capitalism with social protection.

libertarianism

IPA: /ˌlɪbəˈteəriənɪzəm/
Pronunciation: lib-uh-TAIR-ee-uh-niz-uhm
French: libertarianisme

Definition: A political ideology that emphasises individual freedom, limited government and free markets.

Example: Libertarianism is sceptical of state intervention.

neoliberalism

IPA: /ˌniːəʊˈlɪbərəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: nee-oh-LIB-uh-ruh-liz-uhm
French: néolibéralisme

Definition: An ideology favouring free markets, deregulation, privatisation and a reduced role for the state in the economy.

Example: Critics blame neoliberalism for rising inequality.

environmentalism

IPA: /ɪnˌvaɪrənˈmentəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: in-vy-ruhn-MEN-tuh-liz-uhm
French: écologisme ; environnementalisme

Definition: A political and social movement that seeks to protect the environment.

Example: Environmentalism has become a major force in European politics.

green politics

IPA: /ˌɡriːn ˈpɒlɪtɪks/
Pronunciation: green POL-i-tiks
French: politique écologiste

Definition: Political ideas focused on environmental protection, sustainability and climate action.

Example: Green politics has pushed climate change higher on the political agenda.

feminism

IPA: /ˈfemɪnɪzəm/
Pronunciation: FEM-i-niz-uhm
French: féminisme

Definition: A movement and ideology supporting equal rights and opportunities for women and men.

Example: Feminism has reshaped debates on work, family and political representation.

identity politics

IPA: /aɪˈdentəti ˈpɒlɪtɪks/
Pronunciation: eye-DEN-tuh-tee POL-i-tiks
French: politique identitaire

Definition: Political positions based on the interests or experiences of particular identity groups.

Example: Critics and supporters disagree sharply over the role of identity politics.

culture wars

IPA: /ˈkʌltʃə wɔːz/
Pronunciation: KUL-chuh wawz
French: guerres culturelles

Definition: Political conflicts over values, identity, education, religion, gender or national history.

Example: Culture wars have become a central feature of American politics.

progressive

IPA: /prəˈɡresɪv/
Pronunciation: pruh-GRES-iv
French: progressiste

Definition: Supporting social reform, equality and change, especially on social issues.

Example: Progressive voters demanded stronger action on climate change.

reactionary

IPA: /riˈækʃənəri/
Pronunciation: ree-AK-shuh-nuh-ree
French: réactionnaire

Definition: Opposed to social or political change and wishing to return to a previous order.

Example: The policy was criticised as reactionary by civil rights groups.

hardline

IPA: /ˈhɑːdlaɪn/
Pronunciation: HARD-line
French: intransigeant ; dur

Definition: Taking a strict, uncompromising political position.

Example: The government adopted a hardline stance on immigration.

hawkish

IPA: /ˈhɔːkɪʃ/
Pronunciation: HAW-kish
French: belliciste ; partisan d’une ligne dure

Definition: Supporting strong military action or a tough foreign policy.

Example: Hawkish lawmakers called for tougher sanctions.

D. Political attitudes and divisions

dovish

IPA: /ˈdʌvɪʃ/
Pronunciation: DUV-ish
French: pacifique ; partisan de la conciliation

Definition: Supporting diplomacy, compromise or a softer foreign policy.

Example: Dovish politicians warned against military escalation.

pragmatic

IPA: /præɡˈmætɪk/
Pronunciation: prag-MAT-ik
French: pragmatique

Definition: Focused on practical results rather than ideology.

Example: The mayor took a pragmatic approach to the housing crisis.

polarisation

IPA: /ˌpəʊləraɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: poh-luh-ry-ZAY-shuhn
French: polarisation

Definition: The process by which political opinions become more divided and extreme.

Example: Political polarisation has made compromise more difficult.

partisanship

IPA: /ˈpɑːtizənʃɪp/
Pronunciation: PAR-ti-zuhn-ship
French: esprit partisan ; partisanerie

Definition: Strong loyalty to a political party, sometimes at the expense of fairness or compromise.

Example: Extreme partisanship can weaken trust in institutions.

tribalism

IPA: /ˈtraɪbəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: TRY-buh-liz-uhm
French: tribalisme politique

Definition: A strong loyalty to one’s political group, often leading to hostility towards opponents.

Example: Social media can intensify political tribalism.

bipartisanship

IPA: /ˌbaɪˈpɑːtizənʃɪp/
Pronunciation: bye-PAR-ti-zuhn-ship
French: coopération bipartisane

Definition: Cooperation between two major political parties.

Example: The reform passed thanks to rare bipartisanship.

cross-party support

IPA: /ˌkrɒs ˈpɑːti səˈpɔːt/
Pronunciation: kross PAR-tee suh-PORT
French: soutien transpartisan

Definition: Support from members of different political parties.

Example: The bill received cross-party support.

party discipline

IPA: /ˈpɑːti ˈdɪsəplɪn/
Pronunciation: PAR-tee DIS-uh-plin
French: discipline de parti

Definition: The pressure on elected representatives to vote according to their party’s position.

Example: Party discipline prevented many MPs from rebelling.

party line

IPA: /ˈpɑːti laɪn/
Pronunciation: PAR-tee line
French: ligne du parti

Definition: The official position of a political party on an issue.

Example: Several lawmakers refused to follow the party line.

backbencher

IPA: /ˌbækˈbentʃə/
Pronunciation: bak-BEN-chuh
French: député de base ; parlementaire sans fonction ministérielle

Definition: An ordinary MP who is not a minister or party leader, especially in the UK.

Example: Backbenchers put pressure on the prime minister to change course.

whip

IPA: /wɪp/
Pronunciation: wip
French: whip ; responsable de discipline parlementaire

Definition: A party official responsible for ensuring that elected representatives vote according to the party line.

Example: The party whip urged MPs to support the bill.

rebel MP

IPA: /ˈrebəl ˌem ˈpiː/
Pronunciation: REB-uhl em pee
French: député dissident

Definition: A Member of Parliament who votes against their own party’s position.

Example: Several rebel MPs opposed the government’s plan.

E. Party discipline, factions and strategic verbs

defection

IPA: /dɪˈfekʃən/
Pronunciation: di-FEK-shuhn
French: défection ; changement de camp

Definition: The act of leaving one political party to join another or become independent.

Example: The defection embarrassed the party leadership.

faction

IPA: /ˈfækʃən/
Pronunciation: FAK-shuhn
French: faction ; courant interne

Definition: A smaller group within a party that has its own views or interests.

Example: The party is divided between moderate and radical factions.

split

IPA: /splɪt/
Pronunciation: split
French: scission ; division

Definition: A serious division within a party, movement or group.

Example: The leadership contest exposed a deep split within the party.

realignment

IPA: /ˌriːəˈlaɪnmənt/
Pronunciation: ree-uh-LYNE-muhnt
French: recomposition politique ; réalignement

Definition: A major and lasting change in the support base of parties or political groups.

Example: The election signalled a realignment of working-class voters.

to shift to the right

IPA: /ʃɪft tə ðə ˈraɪt/
Pronunciation: shift tuh thuh right
French: se déplacer vers la droite

Definition: To adopt more right-wing positions.

Example: The party shifted to the right on immigration.

to move to the centre

IPA: /muːv tə ðə ˈsentə/
Pronunciation: moov tuh thuh SEN-tuh
French: se recentrer

Definition: To adopt more moderate positions.

Example: After losing the election, the party tried to move to the centre.

to appeal to voters

IPA: /əˈpiːl tə ˈvəʊtəz/
Pronunciation: uh-PEEL tuh VOH-tuhz
French: séduire les électeurs ; parler aux électeurs

Definition: To attract or persuade voters.

Example: The party tried to appeal to younger voters.

to alienate voters

IPA: /ˈeɪliəneɪt ˈvəʊtəz/
Pronunciation: AY-lee-uh-nayt VOH-tuhz
French: aliéner ; se mettre à dos les électeurs

Definition: To make voters feel rejected, ignored or hostile.

Example: The policy alienated many moderate voters.

to broaden one’s appeal

IPA: /ˈbrɔːdən wʌnz əˈpiːl/
Pronunciation: BRAW-duhn wunz uh-PEEL
French: élargir son électorat ; élargir son audience

Definition: To become attractive to a wider range of voters.

Example: The candidate tried to broaden her appeal beyond the party base.
