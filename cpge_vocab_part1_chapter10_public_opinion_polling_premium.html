Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse polls, public opinion, approval ratings, voter behaviour, polling methodology, social divides and changes in political mood. Each entry includes the English term, IPA phonetics, a practical pronunciation guide, an English definition, a French translation, and an exam-ready example sentence.

How to use this chapter

For press articles Focus on terms such as margin of error, approval rating, voting intention, too close to call, swing voters, public mood and trust in institutions.
For oral exams Use this vocabulary to avoid vague comments like “people think”. Explain what polls measure, who was surveyed, and how public opinion changes.
For essays Memorise the collocations: to lead in the polls, to close the gap, to shape public opinion, to sway voters, to misread public opinion.

A. Polling basics and methodology

public opinion

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk əˈpɪnjən/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik uh-PIN-yuhn
French: opinion publique

Definition: The views and attitudes held by the public on political or social issues.

Example: Public opinion shifted after the televised debate.

poll

IPA: /pəʊl/
Pronunciation: pohl
French: sondage

Definition: A survey used to measure public opinion, especially before an election.

Example: The latest poll shows the two candidates are tied.

opinion poll

IPA: /əˈpɪnjən pəʊl/
Pronunciation: uh-PIN-yuhn pohl
French: sondage d’opinion

Definition: A survey asking people what they think about a political issue, party or candidate.

Example: An opinion poll suggested growing support for the reform.

polling

IPA: /ˈpəʊlɪŋ/
Pronunciation: POH-ling
French: sondages ; pratique du sondage

Definition: The activity of conducting polls or measuring public opinion.

Example: Polling has become central to modern political campaigns.

pollster

IPA: /ˈpəʊlstə/
Pronunciation: POHL-stuh
French: sondeur ; institut de sondage

Definition: A person or organisation that conducts opinion polls.

Example: Pollsters underestimated support for the challenger.

survey

IPA: /ˈsɜːveɪ/
Pronunciation: SUR-vay
French: enquête ; sondage

Definition: A set of questions used to collect information from a group of people.

Example: The survey asked voters about healthcare and inflation.

questionnaire

IPA: /ˌkwestʃəˈneə/
Pronunciation: kwes-chuh-NAIR
French: questionnaire

Definition: A written or online list of questions used to gather information.

Example: The questionnaire included questions about voting intentions.

sample

IPA: /ˈsɑːmpəl/
Pronunciation: SAM-puhl
French: échantillon

Definition: A smaller group of people selected to represent a larger population.

Example: A poll is only reliable if the sample is representative.

representative sample

IPA: /ˌreprɪˈzentətɪv ˈsɑːmpəl/
Pronunciation: rep-ri-ZEN-tuh-tiv SAM-puhl
French: échantillon représentatif

Definition: A sample that reflects the characteristics of the wider population.

Example: The poll used a representative sample of 2,000 adults.

sample size

IPA: /ˈsɑːmpəl saɪz/
Pronunciation: SAM-puhl syze
French: taille de l’échantillon

Definition: The number of people included in a survey or poll.

Example: A larger sample size can reduce statistical uncertainty.

respondent

IPA: /rɪˈspɒndənt/
Pronunciation: ri-SPON-duhnt
French: répondant ; personne interrogée

Definition: A person who answers questions in a survey or poll.

Example: Only 34% of respondents trusted the government.

demographic group

IPA: /ˌdeməˈɡræfɪk ɡruːp/
Pronunciation: dem-uh-GRAF-ik group
French: groupe démographique

Definition: A category of people defined by features such as age, gender, income, education or location.

Example: The candidate performed well among younger demographic groups.

breakdown

IPA: /ˈbreɪkdaʊn/
Pronunciation: BRAYK-down
French: répartition ; ventilation des résultats

Definition: A detailed analysis showing how results differ between groups.

Example: The poll included a breakdown by age and region.

margin of error

IPA: /ˌmɑːdʒɪn əv ˈerə/
Pronunciation: MAR-jin ov AIR-uh
French: marge d’erreur

Definition: The amount by which poll results may differ from the true value in the population.

Example: The lead is within the margin of error.

confidence level

IPA: /ˈkɒnfɪdəns ˈlevəl/
Pronunciation: KON-fi-duhns LEV-uhl
French: niveau de confiance

Definition: A statistical measure showing how likely it is that poll results fall within the margin of error.

Example: The poll had a 95% confidence level.

weighting

IPA: /ˈweɪtɪŋ/
Pronunciation: WAY-ting
French: pondération

Definition: A statistical adjustment used to make survey results better reflect the population.

Example: Weighting can correct imbalances in a polling sample.

methodology

IPA: /ˌmeθəˈdɒlədʒi/
Pronunciation: meth-uh-DOL-uh-jee
French: méthodologie

Definition: The methods and procedures used to conduct a survey or poll.

Example: The poll’s methodology was criticised by analysts.

data

IPA: /ˈdeɪtə/
Pronunciation: DAY-tuh
French: données

Definition: Information collected for analysis, especially numbers or survey responses.

Example: The data show a sharp decline in trust.

raw data

IPA: /ˌrɔː ˈdeɪtə/
Pronunciation: raw DAY-tuh
French: données brutes

Definition: Data before it has been processed or adjusted.

Example: The raw data were later adjusted through weighting.

cross-tabulation

IPA: /ˌkrɒs ˌtæbjʊˈleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: kross tab-yuh-LAY-shuhn
French: tableau croisé

Definition: A table showing how answers vary across different categories or groups.

Example: The cross-tabulation showed a gender gap in voting intentions.

trend

IPA: /trend/
Pronunciation: trend
French: tendance

Definition: A general direction of change over time.

Example: The trend suggests rising concern about climate change.

long-term trend

IPA: /ˌlɒŋ tɜːm ˈtrend/
Pronunciation: long term trend
French: tendance de long terme

Definition: A pattern of change observed over a long period.

Example: The long-term trend shows declining party loyalty.

snapshot

IPA: /ˈsnæpʃɒt/
Pronunciation: SNAP-shot
French: instantané ; photographie à un moment donné

Definition: A picture of public opinion at one moment in time.

Example: A poll is only a snapshot, not a prediction.

B. Approval, voting intentions and electoral uncertainty

approval rating

IPA: /əˈpruːvəl ˈreɪtɪŋ/
Pronunciation: uh-PROO-vuhl RAY-ting
French: taux d’approbation ; cote de popularité

Definition: The percentage of people who approve of a leader, government or institution.

Example: The president’s approval rating fell after the scandal.

disapproval rating

IPA: /ˌdɪsəˈpruːvəl ˈreɪtɪŋ/
Pronunciation: dis-uh-PROO-vuhl RAY-ting
French: taux de désapprobation

Definition: The percentage of people who disapprove of a leader, government or institution.

Example: The prime minister’s disapproval rating reached a record high.

net approval

IPA: /ˌnet əˈpruːvəl/
Pronunciation: net uh-PROO-vuhl
French: solde d’approbation

Definition: The difference between approval and disapproval ratings.

Example: The leader’s net approval turned negative.

favourability rating

IPA: /ˌfeɪvərəˈbɪləti ˈreɪtɪŋ/
Pronunciation: fay-vuh-ruh-BIL-uh-tee RAY-ting
French: cote de sympathie ; image favorable

Definition: A measure of whether people have a positive or negative view of a person, party or institution.

Example: The candidate’s favourability rating improved after the debate.

trust rating

IPA: /ˈtrʌst ˈreɪtɪŋ/
Pronunciation: trust RAY-ting
French: niveau de confiance

Definition: A measure of how much people trust a person, party or institution.

Example: The media’s trust rating has declined in many countries.

voting intention

IPA: /ˈvəʊtɪŋ ɪnˌtenʃən/
Pronunciation: VOH-ting in-TEN-shuhn
French: intention de vote

Definition: The candidate or party a voter says they plan to support.

Example: Voting intention polls showed a narrow lead for the opposition.

likely voter

IPA: /ˌlaɪkli ˈvəʊtə/
Pronunciation: LYK-lee VOH-tuh
French: électeur probable

Definition: A person considered likely to vote in an upcoming election.

Example: Polls of likely voters may differ from polls of all adults.

registered voter

IPA: /ˌredʒɪstəd ˈvəʊtə/
Pronunciation: REJ-i-stuhd VOH-tuh
French: électeur inscrit

Definition: A person officially registered to vote.

Example: The poll surveyed registered voters only.

undecided voter

IPA: /ˌʌndɪˈsaɪdɪd ˈvəʊtə/
Pronunciation: un-di-SY-did VOH-tuh
French: électeur indécis

Definition: A voter who has not yet chosen a candidate or party.

Example: Undecided voters could decide the election.

swing voter

IPA: /ˈswɪŋ ˌvəʊtə/
Pronunciation: swing VOH-tuh
French: électeur flottant ; électeur indécis

Definition: A voter who may switch between parties or candidates.

Example: Both parties targeted swing voters in suburban areas.

floating voter

IPA: /ˌfləʊtɪŋ ˈvəʊtə/
Pronunciation: FLOH-ting VOH-tuh
French: électeur flottant

Definition: A voter who does not consistently support the same party.

Example: Floating voters are crucial in close contests.

base voter

IPA: /ˈbeɪs ˌvəʊtə/
Pronunciation: base VOH-tuh
French: électeur de base ; soutien fidèle

Definition: A loyal supporter of a party or candidate.

Example: The speech was designed to energise base voters.

swing state

IPA: /ˈswɪŋ steɪt/
Pronunciation: swing stayt
French: État pivot ; État indécis

Definition: A US state where both major parties have a realistic chance of winning.

Example: Polls in swing states received intense media attention.

battleground state

IPA: /ˈbætəlɡraʊnd steɪt/
Pronunciation: BAT-uhl-ground stayt
French: État clé ; État disputé

Definition: A state where the electoral result is uncertain and politically decisive.

Example: The campaign spent heavily in battleground states.

too close to call

IPA: /tuː ˌkləʊs tə ˈkɔːl/
Pronunciation: too klohss tuh call
French: trop serré pour être prédit

Definition: So close that the result cannot be predicted with confidence.

Example: The race is still too close to call.

neck and neck

IPA: /ˌnek ən ˈnek/
Pronunciation: nek uhn nek
French: au coude-à-coude

Definition: Very close in a race or competition.

Example: The two candidates are neck and neck in the latest poll.

lead

IPA: /liːd/
Pronunciation: leed
French: avance

Definition: An advantage over another candidate, party or option.

Example: The opposition has a five-point lead.

narrow lead

IPA: /ˌnærəʊ ˈliːd/
Pronunciation: NAR-oh leed
French: faible avance

Definition: A small advantage in a poll or election.

Example: The incumbent has only a narrow lead.

commanding lead

IPA: /kəˌmɑːndɪŋ ˈliːd/
Pronunciation: kuh-MAN-ding leed
French: avance confortable

Definition: A large and convincing advantage.

Example: The candidate has a commanding lead among older voters.

lead widens

IPA: /ˈliːd ˈwaɪdənz/
Pronunciation: leed WY-duhnz
French: l’avance se creuse

Definition: A situation in which a candidate’s advantage becomes larger.

Example: The president’s lead widened after the debate.

lead narrows

IPA: /ˈliːd ˈnærəʊz/
Pronunciation: leed NAR-ohz
French: l’avance se réduit

Definition: A situation in which a candidate’s advantage becomes smaller.

Example: The opposition’s lead narrowed in the final week.

surge

IPA: /sɜːdʒ/
Pronunciation: surj
French: bond ; poussée

Definition: A sudden increase in support or popularity.

Example: The party enjoyed a surge in support.

slump

IPA: /slʌmp/
Pronunciation: slump
French: chute ; recul

Definition: A sudden or significant fall in support or popularity.

Example: The government suffered a slump in the polls.

bounce

IPA: /baʊns/
Pronunciation: bownss
French: rebond

Definition: A temporary rise in popularity after an event such as a convention, debate or speech.

Example: The candidate received a post-debate bounce.

momentum

IPA: /məˈmentəm/
Pronunciation: muh-MEN-tuhm
French: dynamique ; élan

Definition: A sense of growing political support or success.

Example: The campaign gained momentum after the primary victory.

polling average

IPA: /ˈpəʊlɪŋ ˈævərɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: POH-ling AV-uh-rij
French: moyenne des sondages

Definition: An average of several polls used to reduce uncertainty.

Example: The polling average showed a stable race.

forecast

IPA: /ˈfɔːkɑːst/
Pronunciation: FOR-kast
French: prévision

Definition: A prediction about a future event, such as an election result.

Example: The election forecast gave the opposition a 60% chance of winning.

projection

IPA: /prəˈdʒekʃən/
Pronunciation: pruh-JEK-shuhn
French: projection

Definition: An estimate of a future result based on current data.

Example: Early projections suggested a hung parliament.

exit poll

IPA: /ˈeksɪt pəʊl/
Pronunciation: EK-sit pohl
French: sondage de sortie des urnes

Definition: A survey of voters just after they have voted, used to predict results.

Example: The exit poll predicted a landslide victory.

C. Social groups, divides and voter blocs

tracking poll

IPA: /ˈtrækɪŋ pəʊl/
Pronunciation: TRAK-ing pohl
French: sondage de suivi

Definition: A poll repeated regularly to measure changes over time.

Example: The tracking poll showed daily changes in support.

focus group

IPA: /ˈfəʊkəs ɡruːp/
Pronunciation: FOH-kuhs group
French: groupe de discussion

Definition: A small group of people asked to discuss opinions about a candidate, policy or message.

Example: The campaign tested its slogan with a focus group.

qualitative research

IPA: /ˈkwɒlɪtətɪv rɪˈsɜːtʃ/
Pronunciation: KWOL-i-tuh-tiv ri-SURCH
French: recherche qualitative

Definition: Research based on detailed opinions, explanations or interviews rather than numbers alone.

Example: Focus groups are a form of qualitative research.

quantitative research

IPA: /ˈkwɒntɪtətɪv rɪˈsɜːtʃ/
Pronunciation: KWON-ti-tuh-tiv ri-SURCH
French: recherche quantitative

Definition: Research based on numbers, measurements and statistical analysis.

Example: Opinion polls are a form of quantitative research.

approval gap

IPA: /əˈpruːvəl ɡæp/
Pronunciation: uh-PROO-vuhl gap
French: écart d’approbation

Definition: A difference in approval between groups, regions or parties.

Example: There is a large approval gap between urban and rural voters.

gender gap

IPA: /ˈdʒendə ɡæp/
Pronunciation: JEN-duh gap
French: écart hommes-femmes

Definition: A difference between men and women in political opinions or voting behaviour.

Example: The gender gap widened during the campaign.

generation gap

IPA: /ˌdʒenəˈreɪʃən ɡæp/
Pronunciation: jen-uh-RAY-shuhn gap
French: écart générationnel

Definition: A difference between age groups in attitudes or voting behaviour.

Example: The generation gap is clear on climate policy.

urban-rural divide

IPA: /ˌɜːbən ˈrʊərəl dɪˈvaɪd/
Pronunciation: UR-buhn ROOR-uhl di-VYDE
French: fracture urbain-rural

Definition: A political difference between cities and rural areas.

Example: The urban-rural divide shaped the election result.

partisan divide

IPA: /ˌpɑːtɪzən dɪˈvaɪd/
Pronunciation: PAR-ti-zuhn di-VYDE
French: clivage partisan

Definition: A difference in opinion based on party allegiance.

Example: The partisan divide on immigration has grown.

education divide

IPA: /ˌedjʊˈkeɪʃən dɪˈvaɪd/
Pronunciation: ej-yoo-KAY-shuhn di-VYDE
French: fracture liée au niveau d’éducation

Definition: A political difference linked to levels of education.

Example: The education divide has reshaped voting patterns.

income group

IPA: /ˈɪnkʌm ɡruːp/
Pronunciation: IN-kum group
French: groupe de revenu

Definition: A category of people based on income level.

Example: Support varied significantly by income group.

working-class voters

IPA: /ˌwɜːkɪŋ klɑːs ˈvəʊtəz/
Pronunciation: WUR-king klass VOH-tuhz
French: électeurs populaires ; électorat ouvrier

Definition: Voters belonging to or identifying with the working class.

Example: Working-class voters shifted away from the party.

suburban voters

IPA: /səˈbɜːbən ˈvəʊtəz/
Pronunciation: suh-BUR-buhn VOH-tuhz
French: électeurs des banlieues résidentielles

Definition: Voters living in suburban areas.

Example: Suburban voters became crucial in the election.

youth vote

IPA: /ˈjuːθ vəʊt/
Pronunciation: yooth voht
French: vote des jeunes

Definition: The voting behaviour or support of young voters.

Example: The youth vote strongly favoured climate action.

senior voters

IPA: /ˈsiːniə ˈvəʊtəz/
Pronunciation: SEE-nee-uh VOH-tuhz
French: électeurs âgés ; seniors

Definition: Older voters, often retirees or people over a certain age.

Example: Senior voters were more likely to support the incumbent.

D. Public mood, trust and political attitudes

public mood

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk ˈmuːd/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik mood
French: état d’esprit de l’opinion

Definition: The general emotional attitude of the public at a given time.

Example: The public mood was gloomy after months of inflation.

national mood

IPA: /ˌnæʃənəl ˈmuːd/
Pronunciation: NASH-uh-nuhl mood
French: climat national ; humeur du pays

Definition: The general feeling across a country about politics or society.

Example: The national mood favoured change.

mood of the country

IPA: /ˌmuːd əv ðə ˈkʌntri/
Pronunciation: mood ov thuh KUN-tree
French: état d’esprit du pays

Definition: The overall political or emotional atmosphere in a country.

Example: The mood of the country was hostile to the ruling party.

public confidence

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk ˈkɒnfɪdəns/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik KON-fi-duhns
French: confiance du public

Definition: The level of trust citizens have in institutions or leaders.

Example: Public confidence in parliament fell sharply.

trust in institutions

IPA: /ˌtrʌst ɪn ˌɪnstɪˈtjuːʃənz/
Pronunciation: trust in in-sti-TOO-shuhnz
French: confiance dans les institutions

Definition: The degree to which citizens believe institutions are reliable and legitimate.

Example: Trust in institutions is essential for democracy.

political trust

IPA: /pəˌlɪtɪkəl ˈtrʌst/
Pronunciation: puh-LIT-i-kuhl trust
French: confiance politique

Definition: Confidence in political leaders, parties or institutions.

Example: Political trust declined after the corruption scandal.

distrust

IPA: /dɪsˈtrʌst/
Pronunciation: dis-TRUST
French: méfiance ; défiance

Definition: A lack of trust or confidence.

Example: Distrust of politicians has increased.

disillusionment

IPA: /ˌdɪsɪˈluːʒənmənt/
Pronunciation: dis-i-LOO-zhuhn-muhnt
French: désillusion ; désenchantement

Definition: A feeling of disappointment after losing faith in someone or something.

Example: Disillusionment with politics fuelled abstention.

political apathy

IPA: /pəˈlɪtɪkəl ˈæpəθi/
Pronunciation: puh-LIT-i-kuhl AP-uh-thee
French: apathie politique

Definition: A lack of interest in politics.

Example: Political apathy can lead to low turnout.

voter fatigue

IPA: /ˈvəʊtə fəˈtiːɡ/
Pronunciation: VOH-tuh fuh-TEEG
French: lassitude électorale

Definition: A loss of voter interest or motivation, often after repeated elections or campaigns.

Example: Voter fatigue may explain the low turnout.

issue salience

IPA: /ˈɪʃuː ˈseɪliəns/
Pronunciation: ISH-oo SAY-lee-uhns
French: importance d’un enjeu pour les électeurs

Definition: The importance of an issue in voters’ minds.

Example: Issue salience increased after the crisis.

top concern

IPA: /ˌtɒp kənˈsɜːn/
Pronunciation: top kuhn-SURN
French: préoccupation principale

Definition: The issue that worries voters the most.

Example: Inflation remained voters’ top concern.

kitchen-table issue

IPA: /ˌkɪtʃən ˈteɪbəl ˈɪʃuː/
Pronunciation: KITCH-uhn TAY-buhl ISH-oo
French: problème du quotidien ; enjeu de pouvoir d’achat

Definition: An everyday economic issue affecting households, such as wages, bills or prices.

Example: The campaign focused on kitchen-table issues.

cost-of-living concern

IPA: /ˌkɒst əv ˈlɪvɪŋ kənˈsɜːn/
Pronunciation: kost ov LIV-ing kuhn-SURN
French: préoccupation liée au coût de la vie

Definition: Public worry about rising prices and household expenses.

Example: Cost-of-living concerns dominated the poll.

perception

IPA: /pəˈsepʃən/
Pronunciation: puh-SEP-shuhn
French: perception

Definition: The way people see, understand or interpret something.

Example: Public perception of the policy was negative.

to poll well

IPA: /pəʊl ˈwel/
Pronunciation: pohl wel
French: être bien placé dans les sondages

Definition: To receive good results in opinion polls.

Example: The candidate polls well among younger voters.

E. High-value verbs and exam-ready polling expressions

to poll badly

IPA: /pəʊl ˈbædli/
Pronunciation: pohl BAD-lee
French: être mal placé dans les sondages

Definition: To receive poor results in opinion polls.

Example: The party polls badly in rural areas.

to gain ground

IPA: /ɡeɪn ˈɡraʊnd/
Pronunciation: gayn grownd
French: gagner du terrain

Definition: To become more popular or successful.

Example: The opposition is gaining ground.

to lose ground

IPA: /luːz ˈɡraʊnd/
Pronunciation: looz grownd
French: perdre du terrain

Definition: To become less popular or successful.

Example: The government is losing ground in the polls.

to lead in the polls

IPA: /liːd ɪn ðə ˈpəʊlz/
Pronunciation: leed in thuh pohlz
French: être en tête dans les sondages

Definition: To be ahead of others in opinion polls.

Example: The incumbent leads in the polls.

to trail behind

IPA: /treɪl bɪˈhaɪnd/
Pronunciation: trayl bi-HYND
French: être à la traîne ; être derrière

Definition: To be behind another candidate or party.

Example: The challenger is trailing behind by six points.

to close the gap

IPA: /kləʊz ðə ˈɡæp/
Pronunciation: klohz thuh gap
French: réduire l’écart

Definition: To reduce the difference between two candidates or parties.

Example: The opposition closed the gap in the final week.

to shape public opinion

IPA: /ʃeɪp ˌpʌblɪk əˈpɪnjən/
Pronunciation: shayp PUB-lik uh-PIN-yuhn
French: façonner l’opinion publique

Definition: To influence what people think about an issue.

Example: Media coverage can shape public opinion.

to sway voters

IPA: /sweɪ ˈvəʊtəz/
Pronunciation: sway VOH-tuhz
French: faire changer d’avis les électeurs

Definition: To influence voters and change their opinion or choice.

Example: The scandal may sway undecided voters.

to predict the outcome

IPA: /prɪˈdɪkt ði ˈaʊtkʌm/
Pronunciation: pri-DIKT thee OWT-kum
French: prédire le résultat

Definition: To forecast the result of an election or political event.

Example: Polls cannot always predict the outcome accurately.

to misread public opinion

IPA: /ˌmɪsˈriːd ˌpʌblɪk əˈpɪnjən/
Pronunciation: mis-REED PUB-lik uh-PIN-yuhn
French: mal interpréter l’opinion publique

Definition: To misunderstand what the public thinks.

Example: The government misread public opinion on the reform.

to track voter sentiment

IPA: /træk ˈvəʊtə ˈsentɪmənt/
Pronunciation: trak VOH-tuh SEN-ti-muhnt
French: suivre l’état d’esprit des électeurs

Definition: To follow changes in how voters feel.

Example: Campaigns use polling to track voter sentiment.
