The golden rule

Choose the connector according to the logic
however = contrast
therefore = consequence
although = concession
because = cause
Main useUse conjunctions and linking words to show how ideas relate: addition, contrast, concession, cause, result, purpose, sequence or example.
CPGE habitDo not use connectors just because they sound formal. First identify the logical relationship between the ideas.
Key trapDespite is followed by a noun or gerund: Despite the cost, not Despite the reform is costly.

A. Lesson — What are conjunctions and linking words?

Conjunctions

French: conjonctions

Rule / explanation: Conjunctions connect words, phrases or clauses inside a sentence.

Example / answer: The reform is ambitious, but it remains controversial.

Linking words

French: mots de liaison

Rule / explanation: Linking words connect sentences, paragraphs and stages of an argument.

Example / answer: The reform is ambitious. However, it remains controversial.

Coordinating conjunctions

French: conjonctions de coordination

Rule / explanation: Words such as and, but, or, so and yet connect elements of equal grammatical value.

Example / answer: The article is clear and well structured.

Subordinating conjunctions

French: conjonctions de subordination

Rule / explanation: Words such as because, although, if, while and whereas introduce dependent clauses.

Example / answer: Although the policy is necessary, it may be unpopular.

Discourse markers

French: marqueurs discursifs

Rule / explanation: Words or phrases that organise an argument or guide the reader.

Example / answer: First, the article defines the crisis. Then, it analyses its causes.

Logical relationship

French: relation logique

Rule / explanation: A connector should show the real relationship between ideas: addition, contrast, cause, consequence or concession.

Example / answer: The data are limited. Therefore, the conclusion should be cautious.

Sentence position

French: position dans la phrase

Rule / explanation: Some connectors join clauses; others start a new sentence.

Example / answer: Because the policy is costly, voters may reject it. / The policy is costly. Therefore, voters may reject it.

CPGE use

French: usage CPGE

Rule / explanation: Conjunctions and linking words are essential for essays, syntheses, khôlles, article summaries and oral argumentation.

Example / answer: The author makes a persuasive claim; however, the evidence remains incomplete.

B. Addition and reinforcement

And

French: et

Rule / explanation: Basic conjunction used to connect similar ideas.

Example / answer: The article analyses poverty and discusses political distrust.

Also

French: aussi

Rule / explanation: Adds information; often placed before the main verb or after be.

Example / answer: The author also examines the role of social media.

Moreover

French: de plus

Rule / explanation: Formal linker used to add a stronger or additional point.

Example / answer: Moreover, the policy could reduce long-term costs.

Furthermore

French: en outre

Rule / explanation: Formal equivalent of moreover.

Example / answer: Furthermore, the article relies on recent data.

In addition

French: de plus

Rule / explanation: Neutral formal linker for adding information.

Example / answer: In addition, the proposal may improve access to education.

Besides

French: d’ailleurs / en plus

Rule / explanation: Adds an extra point; more conversational than moreover.

Example / answer: Besides, voters may not trust the government’s promises.

Not only... but also

French: non seulement... mais aussi

Rule / explanation: Adds emphasis and balance.

Example / answer: The crisis affects not only the poor but also the middle class.

Another key point is that

French: autre point essentiel

Rule / explanation: Useful to structure paragraphs clearly.

Example / answer: Another key point is that inequality damages democratic participation.

C. Contrast and opposition

But

French: mais

Rule / explanation: Basic contrast conjunction inside a sentence.

Example / answer: The proposal is attractive, but it is expensive.

However

French: cependant

Rule / explanation: Formal contrast linker, often at the beginning of a sentence.

Example / answer: However, the evidence remains limited.

Nevertheless

French: néanmoins

Rule / explanation: Stronger or more formal contrast.

Example / answer: Nevertheless, the article is convincing.

Nonetheless

French: néanmoins

Rule / explanation: Formal equivalent of nevertheless.

Example / answer: Nonetheless, the policy raises serious questions.

Yet

French: pourtant

Rule / explanation: Concise contrast; often elegant in essays.

Example / answer: The reform is popular, yet its cost remains unclear.

Whereas

French: tandis que

Rule / explanation: Directly contrasts two clauses.

Example / answer: Whereas older voters support the measure, younger voters oppose it.

While

French: tandis que / bien que

Rule / explanation: Can mean whereas or although depending on context.

Example / answer: While the reform seems fair, it may be hard to implement.

By contrast

French: par contraste

Rule / explanation: Compares two different situations or arguments.

Example / answer: By contrast, the second article focuses on economic causes.

D. Concession and qualification

Although

French: bien que

Rule / explanation: Introduces a concession; followed by subject + verb.

Example / answer: Although the reform is necessary, it remains controversial.

Even though

French: même si / bien que

Rule / explanation: Stronger and more emphatic than although.

Example / answer: Even though the article is persuasive, it lacks concrete examples.

Despite

French: malgré

Rule / explanation: Followed by a noun phrase or gerund, not a full clause.

Example / answer: Despite strong opposition, the bill passed.

In spite of

French: malgré

Rule / explanation: Same structure as despite.

Example / answer: In spite of rising costs, the policy was maintained.

Admittedly

French: certes

Rule / explanation: Concedes a point before qualifying it.

Example / answer: Admittedly, the proposal is costly, but it may prevent deeper problems.

Granted

French: certes

Rule / explanation: Formal concession, often followed by a counterargument.

Example / answer: Granted, the reform is imperfect, but doing nothing would be worse.

Even so

French: malgré cela

Rule / explanation: Means despite that.

Example / answer: The evidence is limited. Even so, the conclusion is plausible.

That said

French: cela dit

Rule / explanation: Useful for nuanced transitions.

Example / answer: The author makes a strong case. That said, the article lacks balance.

E. Cause, reason and explanation

Because

French: parce que

Rule / explanation: Introduces a direct reason.

Example / answer: The policy failed because it was underfunded.

Since

French: puisque / étant donné que

Rule / explanation: Can mean because in formal argumentation.

Example / answer: Since the data are incomplete, the conclusion should be cautious.

As

French: comme / étant donné que

Rule / explanation: Formal and concise way to introduce a reason.

Example / answer: As inequality rises, social tensions may increase.

Because of

French: à cause de / en raison de

Rule / explanation: Followed by a noun phrase or gerund.

Example / answer: Because of rising rents, many students struggle financially.

Due to

French: en raison de

Rule / explanation: Formal cause linker followed by a noun phrase.

Example / answer: Due to inflation, households are under pressure.

Owing to

French: en raison de

Rule / explanation: Formal equivalent of due to.

Example / answer: Owing to weak evidence, the argument is not fully convincing.

This is because

French: cela s’explique par

Rule / explanation: Useful for explaining the previous sentence.

Example / answer: The reform is unpopular. This is because it affects low-income families.

The reason why

French: la raison pour laquelle

Rule / explanation: Useful but should not be overused.

Example / answer: The reason why voters abstain is not simply apathy.

F. Consequence and result

So

French: donc

Rule / explanation: Basic result conjunction; common but less formal.

Example / answer: The policy was unclear, so voters rejected it.

Therefore

French: par conséquent

Rule / explanation: Formal result linker.

Example / answer: Therefore, the government must provide clearer evidence.

Thus

French: ainsi

Rule / explanation: Concise and formal; common in academic writing.

Example / answer: Thus, the article presents inequality as a political threat.

As a result

French: en conséquence

Rule / explanation: Clear result linker.

Example / answer: As a result, public trust has declined.

Consequently

French: par conséquent

Rule / explanation: Formal equivalent of as a result.

Example / answer: Consequently, the reform may face legal challenges.

Hence

French: d’où / par conséquent

Rule / explanation: Very concise and formal; use sparingly.

Example / answer: Hence the need for stronger regulation.

This leads to

French: cela conduit à

Rule / explanation: Useful for cause-result development.

Example / answer: This leads to a wider debate about democracy.

For this reason

French: pour cette raison

Rule / explanation: Useful before a conclusion or recommendation.

Example / answer: For this reason, the author calls for urgent reform.

G. Purpose and objective

To

French: pour

Rule / explanation: Simple and effective purpose marker.

Example / answer: The article uses statistics to support its argument.

In order to

French: afin de

Rule / explanation: Formal purpose linker followed by the base verb.

Example / answer: The policy was introduced in order to reduce poverty.

So as to

French: afin de

Rule / explanation: Formal and slightly more elegant than in order to.

Example / answer: The government revised the bill so as to reassure voters.

So that

French: afin que

Rule / explanation: Introduces a clause with subject + verb.

Example / answer: The law was changed so that citizens could appeal decisions.

With a view to

French: dans le but de

Rule / explanation: Formal; followed by a gerund.

Example / answer: The measure was introduced with a view to reducing inequality.

For the purpose of

French: dans le but de

Rule / explanation: Very formal; followed by a gerund or noun.

Example / answer: The data were collected for the purpose of assessing poverty.

The aim is to

French: le but est de

Rule / explanation: Useful in summaries and essays.

Example / answer: The aim is to restore trust in institutions.

Intended to

French: destiné à

Rule / explanation: Useful passive or adjective structure.

Example / answer: The reform is intended to protect vulnerable households.

H. Time, sequence and structure

First / firstly

French: premièrement

Rule / explanation: Introduces the first stage of an argument.

Example / answer: First, the article defines the problem.

Then

French: ensuite

Rule / explanation: Shows the next stage in a sequence.

Example / answer: Then, the author examines political consequences.

Next

French: ensuite

Rule / explanation: Organises a sequence clearly.

Example / answer: Next, the article turns to economic causes.

Finally

French: enfin

Rule / explanation: Introduces the final stage or point.

Example / answer: Finally, the author proposes several solutions.

Meanwhile

French: pendant ce temps

Rule / explanation: Shows that two things happen at the same time.

Example / answer: Meanwhile, public trust continues to decline.

At the same time

French: en même temps

Rule / explanation: Useful for simultaneous or balancing ideas.

Example / answer: At the same time, regulation may threaten freedom of speech.

Eventually

French: finalement

Rule / explanation: Means in the end, not possibly.

Example / answer: Eventually, the government withdrew the bill.

In the long run

French: à long terme

Rule / explanation: Useful for long-term consequences.

Example / answer: In the long run, underfunding may damage public services.

I. Examples and clarification

For example

French: par exemple

Rule / explanation: Introduces an example.

Example / answer: For example, rising rents affect students directly.

For instance

French: par exemple

Rule / explanation: Formal equivalent of for example.

Example / answer: For instance, the article mentions London and Manchester.

Such as

French: comme / tel que

Rule / explanation: Introduces examples inside a noun phrase.

Example / answer: Social issues such as poverty and housing dominate the debate.

Namely

French: à savoir

Rule / explanation: Introduces specific clarification.

Example / answer: The article focuses on one major issue, namely inequality.

In other words

French: autrement dit

Rule / explanation: Rephrases an idea more clearly.

Example / answer: In other words, the reform may worsen the problem it tries to solve.

That is to say

French: c’est-à-dire

Rule / explanation: Formal clarification.

Example / answer: The crisis is structural, that is to say, it cannot be solved quickly.

More precisely

French: plus précisément

Rule / explanation: Adds precision to a previous statement.

Example / answer: More precisely, the author criticises the lack of funding.

To put it differently

French: pour le dire autrement

Rule / explanation: Useful to reformulate in oral exams.

Example / answer: To put it differently, the policy treats symptoms rather than causes.

J. Typical mistakes by French speakers

Despite + clause

French: despite

Rule / explanation: Despite is followed by a noun phrase or gerund, not subject + verb.

Example / answer: Wrong: Despite the reform is costly. Correct: Although the reform is costly / Despite the cost.

Although + noun only

French: although

Rule / explanation: Although needs a clause with subject + verb.

Example / answer: Wrong: Although the cost. Correct: Although the reform is costly.

Because of + clause

French: because of

Rule / explanation: Because of is followed by a noun phrase or gerund.

Example / answer: Wrong: Because of rents are rising. Correct: Because rents are rising / Because of rising rents.

Therefore after comma only

French: therefore

Rule / explanation: Therefore usually starts a new sentence or is set off by punctuation.

Example / answer: Weak: The policy failed, therefore voters reacted. Better: The policy failed. Therefore, voters reacted.

Confusing actually and currently

French: actually

Rule / explanation: Actually means in fact, not currently.

Example / answer: Wrong: Actually, the bill is being debated. Correct: Currently, the bill is being debated.

Eventually vs éventuellement

French: eventually

Rule / explanation: Eventually means in the end, not possibly.

Example / answer: Wrong: Eventually, the reform may fail. Correct: The reform may possibly fail.

Moreover without addition

French: moreover

Rule / explanation: Do not use moreover if the second point contrasts or results from the first.

Example / answer: Use however for contrast and therefore for consequence.

Overusing firstly/secondly

French: firstly / secondly

Rule / explanation: Useful, but mechanical if repeated too often.

Example / answer: Vary with another key point is, moreover, by contrast, as a result.

K. Exercises

Exercise 1 — Choose the best connector

French: exercice

Rule / explanation: The reform is necessary. ___, it may be difficult to fund. (However / Therefore)

Example / answer: Answer: However

Exercise 2 — Choose the best connector

French: exercice

Rule / explanation: The data are incomplete. ___, the conclusion should be cautious. (Therefore / Although)

Example / answer: Answer: Therefore

Exercise 3 — Correct the mistake

French: exercice

Rule / explanation: Despite the reform is costly, it may be necessary.

Example / answer: Answer: Although the reform is costly, it may be necessary. / Despite the cost, it may be necessary.

Exercise 4 — Correct the mistake

French: exercice

Rule / explanation: Because of rents are rising, students struggle.

Example / answer: Answer: Because rents are rising, students struggle. / Because of rising rents, students struggle.

Exercise 5 — Combine with although

French: exercice

Rule / explanation: The article is persuasive. It lacks examples.

Example / answer: Answer: Although the article is persuasive, it lacks examples.

Exercise 6 — Combine with so that

French: exercice

Rule / explanation: The government changed the law. Citizens could appeal decisions.

Example / answer: Answer: The government changed the law so that citizens could appeal decisions.

Exercise 7 — Use a formal addition linker

French: exercice

Rule / explanation: The article discusses inequality. It examines political distrust.

Example / answer: Answer: The article discusses inequality. Moreover/Furthermore, it examines political distrust.

Exercise 8 — Use a consequence linker

French: exercice

Rule / explanation: The policy was underfunded. It failed.

Example / answer: Answer: The policy was underfunded. As a result, it failed.

Exercise 9 — Translate

French: exercice

Rule / explanation: Bien que la réforme soit nécessaire, elle reste controversée.

Example / answer: Answer: Although the reform is necessary, it remains controversial.

Exercise 10 — Translate

French: exercice

Rule / explanation: Par conséquent, le gouvernement doit agir rapidement.

Example / answer: Answer: Therefore / Consequently, the government must act quickly.

Exercise 11 — Translate

French: exercice

Rule / explanation: La mesure a été introduite afin de réduire les inégalités.

Example / answer: Answer: The measure was introduced in order to reduce inequality.

Exercise 12 — Mini concours challenge

French: exercice

Rule / explanation: Write a short paragraph using however, moreover, although, because and therefore.

Example / answer: Possible answer: Although the reform is ambitious, it remains controversial. It could reduce inequality because it targets low-income families. However, its cost is unclear. Moreover, voters may distrust the government’s promises. Therefore, the policy needs stronger justification.
