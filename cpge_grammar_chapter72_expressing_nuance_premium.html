Chapter 72 — Expressing Nuance
B2 → C1 grammar for precise qualification, partial agreement and sophisticated CPGE analysis.

Chapter objective

This premium chapter develops advanced control of nuance: partial agreement, qualification, limited generalisation, degree, scope, mixed evaluation, conditional judgement and balanced use in essays, article commentary and khôlles.

The golden rule

Replace absolute judgement with degree, scope, condition or criterion.
Not: “The reform failed.”
Better: “The reform achieved limited success in urban areas but failed to reduce regional inequality.”
Degreeslightly, somewhat, largely, to some extent
Scopein this context, in most cases, within the sample
Conditionprovided that, only if, unless, even if
Mixed judgementwhile, although, in some respects, on balance

A. What nuance means

Nuance

Rule / explanation: Express an idea with precision instead of presenting it as completely true, false, good or bad.

Example / answer: The reform is not entirely ineffective, but its benefits remain limited.

Degrees, not absolutes

Rule / explanation: Replace binary judgements with degrees, conditions, exceptions and contrasts.

Example / answer: The policy is broadly successful, although implementation varies across regions.

Intellectual honesty

Rule / explanation: Nuance recognises complexity and competing evidence.

Example / answer: The argument is persuasive in some respects but insufficiently supported in others.

CPGE relevance

Rule / explanation: Nuance is essential in essays, article commentary and khôlles.

Example / answer: The measure may be economically efficient without necessarily being socially equitable.

B. Partial agreement

To some extent

Rule / explanation: Accept part of an argument while preserving reservations.

Example / answer: To some extent, the government’s caution is understandable.

Up to a point

Rule / explanation: A slightly more conversational form, useful in oral debate.

Example / answer: I agree up to a point, but the analysis ignores regional inequality.

In part

Rule / explanation: Attribute only part of an outcome or explanation.

Example / answer: The decline can be explained in part by economic insecurity.

Partly

Rule / explanation: A concise adverb for partial validity or causation.

Example / answer: The reform was partly successful.

There is some truth in

Rule / explanation: Acknowledge a valid element in a disputed claim.

Example / answer: There is some truth in the argument that regulation may slow innovation.

C. Concession and qualification

Admittedly

Rule / explanation: Acknowledge a valid opposing point before maintaining your argument.

Example / answer: Admittedly, the reform reduced costs in the short term.

Although

Rule / explanation: Introduce a concession inside a complex sentence.

Example / answer: Although the policy improved efficiency, it widened regional disparities.

While

Rule / explanation: Concede one point and contrast it with another.

Example / answer: While the article raises an important issue, its evidence remains limited.

That said

Rule / explanation: Qualify or counter the preceding statement.

Example / answer: The proposal is ambitious. That said, its funding is uncertain.

Nevertheless

Rule / explanation: Maintain a conclusion despite a recognised difficulty.

Example / answer: The data are incomplete; nevertheless, the trend is difficult to ignore.

D. Limiting generalisations

Generally

Rule / explanation: Describe a broad tendency with possible exceptions.

Example / answer: Public support is generally stronger when consultation is transparent.

In most cases

Rule / explanation: Avoid claiming universality.

Example / answer: In most cases, local authorities lacked sufficient resources.

Tend to

Rule / explanation: Describe a recurring pattern rather than an absolute rule.

Example / answer: Younger voters tend to obtain news from digital platforms.

Often

Rule / explanation: Express frequency without implying always.

Example / answer: Economic crises often intensify political distrust.

Not necessarily

Rule / explanation: Reject an automatic connection.

Example / answer: Economic growth does not necessarily reduce inequality.

E. Restricting scope

In this context

Rule / explanation: Limit a conclusion to a particular situation.

Example / answer: In this context, stronger regulation appears justified.

In the short term

Rule / explanation: Restrict a claim to a specific time frame.

Example / answer: In the short term, the reform may reduce costs.

Within the sample

Rule / explanation: Prevent generalisation beyond the data.

Example / answer: Within the sample, younger respondents were more supportive.

Under certain conditions

Rule / explanation: Make validity dependent on circumstances.

Example / answer: The programme could succeed under certain conditions.

With regard to

Rule / explanation: Limit discussion to one aspect.

Example / answer: With regard to access, the policy has produced mixed results.

F. Degree adverbs

Slightly

Rule / explanation: Express a small degree of difference or change.

Example / answer: Turnout was slightly lower than expected.

Somewhat

Rule / explanation: Soften an adjective, often in formal criticism.

Example / answer: The conclusion is somewhat overstated.

Relatively

Rule / explanation: Compare with an implied standard.

Example / answer: The measure was relatively effective in urban areas.

Considerably

Rule / explanation: Express a substantial degree.

Example / answer: Regional outcomes varied considerably.

Largely

Rule / explanation: Mean mainly, but not completely.

Example / answer: The programme was largely successful.

G. Almost-complete meanings

Almost

Rule / explanation: Close to, but not reaching, a point.

Example / answer: Almost half of respondents opposed the proposal.

Nearly

Rule / explanation: Very close in meaning to almost.

Example / answer: Nearly two thirds of participants expressed concern.

Virtually

Rule / explanation: Almost completely true.

Example / answer: The opposition was virtually unanimous.

All but

Rule / explanation: A formal expression meaning almost completely.

Example / answer: The reform has all but disappeared from the political agenda.

Not quite

Rule / explanation: Express a small shortfall or polite correction.

Example / answer: The results are not quite as positive as the government suggests.

H. Partial negation

Not entirely

Rule / explanation: Reject a statement partially rather than completely.

Example / answer: The author’s explanation is not entirely convincing.

Not wholly

Rule / explanation: A formal equivalent of not entirely.

Example / answer: The decline cannot be attributed wholly to economic factors.

Not simply

Rule / explanation: Reject an oversimplified explanation.

Example / answer: The crisis is not simply a question of funding.

Not merely

Rule / explanation: Show that something has wider significance.

Example / answer: The issue is not merely economic but also political.

Not altogether

Rule / explanation: Express partial disagreement or incomplete truth.

Example / answer: The government’s response was not altogether ineffective.

I. Perspective and criteria

In some respects

Rule / explanation: Identify dimensions in which a judgement is valid.

Example / answer: In some respects, the reform represents genuine progress.

In other respects

Rule / explanation: Introduce dimensions where the judgement changes.

Example / answer: In other respects, it reproduces existing inequalities.

From one perspective

Rule / explanation: Frame an interpretation from a particular viewpoint.

Example / answer: From one perspective, the policy is economically rational.

From another perspective

Rule / explanation: Introduce a competing evaluation.

Example / answer: From another perspective, it is socially regressive.

Depending on

Rule / explanation: Show that evaluation varies according to a criterion.

Example / answer: The reform appears successful or unsuccessful depending on the indicator used.

J. Balanced contrast

On the one hand / on the other hand

Rule / explanation: Present two substantial and competing sides.

Example / answer: On the one hand, regulation protects consumers; on the other hand, it may increase costs.

Parallel structure

Rule / explanation: Keep both sides grammatically and logically balanced.

Example / answer: On the one hand, the reform improves access; on the other hand, it reduces local autonomy.

Genuine tension

Rule / explanation: Reserve the pair for real opposition, not minor differences.

Example / answer: Use it when the two dimensions genuinely compete.

Oral usefulness

Rule / explanation: Use it in khôlles to weigh arguments aloud.

Example / answer: On the one hand, the article is persuasive; on the other hand, its sample is narrow.

Overall judgement

Rule / explanation: Follow the contrast with a conclusion.

Example / answer: On balance, the benefits appear to outweigh the costs.

K. Complex simultaneous qualities

Both...and

Rule / explanation: Show that two apparently conflicting claims can both be valid.

Example / answer: The reform is both necessary and deeply flawed.

At once...and

Rule / explanation: A formal or literary structure for simultaneous qualities.

Example / answer: The article is at once insightful and frustratingly incomplete.

Without being

Rule / explanation: Separate qualities often assumed to coincide.

Example / answer: The policy is ambitious without being unrealistic.

While still

Rule / explanation: Maintain one property alongside another.

Example / answer: The proposal reduces costs while still protecting essential services.

Not incompatible with

Rule / explanation: Reject a false opposition.

Example / answer: Economic efficiency is not necessarily incompatible with social justice.

L. Nuanced causation

Contribute to

Rule / explanation: Present one factor within a larger causal chain.

Example / answer: Weak communication contributed to public opposition.

Partly account for

Rule / explanation: Explain only part of an outcome.

Example / answer: Demographic change may partly account for the decline.

Cannot be reduced to

Rule / explanation: Reject a single-factor explanation.

Example / answer: The crisis cannot be reduced to economic insecurity.

One factor among several

Rule / explanation: Place a cause within a wider set.

Example / answer: Funding was one factor among several.

Played a role

Rule / explanation: State influence cautiously.

Example / answer: Media coverage played a role in shaping public perception.

M. Mixed success and failure

Qualified success

Rule / explanation: An outcome that achieved important aims but had major limits.

Example / answer: The programme was a qualified success.

Mixed results

Rule / explanation: Positive and negative outcomes coexist.

Example / answer: The reform produced mixed results across regions.

Limited effectiveness

Rule / explanation: The intervention worked only partially.

Example / answer: The measure demonstrated limited effectiveness.

Far from a complete failure

Rule / explanation: Reject an excessively negative judgement.

Example / answer: The policy was far from a complete failure.

Criterion-based success

Rule / explanation: Clarify the standard used.

Example / answer: The reform was successful financially but not in terms of equality.

N. Nuanced disagreement

Not entirely convinced

Rule / explanation: Express measured scepticism.

Example / answer: I am not entirely convinced that the evidence supports the conclusion.

Open to question

Rule / explanation: Challenge an interpretation formally.

Example / answer: The assumption that growth benefits everyone equally is open to question.

May be an overstatement

Rule / explanation: Criticise the strength of a claim rather than reject it fully.

Example / answer: The prediction of institutional collapse may be an overstatement.

Has some merit, but

Rule / explanation: Recognise value before identifying a limitation.

Example / answer: The argument has some merit, but it overlooks implementation costs.

A more nuanced view

Rule / explanation: Replace a binary claim with a balanced interpretation.

Example / answer: A more nuanced view would be that the reform succeeded unevenly.

O. Nuanced agreement

Broadly speaking

Rule / explanation: Agree with the general direction while allowing exceptions.

Example / answer: Broadly speaking, the author’s diagnosis is convincing.

For the most part

Rule / explanation: Express substantial but incomplete agreement.

Example / answer: For the most part, the report’s recommendations are realistic.

I largely agree

Rule / explanation: Show strong but not total agreement.

Example / answer: I largely agree with the author’s assessment.

Central claim is valid

Rule / explanation: Agree with the main proposition while criticising details.

Example / answer: The central claim is valid, although several examples are weak.

Persuasive overall

Rule / explanation: Give a positive overall judgement with local reservations.

Example / answer: The argument is persuasive overall, despite methodological weaknesses.

P. Nuance through conditionals

If properly implemented

Rule / explanation: Use a reduced conditional to limit a judgement.

Example / answer: If properly implemented, the reform could improve access.

Provided that

Rule / explanation: State a necessary condition for success.

Example / answer: The programme may succeed provided that funding remains stable.

Unless

Rule / explanation: Identify the condition whose absence makes success unlikely.

Example / answer: The policy will remain ineffective unless local authorities are involved.

Even if

Rule / explanation: Show that one fact does not automatically change the conclusion.

Example / answer: Even if costs fall, the policy may remain socially unfair.

Only insofar as

Rule / explanation: Restrict validity with precision.

Example / answer: The reform is successful only insofar as efficiency is the sole criterion.

Q. Nuance through comparison

More X than Y

Rule / explanation: Redefine the dominant nature of something.

Example / answer: The crisis is more political than economic.

Less X than Y

Rule / explanation: Weaken one interpretation relative to another.

Example / answer: The article is less objective than it initially appears.

Not so much X as Y

Rule / explanation: Replace one characterisation with a more accurate one.

Example / answer: The problem is not so much a lack of resources as poor coordination.

As much X as Y

Rule / explanation: Give equal weight to two explanations.

Example / answer: The conflict is as much cultural as economic.

Rather than

Rule / explanation: Contrast the more accurate interpretation.

Example / answer: The reform redistributed costs rather than reducing them.

R. CPGE application

Article commentary

Rule / explanation: Evaluate content, evidence, tone and structure separately.

Example / answer: The reasoning is coherent, although the evidence is limited.

Essay thesis

Rule / explanation: State a clear answer while preserving limits.

Example / answer: Regulation is necessary, although its effectiveness depends on implementation.

Avoid yes/no plans

Rule / explanation: Organise around tensions, conditions, criteria and degrees.

Example / answer: The issue is under what conditions technology serves the public interest.

Conclusion

Rule / explanation: Answer with a degree, condition or distinction.

Example / answer: The reform can restore trust only if it is perceived as legitimate.

Oral fluency

Rule / explanation: Use familiar nuance markers naturally.

Example / answer: To some extent, however, that interpretation remains convincing.

S. Common mistakes and exercises

Not all

Rule / explanation: Use not all for partial negation.

Example / answer: Not all voters supported the reform.

Actually / currently

Rule / explanation: Actually means in fact; currently means at present.

Example / answer: The policy is currently under review.

Eventually / possibly

Rule / explanation: Eventually means in the end; possibly expresses uncertainty.

Example / answer: The reform may eventually succeed.

Globally

Rule / explanation: Globally usually means worldwide; use overall or broadly speaking for a general assessment.

Example / answer: Overall, the article is convincing.

Mini challenge

Rule / explanation: Combine partial agreement, contrast, condition and overall judgement.

Example / answer: To some extent, the reform improved efficiency. However, it can be considered successful only if equality is ignored. On balance, it represents limited progress rather than structural change.
