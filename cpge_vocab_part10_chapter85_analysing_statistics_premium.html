CPGE English Vocabulary Bible

Part 10 — Concours Toolkit | Chapter 85 — Analysing Statistics
Essential vocabulary for charts, polls, data, graphs, essays and khôlles.

Chapter objective

This premium toolkit chapter gives CPGE students the language needed to analyse statistics accurately: data, polls, samples, margins of error, trends, proportions, averages, gaps, correlations, causation and misleading figures. The aim is to help students describe numbers clearly, interpret them cautiously and use them as evidence in essays and oral exams.

How to use this chapter

For essaysUse expressions such as the figures suggest, the trend points to, the data should be interpreted cautiously, the difference is within the margin of error, and correlation does not imply causation.
For khôllesUse this vocabulary to describe graphs quickly: “There was a sharp rise...”, “The trend then plateaued...”, “The gap widened...”, “The data reveal a long-term decline...”
WarningDo not confuse percentage and percentage point. A rise from 20% to 25% is a rise of 5 percentage points, not 5%.

A. Core statistical vocabulary

IPA: /stəˈtɪstɪks/

French: statistiques

Example: Statistics can clarify social problems, but they can also be misleading.

Pronunciation: KON-fi-duhns IN-tuh-vuhl

French: montrer une corrélation

Definition: To indicate a relationship between variables.

Example: The data show a correlation, not necessarily causation.

E. High-value verbs for analysing figures

to establish causation

IPA: /ɪˈstæblɪʃ kɔːˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: i-STAB-lish kaw-ZAY-shuhn
French: établir une causalité

Definition: To prove that one thing causes another.

Example: The study fails to establish causation.

to misrepresent data

IPA: /ˌmɪsreprɪˈzent ˈdeɪtə/
Pronunciation: mis-rep-ri-ZENT DAY-tuh
French: déformer les données

Definition: To present data inaccurately or misleadingly.

Example: The graph misrepresents data by changing the scale.

to cherry-pick figures

IPA: /ˈtʃeri pɪk ˈfɪɡəz/
Pronunciation: CHERR-ee-pik FIG-uhz
French: sélectionner les chiffres de façon biaisée

Definition: To select only figures that support one’s argument.

Example: Politicians often cherry-pick figures.

to distort the picture

IPA: /dɪˈstɔːt ðə ˈpɪktʃə/
Pronunciation: di-STORT thuh PIK-chuh
French: déformer la réalité

Definition: To give a misleading impression.

Example: Averages can distort the picture.

to be statistically significant

IPA: /bi stəˌtɪstɪkəli sɪɡˈnɪfɪkənt/
Pronunciation: bee stuh-TIS-ti-kuh-lee sig-NIF-i-kuhnt
French: être statistiquement significatif

Definition: To be unlikely to be due to chance.

Example: The difference is statistically significant.

to be within the margin of error

IPA: /bi wɪˈðɪn ðə ˈmɑːdʒɪn əv ˈerə/
Pronunciation: bee with-IN thuh MAR-jin ov ERR-uh
French: être dans la marge d’erreur

Definition: To be too close to treat as clearly different.

Example: The candidates are within the margin of error.

correlation does not imply causation

IPA: /ˌkɒrəˈleɪʃən dʌz nɒt ɪmˈplaɪ kɔːˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: kor-uh-LAY-shuhn duz not im-PLY kaw-ZAY-shuhn
French: corrélation n’implique pas causalité

Definition: A relationship between two variables does not prove one causes the other.

Example: Correlation does not imply causation, so the claim must be qualified.
