Part 4 — Society | Chapter 32 — Housing
Essential vocabulary for khôlles, essays, press reviews and competitive exams.

Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse housing: affordability, rent, mortgages, social housing, planning, gentrification, homelessness, tenant rights, poor housing conditions, speculation, intergenerational inequality and the political debate over whether housing should be treated as a market good or a basic right.

How to use this chapter

For press articlesFocus on terms such as housing crisis, rent burden, social housing, planning permission, gentrification, no-fault eviction, fuel poverty and generation rent.
For oral examsUse this vocabulary to explain how housing connects wealth, poverty, health, education, urban policy, environmental planning and generational inequality.
For essaysMemorise the collocations: to get on the property ladder, to be priced out, to push up rents, to tackle homelessness, to make housing affordable.

A. Core housing vocabulary and affordability

housing

IPA: /ˈhaʊzɪŋ/
Pronunciation: HOW-zing
French: logement

Definition: Homes, flats and accommodation available for people to live in.

Example: Housing has become one of the central issues in modern societies.

housing crisis

IPA: /ˈhaʊzɪŋ ˈkraɪsɪs/
Pronunciation: HOW-zing KRY-sis
French: crise du logement

Definition: A serious shortage or unaffordability of decent homes.

Example: The housing crisis affects renters, young people and low-income families.

housing market

IPA: /ˈhaʊzɪŋ ˌmɑːkɪt/
Pronunciation: HOW-zing MAR-kit
French: marché du logement

Definition: The system through which homes are bought, sold and rented.

Example: The housing market has become increasingly unaffordable.

property market

IPA: /ˈprɒpəti ˌmɑːkɪt/
Pronunciation: PROP-uh-tee MAR-kit
French: marché immobilier

Definition: The market for buying and selling land, houses and flats.

Example: The property market has widened the wealth gap.

real estate

IPA: /ˈrɪəl ɪˌsteɪt/
Pronunciation: REE-uhl i-STAYT
French: immobilier

Definition: Property consisting of land and buildings.

Example: Real estate is a major source of wealth.

home

IPA: /həʊm/
Pronunciation: hohm
French: foyer ; logement

Definition: A place where someone lives and feels secure or rooted.

Example: A home is more than a physical shelter.

house

IPA: /haʊs/
Pronunciation: howss
French: maison

Definition: A building for people to live in, usually for one household.

Example: Owning a house has become harder for younger generations.

flat

IPA: /flæt/
Pronunciation: flat
French: appartement

Definition: British English for an apartment.

Example: Many young professionals rent a flat in large cities.

apartment

IPA: /əˈpɑːtmənt/
Pronunciation: uh-PART-muhnt
French: appartement

Definition: A set of rooms for living in, usually in a larger building.

Example: Apartment living is common in dense urban areas.

tenant

IPA: /ˈtenənt/
Pronunciation: TEN-uhnt
French: locataire

Definition: A person who rents a home from a landlord.

Example: Tenants often face rent increases and insecurity.

landlord

IPA: /ˈlændlɔːd/
Pronunciation: LAND-lord
French: propriétaire bailleur

Definition: A person or company that rents property to tenants.

Example: Landlords have been criticised for poor housing conditions.

homeowner

IPA: /ˈhəʊmˌəʊnə/
Pronunciation: HOHM-oh-nuh
French: propriétaire occupant

Definition: A person who owns their home.

Example: Homeowners have benefited from rising house prices.

home ownership

IPA: /ˈhəʊm ˌəʊnəʃɪp/
Pronunciation: HOHM OH-nuh-ship
French: propriété immobilière ; accession à la propriété

Definition: The state of owning one’s home.

Example: Home ownership is a major source of wealth accumulation.

owner-occupier

IPA: /ˌəʊnər ˈɒkjʊpaɪə/
Pronunciation: OH-nuh OK-yuh-py-uh
French: propriétaire occupant

Definition: Someone who owns and lives in their home.

Example: Owner-occupiers often benefit from property price rises.

renter

IPA: /ˈrentə/
Pronunciation: REN-tuh
French: locataire

Definition: A person who pays to live in a property they do not own.

Example: Renters often have less security than homeowners.

private renter

IPA: /ˌpraɪvət ˈrentə/
Pronunciation: PRY-vuht REN-tuh
French: locataire du parc privé

Definition: A person who rents from a private landlord.

Example: Private renters are exposed to market rents.

social tenant

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl ˈtenənt/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl TEN-uhnt
French: locataire du logement social

Definition: A person renting social housing.

Example: Social tenants usually pay below-market rents.

mortgage

IPA: /ˈmɔːɡɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: MOR-gij
French: prêt immobilier ; hypothèque

Definition: A loan used to buy a home, repaid over time.

Example: Higher interest rates make mortgages more expensive.

deposit

IPA: /dɪˈpɒzɪt/
Pronunciation: di-POZ-it
French: apport ; dépôt de garantie

Definition: Money paid upfront to buy a home or secure a rental property.

Example: Saving for a deposit is difficult for many young adults.

down payment

IPA: /ˌdaʊn ˈpeɪmənt/
Pronunciation: down PAY-muhnt
French: apport initial

Definition: US term for money paid upfront when buying a home.

Example: A large down payment is often required to buy property.

rent

IPA: /rent/
Pronunciation: rent
French: loyer

Definition: Money paid regularly to live in someone else’s property.

Example: Rent has risen faster than wages in many cities.

rent increase

IPA: /ˈrent ˌɪnkriːs/
Pronunciation: rent IN-kreess
French: augmentation de loyer

Definition: A rise in the amount of rent tenants must pay.

Example: Rent increases can force families to move.

rent burden

IPA: /ˈrent ˌbɜːdən/
Pronunciation: rent BUR-duhn
French: poids du loyer

Definition: A situation where rent takes up a large share of income.

Example: Rent burden prevents households from saving.

housing cost

IPA: /ˈhaʊzɪŋ kɒst/
Pronunciation: HOW-zing kost
French: coût du logement

Definition: The money spent on rent, mortgage payments, bills and housing-related expenses.

Example: Housing costs have become a major driver of poverty.

housing affordability

IPA: /ˈhaʊzɪŋ əˌfɔːd əˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: HOW-zing uh-FOR-duh-BIL-uh-tee
French: accessibilité financière du logement

Definition: The extent to which people can afford decent housing.

Example: Housing affordability has deteriorated in many urban areas.

affordable housing

IPA: /əˌfɔːdəbəl ˈhaʊzɪŋ/
Pronunciation: uh-FOR-duh-buhl HOW-zing
French: logement abordable

Definition: Housing that people on ordinary or low incomes can afford.

Example: Affordable housing is essential for social stability.

social housing

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl ˈhaʊzɪŋ/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl HOW-zing
French: logement social

Definition: Affordable housing provided by councils or housing associations.

Example: Social housing can reduce homelessness and insecurity.

council housing

IPA: /ˈkaʊnsəl ˌhaʊzɪŋ/
Pronunciation: KOWN-suhl HOW-zing
French: logement municipal britannique

Definition: Public housing provided by local councils in Britain.

Example: Council housing was once central to the British welfare state.

public housing

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk ˈhaʊzɪŋ/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik HOW-zing
French: logement public

Definition: Housing provided or funded by the state, especially in the US.

Example: Public housing is often underfunded and stigmatised.

housing association

IPA: /ˈhaʊzɪŋ əˌsəʊsiˈeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: HOW-zing uh-soh-see-AY-shuhn
French: organisme de logement social

Definition: A non-profit organisation providing affordable or social housing.

Example: Housing associations manage many affordable homes.

subsidised housing

IPA: /ˈsʌbsɪdaɪzd ˈhaʊzɪŋ/
Pronunciation: SUB-si-dyzd HOW-zing
French: logement subventionné

Definition: Housing made cheaper through public financial support.

Example: Subsidised housing helps low-income families.

rent control

IPA: /ˈrent kənˌtrəʊl/
Pronunciation: rent kuhn-TROHL
French: encadrement des loyers

Definition: Rules limiting how much landlords can raise rents.

Example: Rent control is debated as a solution to unaffordable housing.

rent cap

IPA: /ˈrent kæp/
Pronunciation: rent kap
French: plafonnement des loyers

Definition: A maximum limit on rent or rent increases.

Example: A rent cap can protect tenants from sudden increases.

housing benefit

IPA: /ˈhaʊzɪŋ ˌbenɪfɪt/
Pronunciation: HOW-zing BEN-i-fit
French: allocation logement

Definition: State support helping low-income people pay rent.

Example: Housing benefit can prevent homelessness.

local housing allowance

IPA: /ˌləʊkəl ˈhaʊzɪŋ əˈlaʊəns/
Pronunciation: LOH-kuhl HOW-zing uh-LOW-uhns
French: allocation logement locale britannique

Definition: UK support used to help private renters on low incomes pay rent.

Example: Local housing allowance often fails to match market rents.

right to buy

IPA: /ˌraɪt tə ˈbaɪ/
Pronunciation: rite tuh buy
French: Right to Buy ; droit d’achat du logement social

Definition: A UK policy allowing council tenants to buy their homes at a discount.

Example: Right to Buy reduced the stock of council housing.

B. Housing supply, planning and urban change

housing stock

IPA: /ˈhaʊzɪŋ stɒk/
Pronunciation: HOW-zing stok
French: parc de logements

Definition: The total number of homes available in an area or country.

Example: The social housing stock has declined in many areas.

housing supply

IPA: /ˈhaʊzɪŋ səˌplaɪ/
Pronunciation: HOW-zing suh-PLY
French: offre de logements

Definition: The number of homes available to buy or rent.

Example: A lack of housing supply pushes prices up.

housing demand

IPA: /ˈhaʊzɪŋ dɪˌmɑːnd/
Pronunciation: HOW-zing di-MAND
French: demande de logements

Definition: The number of people wanting homes in a place.

Example: Housing demand is high in major cities.

shortage of homes

IPA: /ˈʃɔːtɪdʒ əv həʊmz/
Pronunciation: SHOR-tij ov hohmz
French: pénurie de logements

Definition: A lack of enough homes for the population.

Example: The shortage of homes has driven up rents.

housing shortage

IPA: /ˈhaʊzɪŋ ˌʃɔːtɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: HOW-zing SHOR-tij
French: pénurie de logements

Definition: A lack of sufficient housing.

Example: The housing shortage is particularly acute in London.

new-build housing

IPA: /ˌnjuː bɪld ˈhaʊzɪŋ/
Pronunciation: nyoo-bild HOW-zing
French: logements neufs

Definition: Recently constructed housing.

Example: New-build housing is often marketed as a solution to shortages.

housebuilding

IPA: /ˈhaʊsˌbɪldɪŋ/
Pronunciation: HOWSS-bil-ding
French: construction de logements

Definition: The construction of new homes.

Example: Housebuilding has not kept pace with demand.

planning permission

IPA: /ˈplænɪŋ pəˌmɪʃən/
Pronunciation: PLAN-ing puh-MISH-uhn
French: permis de construire

Definition: Official approval to build or change buildings.

Example: Planning permission can delay new housing projects.

zoning

IPA: /ˈzəʊnɪŋ/
Pronunciation: ZOH-ning
French: zonage

Definition: Rules controlling how land can be used.

Example: Zoning restrictions can limit housing supply.

land use

IPA: /ˈlænd juːs/
Pronunciation: land yooss
French: usage des sols

Definition: The way land is used for housing, business, farming or public space.

Example: Land use policy affects housing affordability.

green belt

IPA: /ˌɡriːn ˈbelt/
Pronunciation: green belt
French: ceinture verte

Definition: Protected land around cities where building is restricted.

Example: Green belt policy is controversial in housing debates.

urban sprawl

IPA: /ˌɜːbən ˈsprɔːl/
Pronunciation: UR-buhn sprawl
French: étalement urbain

Definition: Uncontrolled expansion of cities into surrounding areas.

Example: Urban sprawl can damage the environment and increase car dependence.

densification

IPA: /ˌdensɪfɪˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: den-si-fi-KAY-shuhn
French: densification

Definition: Increasing the number of homes or people in an urban area.

Example: Densification can create more homes near jobs and transport.

high-rise building

IPA: /ˌhaɪ raɪz ˈbɪldɪŋ/
Pronunciation: hy-ryze BIL-ding
French: immeuble de grande hauteur

Definition: A very tall building with many floors.

Example: High-rise buildings can provide density but raise safety concerns.

housing development

IPA: /ˈhaʊzɪŋ dɪˌveləpmənt/
Pronunciation: HOW-zing di-VEL-uhp-muhnt
French: programme immobilier résidentiel

Definition: A planned group of new homes.

Example: New housing developments often face local opposition.

real-estate developer

IPA: /ˈrɪəl ɪˌsteɪt dɪˌveləpə/
Pronunciation: REE-uhl i-STAYT di-VEL-uh-puh
French: promoteur immobilier

Definition: A person or company that builds or renovates property for profit.

Example: Real-estate developers are often accused of prioritising profit.

NIMBY

IPA: /ˈnɪmbi/
Pronunciation: NIM-bee
French: NIMBY ; opposant local à un projet

Definition: Someone who opposes development near where they live; from “Not In My Back Yard.”

Example: NIMBY opposition can block new housing.

YIMBY

IPA: /ˈjɪmbi/
Pronunciation: YIM-bee
French: YIMBY ; partisan de la construction locale

Definition: Someone who supports more housing development; from “Yes In My Back Yard.”

Example: YIMBY activists call for more homes in high-demand areas.

gentrification

IPA: /ˌdʒentrɪfɪˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: jen-tri-fi-KAY-shuhn
French: gentrification

Definition: The transformation of a poorer area by wealthier residents, often raising prices and displacing locals.

Example: Gentrification can bring investment but also displacement.

displacement

IPA: /dɪsˈpleɪsmənt/
Pronunciation: dis-PLAYS-muhnt
French: déplacement forcé ; éviction sociale

Definition: The forced movement of residents due to rising costs or redevelopment.

Example: Displacement often affects low-income communities.

regeneration

IPA: /rɪˌdʒenəˈreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: ri-JEN-uh-RAY-shuhn
French: régénération urbaine

Definition: Investment and renewal in a declining urban area.

Example: Urban regeneration can improve areas but may exclude existing residents.

urban renewal

IPA: /ˌɜːbən rɪˈnjuːəl/
Pronunciation: UR-buhn ri-NYOO-uhl
French: rénovation urbaine

Definition: The redevelopment of urban areas, often replacing old buildings.

Example: Urban renewal has sometimes destroyed working-class communities.

neighbourhood

IPA: /ˈneɪbəhʊd/
Pronunciation: NAY-buh-hood
French: quartier

Definition: A local area where people live.

Example: Neighbourhood quality affects life chances.

deprived area

IPA: /dɪˌpraɪvd ˈeəriə/
Pronunciation: di-PRYVD AIR-ee-uh
French: quartier défavorisé

Definition: An area lacking resources, services and opportunities.

Example: Deprived areas often face poor housing and underfunded services.

deprived neighbourhood

IPA: /dɪˌpraɪvd ˈneɪbəhʊd/
Pronunciation: di-PRYVD NAY-buh-hood
French: quartier défavorisé

Definition: A neighbourhood affected by poverty and lack of services.

Example: Children in deprived neighbourhoods face multiple barriers.

inner city

IPA: /ˌɪnə ˈsɪti/
Pronunciation: IN-uh SIT-ee
French: centre-ville populaire ; quartiers centraux

Definition: The central area of a city, often associated with poverty or density in some contexts.

Example: Inner-city housing has changed through gentrification.

suburb

IPA: /ˈsʌbɜːb/
Pronunciation: SUB-urb
French: banlieue résidentielle

Definition: A residential area outside the centre of a city.

Example: Suburbs are often associated with home ownership.

suburbanisation

IPA: /səˌbɜːbənaɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: suh-BUR-buh-ny-ZAY-shuhn
French: périurbanisation ; suburbanisation

Definition: The growth of suburbs as people move away from city centres.

Example: Suburbanisation has reshaped commuting and housing patterns.

urban poverty

IPA: /ˌɜːbən ˈpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: UR-buhn POV-uh-tee
French: pauvreté urbaine

Definition: Poverty concentrated in cities.

Example: Urban poverty is often linked to housing costs.

rural housing crisis

IPA: /ˌrʊərəl ˈhaʊzɪŋ ˈkraɪsɪs/
Pronunciation: ROOR-uhl HOW-zing KRY-sis
French: crise du logement rural

Definition: A shortage or unaffordability of homes in rural areas.

Example: Second homes can worsen the rural housing crisis.

second home

IPA: /ˌsekənd ˈhəʊm/
Pronunciation: SEK-uhnd hohm
French: résidence secondaire

Definition: A home owned in addition to a main residence.

Example: Second homes can price local residents out of rural areas.

holiday let

IPA: /ˈhɒlədeɪ let/
Pronunciation: HOL-uh-day let
French: location saisonnière

Definition: A property rented to tourists for short stays.

Example: Holiday lets reduce the supply of homes for local residents.

C. Insecurity, conditions and homelessness

short-term rental

IPA: /ˌʃɔːt tɜːm ˈrentəl/
Pronunciation: short-term REN-tuhl
French: location courte durée

Definition: A property rented for a short period, often through online platforms.

Example: Short-term rentals can push up rents in tourist cities.

Airbnb effect

IPA: /ˈeəbienbi ɪˌfekt/
Pronunciation: AIR-bee-en-bee i-FEKT
French: effet Airbnb

Definition: The impact of short-term rentals on housing supply, rents and neighbourhoods.

Example: The Airbnb effect is controversial in major cities.

housing insecurity

IPA: /ˈhaʊzɪŋ ˌɪnsɪˈkjʊərəti/
Pronunciation: HOW-zing in-si-KYOOR-uh-tee
French: précarité du logement

Definition: Unstable, unaffordable or unsafe housing.

Example: Housing insecurity affects work, health and education.

tenure insecurity

IPA: /ˈtenjə ˌɪnsɪˈkjʊərəti/
Pronunciation: TEN-yuh in-si-KYOOR-uh-tee
French: insécurité résidentielle

Definition: Lack of security about staying in one’s home.

Example: Short leases create tenure insecurity for renters.

eviction

IPA: /ɪˈvɪkʃən/
Pronunciation: i-VIK-shuhn
French: expulsion locative

Definition: The forced removal of a tenant from a property.

Example: Eviction can push families into homelessness.

no-fault eviction

IPA: /ˌnəʊ fɔːlt ɪˈvɪkʃən/
Pronunciation: noh-fawlt i-VIK-shuhn
French: expulsion sans faute

Definition: An eviction where tenants can be asked to leave without being blamed for wrongdoing.

Example: No-fault evictions are criticised for creating insecurity.

eviction notice

IPA: /ɪˈvɪkʃən ˌnəʊtɪs/
Pronunciation: i-VIK-shuhn NOH-tis
French: avis d’expulsion

Definition: An official notice telling a tenant to leave a property.

Example: An eviction notice can trigger a homelessness crisis.

arrears

IPA: /əˈrɪəz/
Pronunciation: uh-REERZ
French: arriérés

Definition: Money that is owed and overdue, especially rent or mortgage payments.

Example: Rent arrears can lead to eviction.

rent arrears

IPA: /ˈrent əˌrɪəz/
Pronunciation: rent uh-REERZ
French: arriérés de loyer

Definition: Overdue rent owed to a landlord.

Example: Rent arrears rose during the cost-of-living crisis.

mortgage arrears

IPA: /ˈmɔːɡɪdʒ əˌrɪəz/
Pronunciation: MOR-gij uh-REERZ
French: arriérés de prêt immobilier

Definition: Overdue mortgage payments.

Example: Mortgage arrears can lead to repossession.

repossession

IPA: /ˌriːpəˈzeʃən/
Pronunciation: ree-puh-ZESH-uhn
French: saisie immobilière

Definition: The process by which a lender takes back a home when mortgage payments are not made.

Example: Repossession is a risk when households cannot meet mortgage payments.

overcrowding

IPA: /ˌəʊvəˈkraʊdɪŋ/
Pronunciation: OH-vuh-KROW-ding
French: surpeuplement

Definition: Too many people living in too little space.

Example: Overcrowding damages health and children’s education.

overcrowded housing

IPA: /ˌəʊvəˌkraʊdɪd ˈhaʊzɪŋ/
Pronunciation: OH-vuh-KROW-did HOW-zing
French: logement surpeuplé

Definition: Housing with too many residents for the available space.

Example: Overcrowded housing is common among low-income families.

poor housing conditions

IPA: /ˌpɔː ˈhaʊzɪŋ kənˌdɪʃənz/
Pronunciation: poor HOW-zing kuhn-DISH-uhnz
French: mauvaises conditions de logement

Definition: Unsafe, unhealthy or inadequate conditions in a home.

Example: Poor housing conditions worsen respiratory illness.

substandard housing

IPA: /ˌsʌbˈstændəd ˈhaʊzɪŋ/
Pronunciation: sub-STAN-duhd HOW-zing
French: logement insalubre ou non conforme

Definition: Housing below acceptable quality or safety standards.

Example: Substandard housing is often found in neglected rental markets.

damp

IPA: /dæmp/
Pronunciation: damp
French: humidité

Definition: Moisture in walls or buildings, often causing health problems.

Example: Damp can trigger asthma and other health issues.

mould

IPA: /məʊld/
Pronunciation: mohld
French: moisissure

Definition: Fungal growth caused by damp, often harmful to health.

Example: Mould in housing is a serious public health issue.

insulation

IPA: /ˌɪnsjʊˈleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: IN-syuh-LAY-shuhn
French: isolation

Definition: Material that keeps heat inside a building.

Example: Poor insulation increases energy bills.

energy efficiency

IPA: /ˈenədʒi ɪˌfɪʃənsi/
Pronunciation: EN-uh-jee i-FISH-uhn-see
French: efficacité énergétique

Definition: Using less energy to provide the same heating, lighting or comfort.

Example: Energy efficiency can reduce fuel poverty.

fuel poverty

IPA: /ˈfjuːəl ˌpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: FYOO-uhl POV-uh-tee
French: précarité énergétique

Definition: Inability to afford adequate heating or energy.

Example: Fuel poverty is linked to poor insulation and low incomes.

homelessness

IPA: /ˈhəʊmləsnəs/
Pronunciation: HOHM-luhs-nuhs
French: sans-abrisme ; absence de logement

Definition: The condition of having no stable or safe home.

Example: Homelessness is both a housing and poverty issue.

rough sleeping

IPA: /ˌrʌf ˈsliːpɪŋ/
Pronunciation: ruff SLEE-ping
French: sans-abrisme de rue

Definition: Sleeping outside because one has no home.

Example: Rough sleeping is the most visible form of homelessness.

hidden homelessness

IPA: /ˌhɪdən ˈhəʊmləsnəs/
Pronunciation: HID-uhn HOHM-luhs-nuhs
French: sans-abrisme caché

Definition: Homelessness not visible in official counts, such as staying temporarily with others.

Example: Hidden homelessness is difficult to measure.

temporary accommodation

IPA: /ˌtempərəri əˌkɒməˈdeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: TEM-puh-ruh-ree uh-kom-uh-DAY-shuhn
French: hébergement temporaire

Definition: Short-term housing provided to people without a permanent home.

Example: Many families spend months in temporary accommodation.

emergency shelter

IPA: /ɪˌmɜːdʒənsi ˈʃeltə/
Pronunciation: i-MUR-juhn-see SHEL-tuh
French: hébergement d’urgence

Definition: A place providing urgent short-term accommodation.

Example: Emergency shelters save lives but do not solve homelessness.

shelter

IPA: /ˈʃeltə/
Pronunciation: SHEL-tuh
French: abri ; centre d’hébergement

Definition: A place giving protection or accommodation.

Example: A shelter can provide immediate help to rough sleepers.

social cleansing

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl ˈklenzɪŋ/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl KLEN-zing
French: nettoyage social ; éviction des classes populaires

Definition: A critical term for pushing poorer residents out of valuable urban areas.

Example: Critics say some regeneration projects amount to social cleansing.

right to housing

IPA: /ˌraɪt tə ˈhaʊzɪŋ/
Pronunciation: rite tuh HOW-zing
French: droit au logement

Definition: The idea that everyone should have access to adequate housing as a basic right.

Example: Housing campaigners argue for a right to housing.

D. Policy, investment and property markets

housing justice
