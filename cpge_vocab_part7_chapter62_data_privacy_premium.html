Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse data privacy: personal data, consent, cookies, tracking, data breaches, encryption, anonymisation, profiling, surveillance capitalism, digital rights, GDPR, compliance, data sovereignty and the tension between privacy, security, innovation and profit.

A. Core data privacy concepts

data privacy

IPA: /ˈdeɪtə ˈprɪvəsi/
Pronunciation: DAY-tuh PRIV-uh-see
French: confidentialité des données

Definition: The protection of personal information from misuse, excessive collection or unauthorised access.

Example: Data privacy has become a major concern in the digital economy.

privacy

IPA: /ˈprɪvəsi/
Pronunciation: PRIV-uh-see
French: vie privée

Definition: The right to control one’s personal life and information.

Example: Privacy is harder to protect when daily life is increasingly online.

personal data

IPA: /ˌpɜːsənəl ˈdeɪtə/
Pronunciation: PUR-suh-nuhl DAY-tuh
French: données personnelles

Definition: Information relating to an identifiable person.

Example: Names, addresses and location history are personal data.

personally identifiable information

IPA: /ˌpɜːsənəli aɪˌdentɪfaɪəbəl ˌɪnfəˈmeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: PUR-suh-nuh-lee eye-DEN-ti-fy-uh-buhl in-fuh-MAY-shuhn
French: informations personnellement identifiables

Definition: Information that can identify a specific person.

Example: Personally identifiable information must be protected carefully.

PII

IPA: /ˌpiː aɪ ˈaɪ/
Pronunciation: pee-eye-eye
French: données identifiantes

Definition: Abbreviation for personally identifiable information.

Example: Companies must protect PII from leaks and misuse.

sensitive data

IPA: /ˌsensɪtɪv ˈdeɪtə/
Pronunciation: SEN-si-tiv DAY-tuh
French: données sensibles

Definition: Personal data requiring special protection because misuse could cause harm.

Example: Health data and biometric data are sensitive data.

biometric data

IPA: /ˌbaɪəʊˈmetrɪk ˈdeɪtə/
Pronunciation: by-oh-MET-rik DAY-tuh
French: données biométriques

Definition: Data based on physical or behavioural traits such as fingerprints, face or voice.

Example: Facial recognition systems rely on biometric data.

health data

IPA: /ˈhelθ ˌdeɪtə/
Pronunciation: health DAY-tuh
French: données de santé

Definition: Personal information about someone’s health or medical history.

Example: Health data is especially sensitive.

financial data

IPA: /faɪˈnænʃəl ˈdeɪtə/
Pronunciation: fy-NAN-shuhl DAY-tuh
French: données financières

Definition: Personal information about money, banking or transactions.

Example: Financial data can be used for fraud if stolen.

location data

IPA: /ləʊˈkeɪʃən ˈdeɪtə/
Pronunciation: loh-KAY-shuhn DAY-tuh
French: données de localisation

Definition: Data showing where a person or device is or has been.

Example: Location data can reveal habits, beliefs and relationships.

browsing history

IPA: /ˈbraʊzɪŋ ˌhɪstəri/
Pronunciation: BROW-zing HIS-tuh-ree
French: historique de navigation

Definition: A record of websites and pages visited online.

Example: Browsing history can reveal private interests.

search history

IPA: /ˈsɜːtʃ ˌhɪstəri/
Pronunciation: SURCH HIS-tuh-ree
French: historique de recherche

Definition: A record of online searches made by a user.

Example: Search history can be highly revealing.

metadata

IPA: /ˈmetəˌdeɪtə/
Pronunciation: MET-uh-DAY-tuh
French: métadonnées

Definition: Data about data, such as time, location, sender or device information.

Example: Metadata can reveal patterns even without message content.

data subject

IPA: /ˈdeɪtə ˌsʌbdʒekt/
Pronunciation: DAY-tuh SUB-jekt
French: personne concernée

Definition: The person whose personal data is collected or processed.

Example: Under privacy law, data subjects have rights.

data controller

IPA: /ˈdeɪtə kənˌtrəʊlə/
Pronunciation: DAY-tuh kuhn-TROH-luh
French: responsable du traitement

Definition: The organisation deciding why and how personal data is processed.

Example: A data controller must justify data collection.

data processor

IPA: /ˈdeɪtə ˌprəʊsesə/
Pronunciation: DAY-tuh PROH-ses-uh
French: sous-traitant

Definition: An organisation processing data on behalf of a controller.

Example: A cloud provider may act as a data processor.

data processing

IPA: /ˈdeɪtə ˌprəʊsesɪŋ/
Pronunciation: DAY-tuh PROH-ses-ing
French: traitement des données

Definition: Any operation performed on data, such as collection, storage, analysis or sharing.

Example: Data processing must have a legal basis.

data collection

IPA: /ˈdeɪtə kəˌlekʃən/
Pronunciation: DAY-tuh kuh-LEK-shuhn
French: collecte de données

Definition: The gathering of information from users, devices or services.

Example: Data collection often happens invisibly in apps.

data storage

IPA: /ˈdeɪtə ˌstɔːrɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: DAY-tuh STOR-ij
French: stockage des données

Definition: Keeping data in databases, servers or devices.

Example: Data storage creates security responsibilities.

data sharing

IPA: /ˈdeɪtə ˌʃeərɪŋ/
Pronunciation: DAY-tuh SHARE-ing
French: partage de données

Definition: Providing data to another person, company or organisation.

Example: Data sharing can improve services but increase privacy risks.

data transfer

IPA: /ˈdeɪtə ˌtrænsfɜː/
Pronunciation: DAY-tuh TRANS-fur
French: transfert de données

Definition: Moving data from one place, system or country to another.

Example: International data transfers are regulated.

cross-border data transfer

IPA: /ˌkrɒs bɔːdə ˈdeɪtə ˌtrænsfɜː/
Pronunciation: cross-BOR-duh DAY-tuh TRANS-fur
French: transfert transfrontalier de données

Definition: Movement of data between countries.

Example: Cross-border data transfers raise sovereignty and privacy concerns.

data retention

IPA: /ˈdeɪtə rɪˌtenʃən/
Pronunciation: DAY-tuh ri-TEN-shuhn
French: conservation des données

Definition: Keeping data for a certain period.

Example: Data retention should be limited to what is necessary.

data deletion

IPA: /ˈdeɪtə dɪˌliːʃən/
Pronunciation: DAY-tuh di-LEE-shuhn
French: suppression des données

Definition: Removing data permanently from a system.

Example: Users may request data deletion.

data minimisation

IPA: /ˈdeɪtə ˌmɪnɪmaɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: DAY-tuh min-i-my-ZAY-shuhn
French: minimisation des données

Definition: The principle of collecting only the data that is necessary.

Example: Data minimisation reduces privacy risk.

purpose limitation

IPA: /ˈpɜːpəs ˌlɪmɪˈteɪʃən/
Pronunciation: PUR-puhs lim-i-TAY-shuhn
French: limitation des finalités

Definition: The principle that data should be used only for specified purposes.

Example: Purpose limitation prevents data from being reused unfairly.

lawful basis

IPA: /ˈlɔːfəl ˈbeɪsɪs/
Pronunciation: LAW-fuhl BAY-sis
French: base légale

Definition: A legal reason allowing personal data processing.

Example: Consent is one possible lawful basis.

consent

IPA: /kənˈsent/
Pronunciation: kuhn-SENT
French: consentement

Definition: Permission given by a user for data use or processing.

Example: Consent must be informed and freely given.

informed consent

IPA: /ɪnˌfɔːmd kənˈsent/
Pronunciation: in-FORMD kuhn-SENT
French: consentement éclairé

Definition: Consent given with clear understanding of what will happen.

Example: Informed consent requires clear explanations.

explicit consent

IPA: /ɪkˌsplɪsɪt kənˈsent/
Pronunciation: ik-SPLIS-it kuhn-SENT
French: consentement explicite

Definition: Clear and direct permission, often required for sensitive data.

Example: Explicit consent may be needed for health data.

opt-in

IPA: /ˌɒpt ˈɪn/
Pronunciation: opt-in
French: opt-in ; consentement actif

Definition: A choice to actively agree to something.

Example: Privacy-friendly systems often use opt-in settings.

opt-out

IPA: /ˌɒpt ˈaʊt/
Pronunciation: opt-out
French: opt-out ; refus

Definition: A choice to refuse or withdraw from something.

Example: Users should be able to opt out of tracking.

withdraw consent

IPA: /wɪðˌdrɔː kənˈsent/
Pronunciation: with-DRAW kuhn-SENT
French: retirer son consentement

Definition: To take back permission previously given.

Example: Users must be able to withdraw consent easily.

privacy policy

IPA: /ˈprɪvəsi ˌpɒləsi/
Pronunciation: PRIV-uh-see POL-uh-see
French: politique de confidentialité

Definition: A document explaining how an organisation collects and uses personal data.

Example: Privacy policies are often too long and unclear.

terms of service

IPA: /ˌtɜːmz əv ˈsɜːvɪs/
Pronunciation: terms ov SUR-vis
French: conditions d’utilisation

Definition: The legal conditions users accept when using a digital service.

Example: Few users read the terms of service.

cookie

IPA: /ˈkʊki/
Pronunciation: KUUK-ee
French: cookie

Definition: A small file stored on a device to remember information about a user.

Example: Cookies can be used for login, analytics or tracking.

tracking cookie

IPA: /ˈtrækɪŋ ˌkʊki/
Pronunciation: TRAK-ing KUUK-ee
French: cookie de suivi

Definition: A cookie used to follow users across websites.

Example: Tracking cookies support targeted advertising.

third-party cookie

IPA: /ˌθɜːd pɑːti ˈkʊki/
Pronunciation: third-PAR-tee KUUK-ee
French: cookie tiers

Definition: A cookie set by a domain other than the website being visited.

Example: Third-party cookies have been central to online advertising.

cookie banner

IPA: /ˈkʊki ˌbænə/
Pronunciation: KUUK-ee BAN-uh
French: bannière de cookies

Definition: A notice asking users to accept or reject cookies.

Example: Cookie banners often push users towards accepting tracking.

dark pattern

IPA: /ˈdɑːk ˌpætən/
Pronunciation: dark PAT-uhn
French: interface trompeuse ; dark pattern

Definition: A manipulative design choice that nudges users into decisions.

Example: Dark patterns can make privacy choices confusing.

privacy by design

IPA: /ˌprɪvəsi baɪ dɪˈzaɪn/
Pronunciation: PRIV-uh-see by di-ZYNE
French: protection de la vie privée dès la conception

Definition: Designing systems with privacy protections built in from the start.

Example: Privacy by design reduces risks before harm occurs.

privacy by default

IPA: /ˌprɪvəsi baɪ dɪˈfɔːlt/
Pronunciation: PRIV-uh-see by di-FAWLT
French: protection de la vie privée par défaut

Definition: Using the most privacy-protective settings automatically.

Example: Privacy by default means users should not have to fight for privacy.

security by design

IPA: /sɪˌkjʊərəti baɪ dɪˈzaɪn/
Pronunciation: si-KYOOR-uh-tee by di-ZYNE
French: sécurité dès la conception

Definition: Building security protections into systems from the start.

Example: Security by design reduces data breaches.

B. Consent, cookies, privacy by design and security breaches

data breach

IPA: /ˈdeɪtə briːtʃ/
Pronunciation: DAY-tuh breech
French: violation de données

Definition: Unauthorised access to personal or confidential data.

Example: A data breach can expose millions of users.

security breach

IPA: /sɪˈkjʊərəti briːtʃ/
Pronunciation: si-KYOOR-uh-tee breech
French: faille de sécurité

Definition: A failure allowing unauthorised access to systems or data.

Example: A security breach may lead to identity theft.

data leak

IPA: /ˈdeɪtə liːk/
Pronunciation: DAY-tuh leek
French: fuite de données

Definition: Accidental or deliberate exposure of data.

Example: A data leak can damage public trust.

unauthorised access

IPA: /ʌnˌɔːθəraɪzd ˈækses/
Pronunciation: un-AW-thuh-ryzd AK-ses
French: accès non autorisé

Definition: Access to systems or data without permission.

Example: Unauthorised access must be reported quickly.

identity theft

IPA: /aɪˈdentəti θeft/
Pronunciation: eye-DEN-tuh-tee theft
French: usurpation d’identité

Definition: Using someone’s personal information fraudulently.

Example: Identity theft can follow a data breach.

phishing

IPA: /ˈfɪʃɪŋ/
Pronunciation: FISH-ing
French: hameçonnage ; phishing

Definition: Fraudulent messages designed to steal information or credentials.

Example: Phishing emails often imitate trusted companies.

credential theft

IPA: /krɪˈdenʃəl θeft/
Pronunciation: kri-DEN-shuhl theft
French: vol d’identifiants

Definition: Stealing usernames, passwords or login details.

Example: Credential theft can compromise many accounts.

password

IPA: /ˈpɑːswɜːd/
Pronunciation: PASS-word
French: mot de passe

Definition: A secret word or phrase used to access an account.

Example: Weak passwords increase security risks.

passkey

IPA: /ˈpɑːskiː/
Pronunciation: PASS-key
French: clé d’accès ; passkey

Definition: A passwordless login method using cryptographic keys.

Example: Passkeys can reduce phishing risks.

two-factor authentication

IPA: /ˌtuː fæktər ɔːˌθentɪˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: two-FAK-tuh aw-then-ti-KAY-shuhn
French: authentification à deux facteurs

Definition: A login method requiring two forms of verification.

Example: Two-factor authentication protects accounts.

2FA

IPA: /ˌtuː ef ˈeɪ/
Pronunciation: two-eff-ay
French: 2FA

Definition: Abbreviation for two-factor authentication.

Example: 2FA is strongly recommended for sensitive accounts.

multi-factor authentication

IPA: /ˌmʌlti fæktər ɔːˌθentɪˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: MUL-tee-FAK-tuh aw-then-ti-KAY-shuhn
French: authentification multifacteur

Definition: Authentication requiring multiple verification factors.

Example: Multi-factor authentication improves security.

MFA

IPA: /ˌem ef ˈeɪ/
Pronunciation: em-eff-ay
French: MFA

Definition: Abbreviation for multi-factor authentication.

Example: MFA reduces the impact of stolen passwords.

encryption

IPA: /ɪnˈkrɪpʃən/
Pronunciation: in-KRIP-shuhn
French: chiffrement

Definition: Protecting information by making it unreadable without a key.

Example: Encryption protects data in transit and at rest.

end-to-end encryption

IPA: /ˌend tuː end ɪnˈkrɪpʃən/
Pronunciation: end-to-end in-KRIP-shuhn
French: chiffrement de bout en bout

Definition: Encryption allowing only sender and receiver to read messages.

Example: End-to-end encryption protects private conversations.

encrypted data

IPA: /ɪnˌkrɪptɪd ˈdeɪtə/
Pronunciation: in-KRIP-tid DAY-tuh
French: données chiffrées

Definition: Data protected through encryption.

Example: Encrypted data is harder to misuse if stolen.

anonymisation

IPA: /əˌnɒnɪmaɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: uh-non-i-my-ZAY-shuhn
French: anonymisation

Definition: Removing identifying information so data can no longer be linked to a person.

Example: True anonymisation is difficult to guarantee.

pseudonymisation

IPA: /ˌsuːdənɪmaɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: soo-duh-ni-my-ZAY-shuhn
French: pseudonymisation

Definition: Replacing identifying details with codes while keeping possible re-identification separate.

Example: Pseudonymisation lowers risk but is not full anonymisation.

de-identification

IPA: /ˌdiː aɪˌdentɪfɪˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: dee-eye-den-ti-fi-KAY-shuhn
French: désidentification

Definition: Removing or altering identifiers in data.

Example: De-identification helps protect research participants.

re-identification

IPA: /ˌriː aɪˌdentɪfɪˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: ree-eye-den-ti-fi-KAY-shuhn
French: réidentification

Definition: Linking supposedly anonymous data back to a person.

Example: Re-identification is a major privacy risk.

data aggregation

IPA: /ˈdeɪtə ˌæɡrɪˈɡeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: DAY-tuh ag-ri-GAY-shuhn
French: agrégation de données

Definition: Combining data from many sources or people.

Example: Data aggregation can reveal patterns while hiding individuals.

data profiling

IPA: /ˈdeɪtə ˌprəʊfaɪlɪŋ/
Pronunciation: DAY-tuh PROH-fy-ling
French: profilage de données

Definition: Building profiles of people based on collected data.

Example: Data profiling can affect advertising, loans and insurance.

user profiling

IPA: /ˈjuːzə ˌprəʊfaɪlɪŋ/
Pronunciation: YOO-zuh PROH-fy-ling
French: profilage des utilisateurs

Definition: Creating a profile of a user’s behaviour, interests or traits.

Example: User profiling powers personalised feeds.

behavioural data

IPA: /bɪˌheɪvjərəl ˈdeɪtə/
Pronunciation: bi-HAY-vyuh-ruhl DAY-tuh
French: données comportementales

Definition: Data about actions, habits and preferences.

Example: Behavioural data can predict future choices.

predictive analytics

IPA: /prɪˌdɪktɪv ˌænəˈlɪtɪks/
Pronunciation: pri-DIK-tiv an-uh-LIT-iks
French: analyse prédictive

Definition: Using data to predict future behaviour or events.

Example: Predictive analytics can be useful but intrusive.

automated decision-making

IPA: /ˈɔːtəmeɪtɪd dɪˈsɪʒən ˌmeɪkɪŋ/
Pronunciation: AW-tuh-may-tid di-SIZH-uhn MAY-king
French: prise de décision automatisée

Definition: Decisions made by software with limited human involvement.

Example: Automated decision-making can affect access to services.

algorithmic decision

IPA: /ˌælɡəˈrɪðmɪk dɪˈsɪʒən/
Pronunciation: al-guh-RITH-mik di-SIZH-uhn
French: décision algorithmique

Definition: A decision produced or influenced by an algorithm.

Example: Algorithmic decisions should be explainable.

algorithmic transparency

IPA: /ˌælɡəˈrɪðmɪk trænˈspærənsi/
Pronunciation: al-guh-RITH-mik tran-SPARR-uhn-see
French: transparence algorithmique

Definition: Openness about how algorithms use data and make decisions.

Example: Algorithmic transparency helps protect rights.

right to explanation

IPA: /ˌraɪt tə ˌekspləˈneɪʃən/
Pronunciation: right tuh ek-spluh-NAY-shuhn
French: droit à l’explication

Definition: The idea that people should understand automated decisions affecting them.

Example: The right to explanation is debated in AI regulation.

digital footprint

IPA: /ˌdɪdʒɪtəl ˈfʊtprɪnt/
Pronunciation: DIJ-i-tuhl FOOT-print
French: empreinte numérique

Definition: The trail of data someone leaves online.

Example: A digital footprint can last for years.

online tracking

IPA: /ˌɒnlaɪn ˈtrækɪŋ/
Pronunciation: ON-line TRAK-ing
French: suivi en ligne

Definition: Monitoring users’ behaviour online.

Example: Online tracking funds much of the advertising economy.

C. Encryption, anonymisation, profiling and surveillance

device fingerprinting

IPA: /dɪˌvaɪs ˈfɪŋɡəprɪntɪŋ/
Pronunciation: di-VYSS FING-guh-print-ing
French: empreinte numérique d’un appareil

Definition: Identifying a device through its technical characteristics.

Example: Device fingerprinting can track users without cookies.

surveillance

IPA: /səˈveɪləns/
Pronunciation: suh-VAY-luhns
French: surveillance

Definition: Monitoring people, communications or behaviour.

Example: Digital surveillance can be carried out by states or companies.

mass surveillance

IPA: /ˌmæs səˈveɪləns/
Pronunciation: mass suh-VAY-luhns
French: surveillance de masse

Definition: Large-scale monitoring of populations.

Example: Mass surveillance threatens civil liberties.

corporate surveillance

IPA: /ˈkɔːpərət səˌveɪləns/
Pronunciation: KOR-puh-ruht suh-VAY-luhns
French: surveillance d’entreprise

Definition: Monitoring people by companies for profit or control.

Example: Corporate surveillance is central to targeted advertising.

state surveillance

IPA: /ˌsteɪt səˈveɪləns/
Pronunciation: state suh-VAY-luhns
French: surveillance étatique

Definition: Monitoring people by government authorities.

Example: State surveillance can be justified by security but abused politically.

surveillance capitalism

IPA: /səˈveɪləns ˈkæpɪtəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: suh-VAY-luhns KAP-i-tuhl-iz-uhm
French: capitalisme de surveillance

Definition: An economic model based on collecting and monetising behavioural data.

Example: Surveillance capitalism turns private behaviour into profit.

data broker

IPA: /ˈdeɪtə ˌbrəʊkə/
Pronunciation: DAY-tuh BROH-kuh
French: courtier en données

Definition: A company that collects, analyses and sells personal data.

Example: Data brokers often operate out of public view.

data marketplace

IPA: /ˈdeɪtə ˌmɑːkɪtpleɪs/
Pronunciation: DAY-tuh MAR-kit-place
French: marché des données

Definition: A market where data is bought, sold or exchanged.

Example: Data marketplaces raise privacy and consent questions.

targeted advertising

IPA: /ˈtɑːɡɪtɪd ˈædvətaɪzɪŋ/
Pronunciation: TAR-git-id AD-vuh-ty-zing
French: publicité ciblée

Definition: Advertising aimed at specific users based on data.

Example: Targeted advertising relies on personal data.

behavioural advertising

IPA: /bɪˌheɪvjərəl ˈædvətaɪzɪŋ/
Pronunciation: bi-HAY-vyuh-ruhl AD-vuh-ty-zing
French: publicité comportementale

Definition: Advertising based on online behaviour.

Example: Behavioural advertising can feel invasive.

microtargeting

IPA: /ˌmaɪkrəʊˈtɑːɡɪtɪŋ/
Pronunciation: MY-kroh-TAR-git-ing
French: microciblage

Definition: Targeting very specific groups with tailored messages.

Example: Political microtargeting can manipulate voters.

data monetisation

IPA: /ˈdeɪtə ˌmʌnɪtaɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: DAY-tuh mun-i-ty-ZAY-shuhn
French: monétisation des données

Definition: Turning data into money or business value.

Example: Data monetisation is central to many digital platforms.

data exploitation

IPA: /ˈdeɪtə ˌeksplɔɪˈteɪʃən/
Pronunciation: DAY-tuh ek-sploy-TAY-shuhn
French: exploitation des données

Definition: Using data in ways that benefit one party, often unfairly.

Example: Data exploitation can harm vulnerable users.

consent fatigue

IPA: /kənˈsent fəˌtiːɡ/
Pronunciation: kuhn-SENT fuh-TEEG
French: fatigue du consentement

Definition: Users becoming tired of repeated consent requests and accepting without reading.

Example: Cookie banners can create consent fatigue.

privacy fatigue

IPA: /ˈprɪvəsi fəˌtiːɡ/
Pronunciation: PRIV-uh-see fuh-TEEG
French: fatigue liée à la vie privée

Definition: Feeling overwhelmed by privacy choices and risks.

Example: Privacy fatigue makes users less careful.

privacy paradox

IPA: /ˈprɪvəsi ˌpærədɒks/
Pronunciation: PRIV-uh-see PARR-uh-doks
French: paradoxe de la vie privée

Definition: The gap between users saying they value privacy and still sharing data.

Example: The privacy paradox reflects convenience, pressure and lack of control.

digital rights

IPA: /ˌdɪdʒɪtəl ˈraɪts/
Pronunciation: DIJ-i-tuhl rites
French: droits numériques

Definition: Rights relating to privacy, access, expression and fairness online.

Example: Digital rights groups defend privacy and transparency.

right to privacy

IPA: /ˌraɪt tə ˈprɪvəsi/
Pronunciation: right tuh PRIV-uh-see
French: droit à la vie privée

Definition: The right to protect personal life and information.

Example: The right to privacy is challenged by mass data collection.

right of access

IPA: /ˌraɪt əv ˈækses/
