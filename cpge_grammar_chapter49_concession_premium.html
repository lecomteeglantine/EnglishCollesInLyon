Chapter 49 — Concession
B2 → C1 grammar for nuance, counterarguments, contrast and sophisticated academic reasoning.

Chapter objective

This premium grammar chapter helps CPGE students master concession: acknowledging a fact or opposing argument before introducing a contrasting, unexpected or more important point. It covers clauses, prepositions, sentence adverbs and advanced concessive patterns.

Acknowledge one point → present the stronger contrast

A. Lesson — What is concession?

Concession

Rule / explanation: Concession acknowledges a fact or argument before presenting a contrasting or more important point.

Example / answer: Although the reform is ambitious, it remains poorly funded.

Concessive relationship

Rule / explanation: The second idea is surprising or unexpected in light of the first.

Example / answer: Despite strong public support, the bill was rejected.

Argumentative value

Rule / explanation: Concession shows that the writer can recognise complexity instead of ignoring opposing evidence.

Example / answer: Admittedly, the policy reduced costs; nevertheless, it also widened inequality.

CPGE relevance

Rule / explanation: Concession is essential for nuanced essays, article commentary, debate and khôlles.

Example / answer: While the author’s diagnosis is convincing, the proposed solution remains unrealistic.

B. Although, though and even though

Although

Rule / explanation: Use although + subject + verb to introduce a formal concessive clause.

Example / answer: Although the economy has recovered, many households remain vulnerable.

Though

Rule / explanation: Though has the same basic meaning as although but is slightly less formal and can appear at the end.

Example / answer: The proposal is politically risky. It may still succeed, though.

Even though

Rule / explanation: Use even though when the contrast is especially strong or surprising.

Example / answer: Even though the evidence was incomplete, Parliament approved the measure.

No but after although

Rule / explanation: Do not use but in the main clause after although.

Example / answer: Wrong: Although the reform is costly, but it is necessary. Correct: Although the reform is costly, it is necessary.

Clause order

Rule / explanation: The concessive clause may come first or second.

Example / answer: The policy remains popular although its results are disappointing.

C. While and whereas

While for contrast

Rule / explanation: While can contrast two facts, arguments or groups.

Example / answer: While older voters support the reform, younger voters remain sceptical.

Whereas

Rule / explanation: Whereas introduces a clear and often formal contrast between two parallel facts.

Example / answer: Urban areas benefited, whereas rural communities were largely ignored.

Not always temporal

Rule / explanation: In argumentative writing, while often means although or whereas, not during the time that.

Example / answer: While the article is persuasive, it overlooks several historical factors.

Balanced comparison

Rule / explanation: Use while and whereas when both sides deserve similar grammatical weight.

Example / answer: The first text focuses on causes, whereas the second examines consequences.

D. Despite and in spite of

Despite + noun

Rule / explanation: Use despite before a noun or noun phrase.

Example / answer: Despite public opposition, the reform was adopted.

In spite of + noun

Rule / explanation: In spite of has the same meaning but contains three words.

Example / answer: In spite of repeated warnings, the government delayed action.

Despite + gerund

Rule / explanation: Use a gerund when the concessive idea is an action.

Example / answer: Despite facing strong resistance, the minister defended the bill.

Despite the fact that

Rule / explanation: Use despite the fact that + subject + verb when a full clause is needed.

Example / answer: Despite the fact that inflation fell, living standards remained under pressure.

Never despite of

Rule / explanation: Despite is not followed by of.

Example / answer: Wrong: despite of the criticism. Correct: despite the criticism.

E. However, nevertheless and nonetheless

However

Rule / explanation: However links two independent statements and signals contrast or concession.

Example / answer: The reform is expensive. However, it may prove necessary.

Nevertheless

Rule / explanation: Nevertheless means in spite of what has just been said and is more formal.

Example / answer: The evidence is limited. Nevertheless, the conclusion is plausible.

Nonetheless

Rule / explanation: Nonetheless is close in meaning to nevertheless.

Example / answer: The article contains weaknesses. Nonetheless, its central argument remains convincing.

Punctuation

Rule / explanation: These sentence adverbs are normally separated by commas and do not join two clauses with only a comma.

Example / answer: Correct: The policy is flawed; however, it remains popular.

Position flexibility

Rule / explanation: However, nevertheless and nonetheless may appear at the beginning, middle or end of a clause.

Example / answer: The government nevertheless continued with the reform.

F. Admittedly, granted and of course

Admittedly

Rule / explanation: Use admittedly to acknowledge a valid limitation or opposing point before responding.

Example / answer: Admittedly, the reform created jobs; however, most were insecure.

Granted

Rule / explanation: Granted introduces a point accepted for the sake of argument.

Example / answer: Granted, the policy was introduced quickly, but speed does not excuse poor planning.

Of course

Rule / explanation: Of course can acknowledge an obvious point, but it may sound too conversational in formal essays.

Example / answer: Of course, economic growth matters, but it cannot be the only criterion.

Concession-response pattern

Rule / explanation: A strong paragraph can acknowledge, then qualify or refute.

Example / answer: Admittedly, the author identifies a genuine problem. Nevertheless, the proposed remedy is inadequate.

G. Advanced concessive structures

Much as

Rule / explanation: Use much as + clause to mean although, especially in formal style.

Example / answer: Much as the government wishes to reassure voters, distrust remains widespread.

As / though inversion

Rule / explanation: An adjective or adverb can be fronted before as or though in a literary or formal concessive structure.

Example / answer: Costly though the reform may be, it remains necessary.

For all

Rule / explanation: For all + noun means despite.

Example / answer: For all its weaknesses, the article raises an important question.

No matter how

Rule / explanation: Use no matter how + adjective/adverb + clause to express an unlimited concession.

Example / answer: No matter how ambitious the reform is, it cannot succeed without funding.

Whatever / whoever

Rule / explanation: Use concessive relative words to mean regardless of what or who.

Example / answer: Whatever the government decides, public trust will be difficult to restore.

Try as someone might

Rule / explanation: This formal structure means despite making every possible effort.

Example / answer: Try as the minister might, she could not calm the controversy.

H. Concession in CPGE argumentation

Acknowledge the counterargument

Rule / explanation: Present the opposing idea fairly before defending your position.

Example / answer: Although free markets encourage innovation, they do not automatically guarantee fairness.

Partial agreement

Rule / explanation: Use concession to agree with one part of an argument but reject its conclusion.

Example / answer: The author is right to stress urgency; nevertheless, the proposed timetable is unrealistic.

Nuanced thesis

Rule / explanation: A concessive thesis avoids simplistic yes-or-no reasoning.

Example / answer: While regulation may restrict some forms of innovation, it can also protect citizens and strengthen trust.

Paragraph structure

Rule / explanation: A useful pattern is concession → limitation → main claim.

Example / answer: Admittedly, the policy produced short-term growth. However, its social costs make it unsustainable.

Oral debate

Rule / explanation: Concession helps respond politely and strategically.

Example / answer: I take your point; however, the evidence does not support such a broad conclusion.

I. Concession in article summaries and commentary

The author concedes that...

Rule / explanation: Use concede to report an acknowledged limitation.

Example / answer: The author concedes that the reform may be costly.

While acknowledging...

Rule / explanation: Use a participle clause to introduce a recognised point.

Example / answer: While acknowledging the policy’s benefits, the journalist questions its fairness.

Despite recognising...

Rule / explanation: Use this structure for formal synthesis.

Example / answer: Despite recognising the urgency of the crisis, the article rejects immediate intervention.

The argument remains convincing despite...

Rule / explanation: Use despite to evaluate strengths and weaknesses together.

Example / answer: The argument remains convincing despite several factual omissions.

Though valid, this point...

Rule / explanation: Use a reduced concessive clause for concise evaluation.

Example / answer: Though valid, this point does not address the central issue.

J. Typical mistakes by French speakers

Although + but

Rule / explanation: Do not use both connectors in the same sentence.

Example / answer: Wrong: Although it is expensive, but it is useful. Correct: Although it is expensive, it is useful.

Despite + clause

Rule / explanation: Despite is followed by a noun, gerund or the fact that, not directly by a finite clause.

Example / answer: Wrong: Despite the policy is costly. Correct: Although the policy is costly / Despite its cost.

Despite of

Rule / explanation: Never add of after despite.

Example / answer: Wrong: despite of the crisis. Correct: despite the crisis.

Comma splice with however

Rule / explanation: However is not a coordinating conjunction.

Example / answer: Wrong: The policy failed, however it remained popular. Correct: The policy failed; however, it remained popular.

Confusing while and during

Rule / explanation: While introduces a clause; during introduces a noun phrase.

Example / answer: While the debate continued... / During the debate...

Overusing however

Rule / explanation: Vary the structure to avoid mechanical writing.

Example / answer: Use although, whereas, nevertheless, despite and admittedly where appropriate.

K. Exercises

1. Combine with although

Rule / explanation: The reform is expensive. It remains necessary.

Example / answer: Although the reform is expensive, it remains necessary.

2. Rewrite with despite

Rule / explanation: Although the public opposed the bill, Parliament passed it.

Example / answer: Despite public opposition, Parliament passed the bill.

3. Correct the sentence

Rule / explanation: Despite the policy is popular, it has produced few results.

Example / answer: Although the policy is popular, it has produced few results. / Despite its popularity, it has produced few results.

4. Correct the punctuation

Rule / explanation: The evidence is incomplete, however the conclusion is plausible.

Example / answer: The evidence is incomplete; however, the conclusion is plausible.

5. Rewrite with whereas

Rule / explanation: The first article examines causes. The second examines consequences.

Example / answer: The first article examines causes, whereas the second examines consequences.

6. Use an advanced concessive structure

Rule / explanation: The reform may be costly, but it is necessary.

Example / answer: Costly though the reform may be, it is necessary.

7. Translate

Rule / explanation: Malgré ses faiblesses, l’article soulève une question essentielle.

Example / answer: Despite its weaknesses, the article raises an essential question.

8. Mini concours challenge

Rule / explanation: Write a short paragraph using although, admittedly, nevertheless and despite.

Example / answer: Example: Although the policy reduced unemployment, its long-term effects remain uncertain. Admittedly, it restored some confidence. Nevertheless, inequality continued to rise despite stronger economic growth.
