CPGE English Vocabulary Bible

Part 10 — Concours Toolkit | Chapter 84 — Comparing and Contrasting
Essential phrases for comparative essays, khôlles and press article analysis.

Chapter objective

This premium toolkit chapter helps CPGE students compare and contrast ideas, countries, systems, arguments and data with precision. It gives you the language needed to express similarity, difference, degree, perspective, analogy, false equivalence and nuanced comparison without relying only on whereas and however.

ComparisonContrastSimilarityDifferenceConcours ToolkitKhôlles

How to use this chapter

For essaysUse phrases such as whereas, by contrast, in comparison with, what sets them apart is, the comparison has limits, and one should not equate.
For khôllesUse these expressions to compare countries or texts clearly: “Unlike...”, “Similarly...”, “The key difference lies in...”, “This analogy is useful because...”
WarningA good comparison does not just list similarities and differences. It explains why they matter.

A. Core comparison vocabulary

comparison

IPA: /kəmˈpærɪsən/
Pronunciation: kuhm-PAR-i-suhn
French: comparaison

Definition: The act of examining similarities and differences between two or more things.

Example: A comparison between the US and the UK reveals different political traditions.

contrast

IPA: /ˈkɒntrɑːst/
Pronunciation: KON-trast
French: contraste ; opposition

Definition: A clear difference between two things.

Example: The contrast between urban and rural voters is striking.

to compare

IPA: /kəmˈpeə/
Pronunciation: kuhm-PAIR
French: comparer

Definition: To examine similarities and differences.

Example: The article compares American and British institutions.

to contrast

IPA: /kənˈtrɑːst/
Pronunciation: kuhn-TRAST
French: opposer ; mettre en contraste

Definition: To emphasise differences between two things.

Example: The writer contrasts political promises with economic reality.

comparative

IPA: /kəmˈpærətɪv/
Pronunciation: kuhm-PAR-uh-tiv
French: comparatif

Definition: Based on comparison.

Example: A comparative approach helps students avoid narrow analysis.

comparatively

IPA: /kəmˈpærətɪvli/
Pronunciation: kuhm-PAR-uh-tiv-lee
French: comparativement

Definition: Compared with something else.

Example: Comparatively, the British system is more flexible.

relative

IPA: /ˈrelətɪv/
Pronunciation: REL-uh-tiv
French: relatif

Definition: Considered in relation to something else.

Example: The relative success of the policy depends on the criteria used.

relatively

IPA: /ˈrelətɪvli/
Pronunciation: REL-uh-tiv-lee
French: relativement

Definition: Compared with others; to a moderate degree.

Example: The reform was relatively popular among older voters.

similarity

IPA: /ˌsɪməˈlærəti/
Pronunciation: sim-uh-LARR-uh-tee
French: similarité

Definition: A feature shared by two or more things.

Example: There are similarities between the two political crises.

difference

IPA: /ˈdɪfrəns/
Pronunciation: DIF-ruhns
French: différence

Definition: A point on which things are not the same.

Example: The difference lies in the role of the constitution.

parallel

IPA: /ˈpærəlel/
Pronunciation: PARR-uh-lel
French: parallèle

Definition: A similarity between two situations or developments.

Example: There is a parallel between today’s populism and earlier anti-elite movements.

analogy

IPA: /əˈnælədʒi/
Pronunciation: uh-NAL-uh-jee
French: analogie

Definition: A comparison used to explain something by relating it to something similar.

Example: The analogy between the press and a watchdog is common.

equivalence

IPA: /ɪˈkwɪvələns/
Pronunciation: i-KWIV-uh-luhns
French: équivalence

Definition: The state of being equal or comparable in value or meaning.

Example: The essay questions the equivalence between free speech and platform access.

false equivalence

IPA: /ˌfɔːls ɪˈkwɪvələns/
Pronunciation: false i-KWIV-uh-luhns
French: fausse équivalence

Definition: A misleading comparison between things that are not truly equivalent.

Example: False equivalence can distort debates about climate science.

counterpart

IPA: /ˈkaʊntəpɑːt/
Pronunciation: KOWN-tuh-part
French: homologue ; équivalent

Definition: A person or thing with a similar role in another system or country.

Example: The British prime minister has no exact American counterpart.

equivalent

IPA: /ɪˈkwɪvələnt/
Pronunciation: i-KWIV-uh-luhnt
French: équivalent

Definition: Having a similar function, value or meaning.

Example: The House of Commons is not equivalent to the US House in every respect.

respectively

IPA: /rɪˈspektɪvli/
Pronunciation: ri-SPEK-tiv-lee
French: respectivement

Definition: In the order already mentioned.

Example: The US and UK systems are presidential and parliamentary, respectively.

likewise

IPA: /ˈlaɪkwaɪz/
Pronunciation: LYK-wyz
French: de même

Definition: In the same way; similarly.

Example: Likewise, Canada has faced debates over immigration.

similarly

IPA: /ˈsɪmələli/
Pronunciation: SIM-uh-luh-lee
French: de même ; pareillement

Definition: In a similar way.

Example: Similarly, social media has changed British politics.

in the same way

IPA: /ɪn ðə seɪm weɪ/
Pronunciation: in thuh same way
French: de la même manière

Definition: Used to introduce a similarity.

Example: In the same way, streaming has disrupted traditional television.

in the same vein

IPA: /ɪn ðə seɪm veɪn/
Pronunciation: in thuh same vayn
French: dans le même esprit

Definition: In a similar style or line of argument.

Pronunciation: uh-LONG SIM-uh-luh lynz

IPA: /kənˈvɜːdʒ/
Pronunciation: kuhn-VURJ
French: converger

Definition: To become more similar or come together.

Example: The two parties have converged on immigration.

to diverge

IPA: /daɪˈvɜːdʒ/
Pronunciation: dy-VURJ
French: diverger

Example: Public attitudes diverge sharply by age.

IPA: /ˌweərˈæz/
Pronunciation: wair-AZ

C. Expressing contrast

while

IPA: /waɪl/
Pronunciation: wyle
French: tandis que ; alors que

Definition: Used to contrast two ideas or facts.

Example: While the US has a written constitution, the UK relies on conventions.

unlike

IPA: /ˌʌnˈlaɪk/
Pronunciation: un-LYK
French: contrairement à

Definition: Different from.

Example: Unlike the UK, the US has a presidential system.

contrary to

IPA: /ˈkɒntrəri tə/
Pronunciation: KON-truh-ree tuh
French: contrairement à

Definition: In opposition to what is expected or believed.

Example: Contrary to expectations, turnout rose.

as opposed to

IPA: /æz əˈpəʊzd tə/
Pronunciation: az uh-POHZD tuh
French: par opposition à

Definition: In contrast with.

Example: The policy targets renters as opposed to homeowners.

rather than

IPA: /ˈrɑːðə ðæn/
Pronunciation: RAH-thuh than
French: plutôt que

Definition: Instead of; used to contrast alternatives.

Example: The crisis is political rather than purely economic.

instead of

IPA: /ɪnˈsted əv/
Pronunciation: in-STED ov
French: au lieu de

Definition: In place of.

Example: Instead of addressing poverty, leaders blamed immigration.

instead

IPA: /ɪnˈsted/
Pronunciation: in-STED
French: à la place ; au contraire

Definition: As an alternative or contrast.

Example: Instead, the government chose deregulation.

by contrast

IPA: /baɪ ˈkɒntrɑːst/
Pronunciation: by KON-trast
French: par contraste

Definition: Used to emphasise difference.

Example: By contrast, Scandinavian countries have stronger welfare systems.

in contrast

IPA: /ɪn ˈkɒntrɑːst/
Pronunciation: in KON-trast
French: en revanche ; par contraste

Definition: Used to introduce a difference.

on the contrary

IPA: /ɒn ðə ˈkɒntrəri/
Pronunciation: on thuh KON-truh-ree
French: au contraire

Definition: Used to reject a previous idea and assert the opposite.

IPA: /ˈkɒnvɜːsli/
Pronunciation: KON-vurss-lee

IPA: /haʊˈevə/
Pronunciation: how-EV-uh

Example: However, the comparison has limits.

IPA: /ˌnevəðəˈles/

Example: Nevertheless, the two systems share some weaknesses.

Pronunciation: yet

Example: The reform is popular, yet ineffective.

IPA: /stɪl/
Pronunciation: stil

IPA: /ɒn ðə wʌn hænd/
Pronunciation: on thuh one hand
