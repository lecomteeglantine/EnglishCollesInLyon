Chapter 68 — Hedging and Academic English
B2 → C1 grammar for nuance, evidence, cautious argument and sophisticated academic writing.

Chapter objective

This premium grammar chapter develops advanced control of hedging in academic English: cautious modal verbs, lexical hedges, impersonal structures, limited generalisations, source distance, calibrated criticism and balanced CPGE argumentation.

The golden rule

Match the force of the claim to the quality of the evidence.
Strong evidence → clearly / demonstrate
Partial evidence → suggest / appear to
Uncertain interpretation → may / might / could
Modal hedgesmay, might, could, would, should
Lexical hedgessuggest, indicate, appear, seem, tend
Scope limiterssome, often, in many cases, to some extent
Measured criticismmay overlook, appears insufficient, could be questioned

A. Lesson — What is hedging?

Hedging

Rule / explanation: Hedging means reducing the force of a statement so that it reflects uncertainty, limitation or appropriate academic caution.

Example / answer: The reform may have contributed to the decline in public trust.

Why hedge?

Rule / explanation: Academic writers hedge because evidence is often partial, complex or open to interpretation.

Example / answer: The data appear to support a gradual rather than dramatic recovery.

Not weakness

Rule / explanation: Hedging is not indecision: it is a way of matching language to the strength of the evidence.

Example / answer: The evidence strongly suggests that implementation was uneven.

CPGE relevance

Rule / explanation: Hedging is essential in essays, summaries, article commentary and khôlles because it prevents simplistic or unsupported claims.

Example / answer: The author arguably underestimates the political cost of the reform.

B. Modal verbs as hedges

May

Rule / explanation: Use may for a real but uncertain possibility.

Example / answer: The policy may increase regional inequality.

Might

Rule / explanation: Use might for a more tentative or remote possibility.

Example / answer: The decline might partly reflect demographic change.

Could

Rule / explanation: Use could to present one possible explanation or consequence among several.

Example / answer: The discrepancy could result from a sampling error.

Would

Rule / explanation: Use would to make a claim conditional or hypothetical.

Example / answer: A broader survey would probably produce a different result.

Should

Rule / explanation: Use should for cautious expectation rather than certainty.

Example / answer: The new funding should improve access over time.

C. Lexical verbs of caution

Suggest

Rule / explanation: Suggest presents a conclusion as plausible rather than proven.

Example / answer: The figures suggest that public confidence is weakening.

Indicate

Rule / explanation: Indicate is slightly more formal and evidence-based.

Example / answer: The survey indicates that regional differences remain significant.

Appear

Rule / explanation: Appear to presents an inference based on available evidence.

Example / answer: The policy appears to have benefited urban areas more than rural ones.

Seem

Rule / explanation: Seem to expresses a cautious interpretation of a situation.

Example / answer: The government seems unwilling to revise the timetable.

Tend

Rule / explanation: Tend to describes a general pattern while allowing exceptions.

Example / answer: Younger voters tend to distrust traditional political institutions.

D. Adverbs and stance markers

Apparently

Rule / explanation: Apparently signals that a statement is based on appearance, inference or second-hand information.

Example / answer: Apparently, negotiations have resumed.

Arguably

Rule / explanation: Arguably introduces a defensible interpretation while acknowledging that other views are possible.

Example / answer: The reform is arguably the government’s most significant social policy.

Possibly

Rule / explanation: Possibly weakens commitment to a proposition.

Example / answer: The decision was possibly influenced by electoral concerns.

Presumably

Rule / explanation: Presumably presents a conclusion based on reasonable assumptions.

Example / answer: Presumably, the committee will examine the new evidence.

Potentially

Rule / explanation: Potentially focuses on what may become true under certain conditions.

Example / answer: The measure is potentially harmful to small businesses.

E. Adjectives of probability and caution

Possible

Rule / explanation: Use it is possible that or be possible to for an open possibility.

Example / answer: It is possible that the figures underestimate informal employment.

Likely

Rule / explanation: Use be likely to for a probable development.

Example / answer: The reform is likely to face legal challenges.

Unlikely

Rule / explanation: Use be unlikely to for a low-probability result.

Example / answer: The proposal is unlikely to satisfy all stakeholders.

Plausible

Rule / explanation: Plausible means reasonable or credible, though not proven.

Example / answer: A plausible explanation is that rural areas lacked adequate funding.

Uncertain

Rule / explanation: Uncertain explicitly marks a lack of reliable knowledge.

Example / answer: The long-term impact remains uncertain.

F. Nouns that create distance

Possibility

Rule / explanation: Use the possibility that/of to frame an idea as one option.

Example / answer: The possibility that the data are incomplete cannot be excluded.

Likelihood

Rule / explanation: Use the likelihood of/that to discuss probability formally.

Example / answer: The likelihood of an early election has increased.

Suggestion

Rule / explanation: Use the suggestion that to attribute or soften an interpretation.

Example / answer: The suggestion that the policy caused the decline remains controversial.

Assumption

Rule / explanation: Use assumption to identify a premise that may not be established.

Example / answer: The argument rests on the assumption that markets correct themselves.

Indication

Rule / explanation: Use indication for partial evidence rather than proof.

Example / answer: There is some indication that wages are beginning to recover.

G. Impersonal academic structures

It appears that

Rule / explanation: Use this to present an interpretation without foregrounding the writer.

Example / answer: It appears that the policy has had uneven regional effects.

It seems that

Rule / explanation: Use this for a slightly less formal cautious inference.

Example / answer: It seems that public dissatisfaction is increasing.

It could be argued that

Rule / explanation: Use this to introduce a debatable claim carefully.

Example / answer: It could be argued that the reform prioritises efficiency over fairness.

It is possible that

Rule / explanation: Use this for an explicit statement of possibility.

Example / answer: It is possible that the survey excludes the most vulnerable groups.

It remains unclear whether

Rule / explanation: Use this to mark an unresolved issue.

Example / answer: It remains unclear whether the measures will produce lasting change.

H. Passive reporting structures

It is believed that

Rule / explanation: Use an impersonal passive to report a belief without naming the source.

Example / answer: It is believed that the government will revise the proposal.

It is thought that

Rule / explanation: Use this for a widely held but not necessarily proven view.

Example / answer: It is thought that inflation will decline gradually.

X is believed to

Rule / explanation: Use subject + passive reporting verb + infinitive.

Example / answer: The minister is believed to support the compromise.

X is said to have

Rule / explanation: Use the perfect infinitive for an earlier reported action.

Example / answer: The company is said to have concealed important data.

Caution

Rule / explanation: Passive reporting can hide the source, so use it only when the attribution is genuinely unimportant or widely understood.

Example / answer: Where possible, identify the evidence or source.

I. Quantifiers that hedge generalisations

Some

Rule / explanation: Use some to avoid making a universal claim.

Example / answer: Some voters regard the reform as unfair.

Many

Rule / explanation: Many indicates a substantial group without claiming all.

Example / answer: Many experts question the official forecast.

A number of

Rule / explanation: This formal phrase refers to several cases.

Example / answer: A number of regions experienced severe shortages.

A significant proportion of

Rule / explanation: Use this for cautious quantitative generalisation.

Example / answer: A significant proportion of respondents expressed concern.

In many cases

Rule / explanation: Use this to show that a pattern is frequent but not universal.

Example / answer: In many cases, local authorities lacked sufficient resources.

J. Frequency markers as hedges

Often

Rule / explanation: Often signals a recurring but non-universal pattern.

Example / answer: Economic crises often intensify political distrust.

Frequently

Rule / explanation: Frequently is a more formal equivalent.

Example / answer: Policy changes frequently produce unintended consequences.

Sometimes

Rule / explanation: Sometimes limits a claim to certain cases.

Example / answer: Regulation sometimes creates additional administrative costs.

Generally

Rule / explanation: Generally presents an overall tendency with possible exceptions.

Example / answer: Public support is generally stronger when consultation is transparent.

Typically

Rule / explanation: Typically describes a characteristic pattern, not an absolute law.

Example / answer: Younger voters typically obtain news from digital platforms.

K. Approximation and vague quantity

Approximately

Rule / explanation: Use approximately for a numerical estimate.

Example / answer: Approximately 40 per cent of respondents opposed the reform.

Around / about

Rule / explanation: These are common but slightly less formal.

Example / answer: Around one third of households reported higher costs.

Roughly

Rule / explanation: Roughly signals an approximate figure.

Example / answer: Roughly half the sample supported stronger regulation.

Nearly / almost

Rule / explanation: Use these for a value just below a point.

Example / answer: Almost two thirds of participants expressed concern.

Just over / under

Rule / explanation: Use these for a value slightly above or below a threshold.

Example / answer: Just under 20 per cent remained undecided.

L. Limiting the scope of a claim

In this context

Rule / explanation: Use this to restrict the claim to a particular situation.

Example / answer: In this context, regulation appears necessary.

Within the sample

Rule / explanation: Use this to prevent generalising beyond the data.

Example / answer: Within the sample, younger participants were more supportive.

In the short term

Rule / explanation: Use time limits to qualify outcomes.

Example / answer: In the short term, the reform may reduce administrative costs.

To some extent

Rule / explanation: Use this to acknowledge partial validity.

Example / answer: The policy was successful to some extent.

Under certain conditions

Rule / explanation: Use this to make the claim conditional.

Example / answer: The programme could succeed under certain conditions.

M. Hedges for causal claims

May contribute to

Rule / explanation: Use this when one factor is part of a larger causal explanation.

Example / answer: Economic insecurity may contribute to political distrust.

May be associated with

Rule / explanation: Association does not necessarily imply causation.

Example / answer: High housing costs may be associated with lower mobility.

Could result from

Rule / explanation: Use this for one possible cause.

Example / answer: The discrepancy could result from poor data collection.

Appears to influence

Rule / explanation: Use this for evidence of influence without claiming certainty.

Example / answer: Media coverage appears to influence public perception.

Cannot be attributed solely to

Rule / explanation: Use this to reject a single-cause explanation.

Example / answer: The decline cannot be attributed solely to demographic change.

N. Hedges for evaluation

Relatively

Rule / explanation: Use relatively to compare degree against an implied standard.

Example / answer: The measure was relatively effective in urban areas.

Fairly

Rule / explanation: Fairly expresses moderate degree and is common in British English.

Example / answer: The evidence is fairly convincing.

Somewhat

Rule / explanation: Somewhat softens a negative or critical evaluation.

Example / answer: The conclusion is somewhat overstated.

Largely

Rule / explanation: Largely means mainly, but not completely.

Example / answer: The reform was largely successful.

Partly

Rule / explanation: Partly attributes only a portion of the outcome.

Example / answer: The failure was partly caused by inadequate funding.

O. Softening disagreement and criticism

May overlook

Rule / explanation: Use may to criticise without presenting the judgement as absolute.

Example / answer: The author may overlook the role of regional inequality.

Does not fully account for

Rule / explanation: This is more measured than saying an explanation is wrong.

Example / answer: The argument does not fully account for long-term social costs.

Could be questioned

Rule / explanation: Use the passive to introduce a challenge cautiously.

Example / answer: The reliability of the survey could be questioned.

Appears insufficient

Rule / explanation: Use appear to soften direct rejection.

Example / answer: The evidence appears insufficient to support such a broad conclusion.

Arguably overstates

Rule / explanation: Use arguably to frame a defensible but debatable criticism.

Example / answer: The article arguably overstates the benefits of deregulation.

P. Boosters: when stronger language is justified

Clearly

Rule / explanation: Use clearly when the evidence genuinely supports an obvious conclusion.

Example / answer: The policy clearly failed to meet its stated target.

Strongly

Rule / explanation: Use strongly with suggest, indicate or support when evidence is substantial.

Example / answer: The results strongly suggest a structural problem.

Undoubtedly

Rule / explanation: Use this only when reasonable doubt is minimal.

Example / answer: The crisis has undoubtedly altered the political landscape.

Demonstrate

Rule / explanation: Use demonstrate for strong evidence, not mere assertion.

Example / answer: The findings demonstrate that access remains unequal.

Indeed

Rule / explanation: Indeed reinforces or confirms a previous point.

Example / answer: The reform was costly; indeed, expenditure exceeded all forecasts.

Q. Avoiding excessive hedging

Too many hedges

Rule / explanation: Several hedge markers in one clause make the writing weak and awkward.

Example / answer: Weak: It might perhaps possibly appear that costs may rise.

Choose one main hedge

Rule / explanation: Use the single expression that best matches the evidence.

Example / answer: Better: Costs may rise.

Do not hedge facts

Rule / explanation: Verified facts should normally be stated directly.

Example / answer: The bill passed by 312 votes to 298.

Do not hide your thesis

Rule / explanation: A strong essay still needs a clear position.

Example / answer: Although the exact causes remain disputed, institutional trust is central to the crisis.

Balance

Rule / explanation: Use hedges for uncertainty and boosters for well-supported conclusions.

Example / answer: The policy may reduce costs, but it clearly fails to address unequal access.

R. Hedging in article summaries

Faithful attribution

Rule / explanation: Preserve the source’s degree of certainty rather than strengthening or weakening it.

Example / answer: The author suggests that the reform may deepen inequality.

Claim vs evidence

Rule / explanation: Distinguish between what the source states and what the data support.

Example / answer: The government claims that costs have fallen, but the figures appear inconclusive.

Neutral paraphrase

Rule / explanation: Use reports, notes, suggests and argues according to the source’s stance.

Example / answer: The article indicates that public support is weakening.

Avoid invented certainty

Rule / explanation: Do not transform may into will or suggests into proves.

Example / answer: Keep the original level of commitment.

Concise synthesis

Rule / explanation: Hedging helps combine sources that offer competing explanations.

Example / answer: One text attributes the decline to economic pressure, while another suggests that institutional distrust may be more important.

S. Hedging in CPGE essays

Introduction

Rule / explanation: Use measured wording to frame a complex issue.

Example / answer: Technological change may strengthen participation, but it can also intensify surveillance.

Thesis

Rule / explanation: State the central claim clearly, then qualify its limits.

Example / answer: Regulation is necessary, although its effectiveness depends on implementation.

Counterargument

Rule / explanation: Use may, could and arguably to present alternatives fairly.

Example / answer: Critics may argue that stricter rules discourage innovation.

Evidence

Rule / explanation: Use suggest or indicate when the evidence is not conclusive.

Example / answer: Recent surveys suggest that trust is declining.

Conclusion

Rule / explanation: Avoid claiming more than the analysis established.

Example / answer: The reform is likely to produce limited gains rather than a complete transformation.

T. Hedging in khôlles and oral commentary

Thinking aloud

Rule / explanation: Use may, might, perhaps and it seems to offer interpretations while speaking.

Example / answer: The author may be trying to create a sense of urgency.

Nuanced disagreement

Rule / explanation: Use I am not entirely convinced that or this may be questioned.

Example / answer: I am not entirely convinced that the evidence supports the conclusion.

Alternative explanation

Rule / explanation: Use could also be explained by to expand the analysis.

Example / answer: The decline could also be explained by political fatigue.

Confidence control

Rule / explanation: Do not hedge every sentence; make firm points when evidence is clear.

Example / answer: The headline clearly adopts an alarmist tone.

Natural delivery

Rule / explanation: Use a small number of familiar hedging structures fluently.

Example / answer: Perhaps the most important issue is the lack of regional data.

U. Common mistakes by French speakers

Perhaps may

Rule / explanation: Do not usually combine two equivalent possibility markers unnecessarily.

Example / answer: Awkward: Perhaps the policy may fail. Better: The policy may fail.

It seems me

Rule / explanation: Use it seems to me that, not it seems me.

Example / answer: It seems to me that the conclusion is too broad.

According to me

Rule / explanation: Use in my view or I would argue, not according to me.

Example / answer: In my view, the evidence remains limited.

Apparently vs actually

Rule / explanation: Apparently means according to appearances; actually means in fact.

Example / answer: Apparently, the vote was delayed. Actually, it had already taken place.

Prove vs suggest

Rule / explanation: Do not use prove when evidence is partial.

Example / answer: The survey suggests, rather than proves, that trust is declining.

Overgeneralisation

Rule / explanation: Avoid all, always, never and everyone unless genuinely justified.

Example / answer: Many citizens, not all citizens, opposed the measure.

V. Exercises

1. Hedge an overstatement

Rule / explanation: The reform caused the decline in public trust.

Example / answer: The reform may have contributed to the decline in public trust.

2. Limit a generalisation

Rule / explanation: Young voters distrust traditional parties.

Example / answer: Young voters tend to distrust traditional parties.

3. Soften criticism

Rule / explanation: The author ignores regional inequality.

Example / answer: The author may overlook regional inequality.

4. Use an impersonal structure

Rule / explanation: Perhaps the sample is unrepresentative.

Example / answer: It is possible that the sample is unrepresentative.

5. Remove excessive hedging

Rule / explanation: It might perhaps possibly seem that costs may increase.

Example / answer: Costs may increase.

6. Correct the expression

Rule / explanation: According to me, the evidence is weak.

Example / answer: In my view, the evidence is weak.

7. Translate

Rule / explanation: Les données semblent indiquer que la réforme a eu des effets inégaux selon les régions.

Example / answer: The data appear to indicate that the reform had uneven effects across regions.

8. Mini concours challenge

Rule / explanation: Write a paragraph using may, appear to, arguably, tend to and clearly.

Example / answer: Example: The reform may have reduced administrative costs, but its social impact appears to be uneven. Rural communities tend to benefit less from centralised policies, and the article arguably underestimates this problem. What the evidence clearly shows is that national averages conceal substantial regional differences.
