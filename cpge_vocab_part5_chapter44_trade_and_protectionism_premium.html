Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse trade and protectionism: tariffs, quotas, free trade agreements, the WTO, customs unions, comparative advantage, domestic industry, supply chains, trade wars, economic security, reshoring, carbon border taxes and the growing tension between openness and sovereignty.

How to use this chapter

For press articlesFocus on terms such as tariff, non-tariff barrier, free trade agreement, trade war, reshoring, strategic autonomy, carbon border tax and de-risking.
For oral examsUse this vocabulary to explain the tension between lower prices and protected jobs, global efficiency and economic resilience, free trade and national security.
For essaysMemorise the collocations: to impose tariffs, to remove trade barriers, to protect domestic industry, to disrupt supply chains, to balance openness and security.

A. Core trade and protectionism vocabulary

trade

IPA: /treɪd/
Pronunciation: trade
French: commerce ; échanges commerciaux

Definition: The buying and selling of goods and services between people, companies or countries.

Example: Trade connects national economies and shapes global prosperity.

international trade

IPA: /ˌɪntəˈnæʃənəl treɪd/
Pronunciation: IN-tuh-NASH-uh-nuhl trade
French: commerce international

Definition: The exchange of goods and services across national borders.

Example: International trade can lower prices but also expose workers to global competition.

global trade

IPA: /ˌɡləʊbəl ˈtreɪd/
Pronunciation: GLOH-buhl trade
French: commerce mondial

Definition: Trade taking place across the world economy.

Example: Global trade has expanded through container shipping and digital technology.

free trade

IPA: /ˌfriː ˈtreɪd/
Pronunciation: free trade
French: libre-échange

Definition: Trade with few or no tariffs, quotas or restrictions.

Example: Supporters of free trade argue that it increases efficiency and consumer choice.

protectionism

IPA: /prəˈtekʃənɪzəm/
Pronunciation: pruh-TEK-shuh-niz-uhm
French: protectionnisme

Definition: Policies designed to protect domestic industries from foreign competition.

Example: Protectionism can defend jobs but may raise prices.

trade policy

IPA: /ˈtreɪd ˌpɒləsi/
Pronunciation: trade POL-uh-see
French: politique commerciale

Definition: A government’s rules and strategy concerning imports, exports and trade agreements.

Example: Trade policy has become more political since the rise of China.

trade liberalisation

IPA: /ˈtreɪd ˌlɪbərəlaɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: trade LIB-uh-ruh-ly-ZAY-shuhn
French: libéralisation des échanges

Definition: The reduction of barriers to international trade.

Example: Trade liberalisation was a major feature of late twentieth-century globalisation.

open economy

IPA: /ˌəʊpən iˈkɒnəmi/
Pronunciation: OH-puhn i-KON-uh-mee
French: économie ouverte

Definition: An economy strongly connected to international trade and investment.

Example: Small open economies depend heavily on foreign trade.

closed economy

IPA: /ˌkləʊzd iˈkɒnəmi/
Pronunciation: klohzd i-KON-uh-mee
French: économie fermée

Definition: An economy with little trade or financial interaction with the outside world.

Example: A completely closed economy is rare in the modern world.

trade barrier

IPA: /ˈtreɪd ˌbæriə/
Pronunciation: trade BARR-ee-uh
French: barrière commerciale

Definition: A rule, tax or restriction that makes trade more difficult.

Example: Trade barriers can protect producers but hurt consumers.

tariff

IPA: /ˈtærɪf/
Pronunciation: TAR-if
French: droit de douane

Definition: A tax on imported goods.

Example: Tariffs make imported goods more expensive.

import duty

IPA: /ˈɪmpɔːt ˌdjuːti/
Pronunciation: IM-port DYOO-tee
French: droit d’importation

Definition: A tax charged on goods entering a country.

Example: Import duties can be used to protect domestic manufacturers.

customs duty

IPA: /ˈkʌstəmz ˌdjuːti/
Pronunciation: KUS-tuhmz DYOO-tee
French: droit de douane

Definition: A tax paid on goods crossing a border.

Example: Customs duties are collected at borders.

quota

IPA: /ˈkwəʊtə/
Pronunciation: KWOH-tuh
French: quota

Definition: A limit on the quantity of a good that can be imported or exported.

Example: Import quotas restrict the amount of foreign goods entering a market.

import quota

IPA: /ˈɪmpɔːt ˌkwəʊtə/
Pronunciation: IM-port KWOH-tuh
French: quota d’importation

Definition: A limit on how much of a product can be imported.

Example: An import quota can protect domestic farmers.

export quota

IPA: /ˈekspɔːt ˌkwəʊtə/
Pronunciation: EK-sport KWOH-tuh
French: quota d’exportation

Definition: A limit on how much of a product can be exported.

Example: Export quotas can be used to preserve domestic supply.

non-tariff barrier

IPA: /ˌnɒn ˈtærɪf ˌbæriə/
Pronunciation: non TAR-if BARR-ee-uh
French: barrière non tarifaire

Definition: A trade restriction that is not a tax, such as paperwork, rules or standards.

Example: Non-tariff barriers can be more subtle than tariffs.

red tape

IPA: /ˌred ˈteɪp/
Pronunciation: red tape
French: paperasserie ; lourdeurs administratives

Definition: Excessive bureaucracy or paperwork.

Example: Exporters complain that red tape slows trade.

customs checks

IPA: /ˈkʌstəmz tʃeks/
Pronunciation: KUS-tuhmz cheks
French: contrôles douaniers

Definition: Inspections and paperwork for goods crossing borders.

Example: Customs checks can create delays for businesses.

border checks

IPA: /ˈbɔːdə tʃeks/
Pronunciation: BOR-duh cheks
French: contrôles aux frontières

Definition: Inspections at a border to control goods or people.

Example: Border checks became a major issue after Brexit.

rules of origin

IPA: /ˌruːlz əv ˈɒrɪdʒɪn/
Pronunciation: roolz ov OR-i-jin
French: règles d’origine

Definition: Rules determining where a product is considered to come from for trade purposes.

Example: Rules of origin can create complex paperwork for exporters.

regulatory standard

IPA: /ˌreɡjʊlətəri ˈstændəd/
Pronunciation: REG-yuh-luh-tuh-ree STAN-duhd
French: norme réglementaire

Definition: An official rule setting requirements for products, services or industries.

Example: Different regulatory standards can act as trade barriers.

technical standard

IPA: /ˈteknɪkəl ˈstændəd/
Pronunciation: TEK-ni-kuhl STAN-duhd
French: norme technique

Definition: A technical requirement that products must meet.

Example: Technical standards can protect consumers or restrict competition.

sanitary standard

IPA: /ˈsænɪtəri ˈstændəd/
Pronunciation: SAN-i-tuh-ree STAN-duhd
French: norme sanitaire

Definition: A health and hygiene requirement for food or agricultural products.

Example: Sanitary standards are important in food trade.

phytosanitary measure

IPA: /ˌfaɪtəʊˈsænɪtəri ˈmeʒə/
Pronunciation: FY-toh-SAN-i-tuh-ree MEZH-uh
French: mesure phytosanitaire

Definition: A measure protecting plants, animals or humans from pests or diseases in trade.

Example: Phytosanitary measures affect agricultural exports.

subsidy

IPA: /ˈsʌbsɪdi/
Pronunciation: SUB-si-dee
French: subvention

Definition: Financial support given by government to a business or industry.

Example: Subsidies can support domestic industry but distort competition.

state aid

IPA: /ˌsteɪt ˈeɪd/
Pronunciation: state aid
French: aide d’État

Definition: Government support for companies or sectors.

Example: State aid rules aim to prevent unfair competition.

export subsidy

IPA: /ˈekspɔːt ˌsʌbsɪdi/
Pronunciation: EK-sport SUB-si-dee
French: subvention à l’exportation

Definition: Government support that helps domestic firms sell abroad.

Example: Export subsidies can provoke trade disputes.

dumping

IPA: /ˈdʌmpɪŋ/
Pronunciation: DUMP-ing
French: dumping

Definition: Selling goods abroad at unfairly low prices, often below cost.

Example: Dumping can damage domestic producers.

anti-dumping duty

IPA: /ˌænti ˈdʌmpɪŋ ˌdjuːti/
Pronunciation: AN-tee DUMP-ing DYOO-tee
French: droit antidumping

Definition: A tariff imposed to counter unfairly cheap imports.

Example: Anti-dumping duties are used to protect industries from unfair competition.

trade remedy

IPA: /ˈtreɪd ˌremədi/
Pronunciation: trade REM-uh-dee
French: mesure de défense commerciale

Definition: A measure used to respond to unfair trade practices or import surges.

Example: Trade remedies include anti-dumping duties and safeguards.

safeguard measure

IPA: /ˈseɪfɡɑːd ˌmeʒə/
Pronunciation: SAYF-gard MEZH-uh
French: mesure de sauvegarde

Definition: Temporary protection used when imports seriously harm domestic industry.

Example: Safeguard measures are allowed under certain trade rules.

retaliatory tariff

IPA: /rɪˌtæliətəri ˈtærɪf/
Pronunciation: ri-TAL-ee-uh-tuh-ree TAR-if
French: tarif de représailles

Definition: A tariff imposed in response to another country’s tariff.

Example: Retaliatory tariffs can escalate into trade wars.

trade war

IPA: /ˈtreɪd wɔː/
Pronunciation: trade war
French: guerre commerciale

Definition: A conflict in which countries impose tariffs or restrictions on each other.

Example: A trade war can disrupt supply chains and raise consumer prices.

trade dispute

IPA: /ˈtreɪd dɪˌspjuːt/
Pronunciation: trade di-SPYOOT
French: différend commercial

Definition: A disagreement between countries about trade rules or practices.

Example: Trade disputes are often handled through international institutions.

B. Trade rules, agreements and market access

trade tension

IPA: /ˈtreɪd ˌtenʃən/
Pronunciation: trade TEN-shuhn
French: tension commerciale

Definition: Strain or conflict between countries over trade.

Example: Trade tensions between the US and China affect global markets.

World Trade Organization

IPA: /ˌwɜːld ˈtreɪd ˌɔːɡənaɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: world trade OR-guh-ny-ZAY-shuhn
French: Organisation mondiale du commerce ; OMC

Definition: The international body that oversees global trade rules.

Example: The World Trade Organization provides a framework for resolving disputes.

WTO

IPA: /ˌdʌbəljuː tiː ˈəʊ/
Pronunciation: double-yoo tee oh
French: OMC

Definition: Abbreviation for the World Trade Organization.

Example: WTO rules limit some forms of protectionism.

most-favoured-nation treatment

IPA: /ˌməʊst ˌfeɪvəd ˈneɪʃən ˈtriːtmənt/
Pronunciation: mohst FAY-vuhd NAY-shuhn TREET-muhnt
French: traitement de la nation la plus favorisée

Definition: The WTO principle that trade advantages given to one member should be given to all.

Example: Most-favoured-nation treatment is a key WTO principle.

trade agreement

IPA: /ˈtreɪd əˌɡriːmənt/
Pronunciation: trade uh-GREE-muhnt
French: accord commercial

Definition: An agreement between countries setting trade rules.

Example: Trade agreements can reduce tariffs and open markets.

free trade agreement

IPA: /ˌfriː ˈtreɪd əˌɡriːmənt/
Pronunciation: free trade uh-GREE-muhnt
French: accord de libre-échange

Definition: An agreement reducing trade barriers between countries.

Example: Free trade agreements often cover goods, services and investment.

FTA

IPA: /ˌef tiː ˈeɪ/
Pronunciation: eff-tee-ay
French: ALE ; accord de libre-échange

Definition: Abbreviation for free trade agreement.

Example: FTAs can deepen economic ties between countries.

bilateral agreement

IPA: /ˌbaɪˈlætərəl əˈɡriːmənt/
Pronunciation: by-LAT-uh-ruhl uh-GREE-muhnt
French: accord bilatéral

Definition: An agreement between two countries.

Example: A bilateral agreement can be faster to negotiate than a multilateral deal.

multilateral agreement

IPA: /ˌmʌltiˈlætərəl əˈɡriːmənt/
Pronunciation: MUL-tee-LAT-uh-ruhl uh-GREE-muhnt
French: accord multilatéral

Definition: An agreement involving several countries.

Example: Multilateral agreements can create wider common rules.

regional trade agreement

IPA: /ˌriːdʒənəl ˈtreɪd əˌɡriːmənt/
Pronunciation: REE-juh-nuhl trade uh-GREE-muhnt
French: accord commercial régional

Definition: A trade agreement between countries in a region.

Example: Regional trade agreements can complement or challenge WTO rules.

customs union

IPA: /ˈkʌstəmz ˌjuːniən/
Pronunciation: KUS-tuhmz YOO-nee-uhn
French: union douanière

Definition: An arrangement where members remove internal tariffs and apply a common external tariff.

Example: The EU is a customs union as well as a single market.

single market

IPA: /ˌsɪŋɡəl ˈmɑːkɪt/
Pronunciation: SING-guhl MAR-kit
French: marché unique

Definition: A market allowing free movement of goods, services, capital and people.

Example: The single market reduces barriers within the EU.

common market

IPA: /ˌkɒmən ˈmɑːkɪt/
Pronunciation: KOM-uhn MAR-kit
French: marché commun

Definition: A market with reduced barriers and shared economic rules between countries.

Example: A common market requires more integration than a simple trade agreement.

economic integration

IPA: /ˌiːkəˈnɒmɪk ˌɪntɪˈɡreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: ee-kuh-NOM-ik in-ti-GRAY-shuhn
French: intégration économique

Definition: The process of economies becoming more connected through trade and shared rules.

Example: Economic integration can increase efficiency but reduce national autonomy.

trade bloc

IPA: /ˈtreɪd blɒk/
Pronunciation: trade blok
French: bloc commercial

Definition: A group of countries with preferential trade arrangements.

Example: The EU and USMCA are major trade blocs.

USMCA

IPA: /ˌjuː es em siː ˈeɪ/
Pronunciation: yoo-ess-em-see-ay
French: USMCA ; accord États-Unis-Mexique-Canada

Definition: The United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement, replacing NAFTA.

Example: USMCA governs trade across North America.

NAFTA

IPA: /ˈnæftə/
Pronunciation: NAF-tuh
French: ALENA ; NAFTA

Definition: The North American Free Trade Agreement, replaced by USMCA.

Example: NAFTA reshaped manufacturing across North America.

CPTPP

IPA: /ˌsiː piː tiː piː ˈpiː/
Pronunciation: see-pee-tee-pee-pee
French: CPTPP ; accord transpacifique

Definition: A major trade agreement among Pacific-region countries.

Example: The UK joined the CPTPP after Brexit.

market access

IPA: /ˈmɑːkɪt ˌækses/
Pronunciation: MAR-kit AK-sess
French: accès au marché

Definition: The ability to sell goods or services in another country’s market.

Example: Companies seek market access through trade agreements.

preferential access

IPA: /ˌprefəˈrenʃəl ˈækses/
Pronunciation: pref-uh-REN-shuhl AK-sess
French: accès préférentiel

Definition: Better access to a market than other countries receive.

Example: Preferential access can give exporters a competitive advantage.

level playing field

IPA: /ˌlevəl ˈpleɪɪŋ fiːld/
Pronunciation: LEV-uhl PLAY-ing field
French: conditions de concurrence équitables

Definition: Fair competition rules preventing one side from gaining unfair advantage.

Example: The EU often insists on level playing field commitments.

fair trade

IPA: /ˌfeə ˈtreɪd/
Pronunciation: fair trade
French: commerce équitable

Definition: Trade based on fair prices, labour rights and ethical standards.

Example: Fair trade aims to protect producers in poorer countries.

unfair competition

IPA: /ˌʌnfeə ˌkɒmpəˈtɪʃən/
Pronunciation: un-FAIR kom-puh-TISH-uhn
French: concurrence déloyale

Definition: Competition seen as unjust because of subsidies, dumping or weak standards.

Example: Unfair competition is a common argument for protectionism.

comparative advantage

IPA: /kəmˌpærətɪv ədˈvɑːntɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: kuhm-PARR-uh-tiv ad-VAN-tij
French: avantage comparatif

Definition: The ability to produce something at a lower opportunity cost than others.

Example: Comparative advantage is a key argument for free trade.

competitive advantage

IPA: /kəmˌpetɪtɪv ədˈvɑːntɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: kuhm-PET-i-tiv ad-VAN-tij
French: avantage concurrentiel

Definition: An advantage that allows a company or country to compete successfully.

Example: Skilled workers can give a country a competitive advantage.

specialisation

IPA: /ˌspeʃəlaɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: SPESH-uh-ly-ZAY-shuhn
French: spécialisation

Definition: Focusing production on what one does best.

Example: Trade encourages countries to specialise.

division of labour

IPA: /dɪˌvɪʒən əv ˈleɪbə/
Pronunciation: di-VIZH-uhn ov LAY-buh
French: division du travail

Definition: The splitting of production into specialised tasks.

Example: The division of labour can raise productivity.

economies of scale

IPA: /ɪˈkɒnəmiz əv skeɪl/
Pronunciation: i-KON-uh-meez ov skayl
French: économies d’échelle

Definition: Cost advantages from producing on a larger scale.

Example: Global markets help firms achieve economies of scale.

productivity

IPA: /ˌprɒdʌkˈtɪvəti/
Pronunciation: prod-uk-TIV-uh-tee
French: productivité

Definition: Output produced per worker or unit of input.

Example: Trade can raise productivity by increasing competition.

C. Free trade arguments, industry and supply chains

competitiveness

IPA: /kəmˈpetətɪvnəs/
Pronunciation: kuhm-PET-uh-tiv-nuhs
French: compétitivité

Definition: The ability to compete successfully in markets.

Example: Countries invest in skills to improve competitiveness.

consumer choice

IPA: /kənˌsjuːmə ˈtʃɔɪs/
Pronunciation: kuhn-SYOO-muh choice
French: choix du consommateur

Definition: The range of products available to consumers.

Example: Free trade often increases consumer choice.

consumer prices

IPA: /kənˌsjuːmə ˈpraɪsɪz/
Pronunciation: kuhn-SYOO-muh PRY-siz
French: prix à la consommation

Definition: Prices paid by consumers for goods and services.

Example: Tariffs can increase consumer prices.

inflationary pressure

IPA: /ɪnˌfleɪʃənəri ˈpreʃə/
Pronunciation: in-FLAY-shuh-nuh-ree PRESH-uh
French: pression inflationniste

Definition: Forces causing prices to rise.

Example: Import restrictions can create inflationary pressure.

domestic industry

IPA: /dəˌmestɪk ˈɪndəstri/
Pronunciation: duh-MES-tik IN-duh-stree
French: industrie nationale

Definition: Industry operating within a country.

Example: Protectionism is often justified as a way to protect domestic industry.

infant industry

IPA: /ˌɪnfənt ˈɪndəstri/
Pronunciation: IN-fuhnt IN-duh-stree
French: industrie naissante

Definition: A young industry that may need temporary protection to develop.

Example: The infant industry argument supports temporary tariffs.

strategic industry

IPA: /strəˌtiːdʒɪk ˈɪndəstri/
Pronunciation: struh-TEE-jik IN-duh-stree
French: industrie stratégique

Definition: An industry considered important for national security or economic sovereignty.

Example: Semiconductors are now seen as a strategic industry.

manufacturing

IPA: /ˌmænjʊˈfæktʃərɪŋ/
Pronunciation: man-yuh-FAK-chuh-ring
French: industrie manufacturière

Definition: The production of goods in factories.

Example: Manufacturing jobs have declined in many Western countries.

deindustrialisation

IPA: /ˌdiːɪnˌdʌstriəlaɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: dee-in-DUS-tree-uh-ly-ZAY-shuhn
French: désindustrialisation

Definition: The decline of industrial activity and manufacturing employment.

Example: Deindustrialisation has fuelled support for protectionism.

reshoring

IPA: /ˌriːˈʃɔːrɪŋ/
Pronunciation: ree-SHOR-ing
French: relocalisation

Definition: Bringing production back to the home country.

Example: Reshoring is promoted as a way to rebuild domestic industry.

nearshoring

IPA: /ˌnɪəˈʃɔːrɪŋ/
Pronunciation: NEER-SHOR-ing
French: délocalisation de proximité

Definition: Moving production closer to the home market.

Example: Nearshoring can reduce supply-chain risks.

offshoring

IPA: /ˌɒfˈʃɔːrɪŋ/
Pronunciation: OFF-SHOR-ing
French: délocalisation

Definition: Moving production or services to another country to reduce costs.

Example: Offshoring has lowered costs but displaced some workers.

outsourcing

IPA: /ˈaʊtsɔːsɪŋ/
Pronunciation: OWT-sor-sing
French: externalisation ; sous-traitance

Definition: Contracting work to an outside company, often abroad.

Example: Outsourcing can cut costs but weaken job security.

supply chain

IPA: /səˈplaɪ tʃeɪn/
Pronunciation: suh-PLY chayn
French: chaîne d’approvisionnement

Definition: The network of producers, suppliers and transport involved in making and delivering goods.

Example: Global supply chains are vulnerable to shocks.

global value chain

IPA: /ˌɡləʊbəl ˈvæljuː tʃeɪn/
Pronunciation: GLOH-buhl VAL-yoo chayn
French: chaîne de valeur mondiale

Definition: The worldwide stages of production that create a product’s value.

Example: Global value chains make trade more complex.

supply-chain disruption

IPA: /səˈplaɪ tʃeɪn dɪsˌrʌpʃən/
Pronunciation: suh-PLY chayn dis-RUP-shuhn
French: perturbation de la chaîne d’approvisionnement

Definition: An interruption in the flow of goods, parts or materials.

Example: The pandemic caused severe supply-chain disruption.

economic resilience

IPA: /ˌiːkəˈnɒmɪk rɪˈzɪliəns/
Pronunciation: ee-kuh-NOM-ik ri-ZIL-ee-uhns
French: résilience économique

Definition: The ability of an economy to withstand shocks.

Example: Resilience has become more important than maximum efficiency.

self-sufficiency

IPA: /ˌself səˈfɪʃənsi/
Pronunciation: self suh-FISH-uhn-see
French: autosuffisance

Definition: The ability to produce what is needed without depending on others.

Example: Food and energy self-sufficiency are common protectionist arguments.

economic sovereignty

IPA: /ˌiːkəˈnɒmɪk ˈsɒvrənti/
Pronunciation: ee-kuh-NOM-ik SOV-ruhn-tee
French: souveraineté économique

Definition: A country’s ability to control its own economic choices.

Example: Economic sovereignty is often invoked in trade debates.

strategic autonomy

IPA: /strəˌtiːdʒɪk ɔːˈtɒnəmi/
Pronunciation: struh-TEE-jik aw-TON-uh-mee
French: autonomie stratégique

Definition: The ability to act independently in strategically important areas.

Example: The EU has promoted strategic autonomy in response to supply-chain risks.

national security

IPA: /ˌnæʃənəl sɪˈkjʊərəti/
Pronunciation: NASH-uh-nuhl si-KYOOR-uh-tee
French: sécurité nationale

Definition: The protection of a country’s safety and vital interests.

Example: Trade restrictions are increasingly justified by national security.

economic security

IPA: /ˌiːkəˈnɒmɪk sɪˈkjʊərəti/
Pronunciation: ee-kuh-NOM-ik si-KYOOR-uh-tee
French: sécurité économique

Definition: Protection from economic risks, shocks or dependencies.

Example: Economic security has become a key policy goal.

trade dependency

IPA: /ˈtreɪd dɪˌpendənsi/
Pronunciation: trade di-PEN-duhn-see
French: dépendance commerciale

Definition: Reliance on another country for trade, goods or inputs.

Example: Trade dependency can become a geopolitical vulnerability.

dependency risk

IPA: /dɪˈpendənsi rɪsk/
Pronunciation: di-PEN-duhn-see risk
French: risque de dépendance

Definition: The danger of relying too much on one supplier or country.

Example: Dependency risk is high for critical minerals.

critical minerals

IPA: /ˌkrɪtɪkəl ˈmɪnərəlz/
Pronunciation: KRIT-i-kuhl MIN-uh-ruhlz
French: minerais critiques

Definition: Minerals essential for technology, energy or defence.

Example: Critical minerals are central to green technology and trade policy.

semiconductors

IPA: /ˌsemikənˈdʌktəz/
Pronunciation: SEM-ee-kuhn-DUK-tuhz
French: semi-conducteurs

Definition: Computer chips used in electronic devices and advanced technology.

Example: Semiconductors have become a strategic trade issue.

industrial policy

IPA: /ɪnˈdʌstriəl ˌpɒləsi/
Pronunciation: in-DUS-tree-uhl POL-uh-see
French: politique industrielle

Definition: Government strategy to support selected industries or economic sectors.

Example: Industrial policy has returned after decades of free-market thinking.

green industrial policy

IPA: /ˌɡriːn ɪnˈdʌstriəl ˌpɒləsi/
Pronunciation: green in-DUS-tree-uhl POL-uh-see

Pronunciation: in-VY-ruhn-men-tuhl STAN-duhd

labour standard

IPA: /ˈleɪbə ˈstændəd/
Pronunciation: LAY-buh STAN-duhd
French: norme sociale ; norme du travail

Definition: A rule protecting workers’ rights, wages or conditions.

Example: Trade agreements increasingly include labour standards.

race to the bottom

IPA: /ˌreɪs tə ðə ˈbɒtəm/
Pronunciation: race tuh thuh BOT-uhm
French: course vers le bas

Definition: Competition that lowers wages, taxes or standards to attract business.

social dumping

IPA: /ˈsəʊʃəl ˌdʌmpɪŋ/

French: dumping social

Definition: Competing through lower labour standards or wages.

environmental dumping

IPA: /ɪnˌvaɪrənmentəl ˈdʌmpɪŋ/
Pronunciation: in-VY-ruhn-men-tuhl DUMP-ing
French: dumping environnemental

Definition: Competing through weaker environmental rules.

Example: Environmental dumping can undermine climate action.

currency manipulation

Pronunciation: KUR-uhn-see muh-nip-yuh-LAY-shuhn

exchange rate

IPA: /ɪksˈtʃeɪndʒ reɪt/

French: taux de change

trade balance

IPA: /ˈtreɪd ˌbæləns/
Pronunciation: trade BAL-uhns
French: balance commerciale

Definition: The difference between a country’s exports and imports.

Example: A trade balance can be positive or negative.

trade deficit

IPA: /ˈtreɪd ˌdefɪsɪt/
Pronunciation: trade DEF-i-sit
French: déficit commercial

Definition: When a country imports more than it exports.

trade surplus

IPA: /ˈtreɪd ˌsɜːpləs/
Pronunciation: trade SUR-pluhs
French: excédent commercial

current account

French: compte courant

exports

imports

import substitution

French: substitution aux importations

trade openness

IPA: /ˈtreɪd ˌəʊpənnəs/

French: ouverture commerciale

Definition: The degree to which an economy is open to international trade.

French: interdépendance économique

Definition: A situation where economies rely on each other.

decoupling

embargo
