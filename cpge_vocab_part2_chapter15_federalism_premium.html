A. Levels of government and federal structure

federalism

IPA: /ˈfedərəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: FED-uh-ruh-liz-uhm
French: fédéralisme

Definition: A political system in which power is divided between a national government and regional governments.

Example: Federalism is one of the defining features of the American political system.

federal system

IPA: /ˈfedərəl ˈsɪstəm/
Pronunciation: FED-uh-ruhl SIS-tuhm
French: système fédéral

Definition: A system of government in which authority is shared between central and regional levels.

Example: The United States has a federal system with fifty states.

federal government

IPA: /ˈfedərəl ˈɡʌvənmənt/
Pronunciation: FED-uh-ruhl GUV-uhn-muhnt
French: gouvernement fédéral

Definition: The national government of the United States.

Example: The federal government controls defence, foreign policy and federal law.

state government

IPA: /ˌsteɪt ˈɡʌvənmənt/
Pronunciation: stayt GUV-uhn-muhnt
French: gouvernement d’un État fédéré

Definition: The government of one of the US states.

Example: State governments have major powers over education, policing and elections.

state authority

IPA: /ˌsteɪt ɔːˈθɒrəti/
Pronunciation: stayt aw-THOR-uh-tee
French: autorité des États fédérés

Definition: The power held by state governments.

Example: The ruling strengthened state authority over abortion policy.

local government

IPA: /ˌləʊkəl ˈɡʌvənmənt/
Pronunciation: LOH-kuhl GUV-uhn-muhnt
French: gouvernement local ; collectivités locales

Definition: Government at city, county or municipal level.

Example: Local government manages many everyday public services.

county

IPA: /ˈkaʊnti/
Pronunciation: KOWN-tee
French: comté

Definition: A local administrative area within a US state.

Example: The county is responsible for organising local elections.

municipality

IPA: /mjuːˌnɪsɪˈpæləti/
Pronunciation: myoo-nis-i-PAL-uh-tee
French: municipalité

Definition: A city or town with its own local government.

Example: The municipality introduced new housing rules.

state legislature

IPA: /ˌsteɪt ˈledʒɪslətʃə/
Pronunciation: stayt LEDJ-is-luh-chuh
French: assemblée législative d’un État

Definition: The law-making body of a US state.

Example: The state legislature passed a restrictive voting law.

governor

IPA: /ˈɡʌvənə/
Pronunciation: GUV-uh-nuh
French: gouverneur ; gouverneure

Definition: The elected head of a US state government.

Example: The governor signed the bill into law.

state constitution

IPA: /ˌsteɪt ˌkɒnstɪˈtjuːʃən/
Pronunciation: stayt kon-sti-TOO-shuhn
French: constitution d’un État fédéré

Definition: The constitution of a US state, setting out its own institutions and rights.

Example: Some state constitutions provide broader rights than the federal Constitution.

state law

IPA: /ˌsteɪt ˈlɔː/
Pronunciation: stayt law
French: loi d’un État fédéré

Definition: A law passed by a state government.

Example: State law varies widely across the United States.

federal law

IPA: /ˌfedərəl ˈlɔː/
Pronunciation: FED-uh-ruhl law
French: loi fédérale

Definition: A law passed by the national government and applying across the United States.

Example: Federal law takes priority when it conflicts with state law.

national authority

IPA: /ˌnæʃənəl ɔːˈθɒrəti/
Pronunciation: NASH-uh-nuhl aw-THOR-uh-tee
French: autorité nationale ; pouvoir fédéral

Definition: The power held by the national government.

Example: National authority expanded during the twentieth century.

sovereignty

IPA: /ˈsɒvrənti/
Pronunciation: SOV-ruhn-tee
French: souveraineté

Definition: The authority to govern and make laws.

Example: American federalism divides sovereignty between Washington and the states.

dual sovereignty

IPA: /ˌdjuːəl ˈsɒvrənti/
Pronunciation: DYOO-uhl SOV-ruhn-tee
French: double souveraineté

Definition: The idea that both the federal government and state governments have their own authority.

Example: Dual sovereignty creates tensions between national and state power.

division of powers

IPA: /dɪˌvɪʒən əv ˈpaʊəz/
Pronunciation: di-VIZH-uhn ov POW-uhz
French: répartition des pouvoirs

Definition: The allocation of authority between different levels or branches of government.

Example: The division of powers is central to federalism.

shared powers

IPA: /ˌʃeəd ˈpaʊəz/
Pronunciation: shaird POW-uhz
French: pouvoirs partagés

Definition: Powers exercised by both federal and state governments.

Example: Taxation is one example of shared powers.

B. Constitutional powers and federal clauses

delegated powers

IPA: /ˈdelɪɡeɪtɪd ˈpaʊəz/
Pronunciation: DEL-i-gay-tid POW-uhz
French: pouvoirs délégués au gouvernement fédéral

Definition: Powers specifically granted to the federal government by the Constitution.

Example: Declaring war is one of Congress’s delegated powers.

enumerated powers

IPA: /ɪˈnjuːməreɪtɪd ˈpaʊəz/
Pronunciation: i-NYOO-muh-ray-tid POW-uhz
French: pouvoirs énumérés

Definition: Powers explicitly listed in the Constitution.

Example: The Constitution gives Congress enumerated powers.

implied powers

IPA: /ɪmˈplaɪd ˈpaʊəz/
Pronunciation: im-PLYD POW-uhz
French: pouvoirs implicites

Definition: Powers not explicitly stated but considered necessary to carry out constitutional responsibilities.

Example: Implied powers have allowed federal authority to expand.

reserved powers

IPA: /rɪˈzɜːvd ˈpaʊəz/
Pronunciation: ri-ZURVD POW-uhz
French: pouvoirs réservés aux États

Definition: Powers kept by the states rather than given to the federal government.

Example: The Tenth Amendment protects reserved powers.

concurrent powers

IPA: /kənˈkʌrənt ˈpaʊəz/
Pronunciation: kuhn-KUR-uhnt POW-uhz
French: pouvoirs concurrents

Definition: Powers shared by both federal and state governments.

Example: Both federal and state governments have concurrent powers to tax.

police powers

IPA: /pəˈliːs ˈpaʊəz/
Pronunciation: puh-LEESS POW-uhz
French: pouvoirs de police des États

Definition: State powers to regulate health, safety, welfare and morality.

Example: Police powers give states authority over many public health rules.

home rule

IPA: /ˌhəʊm ˈruːl/
Pronunciation: hohm rool
French: autonomie locale

Definition: The power of local governments to manage their own affairs.

Example: Home rule allows cities to adopt local policies.

state autonomy

IPA: /ˌsteɪt ɔːˈtɒnəmi/
Pronunciation: stayt aw-TON-uh-mee
French: autonomie des États fédérés

Definition: The ability of states to govern themselves in certain areas.

Example: State autonomy is a recurring theme in American politics.

Tenth Amendment

IPA: /ˌtenθ əˈmendmənt/
Pronunciation: tenth uh-MEND-muhnt
French: Dixième amendement

Definition: The amendment stating that powers not delegated to the federal government are reserved to the states or the people.

Example: The Tenth Amendment is often invoked in states’ rights arguments.

Supremacy Clause

IPA: /suːˈpreməsi klɔːz/
Pronunciation: soo-PREM-uh-see klawz
French: clause de suprématie

Definition: The constitutional rule that federal law is superior to conflicting state law.

Example: The Supremacy Clause makes federal law the supreme law of the land.

Necessary and Proper Clause

IPA: /ˈnesəsəri ænd ˈprɒpə klɔːz/
Pronunciation: NESS-uh-suh-ree and PROP-uh klawz
French: clause nécessaire et appropriée

Definition: The clause allowing Congress to pass laws needed to carry out its constitutional powers.

Example: The Necessary and Proper Clause supports implied powers.

Commerce Clause

IPA: /ˈkɒmɜːs klɔːz/
Pronunciation: KOM-urs klawz
French: clause du commerce

Definition: The constitutional clause giving Congress power to regulate interstate commerce.

Example: The Commerce Clause has been used to justify federal regulation.

Full Faith and Credit Clause

IPA: /ˌfʊl feɪθ ən ˈkredɪt klɔːz/
Pronunciation: full fayth uhn KRED-it klawz
French: clause de pleine foi et crédit

Definition: The clause requiring states to respect the public acts, records and judicial decisions of other states.

Example: The Full Faith and Credit Clause helps link state legal systems.

federal preemption

IPA: /ˈfedərəl priːˈempʃən/
Pronunciation: FED-uh-ruhl pree-EMP-shuhn
French: préemption fédérale

Definition: The principle that federal law can override state law in certain areas.

Example: Federal preemption can limit state regulation.

preemption

IPA: /priːˈempʃən/
Pronunciation: pree-EMP-shuhn
French: préemption

Definition: The legal principle by which a higher authority overrides lower-level law.

Example: Preemption disputes often arise in environmental law.

constitutional conflict

IPA: /ˌkɒnstɪˈtjuːʃənəl ˈkɒnflɪkt/
Pronunciation: kon-sti-TOO-shuh-nuhl KON-flikt
French: conflit constitutionnel

Definition: A disagreement involving constitutional powers or rules.

Example: The case created a constitutional conflict between the state and federal governments.

states’ rights

IPA: /ˌsteɪts ˈraɪts/
Pronunciation: stayts rites
French: droits des États fédérés

Definition: The belief that US states should retain significant authority against federal power.

Example: States’ rights arguments are common in abortion and gun debates.

C. States’ rights, centralisation and models of federalism

state sovereignty

IPA: /ˌsteɪt ˈsɒvrənti/
Pronunciation: stayt SOV-ruhn-tee
French: souveraineté des États fédérés

Definition: The authority of a state to govern itself within the federal system.

Example: State sovereignty is limited by the federal Constitution.

federal authority

IPA: /ˈfedərəl ɔːˈθɒrəti/
Pronunciation: FED-uh-ruhl aw-THOR-uh-tee
French: autorité fédérale

Definition: The power of the national government.

Example: The Court upheld federal authority over immigration.

federal mandate

IPA: /ˈfedərəl ˈmændeɪt/
Pronunciation: FED-uh-ruhl MAN-dayt
French: obligation fédérale ; mandat fédéral

Definition: A requirement imposed by the federal government on states or individuals.

Example: Governors criticised the federal mandate.

unfunded mandate

IPA: /ˌʌnˈfʌndɪd ˈmændeɪt/
Pronunciation: un-FUN-did MAN-dayt
French: obligation non financée

Definition: A federal requirement imposed on states without providing enough funding.

Example: States complained that the education law created unfunded mandates.

state resistance

IPA: /ˌsteɪt rɪˈzɪstəns/
Pronunciation: stayt ri-ZIS-tuhns
French: résistance des États

Definition: Opposition by state governments to federal laws or policies.

Example: State resistance complicated the implementation of federal policy.

nullification

IPA: /ˌnʌlɪfɪˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: nul-i-fi-KAY-shuhn
French: annulation par un État ; nullification

Definition: The disputed idea that a state can declare a federal law invalid within its borders.

Example: Nullification has been rejected by federal courts.

secession

IPA: /sɪˈseʃən/
Pronunciation: si-SESH-uhn
French: sécession

Definition: The act of formally leaving a political union or state.

Example: Secession was at the heart of the American Civil War.

devolution

IPA: /ˌdiːvəˈluːʃən/
Pronunciation: dee-vuh-LOO-shuhn
French: dévolution

Definition: The transfer of powers from central government to regional or local authorities.

Example: American federalism differs from British devolution.

decentralisation

IPA: /ˌdiːsentrəlaɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: dee-sen-truh-ly-ZAY-shuhn
French: décentralisation

Definition: The transfer or distribution of power away from central government.

Example: Federalism involves a high degree of decentralisation.

centralisation

IPA: /ˌsentrəlaɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: sen-truh-ly-ZAY-shuhn
French: centralisation

Definition: The concentration of power in the central government.

Example: Critics argue that federal programmes have increased centralisation.

cooperative federalism

IPA: /kəʊˌɒpərətɪv ˈfedərəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: koh-OP-uh-ruh-tiv FED-uh-ruh-liz-uhm
French: fédéralisme coopératif

Definition: A model in which federal and state governments work together on policy.

Example: Cooperative federalism expanded during the New Deal.

dual federalism

IPA: /ˌdjuːəl ˈfedərəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: DYOO-uhl FED-uh-ruh-liz-uhm
French: fédéralisme dual

Definition: A model in which federal and state powers are clearly separated.

Example: Dual federalism is often compared to a layer cake.

competitive federalism

IPA: /kəmˌpetɪtɪv ˈfedərəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: kuhm-PET-i-tiv FED-uh-ruh-liz-uhm
French: fédéralisme concurrentiel

Definition: A model in which states compete with each other over policies, taxes or economic conditions.

Example: Competitive federalism can encourage policy innovation or a race to the bottom.

fiscal federalism

IPA: /ˌfɪskəl ˈfedərəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: FIS-kuhl FED-uh-ruh-liz-uhm
French: fédéralisme fiscal

Definition: The financial relationship between federal, state and local governments.

Example: Fiscal federalism shapes education, healthcare and infrastructure policy.

marble-cake federalism

IPA: /ˌmɑːbəl keɪk ˈfedərəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: MAR-buhl-kayk FED-uh-ruh-liz-uhm
French: fédéralisme imbriqué ; modèle du gâteau marbré

Definition: A metaphor for cooperative federalism, where federal and state powers are mixed together.

Example: Marble-cake federalism describes overlapping responsibilities.

layer-cake federalism

IPA: /ˌleɪə keɪk ˈfedərəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: LAY-uh-kayk FED-uh-ruh-liz-uhm
French: fédéralisme en couches séparées

Definition: A metaphor for dual federalism, where federal and state powers are separate layers.

Example: Layer-cake federalism suggests clear boundaries between levels of government.

laboratories of democracy

IPA: /ləˈbɒrətriz əv dɪˈmɒkrəsi/
Pronunciation: luh-BOR-uh-treez ov di-MOK-ruh-see
French: laboratoires de la démocratie

Definition: A phrase describing states as places where policies can be tested before spreading nationally.

Example: States are often called laboratories of democracy.

policy experimentation

IPA: /ˈpɒləsi ɪkˌsperɪmenˈteɪʃən/
Pronunciation: POL-uh-see ik-sper-i-men-TAY-shuhn
French: expérimentation politique

Definition: The testing of different policies by states or local governments.

Example: Federalism allows policy experimentation across states.

policy patchwork

IPA: /ˈpɒləsi ˈpætʃwɜːk/
Pronunciation: POL-uh-see PATCH-wurk
French: mosaïque de politiques publiques

Definition: A situation where rules vary widely from one state or place to another.

Example: After Dobbs, abortion access became a state-by-state policy patchwork.

state-by-state

IPA: /ˌsteɪt baɪ ˈsteɪt/
Pronunciation: stayt by stayt
French: État par État

Definition: Varying from one state to another.

Example: Marijuana laws differ state-by-state.

interstate variation

IPA: /ˌɪntəsteɪt ˌveəriˈeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: IN-tuh-stayt vair-ee-AY-shuhn
French: variation entre États

Definition: Differences between states in law, policy or outcomes.

Example: Interstate variation is a major feature of American healthcare.

regional disparity

IPA: /ˌriːdʒənəl dɪˈspærəti/
Pronunciation: REE-juh-nuhl di-SPAR-uh-tee
French: disparité régionale

Definition: A significant difference between regions in wealth, rights, access or outcomes.

Example: Federalism can worsen regional disparities.

D. Funding, policy areas and state-by-state variation

race to the bottom

IPA: /ˌreɪs tə ðə ˈbɒtəm/
Pronunciation: rays tuh thuh BOT-uhm
French: nivellement par le bas

Definition: Competition that leads governments to lower standards to attract business or avoid costs.

Example: Critics say competitive federalism can create a race to the bottom.

race to the top

IPA: /ˌreɪs tə ðə ˈtɒp/
Pronunciation: rays tuh thuh top
French: course vers le haut

Definition: Competition that encourages higher standards or better policies.

Example: Federal grants can encourage a race to the top in education.

federal grant

IPA: /ˈfedərəl ɡrɑːnt/
Pronunciation: FED-uh-ruhl grant
French: subvention fédérale

Definition: Money given by the federal government to states or local authorities.

Example: Federal grants help states fund transport projects.

block grant

IPA: /ˈblɒk ɡrɑːnt/
Pronunciation: blok grant
French: dotation globale ; subvention globale

Definition: A broad federal grant that states can use with some flexibility.

Example: Block grants give states more discretion.

categorical grant

IPA: /ˌkætəˈɡɒrɪkəl ɡrɑːnt/
Pronunciation: kat-uh-GOR-i-kuhl grant
French: subvention ciblée

Definition: A federal grant for a specific purpose with conditions attached.

Example: Categorical grants can shape state policy.

matching funds

IPA: /ˈmætʃɪŋ fʌndz/
Pronunciation: MATCH-ing fundz
French: fonds de contrepartie

Definition: Funds provided when a state or local government contributes its own money too.

Example: Medicaid relies partly on matching funds.

federal funding

IPA: /ˈfedərəl ˈfʌndɪŋ/
Pronunciation: FED-uh-ruhl FUN-ding
French: financement fédéral

Definition: Money provided by the national government.

Example: States rely on federal funding for many programmes.

conditional funding

IPA: /kənˈdɪʃənəl ˈfʌndɪŋ/
Pronunciation: kuhn-DISH-uh-nuhl FUN-ding
French: financement conditionnel

Definition: Funding given only if states meet certain requirements.

Example: Conditional funding can pressure states to follow federal priorities.

Medicaid

IPA: /ˈmedɪkeɪd/
Pronunciation: MED-i-kayd
French: Medicaid ; assurance santé publique pour bas revenus

Definition: A US health programme for low-income people, jointly funded by federal and state governments.

Example: Medicaid shows how federal and state responsibilities overlap.

public education

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk ˌedjʊˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik ej-yoo-KAY-shuhn
French: enseignement public

Definition: Education provided or funded by government, mainly controlled by states and local districts in the US.

Example: Public education is largely governed at state and local level.

criminal justice

IPA: /ˌkrɪmɪnəl ˈdʒʌstɪs/
Pronunciation: KRIM-i-nuhl JUS-tis
French: justice pénale

Definition: The system of police, courts and prisons dealing with crime.

Example: Criminal justice policy varies significantly between states.

environmental regulation

IPA: /ɪnˌvaɪrənˈmentəl ˌreɡjʊˈleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: in-vy-ruhn-MEN-tuhl reg-yuh-LAY-shuhn
French: réglementation environnementale

Definition: Rules designed to protect the environment, made by federal or state authorities.

Example: Environmental regulation often involves federal-state conflict.

abortion law

IPA: /əˈbɔːʃən lɔː/
Pronunciation: uh-BOR-shuhn law
French: droit de l’avortement ; législation sur l’avortement

Definition: Law regulating access to abortion.

Example: After Dobbs, abortion law became mainly a state issue.

gun law

IPA: /ˈɡʌn lɔː/
Pronunciation: gun law
French: législation sur les armes à feu

Definition: Law regulating firearms ownership, purchase or use.

Example: Gun laws vary widely from state to state.

voting law

IPA: /ˈvəʊtɪŋ lɔː/
Pronunciation: VOH-ting law
French: loi électorale ; loi sur le vote

Definition: Law regulating elections, voter registration and voting procedures.

Example: Voting laws have become highly contested in several states.

immigration enforcement

IPA: /ˌɪmɪˈɡreɪʃən ɪnˈfɔːsmənt/
Pronunciation: im-i-GRAY-shuhn in-FORSS-muhnt
French: application des lois sur l’immigration

Definition: The implementation of immigration laws, usually by federal authorities.

Example: Immigration enforcement is mainly a federal responsibility.

sanctuary city

IPA: /ˈsæŋktʃuəri ˈsɪti/
Pronunciation: SANK-choo-air-ee SIT-ee
French: ville sanctuaire

Definition: A city that limits cooperation with federal immigration enforcement.

Example: Sanctuary cities have clashed with federal immigration authorities.

red state

IPA: /ˌred ˈsteɪt/
Pronunciation: red stayt
French: État républicain

Definition: A US state that usually votes Republican.

Example: Red states often adopt more conservative policies.

blue state

IPA: /ˌbluː ˈsteɪt/
Pronunciation: bloo stayt
French: État démocrate

Definition: A US state that usually votes Democratic.

Example: Blue states tend to adopt more progressive social policies.

purple state

IPA: /ˌpɜːpəl ˈsteɪt/
Pronunciation: PUR-puhl stayt
French: État politiquement partagé

Definition: A state where both Democrats and Republicans have significant support.

Example: Purple states often become election battlegrounds.

state-level policy

IPA: /ˌsteɪt levəl ˈpɒləsi/
Pronunciation: stayt LEV-uhl POL-uh-see
French: politique au niveau des États

Definition: Policy made or implemented by state governments.

Example: State-level policy has become crucial after Supreme Court rulings.

federal-state conflict

French: conflit entre État fédéral et États fédérés

interstate commerce

Pronunciation: IN-tuh-stayt KOM-urs
French: commerce entre États

Example: Congress can regulate interstate commerce.

redistricting

French: redécoupage électoral

gerrymandering

French: charcutage électoral ; découpage partisan

ballot access

French: accès au scrutin ; accès au vote

voter ID law

IPA: /ˌvəʊtər aɪ ˈdiː lɔː/
Pronunciation: VOH-tuhr eye-dee law
French: loi exigeant une pièce d’identité pour voter

Definition: A law requiring voters to show identification before voting.

Example: Voter ID laws are defended as security measures and criticised as barriers to voting.

E. Electoral federalism and high-value verbs

IPA: /ɪkˈspænd ˈfedərəl ˈpaʊə/
Pronunciation: ik-SPAND FED-uh-ruhl POW-uh
French: étendre le pouvoir fédéral

Definition: To increase the authority of the national government.

Example: The New Deal expanded federal power.

to limit federal authority

IPA: /ˈlɪmɪt ˈfedərəl ɔːˈθɒrəti/
Pronunciation: LIM-it FED-uh-ruhl aw-THOR-uh-tee
French: limiter l’autorité fédérale

Definition: To restrict the power of the national government.

Example: The Court limited federal authority in the case.

to defend states’ rights

IPA: /dɪˈfend ˌsteɪts ˈraɪts/
Pronunciation: di-FEND stayts rites
French: défendre les droits des États

Definition: To argue that states should retain significant powers.

Example: Conservatives often defend states’ rights.

to override state law

IPA: /ˌəʊvəˈraɪd steɪt lɔː/
Pronunciation: OH-vuh-ryd stayt law
French: passer outre une loi d’État

Definition: For federal law or authority to take priority over state law.

Example: Federal law can override state law under the Supremacy Clause.

to challenge a federal mandate

IPA: /ˈtʃælɪndʒ ə ˈfedərəl ˈmændeɪt/
Pronunciation: CHAL-inj uh FED-uh-ruhl MAN-dayt
French: contester une obligation fédérale

Definition: To legally or politically contest a federal requirement.

Example: Several states challenged the federal mandate in court.

to decentralise power

IPA: /diːˈsentrəlaɪz ˈpaʊə/
Pronunciation: dee-SEN-truh-lyz POW-uh
French: décentraliser le pouvoir

Definition: To transfer power away from central government.

Example: Federalism decentralises power across states.

to centralise authority

IPA: /ˈsentrəlaɪz ɔːˈθɒrəti/
Pronunciation: SEN-truh-lyz aw-THOR-uh-tee
French: centraliser l’autorité

Definition: To concentrate power in the national government.

Example: Crises often centralise authority.

to vary from state to state

IPA: /ˈveəri frəm steɪt tə steɪt/
Pronunciation: VAIR-ee frum stayt tuh stayt
French: varier d’un État à l’autre

Definition: To be different in each state.

Example: Abortion access now varies from state to state.

to comply with federal law

IPA: /kəmˈplaɪ wɪð ˈfedərəl lɔː/
Pronunciation: kuhm-PLY with FED-uh-ruhl law
French: se conformer à la loi fédérale

Definition: To follow or obey federal law.

Example: States must comply with federal law.

to resist federal intervention

IPA: /rɪˈzɪst ˈfedərəl ˌɪntəˈvenʃən/
Pronunciation: ri-ZIST FED-uh-ruhl in-tuh-VEN-shuhn
French: résister à l’intervention fédérale

Definition: To oppose action by the national government.

Example: Some governors resisted federal intervention.

to create a policy patchwork

IPA: /kriˈeɪt ə ˈpɒləsi ˈpætʃwɜːk/
Pronunciation: kree-AYT uh POL-uh-see PATCH-wurk
French: créer une mosaïque de politiques publiques

Definition: To produce uneven laws or rules across different states.

Example: The ruling created a policy patchwork across the country.
