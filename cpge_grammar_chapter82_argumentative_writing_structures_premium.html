The golden rule

A strong essay does not list ideas.
It transforms the answer step by step.
QUESTION → THESIS → CLAIM → EVIDENCE → REASONING → QUALIFICATION → JUDGEMENT
Introductioncontext → tension → key question → thesis → direction
Paragraphtopic sentence → explanation → evidence → analysis → link
Developmentprogression through causes, criteria, tensions, conditions or consequences
Conclusiondirect answer → synthesis → decisive condition → natural opening

A. Lesson — What is argumentative structure?

Argumentative structure

Rule / explanation: Argumentative structure is the organised progression by which a writer answers a key question through claims, evidence, reasoning and evaluation.

Example / answer: The essay moves from the apparent benefits of the reform to its hidden costs and finally to the conditions of a fairer alternative.

Not a list of ideas

Rule / explanation: A strong essay is not a collection of relevant points; each paragraph must advance the central answer.

Example / answer: The second paragraph qualifies the first rather than introducing an unrelated example.

Structure and reasoning

Rule / explanation: Organisation should reflect the logic of the issue: causes, tensions, conditions, criteria or consequences.

Example / answer: The debate is organised around efficiency, fairness and legitimacy.

CPGE relevance

Rule / explanation: Structured argument is essential in essays, syntheses, khôlles and article commentary.

Example / answer: Each stage of the plan brings the response closer to a nuanced final judgement.

B. The key question

Specific tension

Rule / explanation: A strong key question identifies the real conflict or difficulty in the topic.

Example / answer: How can governments regulate artificial intelligence without preventing innovation?

Avoid generic formulas

Rule / explanation: Do not rely automatically on To what extent or Is X good or bad.

Example / answer: Ask what conditions, mechanisms or contradictions define the problem.

Open but answerable

Rule / explanation: The question should allow discussion while still requiring a precise conclusion.

Example / answer: Can economic efficiency strengthen political legitimacy when its benefits are distributed unequally?

Contextualised

Rule / explanation: The question should emerge naturally from the introduction’s context.

Example / answer: After describing declining trust, ask how institutions can restore legitimacy.

Plan-generating

Rule / explanation: A good question suggests analytical categories without dictating a simplistic yes/no plan.

Example / answer: The key question points towards effectiveness, fairness and implementation.

C. Building a thesis

Thesis

Rule / explanation: The thesis is the essay’s central answer, not merely the topic.

Example / answer: Regulation is necessary, but it can remain legitimate only if it is transparent, proportionate and adaptable.

Qualified answer

Rule / explanation: A strong thesis often contains a condition, distinction or limitation.

Example / answer: The reform is successful in financial terms but inadequate as a response to regional inequality.

Arguable claim

Rule / explanation: The thesis must be defensible rather than obvious or purely factual.

Example / answer: Technological progress does not automatically produce social progress.

Road map

Rule / explanation: The thesis should make the direction of the argument visible.

Example / answer: The issue is less whether reform is needed than how its costs and benefits are distributed.

Consistency

Rule / explanation: Every major paragraph should contribute to proving or refining the thesis.

Example / answer: Do not conclude with a position that the body has not prepared.

D. Introduction structure

Context

Rule / explanation: Begin with precise background that makes the issue intelligible.

Example / answer: Recent advances in generative AI have intensified debates over employment, education and accountability.

Narrowing

Rule / explanation: Move from broad context to the particular tension addressed by the essay.

Example / answer: The controversy concerns not only innovation but also control over its social consequences.

Key terms

Rule / explanation: Define only terms whose meaning is genuinely necessary to the argument.

Example / answer: Accountability refers here to the ability to identify and challenge responsible decision-makers.

Key question

Rule / explanation: State the central analytical question clearly.

Example / answer: How can innovation be encouraged without allowing responsibility to disappear?

Direction

Rule / explanation: Indicate the logic of the answer rather than mechanically announcing three parts.

Example / answer: The discussion must distinguish technological capacity from the political conditions governing its use.

E. Avoiding weak introductions

Empty generalisation

Rule / explanation: Avoid statements such as Since the dawn of time, technology has changed society.

Example / answer: Begin with relevant contemporary or historical context.

Dictionary opening

Rule / explanation: Do not define ordinary words mechanically.

Example / answer: Define only contested or specialised concepts.

Decorative quotation

Rule / explanation: Use a quotation only when it directly frames the problem.

Example / answer: A famous sentence without analysis adds little.

Question avalanche

Rule / explanation: Do not ask several disconnected rhetorical questions.

Example / answer: One precise key question is stronger.

Mechanical plan

Rule / explanation: Avoid First I will speak about advantages, then disadvantages.

Example / answer: Express the analytical movement of the response.

F. Paragraph architecture

Topic sentence

Rule / explanation: State the paragraph’s central analytical claim.

Example / answer: The reform’s first limitation is its unequal regional impact.

Explanation

Rule / explanation: Clarify why the claim matters and how it relates to the thesis.

Example / answer: Equal national rules can produce unequal outcomes when local capacities differ.

Evidence

Rule / explanation: Provide a fact, example, comparison or source.

Example / answer: Rural waiting times rose even as the national average fell.

Analysis

Rule / explanation: Explain what the evidence demonstrates.

Example / answer: The aggregate improvement therefore conceals a distributional failure.

Link

Rule / explanation: Connect the paragraph’s conclusion to the next stage or central question.

Example / answer: Efficiency must consequently be assessed alongside fairness.

G. Claim–evidence–reasoning

Claim

Rule / explanation: A claim states what the writer wants the reader to accept.

Example / answer: The funding model disadvantages rural regions.

Evidence

Rule / explanation: Evidence provides support through data, examples, sources or comparison.

Example / answer: Rural authorities received 14 per cent less funding per patient.

Reasoning

Rule / explanation: Reasoning explains why the evidence supports the claim.

Example / answer: Because rural provision costs more per patient, equal nominal funding creates unequal real capacity.

Evaluation

Rule / explanation: Evaluation considers strength, limits and implications.

Example / answer: The figures are persuasive, although they cover only a three-year period.

Common weakness

Rule / explanation: Evidence without reasoning leaves the connection implicit.

Example / answer: Do not assume that a statistic explains itself.

H. Developing an analytical chain

Cause

Rule / explanation: Identify the mechanism producing the problem.

Example / answer: Centralised funding ignores regional differences.

Immediate effect

Rule / explanation: Explain the first consequence.

Example / answer: Rural authorities cannot maintain the same level of provision.

Wider implication

Rule / explanation: Connect the effect to the broader issue.

Example / answer: Unequal access weakens the reform’s claim to fairness.

Political consequence

Rule / explanation: Show how practical outcomes affect legitimacy or trust.

Example / answer: Citizens may perceive the policy as indifferent to local realities.

Return to thesis

Rule / explanation: Make clear how this chain supports the central judgement.

Example / answer: Implementation conditions therefore determine political legitimacy.

I. Dialectical structure without for/against

Initial appeal

Rule / explanation: Begin by explaining why a position appears convincing.

Example / answer: National standards promise equality and administrative simplicity.

Internal limit

Rule / explanation: Show the contradiction or condition that weakens the initial position.

Example / answer: Yet identical rules operate within deeply unequal regional systems.

Reframing

Rule / explanation: Move beyond the opposition by changing the analytical criterion.

Example / answer: The issue is not uniformity itself but whether uniformity produces equal access.

Conditional synthesis

Rule / explanation: Offer a resolution based on conditions or distinctions.

Example / answer: National standards are useful only when accompanied by differentiated support.

Advantage

Rule / explanation: This structure creates progression rather than alternating disconnected pros and cons.

Example / answer: Each stage transforms the understanding of the issue.

J. Criteria-based structure

Effectiveness

Rule / explanation: Assess whether a policy achieves its stated objective.

Example / answer: Did the reform actually reduce waiting times?

Fairness

Rule / explanation: Assess how costs and benefits are distributed.

Example / answer: Which groups benefited, and which carried the burden?

Feasibility

Rule / explanation: Assess whether implementation is financially, institutionally and politically realistic.

Example / answer: Can local authorities apply the measure with existing resources?

Legitimacy

Rule / explanation: Assess whether the process and outcome are perceived as justified.

Example / answer: Were affected communities consulted?

Sustainability

Rule / explanation: Assess whether gains can endure over time.

Example / answer: Does short-term saving create larger future costs?

K. Cause–consequence–response structure

Causes

Rule / explanation: Distinguish structural, immediate and triggering causes.

Example / answer: The crisis results from long-term underinvestment, demographic pressure and a recent funding cut.

Consequences

Rule / explanation: Separate direct effects from wider social or political implications.

Example / answer: Clinic closures increase travel, deepen inequality and weaken trust.

Responses

Rule / explanation: Evaluate solutions according to relevance and feasibility.

Example / answer: Emergency funding treats the immediate symptom but not the structural shortage.

Limits

Rule / explanation: Show what each response leaves unresolved.

Example / answer: Digital consultations cannot replace every form of care.

Final judgement

Rule / explanation: Identify the level at which durable action is required.

Example / answer: A sustainable response must combine funding, staffing and local accountability.

L. Comparative structure

Common ground

Rule / explanation: Begin with the feature that makes comparison meaningful.

Example / answer: Both systems aim to improve access while controlling costs.

Criterion 1

Rule / explanation: Compare the cases according to the same dimension.

Example / answer: The decentralised system adapts more effectively to regional needs.

Criterion 2

Rule / explanation: Apply a second shared criterion.

Example / answer: The centralised model provides clearer national accountability.

Interpretation

Rule / explanation: Explain why the differences exist.

Example / answer: Institutional design shapes both flexibility and responsibility.

Synthesis

Rule / explanation: Avoid declaring one model absolutely superior.

Example / answer: A hybrid model may combine national guarantees with local adaptation.

M. Problem–solution structure

Define the problem

Rule / explanation: Describe the problem precisely before proposing action.

Example / answer: The central problem is not total lack of funding but unequal capacity to use it.

Explain causes

Rule / explanation: Solutions should address mechanisms rather than symptoms alone.

Example / answer: Local shortages reflect recruitment difficulties and transport constraints.

Set criteria

Rule / explanation: State what a successful solution must achieve.

Example / answer: A response must be fair, feasible and measurable.

Evaluate alternatives

Rule / explanation: Compare several possible responses.

Example / answer: National bonuses may help recruitment, while local training schemes address long-term supply.

Recommend conditionally

Rule / explanation: Present the preferred solution with necessary safeguards.

Example / answer: The strongest approach combines national support with local implementation.

N. Concession and counterargument

Fair presentation

Rule / explanation: State the opposing position accurately and without ridicule.

Example / answer: Critics argue that stricter regulation may slow innovation.

Concession

Rule / explanation: Acknowledge the part of the objection that is valid.

Example / answer: This concern is legitimate, particularly for small firms.

Response

Rule / explanation: Show why the objection does not defeat the thesis.

Example / answer: However, flexible standards can reduce compliance costs without abandoning safeguards.

Integration

Rule / explanation: Use the counterargument to refine rather than interrupt the essay.

Example / answer: The objection demonstrates the need for proportional regulation.

Straw man warning

Rule / explanation: Do not weaken the opposing view artificially.

Example / answer: Respond to the strongest reasonable version.

O. Refutation structures

Challenge the evidence

Rule / explanation: Question reliability, representativeness or relevance.

Example / answer: The survey is too narrow to support a national conclusion.

Challenge the assumption

Rule / explanation: Identify an unproven premise.

Example / answer: The argument assumes that lower taxation automatically produces investment.

Challenge the causal link

Rule / explanation: Show that correlation does not establish causation.

Example / answer: The two trends coincide, but no mechanism is demonstrated.

Offer an alternative explanation

Rule / explanation: Present another cause that fits the evidence.

Example / answer: Demographic change may explain part of the decline.

Limit the conclusion

Rule / explanation: Accept the evidence while reducing the claim’s scope.

Example / answer: The findings apply to urban respondents, not necessarily to the population as a whole.

P. Using examples effectively

Relevant example

Rule / explanation: Choose an example that performs a clear argumentative function.

Example / answer: The Scottish case illustrates how local autonomy can improve implementation.

Contextualise

Rule / explanation: Briefly explain the example before drawing conclusions.

Example / answer: After devolved authorities adapted funding criteria, rural access improved.

Analyse

Rule / explanation: State exactly what the example demonstrates.

Example / answer: The case suggests that flexibility can strengthen rather than weaken national objectives.

Avoid catalogue

Rule / explanation: Several unexplained examples are weaker than one analysed example.

Example / answer: Do not list countries without comparison.

Limit transfer

Rule / explanation: Recognise when a case may not be fully generalisable.

Example / answer: Scotland’s institutional context differs from England’s.

Q. Source integration

Attribute

Rule / explanation: Identify the source and its position accurately.

Example / answer: The first article argues that centralisation improves accountability.

Compare

Rule / explanation: Bring sources into direct relation rather than summarising them separately.

Example / answer: Whereas the first text stresses clarity, the second emphasises regional flexibility.

Evaluate

Rule / explanation: Assess evidence, assumptions and limitations.

Example / answer: The second source offers stronger comparative data.

Synthesize

Rule / explanation: State what becomes visible when the sources are read together.

Example / answer: Taken together, they reveal a tension between uniform standards and unequal local capacity.

Maintain your argument

Rule / explanation: Sources support the essay; they should not replace the writer’s own analytical structure.

Example / answer: Use documents as evidence within your reasoning.

R. Paragraph transitions

Logical bridge

Rule / explanation: A transition should show why the next paragraph follows.

Example / answer: Economic efficiency, however, is only one criterion of success.

Cumulative transition

Rule / explanation: Add another dimension to the same judgement.

Example / answer: Beyond its financial impact, the reform also affects political legitimacy.

Contrastive transition

Rule / explanation: Explain the need to change perspective.

Example / answer: Yet national averages conceal substantial regional disparities.

From effect to cause

Rule / explanation: Signal a move from observation to explanation.

Example / answer: These unequal outcomes raise the question of why implementation varies so sharply.

From analysis to solution

Rule / explanation: Show that recommendations arise from diagnosed mechanisms.

Example / answer: If local capacity is the decisive factor, reform must address regional resources.

S. Conclusion structure

Direct answer

Rule / explanation: Answer the key question clearly and precisely.

Example / answer: Regulation can support innovation only when it remains proportionate and adaptable.

Synthesis

Rule / explanation: Bring together the argument’s central distinctions.

Example / answer: The debate opposes neither freedom and control nor innovation and caution in absolute terms.

Final criterion

Rule / explanation: Identify the decisive condition or principle.

Example / answer: Legitimacy depends on transparent responsibility.

No repetition

Rule / explanation: Do not reproduce the introduction sentence by sentence.

Example / answer: Reformulate the answer at a higher analytical level.

Opening

Rule / explanation: A final opening should emerge naturally from the conclusion.

Example / answer: The issue therefore raises a broader question about democratic control of private technological power.

T. Argumentative language toolkit

Introducing a claim

Rule / explanation: Use I would argue that, the central issue is or one explanation is.

Example / answer: I would argue that implementation matters more than formal policy design.

Qualifying

Rule / explanation: Use to some extent, largely, under certain conditions and not necessarily.

Example / answer: The programme is effective only under certain conditions.

Conceding

Rule / explanation: Use admittedly, although, while and granted.

Example / answer: Admittedly, national rules improve clarity.

Evaluating

Rule / explanation: Use convincing, flawed, proportionate, feasible and sustainable.

Example / answer: The proposal is politically attractive but financially unsustainable.

Concluding

Rule / explanation: Use on balance, ultimately, taken together and it follows that.

Example / answer: On balance, revision is preferable to abandonment.

U. Common structural mistakes

For/against plan

Rule / explanation: A simple list of advantages and disadvantages often prevents genuine progression.

Example / answer: Organise around tensions, criteria or conditions.

One paragraph per example

Rule / explanation: Examples are evidence, not autonomous arguments.

Example / answer: Group examples under analytical claims.

Hidden thesis

Rule / explanation: The reader should not have to guess the essay’s answer.

Example / answer: State a clear direction in the introduction.

Repeated conclusion

Rule / explanation: Do not end every paragraph with the same general statement.

Example / answer: Each paragraph should add a new implication.

Unbalanced development

Rule / explanation: Avoid spending most of the essay on context and rushing the central analysis.

Example / answer: Prioritise argument over background.

New idea in conclusion

Rule / explanation: Do not introduce a major unsupported claim at the end.

Example / answer: The conclusion synthesises rather than restarts the essay.

V. Exercises and model corrections

1. Improve the key question

Rule / explanation: Is artificial intelligence good or bad?

Example / answer: How can societies benefit from artificial intelligence while preserving accountability and equal access?

2. Write a thesis

Rule / explanation: Topic: national standards and regional inequality.

Example / answer: National standards can protect equal rights, but they require differentiated local support if they are to produce equal outcomes.

3. Build a paragraph

Rule / explanation: Claim: remote work may widen inequality. Add evidence, reasoning and evaluation.

Example / answer: Remote work may widen inequality because access to suitable housing and digital infrastructure is uneven. Surveys show that higher-income workers are more likely to have a separate workspace and stable broadband. These differences affect productivity and career visibility, suggesting that flexibility alone cannot guarantee equality. The evidence is persuasive, although occupational differences must also be considered.

4. Add a counterargument

Rule / explanation: Stricter environmental rules are necessary.

Example / answer: Critics rightly note that stricter rules can raise short-term costs for small firms. However, phased implementation and targeted support can reduce this burden without abandoning environmental safeguards.

5. Create a transition

Rule / explanation: Paragraph 1: the reform reduced expenditure. Paragraph 2: access became more unequal.

Example / answer: Lower expenditure, however, does not by itself establish that the reform was successful.

6. Correct the conclusion

Rule / explanation: In conclusion, I talked about costs, fairness and trust.

Example / answer: In conclusion, the reform’s financial gains cannot compensate for unequal access and declining trust; its success therefore depends on a fairer model of implementation.

7. Translate

Rule / explanation: Le problème n’est pas de choisir entre innovation et régulation, mais de déterminer dans quelles conditions elles peuvent se renforcer mutuellement.

Example / answer: The issue is not choosing between innovation and regulation, but determining the conditions under which they can reinforce one another.

8. Mini concours challenge

Rule / explanation: Produce a complete argumentative blueprint with introduction, three-stage development and conclusion.

Example / answer: Model: Context: AI increasingly shapes recruitment and public services. Key question: How can automated decision-making improve efficiency without weakening accountability? Thesis: Efficiency is valuable only when decisions remain explainable, contestable and subject to human responsibility. Part 1: Why automation appears attractive—speed, consistency and scale. Part 2: Why these benefits are limited—bias, opacity and diffusion of responsibility. Part 3: Conditions of legitimate use—auditing, appeal mechanisms and clear human accountability. Conclusion: Automation can support public decision-making, but it must remain a tool within accountable institutions rather than a substitute for responsibility.
