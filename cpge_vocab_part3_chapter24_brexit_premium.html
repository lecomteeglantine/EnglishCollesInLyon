How to use this chapter

For press articles Focus on terms such as take back control, single market, customs union, hard Brexit, Northern Ireland Protocol, trade friction and post-Brexit Britain.
For oral exams Use this vocabulary to explain the tension between sovereignty and economic integration, democratic mandate and parliamentary scrutiny, national identity and territorial unity.

A. Core Brexit vocabulary and campaign language

Brexit

IPA: /ˈbreksɪt/
Pronunciation: BREK-sit
French: Brexit

Definition: The United Kingdom’s withdrawal from the European Union.

Example: Brexit reshaped British politics, trade and national identity.

European Union

IPA: /ˌjʊərəpiːən ˈjuːniən/
Pronunciation: YOO-ruh-pee-uhn YOO-nee-uhn
French: Union européenne

Definition: A political and economic union of European member states.

Example: The UK voted to leave the European Union in 2016.

EU membership

IPA: /ˌiː juː ˈmembəʃɪp/
Pronunciation: ee-yoo MEM-buh-ship
French: appartenance à l’Union européenne

Definition: The status of belonging to the European Union.

Example: EU membership gave Britain access to the single market.

withdrawal

IPA: /wɪðˈdrɔːəl/
Pronunciation: with-DRAW-uhl
French: retrait

Definition: The act of leaving an organisation, treaty or agreement.

Example: The withdrawal from the EU required complex negotiations.

withdrawal agreement

IPA: /wɪðˈdrɔːəl əˌɡriːmənt/
Pronunciation: with-DRAW-uhl uh-GREE-muhnt
French: accord de retrait

Definition: The treaty setting out the terms of the UK’s departure from the EU.

Example: The withdrawal agreement was fiercely debated in Parliament.

referendum

IPA: /ˌrefəˈrendəm/
Pronunciation: ref-uh-REN-duhm
French: référendum

Definition: A direct vote by citizens on a specific political question.

Example: The 2016 referendum asked voters whether the UK should remain in or leave the EU.

Leave campaign

IPA: /ˈliːv kæmˌpeɪn/
Pronunciation: leev kam-PAYN
French: campagne du Leave ; campagne pour la sortie

Definition: The campaign supporting the UK’s departure from the EU.

Example: The Leave campaign promised to take back control.

Remain campaign

IPA: /rɪˈmeɪn kæmˌpeɪn/
Pronunciation: ri-MAYN kam-PAYN
French: campagne du Remain ; campagne pour le maintien

Definition: The campaign supporting continued UK membership of the EU.

Example: The Remain campaign warned of economic disruption.

Leave voter

IPA: /ˈliːv ˌvəʊtə/
Pronunciation: leev VOH-tuh
French: électeur pro-Brexit

Definition: A person who voted for the UK to leave the EU.

Example: Many Leave voters saw Brexit as a way to regain sovereignty.

Remain voter

IPA: /rɪˈmeɪn ˌvəʊtə/
Pronunciation: ri-MAYN VOH-tuh
French: électeur pro-Remain

Definition: A person who voted for the UK to remain in the EU.

Example: Remain voters tended to be younger and more urban.

Brexiteer

IPA: /ˌbreksɪˈtɪə/
Pronunciation: brek-si-TEER
French: partisan du Brexit

Definition: A supporter of Brexit.

Example: Hardline Brexiteers opposed a soft Brexit.

Eurosceptic

IPA: /ˌjʊərəʊˈskeptɪk/
Pronunciation: YOO-roh-SKEP-tik
French: eurosceptique

Definition: A person who is critical of the European Union or European integration.

Example: Eurosceptic politicians had campaigned against the EU for decades.

Euroscepticism

IPA: /ˌjʊərəʊˈskeptɪsɪzəm/
Pronunciation: YOO-roh-SKEP-ti-siz-uhm
French: euroscepticisme

Definition: Criticism of the EU or opposition to European integration.

Example: Euroscepticism grew within the Conservative Party.

Europhile

IPA: /ˈjʊərəfaɪl/
Pronunciation: YOO-ruh-file
French: europhile

Definition: A person who supports the EU or European integration.

Example: Europhiles saw Brexit as a historic mistake.

sovereignty

IPA: /ˈsɒvrənti/
Pronunciation: SOV-ruhn-tee
French: souveraineté

Definition: The supreme authority of a state to make its own laws and decisions.

Example: Sovereignty was a central argument in the Brexit debate.

parliamentary sovereignty

IPA: /ˌpɑːləˈmentəri ˈsɒvrənti/
Pronunciation: par-luh-MEN-tuh-ree SOV-ruhn-tee
French: souveraineté parlementaire

Definition: The principle that the UK Parliament is the supreme law-making body.

Example: Brexit supporters claimed they wanted to restore parliamentary sovereignty.

national sovereignty

IPA: /ˌnæʃənəl ˈsɒvrənti/
Pronunciation: NASH-uh-nuhl SOV-ruhn-tee
French: souveraineté nationale

Definition: A country’s ability to govern itself without outside control.

Example: National sovereignty was presented as being limited by EU law.

take back control

IPA: /ˌteɪk bæk kənˈtrəʊl/
Pronunciation: tayk bak kuhn-TROHL
French: reprendre le contrôle

Definition: A famous Brexit slogan meaning the UK should regain control over laws, borders and money.

Example: Take back control became the defining slogan of the Leave campaign.

control of borders

IPA: /kənˌtrəʊl əv ˈbɔːdəz/
Pronunciation: kuhn-TROHL ov BOR-duhz
French: contrôle des frontières

Definition: The ability to decide who can enter and live in a country.

Example: Control of borders was a major Leave argument.

immigration control

IPA: /ˌɪmɪˈɡreɪʃən kənˌtrəʊl/
Pronunciation: im-i-GRAY-shuhn kuhn-TROHL
French: contrôle de l’immigration

Definition: Policies limiting or regulating immigration.

Example: Immigration control was central to the Brexit campaign.

free movement

IPA: /ˌfriː ˈmuːvmənt/
Pronunciation: free MOOV-muhnt
French: libre circulation

Definition: The EU principle allowing citizens to live and work in other member states.

Example: Ending free movement was a major Brexit objective.

B. Trade, single market and post-Brexit arrangements

single market

IPA: /ˌsɪŋɡəl ˈmɑːkɪt/
Pronunciation: SING-guhl MAR-kit
French: marché unique

Definition: The EU area allowing free movement of goods, services, capital and people.

Example: Leaving the single market created barriers for British businesses.

customs union

IPA: /ˈkʌstəmz ˌjuːniən/
Pronunciation: KUS-tuhmz YOO-nee-uhn
French: union douanière

Definition: An arrangement where members apply common tariffs to goods from outside the union.

Example: Leaving the customs union allowed the UK to negotiate its own trade deals.

common external tariff

IPA: /ˌkɒmən ɪkˈstɜːnəl ˈtærɪf/
Pronunciation: KOM-uhn ik-STUR-nuhl TAR-if
French: tarif extérieur commun

Definition: A shared tariff applied by customs union members to imports from outside.

Example: The EU customs union has a common external tariff.

trade barrier

IPA: /ˈtreɪd ˌbæriə/
Pronunciation: trade BARR-ee-uh
French: barrière commerciale

Definition: A rule, cost or restriction that makes trade more difficult.

Example: Brexit created new trade barriers with the EU.

tariff

IPA: /ˈtærɪf/
Pronunciation: TAR-if
French: droit de douane

Definition: A tax on imported goods.

Example: A free trade deal can reduce tariffs.

non-tariff barrier

IPA: /ˌnɒn ˈtærɪf ˌbæriə/
Pronunciation: non TAR-if BARR-ee-uh
French: barrière non tarifaire

Definition: A rule or administrative requirement that restricts trade without being a tax.

Example: Paperwork and checks became non-tariff barriers after Brexit.

red tape

IPA: /ˌred ˈteɪp/
Pronunciation: red tape
French: paperasserie ; lourdeurs administratives

Definition: Excessive paperwork or bureaucracy.

Example: Businesses complained about red tape after Brexit.

regulatory divergence

IPA: /ˌreɡjʊlətəri daɪˈvɜːdʒəns/
Pronunciation: REG-yuh-luh-tuh-ree dy-VUR-juhns
French: divergence réglementaire

Definition: The process by which UK and EU rules become different over time.

Example: Regulatory divergence may increase trade friction.

alignment

IPA: /əˈlaɪnmənt/
Pronunciation: uh-LYNE-muhnt
French: alignement

Definition: The state of keeping rules or policies similar.

Example: Some firms favour close alignment with EU standards.

level playing field

IPA: /ˌlevəl ˈpleɪɪŋ fiːld/
Pronunciation: LEV-uhl PLAY-ing field
French: conditions de concurrence équitables

Definition: Fair competition rules preventing one side from gaining unfair advantage.

Example: The EU insisted on level playing field commitments.

trade deal

IPA: /ˈtreɪd diːl/
Pronunciation: trade deel
French: accord commercial

Definition: An agreement setting rules for trade between countries or blocs.

Example: The UK signed a trade deal with the EU.

free trade agreement

IPA: /ˌfriː ˈtreɪd əˌɡriːmənt/
Pronunciation: free trade uh-GREE-muhnt
French: accord de libre-échange

Definition: An agreement reducing trade barriers between countries.

Example: The Trade and Cooperation Agreement is a free trade agreement.

Trade and Cooperation Agreement

IPA: /ˌtreɪd ənd kəʊˌɒpəˈreɪʃən əˈɡriːmənt/
Pronunciation: trade and koh-op-uh-RAY-shuhn uh-GREE-muhnt
French: Accord de commerce et de coopération

Definition: The 2020 UK-EU agreement governing post-Brexit trade and cooperation.

Example: The Trade and Cooperation Agreement avoided tariffs but created new paperwork.

TCA

IPA: /ˌtiː siː ˈeɪ/
Pronunciation: tee see ay
French: TCA ; accord de commerce et de coopération

Definition: Abbreviation for the Trade and Cooperation Agreement.

Example: The TCA governs much of the UK-EU relationship.

hard Brexit

IPA: /ˌhɑːd ˈbreksɪt/
Pronunciation: hard BREK-sit
French: Brexit dur

Definition: A form of Brexit involving leaving the single market and customs union.

Example: A hard Brexit prioritised sovereignty over economic integration.

soft Brexit

IPA: /ˌsɒft ˈbreksɪt/
Pronunciation: soft BREK-sit
French: Brexit doux

Definition: A form of Brexit keeping close economic ties with the EU.

Example: A soft Brexit would have reduced trade disruption.

no-deal Brexit

IPA: /ˌnəʊ diːl ˈbreksɪt/
Pronunciation: noh-deel BREK-sit
French: Brexit sans accord

Definition: The UK leaving the EU without a withdrawal or trade agreement.

Example: Businesses feared the disruption of a no-deal Brexit.

cliff edge

IPA: /ˈklɪf edʒ/
Pronunciation: klif ej
French: falaise ; rupture brutale

Definition: A sudden, risky break in rules or arrangements.

Example: A no-deal Brexit was described as a cliff edge.

transition period

IPA: /trænˈzɪʃən ˌpɪəriəd/
Pronunciation: tran-ZISH-uhn PEER-ee-uhd
French: période de transition

Definition: A temporary period after withdrawal during which old rules continued while new arrangements were negotiated.

Example: The transition period gave businesses time to adjust.

Article 50

IPA: /ˌɑːtɪkəl ˈfɪfti/
Pronunciation: AR-ti-kuhl FIF-tee
French: Article 50

Definition: The EU treaty article allowing a member state to leave the EU.

Example: The UK triggered Article 50 in 2017.

C. Negotiations, Article 50 and parliamentary deadlock

to trigger Article 50

IPA: /ˈtrɪɡər ˌɑːtɪkəl ˈfɪfti/
Pronunciation: TRIG-uh AR-ti-kuhl FIF-tee
French: déclencher l’article 50

Definition: To formally start the EU withdrawal process.

Example: Theresa May triggered Article 50 after the referendum.

negotiation

IPA: /nɪˌɡəʊʃiˈeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: ni-goh-shee-AY-shuhn
French: négociation

Definition: A formal discussion aimed at reaching an agreement.

Example: Brexit negotiations were long and politically difficult.

chief negotiator

IPA: /ˌtʃiːf nɪˈɡəʊʃieɪtə/
Pronunciation: cheef ni-GOH-shee-ay-tuh
French: négociateur en chef

Definition: The main person responsible for leading negotiations.

Example: The EU’s chief negotiator insisted on protecting the single market.

red line

IPA: /ˌred ˈlaɪn/
Pronunciation: red line
French: ligne rouge

Definition: A non-negotiable political demand or limit.

Example: Ending free movement was a red line for the UK government.

compromise

IPA: /ˈkɒmprəmaɪz/
Pronunciation: KOM-pruh-myze
French: compromis

Definition: An agreement in which both sides make concessions.

Example: Every Brexit deal required compromise.

concession

IPA: /kənˈseʃən/
Pronunciation: kuhn-SESH-uhn
French: concession

Definition: Something given up in a negotiation.

Example: Both sides made concessions in the final agreement.

deadlock

IPA: /ˈdedlɒk/
Pronunciation: DED-lok
French: blocage ; impasse

Definition: A situation in which agreement is impossible because neither side will move.

Example: Parliamentary deadlock delayed Brexit.

impasse

IPA: /ˈæmpɑːs/
Pronunciation: AM-pass
French: impasse

Definition: A situation where no progress can be made.

Example: The negotiations reached an impasse over Northern Ireland.

extension

IPA: /ɪkˈstenʃən/
Pronunciation: ik-STEN-shuhn
French: prolongation ; extension

Definition: Extra time added to a deadline.

Example: The UK requested an extension to the Article 50 deadline.

backstop

IPA: /ˈbækstɒp/
Pronunciation: BAK-stop
French: backstop ; filet de sécurité irlandais

Definition: A proposed safety mechanism to avoid a hard border in Ireland if no future deal was reached.

Example: The Irish backstop became the most controversial part of Theresa May’s deal.

Irish border

IPA: /ˌaɪrɪʃ ˈbɔːdə/
Pronunciation: EYE-rish BOR-duh
French: frontière irlandaise

Definition: The border between Northern Ireland and the Republic of Ireland.

Example: Brexit made the Irish border a central constitutional problem.

hard border

IPA: /ˌhɑːd ˈbɔːdə/
Pronunciation: hard BOR-duh
French: frontière dure

Definition: A border with physical checks, customs controls or infrastructure.

Example: Both sides wanted to avoid a hard border on the island of Ireland.

Good Friday Agreement

IPA: /ˌɡʊd ˈfraɪdeɪ əˈɡriːmənt/
Pronunciation: good FRY-day uh-GREE-muhnt
French: Accord du Vendredi saint

Definition: The 1998 peace agreement that helped end the Northern Ireland conflict.

Example: Brexit raised concerns about the Good Friday Agreement.

Northern Ireland Protocol

IPA: /ˌnɔːðən ˈaɪələnd ˈprəʊtəkɒl/
Pronunciation: NOR-thuhn EYE-uh-luhnd PROH-tuh-kol
French: protocole sur l’Irlande du Nord

Definition: The post-Brexit arrangement keeping Northern Ireland aligned with some EU rules to avoid a hard border.

Example: The Northern Ireland Protocol created checks in the Irish Sea.

Irish Sea border

IPA: /ˌaɪrɪʃ siː ˈbɔːdə/
Pronunciation: EYE-rish see BOR-duh
French: frontière en mer d’Irlande

Definition: A term for checks between Great Britain and Northern Ireland after Brexit.

Example: Unionists criticised the Irish Sea border.

Windsor Framework

IPA: /ˌwɪnzə ˈfreɪmwɜːk/
Pronunciation: WIN-zuh FRAME-wurk
French: cadre de Windsor

Definition: The 2023 UK-EU agreement modifying the Northern Ireland Protocol.

Example: The Windsor Framework aimed to reduce trade frictions in Northern Ireland.

unionist

IPA: /ˈjuːniənɪst/
Pronunciation: YOO-nee-uhn-ist
French: unioniste

Definition: A person in Northern Ireland who supports remaining part of the UK.

Example: Unionists opposed rules that separated Northern Ireland from Great Britain.

D. Northern Ireland, the Union and constitutional conflict

nationalist

IPA: /ˈnæʃənəlɪst/
Pronunciation: NASH-uh-nuh-list
French: nationaliste irlandais

Definition: In Northern Ireland, a person who supports Irish unity.

Example: Nationalists generally opposed Brexit.

Irish unity

IPA: /ˌaɪrɪʃ ˈjuːnəti/
Pronunciation: EYE-rish YOO-nuh-tee
French: unité irlandaise

Definition: The idea of Northern Ireland joining the Republic of Ireland.

Example: Brexit renewed debates about Irish unity.

Scottish independence

IPA: /ˌskɒtɪʃ ˌɪndɪˈpendəns/
Pronunciation: SKOT-ish in-di-PEN-duhns
French: indépendance écossaise

Definition: The idea that Scotland should become an independent country.

Example: Brexit strengthened arguments for Scottish independence.

Union

IPA: /ˈjuːniən/
Pronunciation: YOO-nee-uhn
French: Union britannique

Definition: The political union of the countries that make up the UK.

Example: Brexit put pressure on the Union.

constitutional crisis

IPA: /ˌkɒnstɪˈtjuːʃənəl ˈkraɪsɪs/
Pronunciation: kon-sti-TOO-shuh-nuhl KRY-sis
French: crise constitutionnelle

Definition: A serious conflict over how constitutional rules should operate.

Example: The prorogation dispute was described as a constitutional crisis.

prorogation

IPA: /ˌprəʊrəˈɡeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: proh-ruh-GAY-shuhn
French: prorogation du Parlement

Definition: The formal suspension of a parliamentary session.

Example: The 2019 prorogation triggered legal and political controversy.

Supreme Court ruling

IPA: /suːˌpriːm ˈkɔːt ˈruːlɪŋ/
Pronunciation: soo-PREEM court ROO-ling
French: décision de la Cour suprême

Definition: A decision made by the UK Supreme Court.

Example: The Supreme Court ruling found the prorogation unlawful.

meaningful vote

IPA: /ˌmiːnɪŋfəl ˈvəʊt/
Pronunciation: MEE-ning-fuhl voht
French: vote significatif

Definition: A parliamentary vote on the Brexit withdrawal agreement.

Example: Theresa May’s deal was rejected in several meaningful votes.

backbench rebellion

IPA: /ˌbækbentʃ rɪˈbeljən/
Pronunciation: BAK-bench ri-BEL-yuhn
French: rébellion des députés de base

Definition: A revolt by MPs against their own party leadership.

Example: Brexit caused repeated backbench rebellions.

party split

IPA: /ˈpɑːti splɪt/
Pronunciation: PAR-tee split
French: division interne du parti

Definition: A serious division within a political party.

Example: Brexit created deep party splits.

parliamentary deadlock

IPA: /ˌpɑːləˈmentəri ˈdedlɒk/
Pronunciation: par-luh-MEN-tuh-ree DED-lok
French: blocage parlementaire

Definition: A situation in which Parliament cannot agree on a way forward.

Example: Parliamentary deadlock led to political chaos.

second referendum

IPA: /ˌsekənd ˌrefəˈrendəm/
Pronunciation: SEK-uhnd ref-uh-REN-duhm
French: deuxième référendum

Definition: A proposed new vote on Brexit after the first referendum.

Example: Campaigners demanded a second referendum.

People’s Vote

IPA: /ˌpiːpəlz ˈvəʊt/
Pronunciation: PEE-puhlz voht
French: People’s Vote ; vote populaire

Definition: The campaign for a second referendum on the final Brexit deal.

Example: The People’s Vote campaign attracted large demonstrations.

Brexit fatigue

IPA: /ˈbreksɪt fəˌtiːɡ/
Pronunciation: BREK-sit fuh-TEEG
French: fatigue liée au Brexit

Definition: Public exhaustion with years of Brexit debate.

Example: Brexit fatigue helped Boris Johnson’s message resonate.

Get Brexit Done

IPA: /ˌɡet ˈbreksɪt dʌn/
Pronunciation: get BREK-sit dun
French: réaliser le Brexit ; slogan Get Brexit Done

Definition: Boris Johnson’s 2019 election slogan promising to end the Brexit process.

Example: Get Brexit Done was a simple and effective campaign message.

economic impact

IPA: /ˌiːkəˈnɒmɪk ˈɪmpækt/
Pronunciation: ee-kuh-NOM-ik IM-pakt
French: impact économique

Definition: The effect of a policy or event on the economy.

Example: The economic impact of Brexit remains contested.

trade friction

IPA: /ˈtreɪd ˌfrɪkʃən/
Pronunciation: trade FRIK-shuhn
French: friction commerciale

Definition: Extra difficulty, cost or delay in trade.

Example: New customs checks increased trade friction.

supply chain disruption

IPA: /səˈplaɪ tʃeɪn dɪsˌrʌpʃən/
Pronunciation: suh-PLY chayn dis-RUP-shuhn
French: perturbation des chaînes d’approvisionnement

Definition: Problems interrupting the flow of goods and materials.

Example: Brexit contributed to supply chain disruption.

labour shortage

IPA: /ˈleɪbə ˈʃɔːtɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: LAY-buh SHOR-tij
French: pénurie de main-d’œuvre

Definition: A situation where there are not enough workers for available jobs.

Example: Sectors such as agriculture and care reported labour shortages.

skills shortage

IPA: /ˈskɪlz ˌʃɔːtɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: skilz SHOR-tij
French: pénurie de compétences

Definition: A lack of workers with needed qualifications or expertise.

Example: Brexit worsened skills shortages in some industries.

agriculture

English nationalism

IPA: /ˌɪŋɡlɪʃ ˈnæʃənəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: ING-glish NASH-uh-nuh-liz-uhm
French: nationalisme anglais

Definition: A political identity centred on England rather than Britain as a whole.

Example: English nationalism helped drive support for Brexit.

cosmopolitanism

IPA: /ˌkɒzməˈpɒlɪtənɪzəm/
Pronunciation: koz-muh-POL-i-tuh-niz-uhm
French: cosmopolitisme

Definition: An outlook open to international cultures, mobility and cooperation.

Example: Remain voters were often associated with cosmopolitanism.

global Britain

IPA: /ˌɡləʊbəl ˈbrɪtən/
Pronunciation: GLOH-buhl BRIT-uhn
French: Global Britain ; Royaume-Uni mondial

Definition: A post-Brexit slogan presenting the UK as an outward-looking global actor.

Example: Global Britain was used to promote a post-EU foreign policy vision.

levelling up

IPA: /ˌlevəlɪŋ ˈʌp/
Pronunciation: LEV-uhl-ing up
French: nivellement par le haut ; rééquilibrage territorial

Definition: A policy slogan about reducing regional inequality across the UK.

Example: Levelling up was linked to winning former Labour seats after Brexit.

left-behind communities

IPA: /ˌleft bɪˈhaɪnd kəˈmjuːnətiz/
Pronunciation: left bi-HYND kuh-MYOO-nuh-teez
French: communautés laissées pour compte

Definition: Communities seen as neglected by globalisation, London and political elites.

Example: Brexit appealed to many left-behind communities.

metropolitan elite

IPA: /ˌmetrəˈpɒlɪtən ɪˈliːt/
Pronunciation: met-ruh-POL-i-tuhn i-LEET
French: élite métropolitaine

Definition: Educated, urban and socially liberal elites, often criticised by populists.

Example: Brexit rhetoric often attacked the metropolitan elite.

London bubble

IPA: /ˈlʌndən ˌbʌbəl/
Pronunciation: LUN-duhn BUB-uhl
French: bulle londonienne

Definition: A term criticising London-based politicians and media as disconnected from the rest of the country.

Example: Leave supporters accused the London bubble of ignoring ordinary voters.

will of the people

IPA: /ˌwɪl əv ðə ˈpiːpəl/
Pronunciation: wil ov thuh PEE-puhl
French: volonté du peuple

Definition: A political phrase claiming democratic legitimacy from popular vote.

Example: Brexiteers argued that Parliament had to respect the will of the people.

popular mandate

IPA: /ˌpɒpjələ ˈmændeɪt/
Pronunciation: POP-yuh-luh MAN-dayt
French: mandat populaire

Definition: Authority claimed from voters after an election or referendum.

Example: The referendum was presented as a popular mandate.

democratic legitimacy

IPA: /ˌdeməˈkrætɪk lɪˈdʒɪtɪməsi/
Pronunciation: dem-uh-KRAT-ik li-JIT-i-muh-see
French: légitimité démocratique

Definition: The right to make decisions because of democratic consent.

Example: The Brexit process raised questions of democratic legitimacy.

representative democracy

IPA: /ˌreprɪˈzentətɪv dɪˈmɒkrəsi/
Pronunciation: rep-ri-ZEN-tuh-tiv di-MOK-ruh-see
French: démocratie représentative

Definition: A system where elected representatives make decisions on behalf of citizens.

Example: Brexit created tension between direct and representative democracy.

direct democracy

IPA: /dəˌrekt dɪˈmɒkrəsi/
Pronunciation: di-REKT di-MOK-ruh-see
French: démocratie directe

Definition: A system where citizens vote directly on policy questions.

Example: The referendum was an example of direct democracy.

post-Brexit Britain

IPA: /ˌpəʊst ˈbreksɪt ˈbrɪtən/
Pronunciation: post BREK-sit BRIT-uhn
French: Royaume-Uni post-Brexit

Definition: The UK after leaving the EU.

Example: Post-Brexit Britain is still redefining its role in the world.

to leave the EU

IPA: /liːv ði ˌiː ˈjuː/
Pronunciation: leev thee ee-yoo
French: quitter l’Union européenne

Definition: To withdraw from the European Union.

Example: The UK voted to leave the EU.

to remain in the EU

IPA: /rɪˈmeɪn ɪn ði ˌiː ˈjuː/
Pronunciation: ri-MAYN in thee ee-yoo
French: rester dans l’Union européenne

Definition: To continue being a member of the European Union.

Example: Scotland voted to remain in the EU.

to negotiate a deal

IPA: /nɪˈɡəʊʃieɪt ə diːl/
Pronunciation: ni-GOH-shee-ayt uh deel
French: négocier un accord

Definition: To discuss terms in order to reach an agreement.

Example: The government tried to negotiate a deal before the deadline.

to reject a deal

IPA: /rɪˈdʒekt ə diːl/
Pronunciation: ri-JEKT uh deel
French: rejeter un accord

Definition: To vote against or refuse an agreement.

Example: MPs repeatedly rejected the withdrawal deal.

to ratify an agreement

IPA: /ˈrætɪfaɪ ən əˈɡriːmənt/
Pronunciation: RAT-i-fy uhn uh-GREE-muhnt
French: ratifier un accord

Definition: To formally approve an agreement so that it becomes legally valid.

Example: Parliament had to ratify the agreement.

to regain sovereignty

IPA: /rɪˈɡeɪn ˈsɒvrənti/
Pronunciation: ri-GAYN SOV-ruhn-tee
French: retrouver sa souveraineté

Definition: To recover supreme national authority.

Example: Leave supporters claimed Brexit would regain sovereignty.

to restore control of borders

IPA: /rɪˈstɔː kənˌtrəʊl əv ˈbɔːdəz/
Pronunciation: ri-STORE kuhn-TROHL ov BOR-duhz
French: rétablir le contrôle des frontières

Definition: To bring border policy back under national control.

Example: The government promised to restore control of borders.

to create trade friction

IPA: /kriˈeɪt ˈtreɪd ˌfrɪkʃən/
Pronunciation: kree-AYT trade FRIK-shuhn
French: créer des frictions commerciales

Definition: To create new costs, checks or delays in trade.

Example: Leaving the single market created trade friction.

to deepen divisions

IPA: /ˈdiːpən dɪˈvɪʒənz/
Pronunciation: DEE-puhn di-VIZH-uhnz
French: creuser les divisions

Definition: To make political or social divisions stronger.

Example: Brexit deepened divisions between generations and regions.

to reshape British politics

IPA: /riːˈʃeɪp ˌbrɪtɪʃ ˈpɒlɪtɪks/
Pronunciation: ree-SHAYP BRIT-ish POL-i-tiks
French: remodeler la politique britannique

Definition: To significantly change British political life.

Example: Brexit reshaped British politics for a generation.
