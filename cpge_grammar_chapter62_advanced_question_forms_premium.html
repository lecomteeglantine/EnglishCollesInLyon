A. Lesson — What are advanced question forms?

Question formation

Rule / explanation: English questions depend on auxiliary verbs, subject–auxiliary inversion and the distinction between subject and object questions.

Example / answer: Why has public confidence declined?

Question purpose

Rule / explanation: Advanced questions can request facts, test assumptions, express surprise, invite reflection or challenge an argument.

Example / answer: What explains the gap between official figures and public perception?

Register

Rule / explanation: The structure changes according to whether the question is direct, indirect, formal, rhetorical or conversational.

Example / answer: Could you explain why the policy was delayed?

CPGE relevance

Rule / explanation: Precise question forms are central to khôlles, interviews, article commentary, problematics and debate.

Example / answer: To what extent can economic growth remain compatible with social justice?

B. Auxiliary–subject inversion

Be

Rule / explanation: When be is the main verb or auxiliary, invert it with the subject.

Example / answer: Why is the reform controversial?

Have

Rule / explanation: When have forms a perfect tense, invert have and the subject.

Example / answer: How has the policy affected rural areas?

Modal verbs

Rule / explanation: Invert the modal and the subject.

Example / answer: Could the measure produce unintended consequences?

Do-support

Rule / explanation: Use do, does or did when no other auxiliary is present.

Example / answer: Why did Parliament reject the proposal?

Main verb form

Rule / explanation: After do-support, use the base form of the main verb.

Example / answer: Wrong: Why did the policy failed? Correct: Why did the policy fail?

C. Subject and object questions

Subject question

Rule / explanation: When the question word is the subject, do not use do-support or inversion.

Example / answer: Who approved the measure?

Object question

Rule / explanation: When the question word is the object, use normal question inversion.

Example / answer: Who did Parliament consult?

What as subject

Rule / explanation: Use a singular or plural verb according to the intended meaning.

Example / answer: What causes political distrust? / What factors cause political distrust?

Which + noun

Rule / explanation: Which can ask about either the subject or object.

Example / answer: Which policy produced the best results? / Which policy did the committee recommend?

Diagnostic test

Rule / explanation: Identify the missing element: the doer requires a subject question; the receiver requires an object question.

Example / answer: Who criticised the minister? / Who did the minister criticise?

D. Questions with prepositions

Stranded preposition

Rule / explanation: In neutral and spoken English, the preposition commonly remains at the end.

Example / answer: What are voters concerned about?

Fronted preposition

Rule / explanation: In formal English, place the preposition before whom or which.

Example / answer: To whom was the report submitted?

No fronted preposition before who

Rule / explanation: Use whom after a fronted preposition in formal grammar.

Example / answer: Formal: With whom did the minister negotiate?

What...for?

Rule / explanation: Use what...for to ask about purpose or intended use.

Example / answer: What was the measure introduced for?

Which preposition?

Rule / explanation: Choose the preposition required by the verb, adjective or noun.

Example / answer: What does the outcome depend on?

E. Negative questions

Contracted form

Rule / explanation: Use a contracted negative auxiliary before the subject in common negative questions.

Example / answer: Why hasn’t the government acted?

Uncontracted formal form

Rule / explanation: In formal style, place not after the subject.

Example / answer: Why has the government not acted?

Expectation

Rule / explanation: Negative questions often suggest that the speaker expected the opposite.

Example / answer: Isn’t the policy supposed to protect consumers?

Surprise or criticism

Rule / explanation: They can express surprise, impatience or challenge.

Example / answer: Why didn’t ministers consult local authorities?

Neutrality warning

Rule / explanation: Avoid negative questions when an unbiased answer is required.

Example / answer: More neutral: Did the government consult local authorities?

F. Questions with how

How + adjective/adverb

Rule / explanation: Use how before adjectives and adverbs to ask about degree or manner.

Example / answer: How effective has the reform been?

How much / many

Rule / explanation: Use how much with uncountable nouns and how many with countable plural nouns.

Example / answer: How much funding is required? / How many regions were affected?

How far

Rule / explanation: Use how far literally for distance or figuratively for extent.

Example / answer: How far can regulation protect consumers?

How come

Rule / explanation: How come is informal and uses statement word order.

Example / answer: How come the figures were not published?

How is it that

Rule / explanation: Use this more formal emphatic structure to express surprise or seek an explanation.

Example / answer: How is it that inequality continues to rise despite economic growth?

G. Choice and alternative questions

Or-question

Rule / explanation: Present two or more alternatives linked by or.

Example / answer: Should the government regulate the market or allow greater competition?

Whether alternatives

Rule / explanation: In indirect and formal questions, use whether...or.

Example / answer: The article asks whether regulation protects innovation or restricts it.

Open alternative

Rule / explanation: End with or not when the two broad possibilities are yes and no.

Example / answer: Can the policy remain sustainable or not?

Balanced alternatives

Rule / explanation: Keep the alternatives grammatically parallel.

Example / answer: Is the problem caused by weak funding or by poor implementation?

Intonation

Rule / explanation: Spoken alternative questions usually rise on the first option and fall on the final option.

Example / answer: Should taxes rise ↗ or should spending fall ↘?

H. Tag questions

Positive statement, negative tag

Rule / explanation: A positive statement normally takes a negative tag.

Example / answer: The policy has failed, hasn’t it?

Negative statement, positive tag

Rule / explanation: A negative statement normally takes a positive tag.

Example / answer: The government did not consult voters, did it?

Matching auxiliary

Rule / explanation: Use the same auxiliary and tense as the statement.

Example / answer: Ministers could intervene, couldn’t they?

Meaning and intonation

Rule / explanation: Falling intonation seeks agreement; rising intonation asks for genuine confirmation.

Example / answer: The report is reliable, isn’t it?

Special cases

Rule / explanation: Use aren’t I? after I am; shall we? after let’s; will you? after imperatives.

Example / answer: Let’s examine the evidence, shall we?

I. Echo questions and questions expressing surprise

Echo question

Rule / explanation: Repeat part of a statement as a question to request confirmation or express surprise.

Example / answer: The government cancelled the vote. — It did what?

Short echo

Rule / explanation: Use an auxiliary and pronoun to react to new information.

Example / answer: The minister resigned. — Did she?

Wh-echo

Rule / explanation: Keep statement word order when the question word repeats surprising information.

Example / answer: They spent how much?

Register

Rule / explanation: Echo questions are mainly conversational and rarely suitable for formal essays.

Example / answer: The report was delayed again?

Oral usefulness

Rule / explanation: They are useful in interviews and spontaneous discussion.

Example / answer: You mean the figures were never verified?

J. Questions with infinitives and gerunds

What to do

Rule / explanation: Use question word + to-infinitive when asking about an action, choice or method.

Example / answer: The government must decide what to do next.

How to

Rule / explanation: Use how + to-infinitive for procedure or method.

Example / answer: The report explains how to reduce administrative delays.

Whether to

Rule / explanation: Use whether, not if, before a to-infinitive.

Example / answer: The committee debated whether to reopen the inquiry.

Who to contact

Rule / explanation: Use who, where, when and which before infinitives when the meaning is clear.

Example / answer: Officials were uncertain who to consult.

No explicit subject

Rule / explanation: The understood subject usually comes from the main clause.

Example / answer: The minister did not know how to respond.

K. Formal and polite direct questions

Could you...?

Rule / explanation: Use could you to make a polite request for information or action.

Example / answer: Could you clarify what the author means by accountability?

Would you mind...?

Rule / explanation: Use would you mind + gerund for a highly polite request.

Example / answer: Would you mind explaining how the figures were calculated?

May I ask...?

Rule / explanation: Use may I ask to introduce a formal question.

Example / answer: May I ask why the committee rejected the proposal?

Would it be possible...?

Rule / explanation: Use this impersonal structure for formal politeness.

Example / answer: Would it be possible to provide a more recent source?

Softening

Rule / explanation: Words such as perhaps, exactly and briefly can adjust the tone.

Example / answer: Could you briefly explain why the reform failed?

L. Questions in interviews and khôlles

Clarification

Rule / explanation: Ask the speaker to define or reformulate an idea.

Example / answer: What exactly do you mean by institutional trust?

Evidence

Rule / explanation: Ask what supports a claim.

Example / answer: What evidence does the author provide for this conclusion?

Cause

Rule / explanation: Ask for an explanation of a development.

Example / answer: What factors may account for the decline in turnout?

Consequence

Rule / explanation: Explore implications and possible outcomes.

Example / answer: What might happen if the government delays reform further?

Evaluation

Rule / explanation: Invite a balanced judgement rather than a factual answer.

Example / answer: How convincing do you find the author’s argument?

M. Questions for article analysis

Content

Rule / explanation: Identify the main topic, event or claim.

Example / answer: What is the central issue addressed in the article?

Structure

Rule / explanation: Ask how the argument is organised.

Example / answer: How does the author develop the argument?

Tone

Rule / explanation: Ask about stance and rhetorical effect.

Example / answer: What tone does the journalist adopt, and to what effect?

Omissions

Rule / explanation: Identify what the text overlooks or assumes.

Example / answer: Which factors does the article fail to consider?

Context

Rule / explanation: Connect the article to wider political, social or historical developments.

Example / answer: How does this debate reflect broader tensions within British society?

N. Questions for argument and debate

Assumption

Rule / explanation: Challenge an underlying premise.

Example / answer: Does this argument assume that economic growth benefits everyone equally?

Counterexample

Rule / explanation: Test the strength of a general claim.

Example / answer: Are there countries where the opposite trend can be observed?

Feasibility

Rule / explanation: Ask whether a proposal can realistically be implemented.

Example / answer: How feasible is the proposed reform?

Trade-off

Rule / explanation: Explore competing values or costs.

Example / answer: What would society gain, and what might it lose?

Alternative

Rule / explanation: Invite another explanation or solution.

Example / answer: Could the same outcome be achieved through a different policy?

O. Problem questions and problématiques

Open tension

Rule / explanation: A strong problématique reveals a tension rather than asking for a simple factual answer.

Example / answer: Can technological progress strengthen democracy without increasing surveillance?

Extent

Rule / explanation: To what extent invites evaluation, but should not become a mechanical default.

Example / answer: To what extent can regulation reconcile innovation with consumer protection?

How far

Rule / explanation: How far is a natural alternative for evaluating limits.

Example / answer: How far can governments reduce inequality without slowing economic growth?

Whether

Rule / explanation: Whether structures frame a genuine dilemma.

Example / answer: The question is whether the reform can be both efficient and socially fair.

Beyond binary thinking

Rule / explanation: Avoid questions that can be answered by a simple yes or no.

Example / answer: Better: How can democratic institutions preserve trust in an age of political fragmentation?

P. Questions with complex noun phrases

Long subject

Rule / explanation: Keep the entire subject after the auxiliary.

Example / answer: Why have recent changes in electoral behaviour alarmed political parties?

Embedded modifier

Rule / explanation: Do not invert words inside a relative or prepositional phrase.

Example / answer: How has the policy introduced last year affected rural communities?

Which of

Rule / explanation: Use which of + determiner/pronoun to select from a known set.

Example / answer: Which of these explanations is the most convincing?

What kind of

Rule / explanation: Use what kind/type/sort of + noun for classification.

Example / answer: What kind of evidence would strengthen the argument?

Whose

Rule / explanation: Use whose before a noun to ask about possession or responsibility.

Example / answer: Whose interests does the reform primarily serve?

Q. Questions with modal nuance

Can

Rule / explanation: Can asks about possibility or ability.

Example / answer: Can the policy achieve its stated objective?

Could

Rule / explanation: Could may express tentative possibility or make the question less direct.

Example / answer: Could the decline be explained by demographic change?

May / might

Rule / explanation: May and might are uncommon in ordinary inversion questions but useful in formal speculation.

Example / answer: Might the reform produce unintended consequences?

Should

Rule / explanation: Should asks about obligation, desirability or expected action.

Example / answer: Should governments regulate artificial intelligence more strictly?

Would

Rule / explanation: Would asks about hypothetical consequence, preference or willingness.

Example / answer: Would voters support the policy if taxes increased?

R. Questions after expressions of opinion

Do you think...?

Rule / explanation: In direct questions, inversion occurs in do you think, not in the embedded clause.

Example / answer: Why do you think the policy failed?

Do you believe...?

Rule / explanation: Use normal statement order after the main question frame.

Example / answer: How far do you believe regulation can protect consumers?

Would you agree...?

Rule / explanation: Use this to invite agreement politely without assuming it.

Example / answer: Would you agree that the article underestimates regional inequality?

What makes you think...?

Rule / explanation: Ask for the basis of an opinion.

Example / answer: What makes you think the reform will succeed?

Position of wh-word

Rule / explanation: Move the question word to the beginning of the complete question.

Example / answer: What do you think the government should do?

S. Common advanced errors

Double inversion

Rule / explanation: Do not invert both the main question and the embedded clause.

Example / answer: Wrong: Why do you think did the policy fail? Correct: Why do you think the policy failed?

Subject-question do-support

Rule / explanation: Do not use do when the question word is the subject.

Example / answer: Wrong: Who did approve the bill? Correct: Who approved the bill?

Missing do-support

Rule / explanation: Use do-support with ordinary lexical verbs.

Example / answer: Wrong: Why the government delayed? Correct: Why did the government delay?

Wrong base form

Rule / explanation: After do, does or did, use the base verb.

Example / answer: Wrong: What does the policy achieves? Correct: What does the policy achieve?

Preposition error

Rule / explanation: Use the preposition required by the expression.

Example / answer: Correct: What does the result depend on?

Overly leading question

Rule / explanation: Avoid building the expected answer into a supposedly neutral question.

Example / answer: Leading: Why is the policy obviously unfair? Neutral: How fair is the policy?

T. Punctuation, capitalisation and style

Question mark

Rule / explanation: Use a question mark after a direct question.

Example / answer: Why did the reform fail?

No question mark after indirect report

Rule / explanation: Do not use a question mark after a statement containing an indirect question.

Example / answer: The article asks why the reform failed.

Colon before a question

Rule / explanation: A complete introductory clause may be followed by a colon.

Example / answer: The central question is this: can the reform succeed?

Series of questions

Rule / explanation: Avoid overloading formal prose with several consecutive rhetorical questions.

Example / answer: Convert some into statements or analytical transitions.

Concise wording

Rule / explanation: Remove unnecessary framing words.

Example / answer: Better: What evidence supports this claim? Not: What is the evidence that can be used to support this claim?

U. Exercises

1. Add the correct auxiliary

Rule / explanation: Why ___ Parliament reject the proposal?

Example / answer: Why did Parliament reject the proposal?

2. Subject or object question

Rule / explanation: Someone criticised the minister. Ask about the person.

Example / answer: Who criticised the minister?

3. Subject or object question

Rule / explanation: The minister criticised someone. Ask about the person.

Example / answer: Who did the minister criticise?

4. Correct the double inversion

Rule / explanation: Why do you think did the reform fail?

Example / answer: Why do you think the reform failed?

5. Formal preposition

Rule / explanation: Who did the minister speak to?

Example / answer: To whom did the minister speak?

6. Negative question

Rule / explanation: Express surprise that the government has not acted.

Example / answer: Why hasn’t the government acted?

7. Translate

Rule / explanation: Dans quelle mesure cette réforme peut-elle concilier efficacité économique et justice sociale ?

Example / answer: To what extent can this reform reconcile economic efficiency with social justice?

8. Mini concours challenge

Rule / explanation: Write five questions: one subject question, one object question, one question with a modal, one formal preposition question and one problématique.

Example / answer: Example: Who designed the reform? Which groups did it affect most? Could it deepen regional inequality? To whom should the government be accountable? How can democratic institutions remain effective without becoming less representative?
