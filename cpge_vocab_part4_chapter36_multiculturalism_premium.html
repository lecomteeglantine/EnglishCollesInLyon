Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse multiculturalism: diversity, integration, assimilation, pluralism, identity, minorities, discrimination, representation, segregation, culture wars, integration policies, colonial legacies and the tension between diversity and social cohesion.

How to use this chapter

For press articlesFocus on terms such as assimilation, social cohesion, hyphenated identity, institutional racism, DEI, segregation, identity politics and cultural mosaic.
For oral examsUse this vocabulary to explain how multiculturalism can enrich society, but also raise tensions around belonging, equality, shared values, segregation, discrimination and national identity.
For essaysMemorise the collocations: to integrate into society, to preserve cultural identity, to foster social cohesion, to combat discrimination, to balance diversity and unity.

A. Core multiculturalism, identity and cohesion vocabulary

multiculturalism

IPA: /ˌmʌltiˈkʌltʃərəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: MUL-tee-KUL-chuh-ruh-liz-uhm
French: multiculturalisme

Definition: The idea or policy that different cultural groups can coexist and be recognised within one society.

Example: Multiculturalism has shaped modern Britain, Canada and many immigrant societies.

multicultural society

IPA: /ˌmʌltiˈkʌltʃərəl səˈsaɪəti/
Pronunciation: MUL-tee-KUL-chuh-ruhl suh-SY-uh-tee
French: société multiculturelle

Definition: A society made up of several cultural, ethnic or religious groups.

Example: A multicultural society must balance diversity and social cohesion.

cultural diversity

IPA: /ˌkʌltʃərəl daɪˈvɜːsəti/
Pronunciation: KUL-chuh-ruhl dy-VUR-suh-tee
French: diversité culturelle

Definition: The presence of different cultures within a society or institution.

Example: Cultural diversity can enrich public life and creativity.

ethnic diversity

IPA: /ˌeθnɪk daɪˈvɜːsəti/
Pronunciation: ETH-nik dy-VUR-suh-tee
French: diversité ethnique

Definition: The presence of different ethnic groups within a population.

Example: Ethnic diversity has increased in many Western cities.

religious diversity

IPA: /rɪˌlɪdʒəs daɪˈvɜːsəti/
Pronunciation: ri-LIJ-uhs dy-VUR-suh-tee
French: diversité religieuse

Definition: The presence of different religions in one society.

Example: Religious diversity raises questions about secularism and tolerance.

pluralism

IPA: /ˈplʊərəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: PLOOR-uh-liz-uhm
French: pluralisme

Definition: The acceptance of different beliefs, identities and ways of life within a society.

Example: Pluralism is central to liberal democracy.

cultural pluralism

IPA: /ˌkʌltʃərəl ˈplʊərəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: KUL-chuh-ruhl PLOOR-uh-liz-uhm
French: pluralisme culturel

Definition: A model where different cultures coexist without being forced to assimilate.

Example: Cultural pluralism rejects the idea that everyone must adopt one dominant culture.

diversity

IPA: /daɪˈvɜːsəti/
Pronunciation: dy-VUR-suh-tee
French: diversité

Definition: The presence of difference within a group or society.

Example: Diversity can be a strength if institutions are inclusive.

inclusion

IPA: /ɪnˈkluːʒən/
Pronunciation: in-KLOO-zhuhn
French: inclusion

Definition: The process of making people feel accepted and able to participate fully.

Example: Inclusion goes beyond simply having diversity.

inclusive society

IPA: /ɪnˌkluːsɪv səˈsaɪəti/
Pronunciation: in-KLOO-siv suh-SY-uh-tee
French: société inclusive

Definition: A society where people from different backgrounds can participate equally.

Example: An inclusive society must fight discrimination and exclusion.

integration

IPA: /ˌɪntɪˈɡreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: in-ti-GRAY-shuhn
French: intégration

Definition: The process by which individuals or groups become part of society while participating in public life.

Example: Integration requires both effort from newcomers and openness from society.

assimilation

IPA: /əˌsɪmɪˈleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: uh-sim-i-LAY-shuhn
French: assimilation

Definition: The process of adopting the dominant culture, often by giving up minority cultural traits.

Example: Assimilation is often criticised for erasing minority identities.

acculturation

IPA: /əˌkʌltʃəˈreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: uh-KUL-chuh-RAY-shuhn
French: acculturation

Definition: The process of adapting to another culture through contact with it.

Example: Acculturation can change language, habits and social norms.

social cohesion

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl kəʊˈhiːʒən/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl koh-HEE-zhuhn
French: cohésion sociale

Definition: The degree of trust, solidarity and connection within society.

Example: Social cohesion can be weakened by inequality and segregation.

community cohesion

IPA: /kəˌmjuːnəti kəʊˈhiːʒən/
Pronunciation: kuh-MYOO-nuh-tee koh-HEE-zhuhn
French: cohésion communautaire

Definition: Positive relations and trust between groups in a local area.

Example: Community cohesion depends on shared spaces and mutual trust.

social fabric

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl ˈfæbrɪk/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl FAB-rik
French: tissu social

Definition: The relationships, values and institutions that hold society together.

Example: Discrimination and poverty can damage the social fabric.

belonging

IPA: /bɪˈlɒŋɪŋ/
Pronunciation: bi-LONG-ing
French: appartenance

Definition: A feeling of being accepted and part of a community.

Example: Belonging is essential for successful integration.

sense of belonging

IPA: /ˌsens əv bɪˈlɒŋɪŋ/
Pronunciation: sens ov bi-LONG-ing
French: sentiment d’appartenance

Definition: The feeling that one is accepted as part of a group or society.

Example: A weak sense of belonging can fuel alienation.

identity

IPA: /aɪˈdentəti/
Pronunciation: eye-DEN-tuh-tee
French: identité

Definition: A person’s sense of who they are, including culture, nationality, ethnicity and values.

Example: Multicultural societies often produce layered identities.

cultural identity

IPA: /ˌkʌltʃərəl aɪˈdentəti/
Pronunciation: KUL-chuh-ruhl eye-DEN-tuh-tee
French: identité culturelle

Definition: A person’s sense of belonging to a culture or tradition.

Example: Cultural identity can be maintained across generations.

ethnic identity

IPA: /ˌeθnɪk aɪˈdentəti/
Pronunciation: ETH-nik eye-DEN-tuh-tee
French: identité ethnique

Definition: A sense of belonging to an ethnic group.

Example: Ethnic identity can be a source of pride or discrimination.

national identity

IPA: /ˌnæʃənəl aɪˈdentəti/
Pronunciation: NASH-uh-nuhl eye-DEN-tuh-tee
French: identité nationale

Definition: A sense of belonging to a nation.

Example: National identity is often debated in multicultural societies.

hyphenated identity

IPA: /ˈhaɪfəneɪtɪd aɪˈdentəti/
Pronunciation: HY-fuh-nay-tid eye-DEN-tuh-tee
French: identité composée ; identité à trait d’union

Definition: An identity combining two cultural or national labels.

Example: British-Pakistani is an example of a hyphenated identity.

dual identity

IPA: /ˌdjuːəl aɪˈdentəti/
Pronunciation: DYOO-uhl eye-DEN-tuh-tee
French: double identité

Definition: A sense of belonging to two groups, cultures or nations.

Example: Dual identity can enrich rather than weaken citizenship.

diaspora

IPA: /daɪˈæspərə/
Pronunciation: dy-AS-puh-ruh
French: diaspora

Definition: A community living outside its ancestral homeland.

Example: Diaspora communities often maintain transnational links.

minority

IPA: /maɪˈnɒrəti/
Pronunciation: my-NOR-uh-tee
French: minorité

Definition: A group that is smaller or less powerful than the majority.

Example: Minorities may need protection against discrimination.

ethnic minority

IPA: /ˌeθnɪk maɪˈnɒrəti/
Pronunciation: ETH-nik my-NOR-uh-tee
French: minorité ethnique

Definition: A minority group sharing a common ethnic background.

Example: Ethnic minority communities are not homogeneous.

visible minority

IPA: /ˌvɪzəbəl maɪˈnɒrəti/
Pronunciation: VIZ-uh-buhl my-NOR-uh-tee
French: minorité visible

Definition: A person or group visibly different from the majority population, often used in Canada.

Example: The term visible minority is used in Canadian public policy.

majority group

IPA: /məˈdʒɒrəti ɡruːp/
Pronunciation: muh-JOR-uh-tee group
French: groupe majoritaire

Definition: The dominant or largest group in a society.

Example: Majority groups may see their norms as neutral.

minority group

IPA: /maɪˈnɒrəti ɡruːp/
Pronunciation: my-NOR-uh-tee group
French: groupe minoritaire

Definition: A group with less numerical or social power.

Example: Minority groups can face stereotypes and exclusion.

host society

IPA: /ˈhəʊst səˌsaɪəti/
Pronunciation: host suh-SY-uh-tee
French: société d’accueil

Definition: The society that receives migrants or minority groups.

Example: The host society also has responsibilities in integration.

mainstream society

IPA: /ˈmeɪnstriːm səˌsaɪəti/
Pronunciation: MAYN-streem suh-SY-uh-tee
French: société dominante

Definition: The dominant social and cultural life of a country.

Example: Some communities feel excluded from mainstream society.

dominant culture

IPA: /ˈdɒmɪnənt ˈkʌltʃə/
Pronunciation: DOM-i-nuhnt KUL-chuh
French: culture dominante

Definition: The culture with the most social power in a society.

Example: Assimilation pressures minorities to adopt the dominant culture.

minority culture

IPA: /maɪˌnɒrəti ˈkʌltʃə/
Pronunciation: my-NOR-uh-tee KUL-chuh
French: culture minoritaire

Definition: A culture practised by a smaller or less powerful group.

Example: Minority cultures can survive through language, religion and family traditions.

heritage

IPA: /ˈherɪtɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: HERR-i-tij
French: héritage culturel

Definition: Cultural traditions, history and values passed down through generations.

Example: Heritage can be preserved through festivals and education.

cultural heritage

IPA: /ˌkʌltʃərəl ˈherɪtɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: KUL-chuh-ruhl HERR-i-tij
French: patrimoine culturel

Definition: Traditions, language, art and history inherited from a culture.

Example: Cultural heritage is central to identity.

mother tongue

IPA: /ˌmʌðə ˈtʌŋ/
Pronunciation: MUTH-uh tung
French: langue maternelle

Definition: The first language learned at home.

Example: A mother tongue can connect children to family heritage.

heritage language

IPA: /ˈherɪtɪdʒ ˌlæŋɡwɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: HERR-i-tij LANG-gwij
French: langue d’héritage

Definition: A language spoken by a family or community but not dominant in society.

Example: Heritage language classes help maintain cultural identity.

bilingualism

IPA: /baɪˈlɪŋɡwəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: by-LING-gwuh-liz-uhm
French: bilinguisme

Definition: The ability to use two languages.

Example: Bilingualism can be an advantage in multicultural societies.

multilingualism

IPA: /ˌmʌltiˈlɪŋɡwəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: MUL-tee-LING-gwuh-liz-uhm
French: multilinguisme

Definition: The ability or presence of several languages in a society.

Example: Multilingualism reflects migration and cultural diversity.

code-switching

IPA: /ˈkəʊd ˌswɪtʃɪŋ/
Pronunciation: code SWITCH-ing
French: alternance codique ; changement de registre

Definition: Switching between languages or styles depending on context.

Example: Code-switching is common among bilingual speakers.

cultural exchange

IPA: /ˌkʌltʃərəl ɪksˈtʃeɪndʒ/
Pronunciation: KUL-chuh-ruhl iks-CHAYNJ
French: échange culturel

Definition: The sharing of ideas, customs and practices between cultures.

Example: Cultural exchange can reduce prejudice.

B. Cultural exchange, language, stereotypes and discrimination

IPA: /ˌkrɒs ˈkʌltʃərəl/
Pronunciation: kross KUL-chuh-ruhl
French: interculturel

Definition: Involving or comparing different cultures.

Example: Cross-cultural communication requires sensitivity.

intercultural dialogue

IPA: /ˌɪntəˈkʌltʃərəl ˈdaɪəlɒɡ/
Pronunciation: IN-tuh-KUL-chuh-ruhl DY-uh-log
French: dialogue interculturel

Definition: Conversation and exchange between cultures.

Example: Intercultural dialogue can strengthen social cohesion.

cultural awareness

IPA: /ˌkʌltʃərəl əˈweənəs/
Pronunciation: KUL-chuh-ruhl uh-WAIR-nuhs
French: sensibilité culturelle

Definition: Understanding and sensitivity towards different cultures.

Example: Cultural awareness is important in schools and workplaces.

cultural sensitivity

IPA: /ˌkʌltʃərəl ˌsensɪˈtɪvəti/
Pronunciation: KUL-chuh-ruhl sen-si-TIV-uh-tee
French: sensibilité culturelle

Definition: Respectful awareness of cultural differences.

Example: Cultural sensitivity helps avoid stereotypes.

IPA: /ˌkʌltʃərəl əˌprəʊpriˈeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: KUL-chuh-ruhl uh-proh-pree-AY-shuhn
French: appropriation culturelle

Definition: Using elements of another culture, often without respect, understanding or permission.

Example: Cultural appropriation is debated in fashion, music and entertainment.

cultural appreciation

IPA: /ˌkʌltʃərəl əˌpriːʃiˈeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: KUL-chuh-ruhl uh-pree-shee-AY-shuhn
French: appréciation culturelle

Definition: Respectful learning from and valuing of another culture.

Example: Cultural appreciation requires context and respect.

stereotype

IPA: /ˈsteriətaɪp/
Pronunciation: STERR-ee-uh-type
French: stéréotype

Definition: A fixed and oversimplified idea about a group.

Example: Stereotypes reduce individuals to group clichés.

prejudice

IPA: /ˈpredʒʊdɪs/
Pronunciation: PREJ-uh-dis
French: préjugé

Definition: A negative opinion formed without fair knowledge or experience.

Example: Prejudice can lead to discrimination.

bias

IPA: /ˈbaɪəs/
Pronunciation: BY-uhs
French: biais

Definition: A tendency to judge unfairly in favour of or against a group.

Example: Unconscious bias can affect hiring decisions.

unconscious bias

IPA: /ʌnˌkɒnʃəs ˈbaɪəs/
Pronunciation: un-KON-shuhs BY-uhs
French: biais inconscient

Definition: Unintentional prejudiced assumptions affecting judgement.

Example: Unconscious bias training is common but debated.

discrimination

IPA: /dɪˌskrɪmɪˈneɪʃən/
Pronunciation: dis-krim-i-NAY-shuhn
French: discrimination

Definition: Unfair treatment based on identity or background.

Example: Discrimination undermines equal citizenship.

racial discrimination

IPA: /ˌreɪʃəl dɪˌskrɪmɪˈneɪʃən/
Pronunciation: RAY-shuhl dis-krim-i-NAY-shuhn
French: discrimination raciale

Definition: Unfair treatment based on race or ethnicity.

Example: Racial discrimination can affect housing, work and policing.

ethnic discrimination

IPA: /ˌeθnɪk dɪˌskrɪmɪˈneɪʃən/
Pronunciation: ETH-nik dis-krim-i-NAY-shuhn
French: discrimination ethnique

Definition: Unfair treatment based on ethnic background.

Example: Ethnic discrimination can be direct or indirect.

xenophobia

IPA: /ˌzenəˈfəʊbiə/
Pronunciation: zen-uh-FOH-bee-uh
French: xénophobie

Definition: Fear or hostility towards foreigners or migrants.

racism

IPA: /ˈreɪsɪzəm/
Pronunciation: RAY-siz-uhm
French: racisme

Definition: Prejudice, discrimination or systems of inequality based on race.

Example: Racism can be individual, institutional or structural.

Pronunciation: in-sti-TOO-shuh-nuhl RAY-siz-uhm
French: racisme institutionnel

structural racism

Pronunciation: STRUK-chuh-ruhl RAY-siz-uhm
French: racisme structurel

Definition: Racism embedded in social, economic and political structures.

Pronunciation: si-STEM-ik RAY-siz-uhm
French: racisme systémique

Definition: Racism produced or reinforced by systems and institutions.

Example: Systemic racism shapes outcomes in health, housing and education.

racial profiling

IPA: /ˌreɪʃəl ˈprəʊfaɪlɪŋ/
Pronunciation: RAY-shuhl PROH-fy-ling
French: profilage racial

Definition: Targeting people for suspicion based on race or ethnicity.

Example: Racial profiling damages trust in the police.

hate crime

IPA: /ˈheɪt kraɪm/
Pronunciation: hate kryme
French: crime de haine

hate speech

IPA: /ˈheɪt spiːtʃ/

French: discours de haine

IPA: /ˌmaɪkrəʊəˈɡreʃən/
Pronunciation: MY-kroh-uh-GRESH-uhn
French: micro-agression

Pronunciation: TOH-kuh-niz-uhm
French: symbolisme ; diversité de façade

overrepresentation

French: surreprésentation
