Part 4 — Society | Chapter 38 — LGBTQ+ Issues
Essential vocabulary for khôlles, essays, press reviews and competitive exams.

Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse LGBTQ+ issues: sexual orientation, gender identity, visibility, discrimination, marriage equality, family rights, trans rights, schools, healthcare, hate crime, culture-war backlash, global persecution and the tension between equality, religious freedom and free speech.

How to use this chapter

For press articlesFocus on terms such as sexual orientation, gender identity, homophobia, transphobia, marriage equality, conversion therapy, gender-affirming care, Don't Say Gay law and moral panic.
For oral examsUse this vocabulary to explain how LGBTQ+ issues involve civil rights, public health, family law, education, free speech, religious freedom and political backlash.
For essaysMemorise the collocations: to protect LGBTQ+ rights, to challenge homophobia, to combat transphobia, to ban conversion therapy, to balance rights.

A. Core LGBTQ+ identities and concepts

LGBTQ+

IPA: /ˌel dʒiː biː tiː ˈkjuː plʌs/
Pronunciation: el-jee-bee-tee-KYOO-plus
French: LGBTQ+

Definition: An acronym for lesbian, gay, bisexual, transgender, queer/questioning and other sexual or gender minorities.

Example: LGBTQ+ rights have become central to contemporary culture-war debates.

sexual orientation

IPA: /ˌsekʃuəl ˌɔːriənˈteɪʃən/
Pronunciation: SEK-shoo-uhl OR-ee-en-TAY-shuhn
French: orientation sexuelle

Definition: A person’s emotional, romantic or sexual attraction to others.

Example: Sexual orientation is protected by anti-discrimination law in many countries.

gender identity

IPA: /ˌdʒendər aɪˈdentəti/
Pronunciation: JEN-duh eye-DEN-tuh-tee
French: identité de genre

Definition: A person’s internal sense of their gender.

Example: Gender identity may differ from the sex assigned at birth.

gender expression

IPA: /ˌdʒendər ɪkˈspreʃən/
Pronunciation: JEN-duh ik-SPRESH-uhn
French: expression de genre

Definition: How a person presents their gender through clothing, behaviour, voice or appearance.

Example: Gender expression does not always fit traditional norms.

sex assigned at birth

IPA: /seks əˌsaɪnd ət ˈbɜːθ/
Pronunciation: seks uh-SYND at birth
French: sexe assigné à la naissance

Definition: The sex recorded for a baby at birth, usually male or female.

Example: Transgender people may not identify with the sex assigned at birth.

cisgender

IPA: /sɪsˈdʒendə/
Pronunciation: sis-JEN-duh
French: cisgenre

Definition: Having a gender identity that matches the sex assigned at birth.

Example: Cisgender people usually do not face discrimination over gender identity.

transgender

IPA: /trænzˈdʒendə/
Pronunciation: tranz-JEN-duh
French: transgenre

Definition: Having a gender identity different from the sex assigned at birth.

Example: Transgender rights have become highly politicised.

trans

IPA: /trænz/
Pronunciation: tranz
French: trans

Definition: Short form of transgender.

Example: Trans people often face barriers in healthcare, work and public life.

non-binary

IPA: /ˌnɒn ˈbaɪnəri/
Pronunciation: non BY-nuh-ree
French: non binaire

Definition: Not identifying exclusively as male or female.

Example: Non-binary identities challenge the idea that gender is only binary.

genderqueer

IPA: /ˈdʒendəkwɪə/
Pronunciation: JEN-duh-kweer
French: genderqueer

Definition: An identity outside conventional gender categories.

Example: Genderqueer people may reject fixed gender labels.

gender-fluid

IPA: /ˈdʒendə ˌfluːɪd/
Pronunciation: JEN-duh FLOO-id
French: genre fluide

Definition: Having a gender identity or expression that changes over time.

Example: A gender-fluid person may use different pronouns in different contexts.

agender

IPA: /eɪˈdʒendə/
Pronunciation: ay-JEN-duh
French: agenre

Definition: Having no gender or not identifying with any gender.

Example: Agender people may reject gender categories altogether.

intersex

IPA: /ˈɪntəseks/
Pronunciation: IN-tuh-seks
French: intersexe

Definition: Having biological sex characteristics that do not fit typical male or female categories.

Example: Intersex people have long been harmed by unnecessary medical interventions.

lesbian

IPA: /ˈlezbiən/
Pronunciation: LEZ-bee-uhn
French: lesbienne

Definition: A woman who is romantically or sexually attracted to women.

Example: Lesbian visibility has often been overshadowed in public debate.

gay

IPA: /ɡeɪ/
Pronunciation: gay
French: gay ; homosexuel

Definition: A person attracted to people of the same gender; often used for men.

Example: Gay rights campaigns changed marriage and adoption laws.

bisexual

IPA: /baɪˈsekʃuəl/
Pronunciation: by-SEK-shoo-uhl
French: bisexuel

Definition: Attracted to more than one gender.

Example: Bisexual people can face misunderstanding from both straight and gay communities.

pansexual

IPA: /pænˈsekʃuəl/
Pronunciation: pan-SEK-shoo-uhl
French: pansexuel

Definition: Attracted to people regardless of gender.

Example: Pansexuality emphasises attraction beyond gender categories.

asexual

IPA: /eɪˈsekʃuəl/
Pronunciation: ay-SEK-shoo-uhl
French: asexuel

Definition: Experiencing little or no sexual attraction.

Example: Asexual people may still have romantic relationships.

aromantic

IPA: /ˌeɪrəʊˈmæntɪk/
Pronunciation: ay-roh-MAN-tik
French: aromantique

Definition: Experiencing little or no romantic attraction.

Example: Aromantic identity challenges assumptions about romance.

queer

IPA: /kwɪə/
Pronunciation: kweer
French: queer

Definition: An umbrella term for non-heterosexual or non-cisgender identities; formerly an insult, now reclaimed by many.

Example: Queer can be empowering, but some people still find the term painful.

questioning

IPA: /ˈkwestʃənɪŋ/
Pronunciation: KWES-chuh-ning
French: en questionnement

Definition: Exploring or unsure about one’s sexual orientation or gender identity.

Example: Questioning young people need safe spaces and support.

straight

IPA: /streɪt/
Pronunciation: strayt
French: hétérosexuel

Definition: Informal term for heterosexual.

Example: Straight people are usually represented as the social norm.

heterosexual

IPA: /ˌhetərəˈsekʃuəl/
Pronunciation: het-uh-roh-SEK-shoo-uhl
French: hétérosexuel

Definition: Attracted to people of the opposite sex or gender.

Example: Heterosexuality is often treated as the default.

homosexual

IPA: /ˌhəʊməˈsekʃuəl/
Pronunciation: hoh-muh-SEK-shoo-uhl
French: homosexuel

Definition: Attracted to people of the same sex or gender; now often considered formal or outdated in everyday use.

Example: The word homosexual can sound clinical in ordinary conversation.

homophobia

IPA: /ˌhəʊməˈfəʊbiə/
Pronunciation: hoh-muh-FOH-bee-uh
French: homophobie

Definition: Hostility, prejudice or discrimination against gay people or homosexuality.

Example: Homophobia can appear in schools, sport, media and politics.

biphobia

IPA: /baɪˈfəʊbiə/
Pronunciation: by-FOH-bee-uh
French: biphobie

Definition: Hostility, prejudice or discrimination against bisexual people.

Example: Biphobia often involves denying that bisexuality is real.

transphobia

IPA: /trænzˈfəʊbiə/
Pronunciation: tranz-FOH-bee-uh
French: transphobie

Definition: Hostility, prejudice or discrimination against transgender people.

Example: Transphobia has intensified in many online debates.

queerphobia

IPA: /ˈkwɪəfəʊbiə/
Pronunciation: KWEER-foh-bee-uh
French: queerphobie

Definition: Hostility towards queer people or identities.

Example: Queerphobia can target people who do not conform to gender norms.

heteronormativity

IPA: /ˌhetərəʊˌnɔːməˈtɪvəti/
Pronunciation: het-uh-roh-nor-muh-TIV-uh-tee
French: hétéronormativité

Definition: The assumption that heterosexuality is normal, natural or default.

Example: Heteronormativity shapes education, advertising and family policy.

cisnormativity

IPA: /sɪsˌnɔːməˈtɪvəti/
Pronunciation: sis-nor-muh-TIV-uh-tee
French: cisnormativité

Definition: The assumption that everyone is or should be cisgender.

Example: Cisnormativity makes trans and non-binary people invisible.

gender binary

IPA: /ˈdʒendə ˈbaɪnəri/
Pronunciation: JEN-duh BY-nuh-ree
French: binarité de genre

Definition: The idea that there are only two opposite genders, male and female.

Example: The gender binary is challenged by non-binary identities.

coming out

IPA: /ˌkʌmɪŋ ˈaʊt/
Pronunciation: KUM-ing owt
French: coming out ; révélation de son orientation ou identité

Definition: The process of telling others about one’s sexual orientation or gender identity.

Example: Coming out can be liberating but also risky.

to come out

IPA: /kʌm ˈaʊt/
Pronunciation: kum owt
French: faire son coming out

Definition: To disclose one’s LGBTQ+ identity to others.

Example: Many people come out several times in different contexts.

outing

IPA: /ˈaʊtɪŋ/
Pronunciation: OWT-ing
French: outing ; révélation forcée

Definition: Revealing someone’s LGBTQ+ identity without their consent.

Example: Outing someone can put them in danger.

closeted

IPA: /ˈklɒzɪtɪd/
Pronunciation: KLOZ-it-id
French: dans le placard ; non déclaré

Definition: Not openly disclosing one’s LGBTQ+ identity.

Example: Closeted workers may fear losing their jobs.

visibility

IPA: /ˌvɪzəˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: viz-uh-BIL-uh-tee
French: visibilité

Definition: Public presence and recognition of a group or identity.

Example: LGBTQ+ visibility can reduce isolation.

representation

IPA: /ˌreprɪzenˈteɪʃən/
Pronunciation: rep-ri-zen-TAY-shuhn
French: représentation

Definition: The presence or portrayal of a group in media, politics or institutions.

Example: Representation matters for LGBTQ+ young people.

role model

IPA: /ˈrəʊl ˌmɒdəl/
Pronunciation: rohl MOD-uhl
French: modèle ; exemple à suivre

Definition: A person whose life or success inspires others.

Example: LGBTQ+ role models can help teenagers feel less alone.

ally

IPA: /ˈælaɪ/
Pronunciation: AL-eye
French: allié

Definition: A person who supports a marginalised group they do not belong to.

Example: Allies can challenge homophobia and transphobia.

allyship

IPA: /ˈælaɪʃɪp/
Pronunciation: AL-eye-ship
French: alliance active ; soutien allié

Definition: Active support for a marginalised group.

Example: Allyship requires listening and action, not just symbols.

B. Visibility, community, rights and family equality

safe space

IPA: /ˈseɪf speɪs/
Pronunciation: sayf space
French: espace sûr

Definition: A space intended to protect people from hostility, harassment or discrimination.

Example: Safe spaces can help LGBTQ+ students speak openly.

chosen family

IPA: /ˌtʃəʊzən ˈfæməli/
Pronunciation: CHOH-zuhn FAM-uh-lee
French: famille choisie

Definition: Supportive friends or community treated as family, especially when biological family rejects someone.

Example: Chosen family has been vital for many LGBTQ+ people.

Pride

IPA: /praɪd/
Pronunciation: pryde
French: Pride ; marche des fiertés

Definition: Celebrations, protests and events affirming LGBTQ+ identities and rights.

Example: Pride began as protest and remains political.

Pride march

IPA: /ˈpraɪd mɑːtʃ/
Pronunciation: pryde march
French: marche des fiertés

Definition: A public demonstration celebrating LGBTQ+ identity and demanding rights.

Example: A Pride march can be both festive and activist.

rainbow flag

IPA: /ˈreɪnbəʊ flæɡ/
Pronunciation: RAYN-boh flag
French: drapeau arc-en-ciel

Definition: A flag symbolising LGBTQ+ pride and diversity.

Example: The rainbow flag is recognised worldwide.

Stonewall riots

IPA: /ˈstəʊnwɔːl ˌraɪəts/
Pronunciation: STOHN-wall RY-uhts
French: émeutes de Stonewall

Definition: 1969 protests in New York often seen as a turning point in LGBTQ+ activism.

Example: The Stonewall riots helped launch modern LGBTQ+ rights movements.

LGBTQ+ rights

IPA: /ˌel dʒiː biː tiː ˈkjuː plʌs raɪts/
Pronunciation: el-jee-bee-tee-KYOO-plus rites
French: droits LGBTQ+

Definition: Rights protecting LGBTQ+ people from discrimination and unequal treatment.

Example: LGBTQ+ rights include marriage, adoption, safety and healthcare.

gay rights

IPA: /ˈɡeɪ raɪts/
Pronunciation: gay rites
French: droits des personnes gays

Definition: Rights protecting gay people from discrimination and inequality.

Example: Gay rights campaigns transformed many Western legal systems.

trans rights

IPA: /ˈtrænz raɪts/
Pronunciation: tranz rites
French: droits des personnes trans

Definition: Rights protecting transgender people from discrimination and exclusion.

Example: Trans rights are one of the most polarising current debates.

equal rights

IPA: /ˌiːkwəl ˈraɪts/
Pronunciation: EE-kwuhl rites
French: égalité des droits

Definition: The same legal rights and protections for all people.

Example: Equal rights do not automatically guarantee equal lived experience.

civil rights

IPA: /ˌsɪvəl ˈraɪts/
Pronunciation: SIV-uhl rites
French: droits civiques

Definition: Rights protecting equal treatment and participation in society.

Example: LGBTQ+ activism is often framed as a civil rights struggle.

human rights

IPA: /ˌhjuːmən ˈraɪts/
Pronunciation: HYOO-muhn rites
French: droits humains

Definition: Basic rights belonging to all people.

Example: Many activists frame LGBTQ+ rights as human rights.

anti-discrimination law

IPA: /ˌænti dɪˌskrɪmɪˈneɪʃən lɔː/
Pronunciation: AN-tee dis-krim-i-NAY-shuhn law
French: loi anti-discrimination

Definition: Law protecting people from unfair treatment based on identity.

Example: Anti-discrimination law can protect LGBTQ+ workers and students.

protected characteristic

IPA: /prəˌtektɪd ˌkærəktəˈrɪstɪk/
Pronunciation: pruh-TEK-tid KARR-uhk-tuh-RIS-tik
French: caractéristique protégée

Definition: A legally protected identity category, such as sex, race or sexual orientation.

Example: Sexual orientation is a protected characteristic in UK equality law.

hate crime

IPA: /ˈheɪt kraɪm/
Pronunciation: hate kryme
French: crime de haine

Definition: A crime motivated by hostility towards identity, including sexuality or gender identity.

Example: LGBTQ+ hate crime can make communities feel unsafe.

hate speech

IPA: /ˈheɪt spiːtʃ/
Pronunciation: hate speech
French: discours de haine

Definition: Speech attacking people based on identity.

Example: Hate speech against LGBTQ+ people spreads quickly online.

conversion therapy

IPA: /kənˈvɜːʃən ˌθerəpi/
Pronunciation: kuhn-VUR-zhuhn THERR-uh-pee
French: thérapie de conversion

Definition: Practices attempting to change someone’s sexual orientation or gender identity.

Example: Conversion therapy is widely condemned as harmful.

ban on conversion therapy

IPA: /ˌbæn ɒn kənˈvɜːʃən ˌθerəpi/
Pronunciation: ban on kuhn-VUR-zhuhn THERR-uh-pee
French: interdiction des thérapies de conversion

Definition: A legal prohibition of conversion therapy.

Example: A ban on conversion therapy is debated in several countries.

marriage equality

IPA: /ˈmærɪdʒ iˌkwɒləti/
Pronunciation: MARR-ij i-KWOL-uh-tee
French: égalité du mariage

Definition: Equal marriage rights for same-sex couples.

Example: Marriage equality was a major milestone for LGBTQ+ rights.

same-sex marriage

IPA: /ˌseɪm seks ˈmærɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: same-seks MARR-ij
French: mariage homosexuel ; mariage pour tous

Definition: Marriage between two people of the same sex.

Example: Same-sex marriage remains illegal in many countries.

civil partnership

IPA: /ˌsɪvəl ˈpɑːtnəʃɪp/
Pronunciation: SIV-uhl PART-nuh-ship
French: partenariat civil

Definition: A legally recognised partnership similar to marriage.

Example: Civil partnerships were introduced before same-sex marriage in the UK.

adoption rights

IPA: /əˈdɒpʃən raɪts/
Pronunciation: uh-DOP-shuhn rites
French: droit à l’adoption

Definition: The right to adopt children, including for same-sex couples.

Example: Adoption rights are part of family equality.

family recognition

IPA: /ˈfæməli ˌrekəɡˈnɪʃən/
Pronunciation: FAM-uh-lee rek-uhg-NISH-uhn
French: reconnaissance familiale

Definition: Legal and social recognition of diverse family forms.

Example: Family recognition protects children of same-sex parents.

parental rights

IPA: /pəˌrentəl ˈraɪts/
Pronunciation: puh-REN-tuhl rites
French: droits parentaux

Definition: Legal rights and responsibilities of parents.

Example: Parental rights for LGBTQ+ families vary across legal systems.

surrogacy

IPA: /ˈsʌrəɡəsi/
Pronunciation: SUR-uh-guh-see
French: gestation pour autrui ; GPA

Definition: An arrangement where someone carries a pregnancy for another person or couple.

Example: Surrogacy raises ethical and legal questions for LGBTQ+ families.

IVF

IPA: /ˌaɪ viː ˈef/
Pronunciation: eye-vee-eff
French: FIV ; fécondation in vitro

Definition: In vitro fertilisation, a medical technique to help people conceive.

Example: IVF access can be unequal for lesbian couples.

donor conception

IPA: /ˈdəʊnə kənˌsepʃən/
Pronunciation: DOH-nuh kuhn-SEP-shuhn
French: conception avec donneur

Definition: Conception using donated sperm, eggs or embryos.

Example: Donor conception is important for many LGBTQ+ families.

legal gender recognition

IPA: /ˈliːɡəl ˈdʒendə ˌrekəɡˈnɪʃən/
Pronunciation: LEE-guhl JEN-duh rek-uhg-NISH-uhn
French: reconnaissance juridique du genre

Definition: Legal recognition of a person’s gender identity.

Example: Legal gender recognition can affect documents, privacy and dignity.

gender marker

IPA: /ˈdʒendə ˌmɑːkə/
Pronunciation: JEN-duh MAR-kuh
French: mention de genre

Definition: A gender label on official documents, such as M, F or X.

Example: Some countries allow an X gender marker on passports.

self-identification

IPA: /ˌself aɪˌdentɪfɪˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: self eye-den-ti-fi-KAY-shuhn
French: auto-identification

Definition: Legal or social recognition based on a person’s own declaration of identity.

Example: Self-identification is controversial in gender recognition debates.

transition

IPA: /trænˈzɪʃən/
Pronunciation: tran-ZISH-uhn
French: transition

Definition: The process of changing social, legal or medical aspects of gender expression or recognition.

Example: Transition may include name changes, pronouns, hormones or surgery.

social transition

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl trænˈzɪʃən/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl tran-ZISH-uhn
French: transition sociale

Definition: Changing name, pronouns, clothing or social role to match gender identity.

Example: Social transition does not necessarily involve medical treatment.

medical transition

IPA: /ˌmedɪkəl trænˈzɪʃən/
Pronunciation: MED-i-kuhl tran-ZISH-uhn
French: transition médicale

Definition: Medical steps such as hormones or surgery taken as part of transition.

Example: Medical transition should be based on informed care.

gender-affirming care

IPA: /ˈdʒendər əˌfɜːmɪŋ keə/
Pronunciation: JEN-duh uh-FUR-ming care
French: soins d’affirmation de genre

Definition: Healthcare supporting a person’s gender identity.

Example: Gender-affirming care is intensely debated for minors.

gender dysphoria

IPA: /ˈdʒendə dɪsˈfɔːriə/
Pronunciation: JEN-duh dis-FOR-ee-uh
French: dysphorie de genre

Definition: Distress caused by mismatch between gender identity and assigned sex.

Example: Gender dysphoria can affect mental health.

C. Trans rights, schools and culture-war debates

puberty blockers

IPA: /ˈpjuːbəti ˌblɒkəz/
Pronunciation: PYOO-buh-tee BLOK-uhz
French: bloqueurs de puberté

Definition: Medication delaying puberty, sometimes used in gender care for young people.

Example: Puberty blockers are at the centre of medical and political controversy.

hormone therapy

IPA: /ˈhɔːməʊn ˌθerəpi/
Pronunciation: HOR-mohn THERR-uh-pee
French: hormonothérapie

Definition: Medical treatment using hormones.

Example: Hormone therapy may be part of gender-affirming care.

pronouns

IPA: /ˈprəʊnaʊnz/
Pronunciation: PROH-nownz
French: pronoms

Definition: Words used to refer to someone, such as she, he or they.

Example: Respecting pronouns can reduce everyday exclusion.

chosen name

IPA: /ˌtʃəʊzən ˈneɪm/
Pronunciation: CHOH-zuhn name
French: prénom choisi

Definition: The name a person chooses to use, often replacing a birth name.

Example: Using a chosen name can affirm someone’s identity.

deadnaming

IPA: /ˈdedˌneɪmɪŋ/
Pronunciation: DED-nay-ming
French: deadnaming ; utilisation de l’ancien prénom

Definition: Calling a trans person by a former name they no longer use.

Example: Deadnaming can be humiliating and unsafe.

misgendering

IPA: /ˌmɪsˈdʒendərɪŋ/
Pronunciation: mis-JEN-duh-ring
French: mégenrage

Definition: Referring to someone with the wrong gender or pronouns.

Example: Repeated misgendering can be experienced as harassment.

bathroom bill

IPA: /ˈbɑːθruːm bɪl/
Pronunciation: BATH-room bil
French: loi sur l’accès aux toilettes

Definition: A law or proposal restricting which toilets transgender people may use.

Example: Bathroom bills have become a symbol of anti-trans politics.

single-sex space

IPA: /ˌsɪŋɡəl seks ˈspeɪs/
Pronunciation: SING-guhl seks space
French: espace non mixte

Definition: A space reserved for one sex, such as some shelters or changing rooms.

Example: Single-sex spaces are debated in relation to safety and inclusion.

inclusive policy

IPA: /ɪnˌkluːsɪv ˈpɒləsi/
Pronunciation: in-KLOO-siv POL-uh-see
French: politique inclusive

Definition: A policy designed to include marginalised or diverse groups.

Example: Inclusive policies can make schools safer for LGBTQ+ students.

school inclusion

IPA: /ˈskuːl ɪnˌkluːʒən/
Pronunciation: school in-KLOO-zhuhn
French: inclusion scolaire

Definition: The inclusion of diverse identities and pupils within school life.

Example: School inclusion can reduce bullying and isolation.

LGBTQ+ education

IPA: /ˌel dʒiː biː tiː ˈkjuː plʌs ˌedjʊˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: el-jee-bee-tee-KYOO-plus ej-yoo-KAY-shuhn
French: éducation aux questions LGBTQ+

Definition: Education about LGBTQ+ identities, rights and history.

Example: LGBTQ+ education is often attacked in culture-war debates.

inclusive curriculum

IPA: /ɪnˌkluːsɪv kəˈrɪkjʊləm/
Pronunciation: in-KLOO-siv kuh-RIK-yuh-luhm
French: programme inclusif

Definition: A curriculum that represents diverse groups and experiences.

Example: An inclusive curriculum can reduce stigma.

relationship education

IPA: /rɪˈleɪʃənʃɪp ˌedjʊˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: ri-LAY-shuhn-ship ej-yoo-KAY-shuhn
French: éducation aux relations

Definition: Education about healthy relationships, respect and communication.

Example: Relationship education may include same-sex relationships.

sex education

IPA: /ˈseks ˌedjʊˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: seks ej-yoo-KAY-shuhn
French: éducation sexuelle

Definition: Education about sexuality, bodies, consent, relationships and contraception.

Example: Sex education is often politically sensitive.

parental rights

IPA: /pəˌrentəl ˈraɪts/
Pronunciation: puh-REN-tuhl rites
French: droits des parents

Definition: The claim that parents should control or influence children’s education and upbringing.

Example: Parental rights rhetoric is often used against LGBTQ+ education.

book ban

IPA: /ˈbʊk bæn/
Pronunciation: book ban
French: interdiction de livres

Definition: A restriction or removal of books from schools or libraries.

Example: Book bans often target LGBTQ+ stories.

Don't Say Gay law

IPA: /ˌdəʊnt seɪ ˈɡeɪ lɔː/
Pronunciation: dohnt say gay law
French: loi dite “Don’t Say Gay”

Definition: A nickname for laws restricting discussion of sexual orientation or gender identity in schools.

Example: Don't Say Gay laws have become a major US controversy.

culture war

IPA: /ˈkʌltʃə wɔː/
Pronunciation: KUL-chuh war
French: guerre culturelle

Definition: A political conflict over values, identity and social norms.

Example: LGBTQ+ issues are a major culture-war battlefield.

moral panic

IPA: /ˌmɒrəl ˈpænɪk/
Pronunciation: MOR-uhl PAN-ik
French: panique morale

Definition: A public fear, often exaggerated, that a group threatens society.
