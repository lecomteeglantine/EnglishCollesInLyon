A2 → B1 grammar for predictions, plans, arrangements, timetables and conditional warnings.

Chapter objective

This premium grammar chapter helps CPGE students choose the right future form in English. It covers will, be going to, the Present Continuous for future arrangements, the Present Simple for timetables, and future meaning in conditionals and time clauses.

The golden rule

will = prediction / spontaneous decision
going to = intention / visible evidence
Present Continuous = fixed arrangement
Present Simple = timetable
Main useFuture forms help students talk about predictions, decisions, plans, scheduled events and likely consequences.
CPGE habitUse future forms for consequences in essays: If inequality keeps rising, social tensions will intensify.
Key trapDo not use will after if in first conditionals: If prices rise, voters will protest.

A. Lesson — What are future forms in English?

Future forms

French: formes du futur

Rule / explanation: English has several ways to talk about the future. The choice depends on meaning: prediction, decision, plan, arrangement or timetable.

Example / answer: The government will face pressure. / The minister is meeting unions tomorrow.

Will

French: will

Rule / explanation: Use will for predictions, spontaneous decisions, promises and offers.

Example / answer: The reform will probably trigger debate.

Going to

French: going to

Rule / explanation: Use be going to for intentions and predictions based on present evidence.

Example / answer: The government is going to introduce a new bill.

Present Continuous for future

French: présent continu à valeur future

Rule / explanation: Use am/is/are + V-ing for fixed arrangements.

Example / answer: The Prime Minister is meeting EU leaders next week.

Present Simple for timetables

French: présent simple à valeur future

Rule / explanation: Use the Present Simple for official schedules and timetables.

Example / answer: The debate starts at 9 a.m.

Future with modal verbs

French: futur avec modaux

Rule / explanation: Use may, might, could, should, must to express probability, advice or obligation about the future.

Example / answer: The policy may increase inequality.

Future in essays

French: futur en dissertation

Rule / explanation: Future forms help you discuss consequences, predictions and policy choices.

Example / answer: If nothing changes, public trust will continue to decline.

Avoid one single French future

French: éviter le calque du français

Rule / explanation: French often uses one future form; English requires a more precise choice.

Example / answer: The minister is meeting voters tomorrow. / The minister will probably lose support.

B. Will — prediction, decision, promise

Prediction

French: prédiction

Rule / explanation: Use will to say what you think will happen.

Example / answer: The issue will dominate the next election.

Spontaneous decision

French: décision spontanée

Rule / explanation: Use will for a decision made at the moment of speaking.

Example / answer: I will check the source before quoting the figure.

Promise

French: promesse

Rule / explanation: Use will to promise something.

Example / answer: The government says it will protect vulnerable families.

Offer

French: proposition d’aide

Rule / explanation: Use will to offer help.

Example / answer: I will summarise the article for the group.

Warning

French: avertissement

Rule / explanation: Use will to warn about consequences.

Example / answer: If wages stagnate, frustration will grow.

Threat

French: menace

Rule / explanation: Use will in political or social warnings.

Example / answer: Critics warn that the reform will deepen inequality.

Formal prediction

French: prédiction formelle

Rule / explanation: Use will with probably, certainly, likely, undoubtedly.

Example / answer: The bill will probably face strong opposition.

Negative with will

French: négation avec will

Rule / explanation: Use will not / won't + base verb.

Example / answer: The reform will not solve the housing crisis.

C. Going to — intention and evidence

Intention

French: intention

Rule / explanation: Use be going to for a plan or intention.

Example / answer: The government is going to reform the welfare system.

Present evidence

French: indices présents

Rule / explanation: Use be going to when present signs make a future event likely.

Example / answer: Prices are rising fast; many households are going to struggle.

Planned action

French: action décidée

Rule / explanation: Use it when someone has already decided to do something.

Example / answer: The opposition is going to vote against the bill.

Informal prediction

French: prédiction concrète

Rule / explanation: Going to can sound more immediate or concrete than will.

Example / answer: This scandal is going to damage the minister.

Negative form

French: négation

Rule / explanation: Use am/is/are not going to + base verb.

Example / answer: The measure is not going to satisfy everyone.

Question form

French: question

Rule / explanation: Use am/is/are + subject + going to + base verb.

Example / answer: Is the government going to raise taxes?

Going to vs will

French: going to vs will

Rule / explanation: Going to often suggests prior intention or visible evidence; will is often a prediction or spontaneous decision.

Example / answer: Look at the polls: the party is going to lose seats.

CPGE use

French: usage CPGE

Rule / explanation: Use going to carefully when a future result seems visible from current evidence.

Example / answer: The data suggest that inequality is going to remain a major issue.

D. Present Continuous and Present Simple for future

Fixed arrangement

French: arrangement fixé

Rule / explanation: Use Present Continuous for arranged events with a time/date.

Example / answer: The committee is meeting on Friday.

Official meeting

French: réunion officielle

Rule / explanation: Use it for political meetings, interviews, speeches and visits.

Example / answer: The minister is visiting Scotland next week.

Scheduled event

French: horaire officiel

Rule / explanation: Use Present Simple for timetables and official schedules.

Example / answer: The conference begins at 10 a.m.

Institutional timetable

French: calendrier institutionnel

Rule / explanation: Use Present Simple for parliamentary or exam schedules.

Example / answer: Parliament votes on the bill tomorrow.

Future arrangement question

French: question futur arrangé

Rule / explanation: Use am/is/are before the subject.

Example / answer: Is the Prime Minister speaking tonight?

Future timetable question

French: question horaire

Rule / explanation: Use do/does if the verb is not be.

Example / answer: What time does the debate start?

Arrangement vs prediction

French: arrangement vs prédiction

Rule / explanation: Arrangement = Present Continuous; prediction = will.

Example / answer: She is meeting voters tomorrow. / She will probably lose support.

Timetable vs intention

French: horaire vs intention

Rule / explanation: Timetable = Present Simple; intention = going to.

Example / answer: The exam starts at 8. / I am going to revise tonight.

E. Future forms in conditionals and time clauses

If + Present Simple + will

French: conditionnel type 1

Rule / explanation: Use Present Simple after if, then will in the result clause.

Example / answer: If rents keep rising, young people will leave the city.

When + Present Simple

French: when futur

Rule / explanation: Use Present Simple after when for future meaning.

Example / answer: When the report is published, journalists will analyse it.

As soon as

French: dès que

Rule / explanation: Use Present Simple after as soon as.

Example / answer: As soon as the results come out, the debate will intensify.

Unless

French: à moins que

Rule / explanation: Use unless + Present Simple to mean if not.

Example / answer: Unless the government acts, inequality will worsen.

Before / after

French: avant / après

Rule / explanation: Use Present Simple after before and after when referring to the future.

Example / answer: Before the bill passes, MPs will debate it.

Do not use will after if

French: pas de will après if

Rule / explanation: In most first conditionals, avoid will directly after if.

Example / answer: Wrong: If prices will rise, voters will protest. Correct: If prices rise, voters will protest.

Future consequence

French: conséquence future

Rule / explanation: Use will for likely consequences.

Example / answer: If trust collapses, turnout will fall.

Policy warning

French: avertissement politique

Rule / explanation: Very useful for essays and khôlles.

Example / answer: Unless institutions regain credibility, populism will grow.

F. Typical mistakes by French speakers

Using will for every future

French: will systématique

Rule / explanation: English has several future forms; do not translate mechanically from French.

Example / answer: Better: The minister is meeting voters tomorrow, not The minister will meet voters tomorrow.

Will after if

French: will après if

Rule / explanation: Do not use will after if in standard first conditional sentences.

Example / answer: Wrong: If the law will pass, taxes will rise. Correct: If the law passes, taxes will rise.

Confusing going to and Present Continuous

French: going to vs présent continu

Rule / explanation: Going to = intention; Present Continuous = arranged event.

Example / answer: I am going to study. / I am meeting my tutor at 4.

Forgetting be in going to

French: oubli de be

Rule / explanation: Going to needs am/is/are.

Example / answer: Wrong: The government going to act. Correct: The government is going to act.

Adding -ing after will

French: -ing après will

Rule / explanation: After will, use the base verb.

Example / answer: Wrong: It will increasing. Correct: It will increase.

Using Present Simple for personal plans

French: présent simple abusif

Rule / explanation: Use Present Continuous or going to for personal plans, not usually Present Simple.

Example / answer: Wrong: I revise tomorrow. Correct: I am revising tomorrow / I am going to revise tomorrow.

Confusing likely with will

French: likely

Rule / explanation: Likely needs be: is likely to.

Example / answer: Wrong: The reform likely fail. Correct: The reform is likely to fail.

Overusing future where English uses present

French: présent après connecteurs

Rule / explanation: After when, if, as soon as, unless, use Present Simple.

Example / answer: Correct: When the article appears, we will discuss it.

G. Exercises

Exercise 1 — Choose the right future

French: exercice

Rule / explanation: Look at the clouds. It ___ rain.

Example / answer: Answer: is going to

French: exercice

Rule / explanation: The debate ___ start at 9 a.m. tomorrow.

Example / answer: Answer: starts

Exercise 3 — Choose the right future

French: exercice

Rule / explanation: The minister ___ meet union leaders tomorrow at 3 p.m.

Example / answer: Answer: is meeting

Exercise 4 — Choose the right future

French: exercice

Rule / explanation: I think the reform ___ create controversy.

Example / answer: Answer: will

Rule / explanation: The policy will reduce inequality.

Example / answer: Answer: The policy will not reduce inequality.

Exercise 8 — Correct the mistake

Example / answer: Answer: The Prime Minister is meeting the President tomorrow at 10.

Rule / explanation: The government going to introduce a new bill.

Example / answer: Answer: The government is going to introduce a new bill.

Exercise 12 — Mini concours challenge

French: exercice

Rule / explanation: Write 4 future sentences about climate change: one prediction, one intention, one arrangement, one conditional warning.

Example / answer: Possible answer: Climate change will increase migration. The government is going to invest in adaptation. World leaders are meeting next month. If emissions do not fall, extreme weather will become more frequent.
