The golden rule

Active meaning → verb-ing
Passive meaning → past participle
Future / necessity → to + infinitive

Key safety ruleThe reduced clause must clearly modify the correct noun or share the same subject.

A. Lesson — What are reduced relative clauses?

Reduced relative clause

Rule / explanation: A reduced relative clause is a shortened form of a relative clause in which the relative pronoun and part of the verb are omitted.

Example / answer: The measures introduced last year remain controversial.

Why reduce?

Rule / explanation: Reduction makes sentences more concise, formal and information-dense.

Example / answer: Policies designed to reduce inequality often require long-term funding.

Defining use

Rule / explanation: Reduced clauses usually identify or describe a noun in a compact way.

Example / answer: Students applying for the programme must submit two references.

CPGE relevance

Rule / explanation: These structures are extremely useful in summaries, essays, reports and data commentary.

Example / answer: The figures published by the ministry reveal widening regional gaps.

B. Active reduction with -ing

Present participle

Rule / explanation: Use verb-ing when the relative clause has an active meaning.

Example / answer: People who live in rural areas → People living in rural areas.

Ongoing action

Rule / explanation: The -ing form often describes an action happening at the same time as the main situation.

Example / answer: Voters waiting outside the polling station were interviewed.

General characteristic

Rule / explanation: The -ing form can describe people or things that regularly perform an action.

Example / answer: Policies encouraging investment may also increase inequality.

State verbs

Rule / explanation: Some state verbs can be reduced, but the result must sound natural.

Example / answer: Countries belonging to the union must follow common rules.

Not only present time

Rule / explanation: The -ing form does not necessarily refer to the present; context determines time.

Example / answer: The protesters demanding reform last year received little media attention.

C. Passive reduction with past participles

Past participle

Rule / explanation: Use the past participle when the relative clause has a passive meaning.

Example / answer: The reform that was introduced in 2025 → The reform introduced in 2025.

Completed action

Rule / explanation: The participle often describes a completed action affecting the noun.

Example / answer: The report published yesterday caused controversy.

Institutional style

Rule / explanation: Past-participle reductions are common in journalism and academic writing.

Example / answer: The data collected by researchers support the hypothesis.

With by-phrase

Rule / explanation: A by-phrase may be retained when the agent matters.

Example / answer: The proposal drafted by local authorities was rejected.

Irregular participles

Rule / explanation: Use the correct past participle form.

Example / answer: The measures taken by the government remain insufficient.

D. Reduction with adjectives and prepositional phrases

Be + adjective

Rule / explanation: A relative clause with be + adjective can often be reduced to an adjective phrase.

Example / answer: Students who are eligible → Students eligible for funding.

Be + prepositional phrase

Rule / explanation: A clause with be + prepositional phrase can often be reduced.

Example / answer: The countries that are in the region → The countries in the region.

Be + noun phrase

Rule / explanation: Some appositive noun phrases replace a relative clause.

Example / answer: The prime minister, who is a former lawyer, → The prime minister, a former lawyer,

Position matters

Rule / explanation: Some adjectives sound more natural after the noun in reduced structures.

Example / answer: The options available remain limited.

Formal economy

Rule / explanation: These reductions are particularly common in notices, reports and formal prose.

Example / answer: All documents relevant to the inquiry must be disclosed.

E. Infinitive relative clauses

The first / next / last to

Rule / explanation: Use to + infinitive after ordinals and superlatives.

Example / answer: She was the first minister to oppose the bill.

Something to do

Rule / explanation: Use an infinitive to express purpose, possibility or necessity.

Example / answer: The government needs a strategy to reduce regional inequality.

Person to do it

Rule / explanation: The infinitive can identify the person expected or able to perform an action.

Example / answer: The official to contact is listed below.

Passive infinitive

Rule / explanation: Use to be + past participle when the noun receives the action.

Example / answer: The issues to be discussed include housing and taxation.

Preposition at the end

Rule / explanation: A preposition may remain at the end of an infinitive clause.

Example / answer: The government needs a problem to focus on.

F. Reduced non-defining clauses

Supplementary reduction

Rule / explanation: A non-defining relative clause can sometimes be reduced when the meaning remains clear.

Example / answer: The minister, criticised by both parties, defended the proposal.

Comma required

Rule / explanation: Reduced non-defining clauses are separated by commas.

Example / answer: The report, published after months of delay, attracted widespread attention.

Active form

Rule / explanation: Use an -ing clause for active supplementary information.

Example / answer: The minister, speaking after the vote, rejected calls to resign.

Passive form

Rule / explanation: Use a past participle for passive supplementary information.

Example / answer: The reforms, introduced without consultation, faced immediate resistance.

Avoid ambiguity

Rule / explanation: The reduced clause must clearly modify the nearest noun.

Example / answer: Clear: The policy, designed to help families, reduced childcare costs.

G. Perfect participle reductions

Having + past participle

Rule / explanation: Use having + past participle when the reduced action happened before the main action.

Example / answer: Having reviewed the evidence, the committee reopened the inquiry.

Having been + past participle

Rule / explanation: Use this form for a prior passive action.

Example / answer: Having been warned repeatedly, the government could not claim ignorance.

Clear subject

Rule / explanation: The understood subject of the participle clause must be the same as the subject of the main clause.

Example / answer: Having examined the figures, the researchers revised their conclusion.

Formal use

Rule / explanation: Perfect participles are formal and useful in analytical writing.

Example / answer: Having failed to secure a majority, the government withdrew the bill.

Do not overuse

Rule / explanation: Repeated perfect-participle openings can make prose stiff.

Example / answer: Use them selectively for clear chronological relationships.

H. Reduced clauses of time, reason and condition

When + participle

Rule / explanation: A time clause may be reduced when both clauses share the same subject.

Example / answer: When questioned, the minister refused to comment.

While + participle

Rule / explanation: Use while + -ing for simultaneous actions.

Example / answer: While discussing the reform, the article overlooks regional inequality.

If + participle

Rule / explanation: Conditional clauses may sometimes be reduced.

Example / answer: If implemented correctly, the policy could reduce waiting times.

Once + participle

Rule / explanation: Use once + past participle for a completed condition.

Example / answer: Once approved, the law will take effect immediately.

Unless + participle

Rule / explanation: Use unless + past participle for a negative condition.

Example / answer: Unless properly funded, the programme will fail.

I. Reduced clauses in article summaries

The article published in...

Rule / explanation: Use passive reduction to add source information concisely.

Example / answer: The article published in The Guardian examines the housing crisis.

The author writing in...

Rule / explanation: Use active reduction to identify the writer’s context.

Example / answer: The author writing in a national newspaper adopts a critical tone.

The evidence presented...

Rule / explanation: Use past participle to introduce analysed material.

Example / answer: The evidence presented in the article remains incomplete.

The arguments developed...

Rule / explanation: Use passive reduction for ideas constructed in a text.

Example / answer: The arguments developed in the final section are more persuasive.

Readers seeking...

Rule / explanation: Use active reduction to describe a target audience or group.

Example / answer: Readers seeking a simple solution may find the article reassuring.

J. Reduced clauses in CPGE essays

Define groups

Rule / explanation: Use reduced clauses to identify a category efficiently.

Example / answer: Countries facing demographic decline must rethink labour policy.

Compress evidence

Rule / explanation: Combine supporting detail with the main claim.

Example / answer: Data collected over twenty years reveal a steady decline in trust.

State conditions

Rule / explanation: Use reduced conditional clauses for concise policy analysis.

Example / answer: If properly regulated, artificial intelligence could improve public services.

Present chronology

Rule / explanation: Use perfect participles to show sequence.

Example / answer: Having ignored early warnings, the government faced a much deeper crisis.

Create formal style

Rule / explanation: Reduced clauses contribute to a polished academic register when used clearly.

Example / answer: Measures intended to protect consumers may also restrict competition.

K. Dangling participles

Same-subject rule

Rule / explanation: The subject understood in the participle clause must match the subject of the main clause.

Example / answer: Correct: Having read the report, the minister changed her position.

Dangling error

Rule / explanation: A dangling participle appears to modify the wrong noun.

Example / answer: Wrong: Having read the report, the policy seemed ineffective.

Repair with full clause

Rule / explanation: Add an explicit subject when the subjects differ.

Example / answer: After the minister had read the report, the policy seemed ineffective.

Introductory clause check

Rule / explanation: Ask who is performing the participle action.

Example / answer: Walking into Parliament, the protesters were stopped by police.

Formal credibility

Rule / explanation: Dangling participles are especially damaging in academic writing because they create illogical meaning.

Example / answer: Always check the implied subject before reducing.

L. When reduction is impossible

Different subjects

Rule / explanation: Do not reduce when the relative clause and main clause require different understood subjects.

Example / answer: The policy, which critics say is ineffective, cannot be reduced safely.

Modal meaning

Rule / explanation: Do not remove words if can, must, should or another modal carries essential meaning.

Example / answer: Students who must register online → not simply students registering online.

Tense contrast

Rule / explanation: Do not reduce if the tense relationship would become unclear.

Example / answer: The law that had previously protected tenants was repealed.

Complex reporting

Rule / explanation: Relative clauses containing reporting structures often need to remain full.

Example / answer: The minister who experts believe misled Parliament resigned.

Clarity first

Rule / explanation: Reduction is optional; a full clause is better when compression creates ambiguity.

Example / answer: Use the clearest structure, not automatically the shortest one.

M. Typical mistakes by French speakers

Wrong voice

Rule / explanation: Choose -ing for active meaning and past participle for passive meaning.

Example / answer: Wrong: the policy introducing last year. Correct: the policy introduced last year.

Missing be in passive infinitive

Rule / explanation: Use to be + past participle for future passive meaning.

Example / answer: Correct: the measures to be implemented.

Dangling participle

Rule / explanation: Make sure the implied subject matches the main-clause subject.

Example / answer: Wrong: Having examined the data, the conclusion was revised.

Over-reduction

Rule / explanation: Do not reduce clauses when essential modal, tense or reporting meaning disappears.

Example / answer: Keep the full clause when necessary.

Comma confusion

Rule / explanation: Use commas for reduced non-defining clauses, not for defining ones.

Example / answer: The policies introduced last year failed. / The policy, introduced last year, failed.

Participle spelling

Rule / explanation: Check irregular and spelling forms carefully.

Example / answer: take → taken / write → written / lie → lying

N. Exercises

1. Reduce the active clause

Rule / explanation: People who live in rural areas often face longer waiting times.

Example / answer: People living in rural areas often face longer waiting times.

2. Reduce the passive clause

Rule / explanation: The reform that was introduced last year remains controversial.

Example / answer: The reform introduced last year remains controversial.

3. Reduce with an adjective

Rule / explanation: Students who are eligible for funding should apply before Friday.

Example / answer: Students eligible for funding should apply before Friday.

4. Use an infinitive clause

Rule / explanation: These are the issues that must be discussed.

Example / answer: These are the issues to be discussed.

5. Correct the dangling participle

Rule / explanation: Having reviewed the evidence, the conclusion was changed.

Example / answer: Having reviewed the evidence, the committee changed its conclusion.

6. Reduce the condition

Rule / explanation: If the policy is implemented correctly, it could reduce costs.

Example / answer: If implemented correctly, the policy could reduce costs.

7. Translate

Rule / explanation: Les données recueillies par les chercheurs confirment l’hypothèse.

Example / answer: The data collected by the researchers support the hypothesis.

8. Mini concours challenge

Rule / explanation: Write a paragraph using one active reduction, one passive reduction, one infinitive relative clause, one perfect participle and one reduced condition.

Example / answer: Example: Governments facing public distrust must improve transparency. Policies introduced without consultation often fail. The first step to take is to restore dialogue. Having ignored earlier warnings, the authorities now face a deeper crisis. If properly funded, the reform could still succeed.
