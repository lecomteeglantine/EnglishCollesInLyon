Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse American culture wars: polarisation, identity politics, wokeness, cancel culture, free speech, schools, race education, LGBTQ+ rights, religion, public health mandates, corporate activism, media outrage and geographic divides. Each entry includes the English term, IPA phonetics, a practical pronunciation guide, an English definition, a French translation, and an exam-ready example sentence.

A. Core culture-war vocabulary and political divides

culture wars

IPA: /ˈkʌltʃə wɔːz/
Pronunciation: KUL-chuh warz
French: guerres culturelles

Definition: Political conflicts over values, identity, morality, education, religion and social norms.

Example: Culture wars have become central to American political polarisation.

culture-war issue

IPA: /ˈkʌltʃə wɔː ˈɪʃuː/
Pronunciation: KUL-chuh war ISH-oo
French: enjeu de guerre culturelle

Definition: A divisive issue linked to values, identity or social change.

Example: Abortion, guns and race education are major culture-war issues.

identity politics

IPA: /aɪˈdentəti ˈpɒlɪtɪks/
Pronunciation: eye-DEN-tuh-tee POL-i-tiks
French: politique identitaire

Definition: Politics organised around the interests and experiences of identity groups.

Example: Critics often accuse progressive movements of promoting identity politics.

polarisation

IPA: /ˌpəʊləraɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: poh-luh-ry-ZAY-shuhn
French: polarisation

Definition: The process by which political opinions become more extreme and divided.

Example: Culture wars have deepened political polarisation.

political polarisation

IPA: /pəˌlɪtɪkəl ˌpəʊləraɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: puh-LIT-i-kuhl poh-luh-ry-ZAY-shuhn
French: polarisation politique

Definition: Deep division between political groups or parties.

Example: Political polarisation makes compromise increasingly difficult.

partisan divide

IPA: /ˌpɑːtɪzən dɪˈvaɪd/
Pronunciation: PAR-ti-zuhn di-VYDE
French: clivage partisan

Definition: A split in opinions based on party allegiance.

Example: The partisan divide over education has widened.

ideological divide

IPA: /ˌaɪdiəˈlɒdʒɪkəl dɪˈvaɪd/
Pronunciation: eye-dee-uh-LOJ-i-kuhl di-VYDE
French: clivage idéologique

Definition: A division based on political ideas or values.

Example: The ideological divide is visible in debates about gender and race.

values divide

IPA: /ˈvæljuːz dɪˌvaɪd/
Pronunciation: VAL-yooz di-VYDE
French: fracture des valeurs

Definition: A conflict between different moral or cultural values.

Example: The values divide often overlaps with geography and religion.

moral panic

IPA: /ˌmɒrəl ˈpænɪk/
Pronunciation: MOR-uhl PAN-ik
French: panique morale

Definition: Public fear, often exaggerated, that a group or trend threatens society’s values.

Example: Some commentators describe the backlash against drag shows as a moral panic.

backlash

IPA: /ˈbæklæʃ/
Pronunciation: BAK-lash
French: réaction hostile ; retour de bâton

Definition: A strong negative reaction against social or political change.

Example: Progressive reforms often trigger conservative backlash.

conservative backlash

IPA: /kənˈsɜːvətɪv ˈbæklæʃ/
Pronunciation: kuhn-SUR-vuh-tiv BAK-lash
French: réaction conservatrice

Definition: A negative reaction by conservatives against social or cultural change.

Example: The conservative backlash focused on schools and gender identity.

progressive activism

IPA: /prəˈɡresɪv ˈæktɪvɪzəm/
Pronunciation: pruh-GRESS-iv AK-ti-viz-uhm
French: militantisme progressiste

Definition: Political activism supporting social reform, equality and inclusion.

Example: Progressive activism has pushed institutions to address racism and sexism.

social conservatism

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl kənˈsɜːvətɪzəm/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl kuhn-SUR-vuh-tiz-uhm
French: conservatisme social

Definition: A political outlook defending traditional values, family structures and moral norms.

Example: Social conservatism is influential in debates on abortion and LGBTQ+ rights.

religious right

IPA: /rɪˌlɪdʒəs ˈraɪt/
Pronunciation: ri-LIJ-uhs right
French: droite religieuse

Definition: A conservative political movement shaped by religious values, especially evangelical Christianity.

Example: The religious right has strongly influenced Republican politics.

evangelical Christianity

IPA: /ˌiːvænˈdʒelɪkəl ˌkrɪstiˈænəti/
Pronunciation: ee-van-JEL-i-kuhl kris-tee-AN-uh-tee
French: christianisme évangélique

Definition: A branch of Protestant Christianity often associated with conservative politics in the US.

Example: Evangelical Christianity plays a major role in culture-war politics.

Christian nationalism

IPA: /ˌkrɪstʃən ˈnæʃənəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: KRIS-chuhn NASH-uh-nuh-liz-uhm
French: nationalisme chrétien

Definition: The belief that a nation should be defined by Christianity and governed according to Christian values.

Example: Christian nationalism has become a controversial force in American politics.

secularism

IPA: /ˈsekjələrɪzəm/
Pronunciation: SEK-yuh-luh-riz-uhm
French: laïcité ; sécularisme

Definition: The principle of separating religion from government and public institutions.

Example: Secularism is often defended in debates over school prayer.

secular liberalism

IPA: /ˈsekjələ ˈlɪbərəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: SEK-yuh-luh LIB-uh-ruhl-iz-uhm
French: libéralisme séculier

Definition: A liberal worldview based on individual rights and limited religious influence in public policy.

Example: Secular liberalism is often criticised by the religious right.

traditional values

IPA: /trəˌdɪʃənəl ˈvæljuːz/
Pronunciation: truh-DISH-uh-nuhl VAL-yooz
French: valeurs traditionnelles

Definition: Moral or family values seen as inherited from the past.

Example: Politicians often promise to defend traditional values.

family values

IPA: /ˈfæməli ˈvæljuːz/
Pronunciation: FAM-uh-lee VAL-yooz
French: valeurs familiales

Definition: Conservative moral values centred on marriage, parenting and family life.

Example: Family values are frequently invoked in debates over education.

liberal values

IPA: /ˈlɪbərəl ˈvæljuːz/
Pronunciation: LIB-uh-ruhl VAL-yooz
French: valeurs libérales

Definition: Values emphasising individual freedom, equality and tolerance.

Example: Liberal values are often defended in debates over minority rights.

free speech

IPA: /ˌfriː ˈspiːtʃ/
Pronunciation: free speech
French: liberté d’expression

Definition: The right to express opinions without unjustified government restriction.

Example: Free speech has become a key culture-war battleground.

B. Free speech, wokeness and campus debates

political correctness

IPA: /pəˌlɪtɪkəl kəˈrektnəs/
Pronunciation: puh-LIT-i-kuhl kuh-REKT-nuhs
French: politiquement correct

Definition: Language or behaviour intended to avoid offending marginalised groups, often criticised by opponents.

Example: Critics claim political correctness restricts open debate.

woke

IPA: /wəʊk/
Pronunciation: wohk
French: woke ; conscient des injustices sociales

Definition: Originally meaning alert to social injustice; now often used politically and sometimes pejoratively.

Example: The word woke has become a powerful conservative attack label.

wokeness

IPA: /ˈwəʊknəs/
Pronunciation: WOHK-nuhs
French: wokisme ; conscience sociale progressiste

Definition: A politicised term for progressive concern with race, gender and social justice.

Example: Conservatives often denounce wokeness in schools and companies.

anti-woke

IPA: /ˌænti ˈwəʊk/
Pronunciation: AN-tee wohk
French: anti-woke

Definition: Opposed to progressive identity-based politics or social justice language.

Example: Anti-woke campaigns have become central to Republican strategy.

cancel culture

IPA: /ˈkænsəl ˌkʌltʃə/
Pronunciation: KAN-suhl KUL-chuh
French: cancel culture ; culture de l’annulation

Definition: The practice or accusation of publicly shaming or excluding people for offensive views or behaviour.

Example: Cancel culture is often presented as a threat to free speech.

call-out culture

IPA: /ˈkɔːl aʊt ˌkʌltʃə/
Pronunciation: call-out KUL-chuh
French: culture de l’interpellation publique

Definition: The practice of publicly criticising someone for harmful language or behaviour.

Example: Call-out culture can promote accountability but also public shaming.

deplatforming

IPA: /diːˈplætfɔːmɪŋ/
Pronunciation: dee-PLAT-form-ing
French: déplateformisation ; retrait de tribune

Definition: Removing someone’s ability to speak on a platform because of controversial views.

Example: Deplatforming is controversial on university campuses.

safe space

IPA: /ˈseɪf speɪs/
Pronunciation: sayf space
French: espace sûr

Definition: A place or environment intended to protect people from discrimination, harassment or trauma.

Example: Safe spaces are defended as inclusive and criticised as overprotective.

trigger warning

IPA: /ˈtrɪɡə ˌwɔːnɪŋ/
Pronunciation: TRIG-uh WAR-ning
French: avertissement de contenu sensible

Definition: A warning that content may be disturbing or traumatic.

Example: Trigger warnings are common in debates over campus culture.

microaggression

IPA: /ˌmaɪkrəʊəˈɡreʃən/
Pronunciation: MY-kroh-uh-GRESH-uhn
French: micro-agression

Definition: A subtle comment or action that may express prejudice towards a marginalised group.

Example: Microaggressions are discussed in diversity training.

hate speech

IPA: /ˈheɪt spiːtʃ/
Pronunciation: hate speech
French: discours de haine

Definition: Speech attacking people based on identity such as race, religion, gender or sexuality.

Example: The line between hate speech and free speech is fiercely debated.

offensive speech

IPA: /əˌfensɪv ˈspiːtʃ/
Pronunciation: uh-FEN-siv speech
French: propos offensants

Definition: Speech that insults, shocks or offends people.

Example: Liberal democracies often protect offensive speech.

censorship

IPA: /ˈsensəʃɪp/
Pronunciation: SEN-suh-ship
French: censure

Definition: The suppression or restriction of speech, books, media or ideas.

Example: Both sides accuse each other of censorship.

book ban

IPA: /ˈbʊk bæn/
Pronunciation: book ban
French: interdiction de livres

Definition: A restriction or removal of books from schools or libraries.

Example: Book bans have become a flashpoint in school politics.

banned book

IPA: /ˌbænd ˈbʊk/
Pronunciation: band book
French: livre interdit

Definition: A book removed or restricted because of its content.

Example: Many banned books deal with race, sexuality or gender.

curriculum

IPA: /kəˈrɪkjʊləm/
Pronunciation: kuh-RIK-yuh-luhm
French: programme scolaire

Definition: The subjects and content taught in schools.

Example: The curriculum became a battleground over American history.

C. Schools, race, history and national memory

school board

IPA: /ˈskuːl bɔːd/
Pronunciation: school board
French: conseil scolaire local

Definition: A local elected or appointed body overseeing schools.

Example: School board meetings have become politically heated.

parents’ rights

IPA: /ˈpeərənts raɪts/
Pronunciation: PAIR-uhnts rites
French: droits des parents

Definition: The claim that parents should control or influence what schools teach children.

Example: Parents’ rights became a major slogan in education debates.

parental control

IPA: /pəˈrentəl kənˌtrəʊl/
Pronunciation: puh-REN-tuhl kuhn-TROHL
French: contrôle parental

Definition: The ability of parents to influence children’s education or exposure to content.

Example: Conservatives called for greater parental control over school materials.

indoctrination

IPA: /ɪnˌdɒktrɪˈneɪʃən/
Pronunciation: in-dok-tri-NAY-shuhn
French: endoctrinement

Definition: The teaching of beliefs in a way that discourages critical thinking.

Example: Both sides accuse schools of indoctrination.

critical race theory

IPA: /ˌkrɪtɪkəl ˈreɪs ˈθɪəri/
Pronunciation: KRIT-i-kuhl rays THEE-uh-ree
French: théorie critique de la race

Definition: An academic framework examining how law and institutions reproduce racial inequality.

Example: Critical race theory became a conservative target in education politics.

CRT

IPA: /ˌsiː ɑː ˈtiː/
Pronunciation: see ar tee
French: CRT

Definition: Abbreviation for critical race theory.

Example: CRT is often used broadly in political debates.

race education

IPA: /ˈreɪs ˌedjʊˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: rays ej-yoo-KAY-shuhn
French: enseignement sur les questions raciales

Definition: Teaching about race, racism and racial inequality.

Example: Race education has become highly contested in schools.

slavery curriculum

IPA: /ˈsleɪvəri kəˈrɪkjʊləm/
Pronunciation: SLAY-vuh-ree kuh-RIK-yuh-luhm
French: programme sur l’esclavage

Definition: Teaching content about slavery and its legacy.

Example: Debates over the slavery curriculum reveal conflicts over national memory.

patriotic education

IPA: /ˌpætriˈɒtɪk ˌedjʊˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: pat-ree-OT-ik ej-yoo-KAY-shuhn
French: éducation patriotique

Definition: Teaching history in a way that emphasises national pride.

Example: Some conservatives call for patriotic education.

national history

IPA: /ˌnæʃənəl ˈhɪstəri/
Pronunciation: NASH-uh-nuhl HIS-tuh-ree
French: histoire nationale

Definition: The story a country tells about its past.

Example: National history is often contested in culture wars.

historical reckoning

IPA: /hɪˌstɒrɪkəl ˈrekənɪŋ/
Pronunciation: hi-STOR-i-kuhl REK-uhn-ing
French: examen critique du passé

Definition: A serious confrontation with past injustice.

Example: The Black Lives Matter movement encouraged a historical reckoning.

statue removal

IPA: /ˈstætʃuː rɪˌmuːvəl/
Pronunciation: STAT-choo ri-MOO-vuhl
French: retrait de statues

Definition: The removal of public statues seen as offensive or linked to injustice.

Example: Statue removal sparked debates about history and memory.

Confederate monument

IPA: /kənˈfedərət ˈmɒnjʊmənt/
Pronunciation: kuhn-FED-uh-ruht MON-yuh-muhnt
French: monument confédéré

Definition: A monument honouring the Confederacy or its leaders.

Example: Confederate monuments became symbols of contested memory.

LGBTQ+ rights

IPA: /ˌel dʒiː biː tiː ˈkjuː plʌs raɪts/
Pronunciation: el jee bee tee kyoo plus rites
French: droits LGBTQ+

Definition: Rights protecting lesbian, gay, bisexual, transgender, queer and other sexual or gender minorities.

Example: LGBTQ+ rights are at the centre of many culture-war debates.

same-sex marriage

IPA: /ˌseɪm seks ˈmærɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: same-sex MARR-ij
French: mariage homosexuel ; mariage pour tous

Definition: Marriage between two people of the same sex.

Example: Same-sex marriage became legal nationwide in 2015.

transgender rights

IPA: /trænzˌdʒendə ˈraɪts/
Pronunciation: tranz-JEN-duh rites
French: droits des personnes transgenres

Definition: Rights protecting transgender people from discrimination.

Example: Transgender rights are heavily contested in schools and sports.

D. Gender, sexuality, religion and public health conflicts

gender identity

IPA: /ˈdʒendər aɪˌdentəti/
Pronunciation: JEN-duhr eye-DEN-tuh-tee
French: identité de genre

Definition: A person’s internal sense of gender.

Example: Gender identity policies have sparked state-level legislation.

gender-affirming care

IPA: /ˌdʒendər əˈfɜːmɪŋ keə/
Pronunciation: JEN-duhr uh-FUR-ming care
French: soins d’affirmation de genre

Definition: Healthcare supporting a person’s gender identity.

Example: Several states have restricted gender-affirming care for minors.

bathroom bill

IPA: /ˈbɑːθruːm bɪl/
Pronunciation: BATH-room bil
French: loi sur l’accès aux toilettes

Definition: A law restricting which bathrooms transgender people may use.

Example: Bathroom bills became an early flashpoint in transgender rights debates.

trans athletes

IPA: /trænz ˈæθliːts/
Pronunciation: tranz ATH-leets
French: athlètes transgenres

Definition: Transgender athletes, often discussed in debates over sports participation.

Example: Trans athletes have become a major culture-war issue.

drag queen

IPA: /ˈdræɡ kwiːn/
Pronunciation: drag queen
French: drag queen

Definition: A performer, often male, who dresses in exaggerated feminine style for entertainment.

Example: Drag queen story hours have been targeted by conservative activists.

drag show

IPA: /ˈdræɡ ʃəʊ/
Pronunciation: drag show
French: spectacle drag

Definition: A performance involving drag artists.

Example: Some states tried to restrict drag shows.

Don’t Say Gay law

IPA: /ˌdəʊnt seɪ ˈɡeɪ lɔː/
Pronunciation: don't say gay law
French: loi dite Don’t Say Gay

Definition: A nickname for laws restricting classroom discussion of sexual orientation or gender identity.

Example: Critics say Don’t Say Gay laws stigmatise LGBTQ+ students.

religious freedom

IPA: /rɪˌlɪdʒəs ˈfriːdəm/
Pronunciation: ri-LIJ-uhs FREE-duhm
French: liberté religieuse

Definition: The right to practise religion without unjustified interference.

Example: Religious freedom is often invoked in conflicts over LGBTQ+ rights.

conscience clause

IPA: /ˈkɒnʃəns klɔːz/
Pronunciation: KON-shuhns klawz
French: clause de conscience

Definition: A legal provision allowing refusal to act against one’s moral or religious beliefs.

Example: Conscience clauses are debated in healthcare and wedding services.

religious exemption

IPA: /rɪˌlɪdʒəs ɪɡˈzempʃən/
Pronunciation: ri-LIJ-uhs ig-ZEMP-shuhn
French: exemption religieuse

Definition: An exemption from a law based on religious beliefs.

Example: Religious exemptions can conflict with anti-discrimination law.

anti-discrimination law

IPA: /ˌænti dɪˌskrɪmɪˈneɪʃən lɔː/
Pronunciation: AN-tee dis-krim-i-NAY-shuhn law
French: loi anti-discrimination

Definition: A law banning unfair treatment based on protected characteristics.

Example: Anti-discrimination laws protect minority groups.

abortion debate

IPA: /əˈbɔːʃən dɪˌbeɪt/
Pronunciation: uh-BOR-shuhn di-BAYT
French: débat sur l’avortement

Definition: The political and moral debate over abortion rights and restrictions.

Example: The abortion debate is one of the oldest culture-war conflicts.

pro-choice

IPA: /ˌprəʊ ˈtʃɔɪs/
Pronunciation: proh choice
French: pro-choice ; favorable au droit à l’avortement

Definition: Supporting legal access to abortion.

Example: Pro-choice activists frame the issue as bodily autonomy.

pro-life

IPA: /ˌprəʊ ˈlaɪf/
Pronunciation: proh life
French: pro-life ; anti-avortement

Definition: Opposing abortion on the grounds of protecting unborn life.

Example: Pro-life voters are often highly mobilised.

gun rights culture

IPA: /ˈɡʌn raɪts ˌkʌltʃə/
Pronunciation: gun rites KUL-chuh
French: culture pro-armes

Definition: A culture that treats gun ownership as a symbol of freedom and identity.

Example: Gun rights culture is especially strong in rural America.

Second Amendment culture

IPA: /ˌsekənd əˈmendmənt ˌkʌltʃə/
Pronunciation: SEK-uhnd uh-MEND-muhnt KUL-chuh
French: culture du Deuxième amendement

Definition: A political culture built around the right to bear arms.

Example: Second Amendment culture overlaps with distrust of government.

mask mandate

IPA: /ˈmɑːsk ˌmændeɪt/
Pronunciation: mask MAN-dayt
French: obligation de porter un masque

Definition: A rule requiring people to wear masks, especially during a pandemic.

Example: Mask mandates became culture-war symbols during Covid-19.

vaccine mandate

IPA: /ˈvæksiːn ˌmændeɪt/
Pronunciation: VAK-seen MAN-dayt
French: obligation vaccinale

Definition: A rule requiring vaccination for certain activities or jobs.

Example: Vaccine mandates triggered protests over personal freedom.

public health mandate

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk ˈhelθ ˌmændeɪt/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik helth MAN-dayt
French: mesure sanitaire obligatoire

Definition: A health rule imposed by authorities to protect the public.

Example: Public health mandates were framed by opponents as government overreach.

personal freedom

IPA: /ˌpɜːsənəl ˈfriːdəm/
Pronunciation: PUR-suh-nuhl FREE-duhm
French: liberté individuelle

Definition: The ability of individuals to make choices without government interference.

Example: Personal freedom is a recurring argument in culture wars.

government overreach

IPA: /ˈɡʌvənmənt ˌəʊvəˈriːtʃ/
Pronunciation: GUV-uhn-muhnt OH-vuh-reech
French: abus de pouvoir de l’État

Definition: Excessive or improper expansion of government power.

Example: Opponents called vaccine mandates government overreach.

climate scepticism

IPA: /ˈklaɪmət ˈskeptɪsɪzəm/
Pronunciation: KLY-muht SKEP-ti-siz-uhm
French: climatoscepticisme

Definition: Doubt or rejection of mainstream climate science or policy.

Example: Climate scepticism has become part of conservative identity politics.

ESG

IPA: /ˌiː es ˈdʒiː/
Pronunciation: ee es jee
French: ESG ; critères environnementaux, sociaux et de gouvernance

Definition: Environmental, social and governance criteria used in business and investment.

Example: ESG policies are attacked by some conservatives as woke capitalism.

woke capitalism

IPA: /ˌwəʊk ˈkæpɪtəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: wohk KAP-i-tuh-liz-uhm
French: capitalisme woke

Definition: A critical term for companies adopting progressive social or environmental positions.

Example: Woke capitalism became a target for anti-ESG campaigns.

E. Media outrage, geography and high-value verbs

corporate activism

IPA: /ˌkɔːpərət ˈæktɪvɪzəm/
Pronunciation: KOR-puh-ruht AK-ti-viz-uhm
French: activisme d’entreprise

Definition: Companies taking public positions on social or political issues.

Example: Corporate activism can attract both praise and boycotts.

brand boycott

IPA: /ˈbrænd ˌbɔɪkɒt/
Pronunciation: brand BOY-kot
French: boycott de marque

Definition: A refusal to buy from a company because of political or moral disagreement.

Example: Brand boycotts have become common in culture-war disputes.

consumer backlash

IPA: /kənˌsjuːmə ˈbæklæʃ/
Pronunciation: kuhn-SYOO-muh BAK-lash
French: réaction hostile des consommateurs

Definition: A negative reaction by customers against a company or product.

Example: The campaign triggered a consumer backlash.

media outrage

IPA: /ˈmiːdiə ˈaʊtreɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: MEE-dee-uh OWT-rayj
French: indignation médiatique

Definition: Intense anger amplified by media coverage.

Example: Media outrage can turn small incidents into national controversies.

outrage cycle

IPA: /ˈaʊtreɪdʒ ˌsaɪkəl/
Pronunciation: OWT-rayj SY-kuhl
French: cycle de l’indignation

Definition: A repeated pattern of anger, media amplification and political reaction.

Example: Social media accelerates the outrage cycle.

social media storm

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl ˈmiːdiə stɔːm/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl MEE-dee-uh storm
French: tempête sur les réseaux sociaux

Definition: A sudden wave of online criticism or controversy.

Example: A social media storm forced the company to apologise.

viral controversy

IPA: /ˈvaɪrəl ˈkɒntrəvɜːsi/
Pronunciation: VY-ruhl KON-truh-vur-see
French: controverse virale

Definition: A controversy that spreads rapidly online.

Example: The video sparked a viral controversy.

echo chamber

IPA: /ˈekəʊ ˌtʃeɪmbə/
Pronunciation: EK-oh CHAYM-buh
French: chambre d’écho

Definition: An environment where people mostly hear opinions they already agree with.

Example: Echo chambers reinforce culture-war polarisation.

filter bubble

IPA: /ˈfɪltə ˌbʌbəl/
Pronunciation: FIL-tuh BUB-uhl
French: bulle de filtres

Definition: An online environment shaped by algorithms that show users similar content.

Example: Filter bubbles can deepen ideological division.

algorithmic amplification

IPA: /ˌælɡəˈrɪðmɪk ˌæmplɪfɪˈkeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: al-guh-RITH-mik am-pli-fi-KAY-shuhn
French: amplification algorithmique

Definition: The way algorithms make some content more visible and influential.

Example: Algorithmic amplification rewards outrage and conflict.

culture-war entrepreneur

IPA: /ˈkʌltʃə wɔː ˌɒntrəprəˈnɜː/
Pronunciation: KUL-chuh war on-truh-pruh-NUR
French: entrepreneur de guerre culturelle

Definition: A political or media figure who gains attention by fuelling cultural conflict.

Example: Culture-war entrepreneurs benefit from permanent outrage.

grievance politics

IPA: /ˈɡriːvəns ˈpɒlɪtɪks/
Pronunciation: GREE-vuhns POL-i-tiks
French: politique du ressentiment

Definition: Politics based on resentment and a sense of being wronged.

Example: Grievance politics can mobilise voters who feel ignored.

resentment

IPA: /rɪˈzentmənt/
Pronunciation: ri-ZENT-muhnt
French: ressentiment

Definition: A feeling of anger or bitterness about perceived unfairness.

Example: Culture-war rhetoric often feeds resentment.

victimhood narrative

IPA: /ˈvɪktɪmhʊd ˈnærətɪv/
Pronunciation: VIK-tim-hood NAR-uh-tiv
French: récit victimaire

Definition: A story presenting a group as unfairly persecuted or silenced.

Example: Both sides sometimes use victimhood narratives.

elite contempt

IPA: /ɪˌliːt kənˈtempt/
Pronunciation: i-LEET kuhn-TEMPT
French: mépris des élites

Definition: The belief that elites look down on ordinary people.

Example: Populists often denounce elite contempt.

liberal elite

IPA: /ˈlɪbərəl ɪˈliːt/
Pronunciation: LIB-uh-ruhl i-LEET
French: élite libérale ; élite progressiste

Definition: A term used to describe educated, progressive and influential groups.

Example: The liberal elite is often blamed for cultural change.

silent majority

IPA: /ˌsaɪlənt məˈdʒɒrəti/
Pronunciation: SY-luhnt muh-JOR-uh-tee
French: majorité silencieuse

Definition: A claimed majority of ordinary people whose views are supposedly ignored.

Example: Politicians invoke the silent majority in culture-war speeches.

ordinary Americans

IPA: /ˌɔːdənəri əˈmerɪkənz/
Pronunciation: OR-duh-ner-ee uh-MER-i-kuhnz
French: Américains ordinaires

Definition: Average citizens, often contrasted with elites.

Example: Culture-war campaigns claim to defend ordinary Americans.

heartland

IPA: /ˈhɑːtlænd/
Pronunciation: HART-land
French: cœur du pays ; Amérique profonde

Definition: The central or rural parts of the US, often presented as more traditional.

Example: The heartland is often contrasted with coastal cities.

coastal elites

IPA: /ˌkəʊstəl ɪˈliːts/
Pronunciation: KOH-stuhl i-LEETS
French: élites côtières

Definition: Educated and wealthy groups living in coastal metropolitan areas, often criticised by conservatives.

Example: The phrase coastal elites is common in populist rhetoric.

red America

IPA: /ˌred əˈmerɪkə/
Pronunciation: red uh-MER-i-kuh
French: Amérique rouge ; Amérique républicaine

Definition: Conservative or Republican-leaning America.

Example: Red America is often associated with rural and religious communities.

blue America

IPA: /ˌbluː əˈmerɪkə/
Pronunciation: bloo uh-MER-i-kuh
French: Amérique bleue ; Amérique démocrate

Definition: Liberal or Democratic-leaning America.

Example: Blue America is often associated with urban and diverse communities.

urban-rural divide

IPA: /ˌɜːbən ˈrʊərəl dɪˈvaɪd/
Pronunciation: UR-buhn ROOR-uhl di-VYDE
French: fracture urbain-rural

Definition: A political and cultural gap between cities and rural areas.

Example: The urban-rural divide fuels culture-war politics.

generational divide

IPA: /ˌdʒenəˈreɪʃənəl dɪˈvaɪd/
Pronunciation: jen-uh-RAY-shuh-nuhl di-VYDE
French: fracture générationnelle

Definition: A difference in values or opinions between age groups.

Example: The generational divide is visible on LGBTQ+ rights.

gender divide

IPA: /ˈdʒendə dɪˌvaɪd/
Pronunciation: JEN-duh di-VYDE
French: fracture de genre

Definition: A difference in political attitudes between men and women.

Example: The gender divide has widened on abortion.

racial divide

IPA: /ˈreɪʃəl dɪˌvaɪd/
Pronunciation: RAY-shuhl di-VYDE
French: fracture raciale

Definition: A division in experiences or political views linked to race.

Example: The racial divide shapes debates about policing and education.

to politicise an issue

IPA: /pəˈlɪtɪsaɪz ən ˈɪʃuː/
Pronunciation: puh-LIT-i-syz uhn ISH-oo
French: politiser une question

Definition: To turn an issue into a matter of political conflict.

Example: Politicians politicised school curricula.

to weaponise an issue

IPA: /ˈwepənaɪz ən ˈɪʃuː/
Pronunciation: WEP-uhn-yz uhn ISH-oo
French: instrumentaliser une question

Definition: To use an issue as a political weapon.

Example: Campaigns weaponised parental fears.

to fuel polarisation

IPA: /ˈfjuːəl ˌpəʊləraɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: FYOO-uhl poh-luh-ry-ZAY-shuhn
French: alimenter la polarisation

Definition: To make political division stronger.

Example: Social media can fuel polarisation.

to stoke resentment

IPA: /stəʊk rɪˈzentmənt/
Pronunciation: stohk ri-ZENT-muhnt
French: attiser le ressentiment

Definition: To deliberately increase anger or bitterness.

Example: Culture-war rhetoric stokes resentment.

to mobilise the base

IPA: /ˈməʊbɪlaɪz ðə beɪs/
Pronunciation: MOH-bi-lyz thuh base
French: mobiliser la base électorale

Definition: To motivate loyal supporters to vote or act.

Example: Culture-war issues help parties mobilise the base.

to appeal to voters

IPA: /əˈpiːl tə ˈvəʊtəz/
Pronunciation: uh-PEEL tuh VOH-tuhz
French: séduire les électeurs

Definition: To attract or persuade voters.

Example: Anti-woke rhetoric appeals to some conservative voters.

to defend traditional values

IPA: /dɪˈfend trəˌdɪʃənəl ˈvæljuːz/
Pronunciation: di-FEND truh-DISH-uh-nuhl VAL-yooz
French: défendre les valeurs traditionnelles

Definition: To protect older moral, religious or family norms.

Example: Republican candidates promised to defend traditional values.

to promote inclusion

IPA: /prəˈməʊt ɪnˈkluːʒən/
Pronunciation: pruh-MOHT in-KLOO-zhuhn
French: promouvoir l’inclusion

Definition: To encourage equal participation and acceptance.

Example: Schools introduced policies to promote inclusion.

to challenge discrimination

IPA: /ˈtʃælɪndʒ dɪˌskrɪmɪˈneɪʃən/
Pronunciation: CHAL-inj dis-krim-i-NAY-shuhn
French: contester la discrimination

Definition: To oppose unfair treatment based on identity.

Example: Activists challenged discrimination against LGBTQ+ students.

to restrict classroom discussion

IPA: /rɪˈstrɪkt ˈklɑːsruːm dɪˌskʌʃən/
Pronunciation: ri-STRIKT KLASS-room di-SKUSH-uhn
French: restreindre les discussions en classe

Definition: To limit what teachers can discuss in school.

Example: New laws restrict classroom discussion of gender identity.

to ban books

IPA: /bæn bʊks/
Pronunciation: ban books
French: interdire des livres

Definition: To remove or prohibit books from schools or libraries.

Example: Campaigners tried to ban books dealing with race and sexuality.

to silence dissent

IPA: /ˈsaɪləns dɪˈsent/
Pronunciation: SY-luhns di-SENT
French: faire taire la dissidence

Definition: To prevent disagreement or criticism from being heard.

Example: Critics argue that cancel culture silences dissent.

to protect free speech

IPA: /prəˈtekt friː ˈspiːtʃ/
Pronunciation: pruh-TEKT free speech
French: protéger la liberté d’expression

Definition: To defend the right to express opinions.

Example: Universities must protect free speech while preventing harassment.

to draw a line between speech and harm

IPA: /drɔː ə laɪn bɪˈtwiːn spiːtʃ ənd hɑːm/
Pronunciation: draw uh line bi-TWEEN speech and harm
French: tracer une frontière entre parole et préjudice

Definition: To distinguish expression from damaging or dangerous conduct.

Example: The debate tries to draw a line between speech and harm.

to turn schools into battlegrounds

IPA: /tɜːn skuːlz ˈɪntuː ˈbætəlɡraʊndz/
Pronunciation: turn schoolz IN-too BAT-uhl-groundz
French: transformer les écoles en champs de bataille idéologiques

Definition: To make schools central sites of political conflict.

Example: Culture wars have turned schools into battlegrounds.

to reshape public debate

IPA: /riːˈʃeɪp ˌpʌblɪk dɪˈbeɪt/
Pronunciation: ree-SHAYP PUB-lik di-BAYT
French: remodeler le débat public

Definition: To significantly change the way issues are discussed publicly.

Example: Social media has reshaped public debate.

to deepen social divisions

IPA: /ˈdiːpən ˈsəʊʃəl dɪˈvɪʒənz/
Pronunciation: DEE-puhn SOH-shuhl di-VIZH-uhnz
French: creuser les divisions sociales

Definition: To make social conflicts stronger.

Example: Culture-war rhetoric can deepen social divisions.
