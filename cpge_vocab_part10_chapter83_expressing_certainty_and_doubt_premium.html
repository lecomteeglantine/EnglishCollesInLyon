CPGE English Vocabulary Bible

Part 10 — Concours Toolkit | Chapter 83 — Expressing Certainty and Doubt
Essential phrases for cautious, precise and convincing argumentation.

Chapter objective

This premium toolkit chapter helps CPGE students express degrees of certainty with precision. It gives you the vocabulary needed to prove, support, question, doubt, hedge and qualify claims without sounding vague. The aim is simple: make your argument confident when evidence is strong, and cautious when evidence is limited.

How to use this chapter

For essaysUse undoubtedly, there is strong evidence that, it is likely that, it remains uncertain whether, the evidence is mixed, and correlation does not imply causation.
For khôllesUse these expressions to avoid sounding either too vague or too absolute: “It seems likely that...”, “We cannot rule out...”, “The data are ambiguous...”
WarningDo not say “it is obvious” when the evidence is weak. In concours writing, the level of certainty must match the evidence.

A. Core certainty and doubt vocabulary

certainty

IPA: /ˈsɜːtənti/
Pronunciation: SUR-tuhn-tee
French: certitude

Definition: The state of being sure that something is true.

Example: Certainty is rare in political analysis.

doubt

IPA: /daʊt/
Pronunciation: dowt
French: doute

Definition: A feeling of uncertainty about something.

Example: Doubt is useful when evidence is incomplete.

uncertainty

IPA: /ʌnˈsɜːtənti/
Pronunciation: un-SUR-tuhn-tee
French: incertitude

Definition: The state of not knowing what will happen or what is true.

Example: Economic uncertainty can affect voting behaviour.

probability

IPA: /ˌprɒbəˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: prob-uh-BIL-uh-tee
French: probabilité

Definition: How likely something is to happen or be true.

Example: The probability of reform depends on public support.

IPA: /ˈlaɪklihʊd/
Pronunciation: LYK-lee-hood
French: probabilité ; vraisemblance

Definition: The chance that something will happen.

Example: The likelihood of a recession has increased.

possibility

IPA: /ˌpɒsəˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: poss-uh-BIL-uh-tee
French: possibilité

Definition: Something that may happen or may be true.

Pronunciation: rayz dowts uh-BOWT
French: soulever des doutes sur

Definition: To create uncertainty about something.

Example: The report raises doubts about the policy’s effectiveness.

to question the reliability of

IPA: /ˈkwestʃən ðə rɪˌlaɪəˈbɪləti əv/
Pronunciation: KWES-chuhn thuh ri-ly-uh-BIL-uh-tee ov
French: mettre en doute la fiabilité de

Definition: To doubt whether something can be trusted.

Example: The article questions the reliability of the figures.

to challenge the assumption that

IPA: /ˈtʃælɪndʒ ði əˈsʌmpʃən ðæt/
Pronunciation: CHAL-inj thee uh-SUMP-shuhn that
French: remettre en cause l’hypothèse selon laquelle

Definition: To question a belief taken for granted.

Example: The data challenge the assumption that growth benefits everyone.

to remain sceptical

IPA: /rɪˈmeɪn ˈskeptɪkəl/
Pronunciation: ri-MAYN SKEP-ti-kuhl
French: rester sceptique

Definition: To continue to doubt something.

Example: Many experts remain sceptical about the proposal.

sceptical

IPA: /ˈskeptɪkəl/
Pronunciation: SKEP-ti-kuhl
French: sceptique

Definition: Doubtful and not easily convinced.

Example: A sceptical approach is useful when evidence is weak.

E. Questioning claims and evidence

scepticism

IPA: /ˈskeptɪsɪzəm/
Pronunciation: SKEP-ti-siz-uhm
French: scepticisme

Definition: An attitude of doubt or questioning.

Example: Public scepticism towards institutions is growing.

doubtful

IPA: /ˈdaʊtfəl/
Pronunciation: DOWT-fuhl
French: douteux ; incertain

Definition: Uncertain or unlikely.

Example: It is doubtful that the reform will be enough.

questionable

IPA: /ˈkwestʃənəbəl/
Pronunciation: KWES-chuh-nuh-buhl
French: contestable ; douteux

Definition: Open to doubt or criticism.

Example: The evidence is questionable.

debatable

IPA: /dɪˈbeɪtəbəl/
Pronunciation: di-BAY-tuh-buhl
French: discutable

Definition: Open to discussion or disagreement.

Example: Whether the policy is fair remains debatable.

unclear

IPA: /ʌnˈklɪə/
Pronunciation: un-KLEER
French: peu clair

Definition: Not clear or certain.

Example: The causes of the backlash remain unclear.

ambiguous

IPA: /æmˈbɪɡjuəs/
Pronunciation: am-BIG-yoo-uhs
French: ambigu

Definition: Open to more than one interpretation.

Example: The message was deliberately ambiguous.

inconclusive

IPA: /ˌɪnkənˈkluːsɪv/
Pronunciation: in-kuhn-KLOO-siv
French: non concluant

Definition: Not leading to a definite conclusion.

Example: The survey results are inconclusive.

tentative

IPA: /ˈtentətɪv/
Pronunciation: TEN-tuh-tiv
French: provisoire ; prudent

Definition: Not final or certain; cautious.

Example: The conclusion must remain tentative.

provisional

IPA: /prəˈvɪʒənəl/
Pronunciation: pruh-VIZH-uh-nuhl
French: provisoire

Definition: Temporary and subject to change.

Example: These are only provisional findings.

speculative

IPA: /ˈspekjʊlətɪv/
Pronunciation: SPEK-yuh-luh-tiv
French: spéculatif

Definition: Based on guesses rather than firm evidence.

Example: The claim is interesting but speculative.

unsubstantiated

IPA: /ˌʌnsəbˈstænʃieɪtɪd/
Pronunciation: un-suhb-STAN-shee-ay-tid
French: non étayé

Definition: Not supported by evidence.

Example: The accusation remains unsubstantiated.

unsupported

IPA: /ˌʌnsəˈpɔːtɪd/
Pronunciation: un-suh-POR-tid
French: non étayé

Definition: Not backed by evidence.

Example: The claim is unsupported by data.

unproven

IPA: /ˌʌnˈpruːvən/
Pronunciation: un-PROO-vuhn
French: non prouvé

Definition: Not proved.

Example: The link between the two events remains unproven.

unverified

IPA: /ʌnˈverɪfaɪd/
Pronunciation: un-VER-i-fyd
French: non vérifié

Definition: Not checked or confirmed.

Example: The rumour is unverified.

to hedge

IPA: /hedʒ/
Pronunciation: hej
French: nuancer ; se couvrir

Definition: To avoid being too definite by using cautious language.

Example: Academic writing often hedges strong claims.

hedging

IPA: /ˈhedʒɪŋ/
Pronunciation: HEJ-ing
French: atténuation ; prudence argumentative

Definition: Using cautious language to avoid overstatement.

Example: Hedging makes an argument more credible.

cautious wording

IPA: /ˌkɔːʃəs ˈwɜːdɪŋ/
Pronunciation: KAW-shuhs WUR-ding
French: formulation prudente

Definition: Careful language that avoids overcertainty.

Example: Cautious wording is useful when evidence is limited.

strong claim

IPA: /strɒŋ kleɪm/
Pronunciation: strong klaym
French: affirmation forte

Definition: A firm statement requiring solid evidence.

Example: A strong claim needs strong evidence.

overstatement

IPA: /ˈəʊvəˌsteɪtmənt/
Pronunciation: OH-vuh-STAYT-muhnt
French: exagération

Definition: A claim that is stronger than the evidence justifies.

Example: Avoid overstatement in essays.

to overstate

IPA: /ˌəʊvəˈsteɪt/
Pronunciation: OH-vuh-stayt
French: exagérer

Definition: To express something too strongly.

Example: The article overstates the role of social media.

to understate

IPA: /ˌʌndəˈsteɪt/
Pronunciation: UN-duh-stayt
French: minimiser

Definition: To make something seem less important than it is.

Example: The government understates the scale of poverty.

to be cautious about

IPA: /bi ˈkɔːʃəs əˌbaʊt/
Pronunciation: bee KAW-shuhs uh-BOWT
French: être prudent au sujet de

Definition: To avoid making strong claims too quickly.

Example: We should be cautious about drawing conclusions.

to avoid jumping to conclusions

IPA: /əˈvɔɪd ˈdʒʌmpɪŋ tə kənˈkluːʒənz/
Pronunciation: uh-VOID JUMP-ing tuh kuhn-KLOO-zhuhnz
French: éviter de tirer des conclusions hâtives

Definition: To avoid deciding too quickly without enough evidence.

Example: Analysts should avoid jumping to conclusions.

to avoid overgeneralising

IPA: /əˈvɔɪd ˌəʊvəˈdʒenərəlaɪzɪŋ/
Pronunciation: uh-VOID OH-vuh-JEN-uh-ruh-ly-zing
French: éviter de généraliser abusivement

Definition: To avoid making a claim too broad.

Example: One should avoid overgeneralising from one poll.

to draw a cautious conclusion

IPA: /drɔː ə ˈkɔːʃəs kənˈkluːʒən/
Pronunciation: draw uh KAW-shuhs kuhn-KLOO-zhuhn
French: tirer une conclusion prudente

Definition: To reach a careful, limited judgement.

Example: We can draw a cautious conclusion from the data.

to remain open to interpretation

IPA: /rɪˈmeɪn ˈəʊpən tʊ ɪnˌtɜːprɪˈteɪʃən/
Pronunciation: ri-MAYN OH-puhn too in-tur-pri-TAY-shuhn
French: rester ouvert à interprétation

Definition: To be not fully settled in meaning.

Example: The figures remain open to interpretation.

the picture is unclear

IPA: /ðə ˈpɪktʃə ɪz ʌnˈklɪə/
Pronunciation: thuh PIK-chuh iz un-KLEER
French: la situation est floue

Definition: The situation is not easy to understand.

Example: The picture is unclear because the data are incomplete.

IPA: /ði ˈevɪdəns ɪz mɪkst/
Pronunciation: the EV-i-duhns iz mikst
French: les éléments sont mitigés

Example: The evidence is mixed on the effects of the policy.

F. Hedging, caution and academic precision

the data are ambiguous

IPA: /ðə ˈdeɪtə ɑːr æmˈbɪɡjuəs/
Pronunciation: thuh DAY-tuh ar am-BIG-yoo-uhs
French: les données sont ambiguës

Definition: The data can be interpreted in several ways.

Example: The data are ambiguous and require caution.

there is insufficient evidence

IPA: /ðeər ɪz ˌɪnsəˈfɪʃənt ˈevɪdəns/
Pronunciation: thair iz in-suh-FISH-uhnt EV-i-duhns
French: les preuves sont insuffisantes

Definition: There is not enough evidence.

Example: There is insufficient evidence to prove causation.

correlation does not imply causation

IPA: /ˌkɒrəˈleɪʃən dʌz nɒt ɪmˈplaɪ kɔːˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: kor-uh-LAY-shuhn duz not im-PLY kaw-ZAY-shuhn
French: corrélation n’implique pas causalité

Definition: A relationship between two facts does not prove one causes the other.

Example: Correlation does not imply causation, especially in social science.
