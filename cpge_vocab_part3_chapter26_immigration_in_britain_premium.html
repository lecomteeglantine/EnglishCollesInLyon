Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse immigration in Britain: post-war migration, Windrush, Brexit and EU citizens, visas, asylum, small boats, the Home Office, the hostile environment, integration, labour shortages, multiculturalism and anti-immigrant rhetoric.

How to use this chapter

For press articles Focus on terms such as net migration, Windrush scandal, hostile environment, settled status, points-based immigration system, small boats and Rwanda plan.

A. Core immigration vocabulary and post-war history

immigration in Britain

IPA: /ˌɪmɪˈɡreɪʃən ɪn ˈbrɪtən/
Pronunciation: im-i-GRAY-shuhn in BRIT-uhn
French: immigration en Grande-Bretagne

Definition: The movement of people into Britain to live, work, study or seek protection.

Example: Immigration in Britain has shaped politics, identity and public services.

immigrant

IPA: /ˈɪmɪɡrənt/
Pronunciation: IM-i-gruhnt
French: immigré ; immigrée

Definition: A person who moves to another country to live there.

Example: Immigrants have contributed to Britain’s economy and culture.

migrant

IPA: /ˈmaɪɡrənt/
Pronunciation: MY-gruhnt
French: migrant ; migrante

Definition: A person who moves from one place to another, often for work, safety or better opportunities.

Example: Migrants are often discussed in debates about borders and labour shortages.

migration

IPA: /maɪˈɡreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: my-GRAY-shuhn
French: migration

Definition: The movement of people across borders or within a country.

Example: Migration can be driven by conflict, poverty, education or work.

net migration

IPA: /ˌnet maɪˈɡreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: net my-GRAY-shuhn
French: solde migratoire

Definition: The difference between the number of people entering and leaving a country.

Example: Net migration has become a major political issue in Britain.

foreign-born population

IPA: /ˌfɒrən bɔːn ˌpɒpjʊˈleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: FOR-uhn born pop-yuh-LAY-shuhn
French: population née à l’étranger

Definition: The share of residents who were born outside the country.

Example: The foreign-born population is especially high in London.

ethnic minority

IPA: /ˌeθnɪk maɪˈnɒrəti/
Pronunciation: ETH-nik my-NOR-uh-tee
French: minorité ethnique

Definition: A group with a shared ethnic background that is smaller or less powerful than the majority.

Example: Ethnic minority communities are part of modern British identity.

minority ethnic background

IPA: /maɪˌnɒrəti ˈeθnɪk ˈbækɡraʊnd/
Pronunciation: my-NOR-uh-tee ETH-nik BAK-ground
French: origine issue d’une minorité ethnique

Definition: A background associated with an ethnic minority group.

Example: People from minority ethnic backgrounds can face discrimination.

British Asian

IPA: /ˌbrɪtɪʃ ˈeɪʒən/
Pronunciation: BRIT-ish AY-zhuhn
French: Britannique d’origine asiatique

Definition: A British person with South Asian or broader Asian heritage.

Example: British Asian communities have shaped British food, business and politics.

Black British

IPA: /ˌblæk ˈbrɪtɪʃ/
Pronunciation: black BRIT-ish
French: Noir britannique ; Afro-Britannique

Definition: A British person of African or Caribbean heritage.

Example: Black British culture has strongly influenced music and sport.

Commonwealth migration

IPA: /ˈkɒmənwelθ maɪˌɡreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: KOM-uhn-welth my-GRAY-shuhn
French: migration du Commonwealth

Definition: Migration from Commonwealth countries to Britain.

Example: Commonwealth migration helped rebuild post-war Britain.

post-war immigration

IPA: /ˌpəʊst wɔː ˌɪmɪˈɡreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: post-war im-i-GRAY-shuhn
French: immigration d’après-guerre

Definition: Immigration to Britain after the Second World War.

Example: Post-war immigration transformed British society.

Windrush generation

IPA: /ˈwɪndrʌʃ ˌdʒenəˈreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: WIND-rush jen-uh-RAY-shuhn
French: génération Windrush

Definition: People from the Caribbean who came to Britain from 1948 onwards, especially to help rebuild the country.

Example: The Windrush generation is central to Britain’s post-war history.

Empire Windrush

IPA: /ˌempaɪə ˈwɪndrʌʃ/
Pronunciation: EM-py-uh WIND-rush
French: Empire Windrush

Definition: The ship that brought Caribbean passengers to Britain in 1948, symbolising post-war migration.

Example: The arrival of the Empire Windrush became a symbolic moment.

Windrush scandal

IPA: /ˈwɪndrʌʃ ˈskændəl/
Pronunciation: WIND-rush SKAN-duhl
French: scandale Windrush

Definition: A scandal in which legal residents were wrongly detained, denied rights or deported.

Example: The Windrush scandal exposed the human cost of hostile immigration policy.

hostile environment

IPA: /ˌhɒstaɪl ɪnˈvaɪrənmənt/
Pronunciation: HOS-tyle in-VY-ruhn-muhnt
French: environnement hostile

Definition: A set of policies designed to make life difficult for undocumented migrants in the UK.

Example: The hostile environment policy was widely criticised after the Windrush scandal.

right to remain

IPA: /ˌraɪt tə rɪˈmeɪn/
Pronunciation: rite tuh ri-MAYN
French: droit de séjour

Definition: Legal permission to stay in a country.

Example: Some Windrush victims struggled to prove their right to remain.

leave to remain

IPA: /ˌliːv tə rɪˈmeɪn/
Pronunciation: leev tuh ri-MAYN
French: autorisation de séjour

Definition: Official permission for a non-citizen to stay in the UK.

Example: Migrants may apply for indefinite leave to remain.

indefinite leave to remain

IPA: /ɪnˌdefɪnət ˌliːv tə rɪˈmeɪn/
Pronunciation: in-DEF-i-nuht leev tuh ri-MAYN
French: autorisation de séjour illimitée

Definition: Permission to live in the UK without a time limit.

Example: Indefinite leave to remain is a key step before citizenship.

settled status

IPA: /ˌsetəld ˈsteɪtəs/
Pronunciation: SET-uhld STAY-tuhs
French: statut de résident permanent pour citoyens européens

Definition: A post-Brexit status allowing eligible EU citizens to continue living in the UK.

Example: Many EU citizens applied for settled status after Brexit.

pre-settled status

IPA: /ˌpriː ˈsetəld ˈsteɪtəs/
Pronunciation: pree-SET-uhld STAY-tuhs
French: pré-statut de résident permanent

Definition: Temporary status for EU citizens who had not yet lived in the UK long enough for settled status.

Example: Pre-settled status created legal uncertainty for some residents.

EU citizens

IPA: /ˌiː juː ˈsɪtɪzənz/
Pronunciation: ee-yoo SIT-i-zuhnz
French: citoyens de l’Union européenne

Definition: Citizens of European Union member states.

Example: EU citizens lost automatic free movement rights after Brexit.

free movement

IPA: /ˌfriː ˈmuːvmənt/
Pronunciation: free MOOV-muhnt
French: libre circulation

Definition: The EU principle allowing citizens to live and work in other member states.

Example: Brexit ended free movement between the UK and the EU.

EU Settlement Scheme

IPA: /ˌiː juː ˈsetəlmənt skiːm/
Pronunciation: ee-yoo SET-uhl-muhnt skeem
French: système de statut de résident pour citoyens européens

Definition: The UK system allowing eligible EU citizens to apply for post-Brexit residence rights.

Example: The EU Settlement Scheme affected millions of Europeans in Britain.

B. Visas, status, borders and the Home Office

points-based immigration system

IPA: /ˈpɔɪnts beɪst ˌɪmɪˈɡreɪʃən ˈsɪstəm/
Pronunciation: points bayst im-i-GRAY-shuhn SIS-tuhm
French: système d’immigration à points

Definition: An immigration system that awards points for skills, salary, language and job offers.

Example: Britain introduced a points-based immigration system after Brexit.

skilled worker visa

IPA: /ˌskɪld ˈwɜːkə ˈviːzə/
Pronunciation: skild WUR-kuh VEE-zuh
French: visa de travailleur qualifié

Definition: A visa allowing qualified workers to take eligible jobs in the UK.

Example: The skilled worker visa aims to attract workers in shortage occupations.

shortage occupation

IPA: /ˈʃɔːtɪdʒ ˌɒkjʊˈpeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: SHOR-tij ok-yuh-PAY-shuhn
French: métier en tension

Definition: A job category where there are not enough workers.

Example: Care work has often been treated as a shortage occupation.

work visa

IPA: /ˈwɜːk ˌviːzə/
Pronunciation: work VEE-zuh
French: visa de travail

Definition: A visa allowing a person to work legally in a country.

Example: Many migrants need a work visa to take a job in Britain.

student visa

IPA: /ˈstjuːdənt ˌviːzə/
Pronunciation: STYOO-duhnt VEE-zuh
French: visa étudiant

Definition: A visa allowing a person to study in another country.

Example: International students contribute to British universities.

international student

IPA: /ˌɪntəˈnæʃənəl ˈstjuːdənt/
Pronunciation: IN-tuh-NASH-uh-nuhl STYOO-duhnt
French: étudiant international

Definition: A student studying in a country other than their own.

Example: International students are included in migration statistics.

dependent

IPA: /dɪˈpendənt/
Pronunciation: di-PEN-duhnt
French: personne à charge

Definition: A family member allowed to accompany or join a migrant under certain conditions.

Example: Restrictions on dependents became politically controversial.

family reunion

IPA: /ˌfæməli riːˈjuːniən/
Pronunciation: FAM-uh-lee ree-YOO-nee-uhn
French: regroupement familial

Definition: The process of family members joining a relative already living in a country.

Example: Family reunion is important for refugees and settled migrants.

spouse visa

IPA: /spaʊs ˈviːzə/
Pronunciation: spowss VEE-zuh
French: visa de conjoint

Definition: A visa allowing someone to join their husband, wife or partner in the UK.

Example: The spouse visa includes income requirements.

income threshold

IPA: /ˈɪnkʌm ˈθreʃhəʊld/
Pronunciation: IN-kum THRESH-hohld
French: seuil de revenu

Definition: A minimum income required to qualify for a visa or benefit.

Example: The income threshold for family visas has been criticised as unfair.

border control

IPA: /ˈbɔːdə kənˌtrəʊl/
Pronunciation: BOR-duh kuhn-TROHL
French: contrôle des frontières

Definition: The regulation of who enters and leaves a country.

Example: Border control was a central Brexit promise.

border security

IPA: /ˈbɔːdə sɪˌkjʊərəti/
Pronunciation: BOR-duh si-KYOOR-uh-tee
French: sécurité aux frontières

Definition: Measures used to protect and monitor borders.

Example: The government promised stronger border security.

Home Office

IPA: /ˈhəʊm ˌɒfɪs/
Pronunciation: home OFF-is
French: ministère de l’Intérieur britannique

Definition: The UK government department responsible for immigration, policing and security.

Example: The Home Office manages immigration applications.

Home Secretary

IPA: /ˈhəʊm ˌsekrətəri/
Pronunciation: home SEK-ruh-tuh-ree
French: ministre de l’Intérieur britannique

Definition: The minister responsible for the Home Office.

Example: The Home Secretary announced a new migration policy.

UK Border Force

IPA: /ˌjuː keɪ ˈbɔːdə fɔːs/
Pronunciation: yoo-kay BOR-duh force
French: police britannique aux frontières

Definition: The law enforcement command responsible for border control in the UK.

Example: UK Border Force officers check passports and goods.

asylum

IPA: /əˈsaɪləm/
Pronunciation: uh-SY-luhm
French: asile

Definition: Protection granted to someone fleeing persecution or serious danger.

Example: Asylum claims have become a major political issue.

C. Asylum, small boats and enforcement

asylum seeker

IPA: /əˈsaɪləm ˌsiːkə/
Pronunciation: uh-SY-luhm SEE-kuh
French: demandeur d’asile

Definition: A person who has applied for refugee protection and is awaiting a decision.

Example: Asylum seekers often wait months or years for a decision.

refugee

IPA: /ˌrefjʊˈdʒiː/
Pronunciation: REF-yoo-JEE
French: réfugié ; réfugiée

Definition: A person forced to leave their country because of war, persecution or violence.

Example: Refugees have legal protection under international law.

persecution

IPA: /ˌpɜːsɪˈkjuːʃən/
Pronunciation: pur-si-KYOO-shuhn
French: persécution

Definition: Mistreatment because of identity, religion, politics or social group.

Example: Victims of persecution may qualify for asylum.

humanitarian protection

IPA: /hjuːˌmænɪˈteəriən prəˈtekʃən/
Pronunciation: hyoo-man-i-TAIR-ee-uhn pruh-TEK-shuhn
French: protection humanitaire

Definition: Protection granted to people at serious risk if returned to their country.

Example: Humanitarian protection applies when return would be dangerous.

refugee resettlement

IPA: /ˌrefjʊˈdʒiː riːˈsetəlmənt/
Pronunciation: REF-yoo-JEE ree-SET-uhl-muhnt
French: réinstallation de réfugiés

Definition: The organised movement of refugees to a safe country where they can rebuild their lives.

Example: Refugee resettlement schemes have helped Syrians and Ukrainians.

safe and legal routes

IPA: /ˌseɪf ənd ˈliːɡəl ruːts/
Pronunciation: sayf and LEE-guhl roots
French: voies sûres et légales

Definition: Official pathways for refugees or migrants to enter a country legally and safely.

Example: Critics argue that Britain needs more safe and legal routes.

small boats

IPA: /ˌsmɔːl ˈbəʊts/
Pronunciation: small boats
French: petites embarcations ; small boats

Definition: Small vessels used by migrants to cross the English Channel, often dangerously.

Example: Small boats have become a symbol of the asylum debate.

Channel crossing

IPA: /ˈtʃænəl ˌkrɒsɪŋ/
Pronunciation: CHAN-uhl KROSS-ing
French: traversée de la Manche

Definition: A journey across the English Channel, especially by migrants in small boats.

Example: Channel crossings are politically sensitive and dangerous.

English Channel

IPA: /ˌɪŋɡlɪʃ ˈtʃænəl/
Pronunciation: ING-glish CHAN-uhl
French: Manche

Definition: The sea between southern England and northern France.

Example: The English Channel has become a migration route.

people smuggler

IPA: /ˈpiːpəl ˌsmʌɡlə/
Pronunciation: PEE-puhl SMUG-luh
French: passeur

Definition: A person who illegally helps migrants cross borders for money.

Example: People smugglers exploit desperate migrants.

smuggling network

IPA: /ˈsmʌɡlɪŋ ˌnetwɜːk/
Pronunciation: SMUG-ling NET-wurk
French: réseau de passeurs

Definition: An organised group illegally helping people or goods cross borders.

Example: The government promised to break smuggling networks.

human trafficking

IPA: /ˌhjuːmən ˈtræfɪkɪŋ/
Pronunciation: HYOO-muhn TRAF-i-king
French: traite des êtres humains

Definition: The illegal exploitation of people through force, coercion or deception.

Example: Migrants can be vulnerable to human trafficking.

detention centre

IPA: /dɪˈtenʃən ˌsentə/
Pronunciation: di-TEN-shuhn SEN-tuh
French: centre de rétention ; centre de détention

Definition: A facility where migrants are held by the authorities.

Example: Immigration detention centres have faced criticism.

immigration detention

IPA: /ˌɪmɪˈɡreɪʃən dɪˈtenʃən/
Pronunciation: im-i-GRAY-shuhn di-TEN-shuhn
French: rétention administrative liée à l’immigration

Definition: The holding of migrants by immigration authorities.

Example: Immigration detention raises human rights concerns.

deportation

IPA: /ˌdiːpɔːˈteɪʃən/
Pronunciation: dee-por-TAY-shuhn
French: expulsion ; reconduite à la frontière

Definition: The forced removal of a non-citizen from a country.

Example: Deportation can separate families and communities.

removal

IPA: /rɪˈmuːvəl/
Pronunciation: ri-MOO-vuhl
French: éloignement ; expulsion

Definition: The official process of forcing someone to leave the country.

Example: The Home Office planned removals of rejected asylum seekers.

Rwanda plan

IPA: /ruːˈændə plæn/
Pronunciation: roo-AN-duh plan
French: plan Rwanda

Definition: A UK policy proposal to send some asylum seekers to Rwanda for processing or settlement.

Example: The Rwanda plan became one of Britain’s most controversial asylum policies.

offshore processing

IPA: /ˌɒfʃɔː ˈprəʊsesɪŋ/
Pronunciation: OFF-shor PROH-sess-ing
French: traitement offshore des demandes d’asile

Definition: Processing asylum claims outside the country where asylum is sought.

Example: Offshore processing was defended as a deterrent and criticised as inhumane.

deterrence

IPA: /dɪˈterəns/
Pronunciation: di-TERR-uhns
French: dissuasion

Definition: A policy designed to discourage people from doing something.

Example: The government framed the Rwanda plan as deterrence.

illegal migration

IPA: /ɪˌliːɡəl maɪˈɡreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: i-LEE-guhl my-GRAY-shuhn
French: migration illégale ; terme politisé

Definition: Migration that breaks immigration law; a politically charged phrase.

Example: Illegal migration is often used in government rhetoric.

irregular migration

IPA: /ɪˌreɡjʊlə maɪˈɡreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: i-REG-yuh-luh my-GRAY-shuhn
French: migration irrégulière

Definition: Migration outside regular legal channels.

Example: Irregular migration can be driven by lack of safe routes.

undocumented migrant

IPA: /ʌnˈdɒkjʊmentɪd ˈmaɪɡrənt/
Pronunciation: un-DOK-yuh-men-tid MY-gruhnt
French: migrant sans papiers ; migrant en situation irrégulière

Definition: A migrant without legal documents or permission to stay.

Example: Undocumented migrants often avoid public services out of fear.

overstayer

IPA: /ˈəʊvəsteɪə/
Pronunciation: OH-vuh-stay-uh
French: personne ayant dépassé la durée de son visa

Definition: A person who remains in a country after their visa has expired.

Example: Some undocumented migrants are visa overstayers.

illegal working

IPA: /ɪˌliːɡəl ˈwɜːkɪŋ/
Pronunciation: i-LEE-guhl WUR-king
French: travail illégal

Definition: Work carried out without legal permission.

Example: Employers can be fined for illegal working.

right to work check

IPA: /ˌraɪt tə ˈwɜːk tʃek/
Pronunciation: rite tuh work chek
French: vérification du droit au travail

Definition: A check employers must make to confirm someone can legally work in the UK.

Example: Right to work checks are part of the hostile environment.

right to rent check

IPA: /ˌraɪt tə ˈrent tʃek/
Pronunciation: rite tuh rent chek
French: vérification du droit de louer

Definition: A check landlords must make to confirm someone can legally rent housing in England.

Example: Right to rent checks have been criticised for encouraging discrimination.

overcrowded housing

IPA: /ˌəʊvəˌkraʊdɪd ˈhaʊzɪŋ/
Pronunciation: OH-vuh-KROW-did HOW-zing
French: logement surpeuplé

Definition: Housing with too many people living in too little space.

Example: Some migrant families face overcrowded housing.

asylum accommodation

IPA: /əˈsaɪləm əˌkɒməˈdeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: uh-SY-luhm uh-kom-uh-DAY-shuhn
French: hébergement des demandeurs d’asile

Definition: Housing provided to asylum seekers while their claims are processed.

Example: Asylum accommodation has been criticised as inadequate.

D. Integration, citizenship, labour and public services

hotel accommodation

IPA: /həʊˈtel əˌkɒməˈdeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: hoh-TEL uh-kom-uh-DAY-shuhn
French: hébergement en hôtel

Definition: Use of hotels to house asylum seekers temporarily.

Example: Hotel accommodation for asylum seekers became politically controversial.

integration

IPA: /ˌɪntɪˈɡreɪʃən/
Pronunciation: in-ti-GRAY-shuhn
French: intégration

Definition: The process by which migrants become part of social, economic and political life.

Example: Language learning can support integration.

assimilation

IPA: /əˌsɪmɪˈleɪʃən/
Pronunciation: uh-sim-i-LAY-shuhn
French: assimilation

Definition: The process of adopting the majority culture, sometimes at the cost of minority identity.

Example: Assimilation is often contrasted with multiculturalism.

multiculturalism

IPA: /ˌmʌltiˈkʌltʃərəlɪzəm/
Pronunciation: MUL-tee-KUL-chuh-ruh-liz-uhm
French: multiculturalisme

Definition: A model where different cultural groups coexist and are recognised.

Example: Multiculturalism has shaped modern Britain.

social cohesion

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl kəʊˈhiːʒən/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl koh-HEE-zhuhn
French: cohésion sociale

Definition: Trust and connection between groups in society.

Example: Integration policies aim to strengthen social cohesion.

community relations

IPA: /kəˌmjuːnəti rɪˈleɪʃənz/
Pronunciation: kuh-MYOO-nuh-tee ri-LAY-shuhnz
French: relations intercommunautaires

Definition: Relations between different groups within society.

Example: Community relations can be damaged by racism and political scapegoating.

parallel lives

IPA: /ˌpærəlel ˈlaɪvz/
Pronunciation: PARR-uh-lel lyvz
French: vies parallèles ; communautés séparées

Definition: A phrase describing communities living separately with limited interaction.

Example: The phrase parallel lives was used in debates about segregation.

segregation

IPA: /ˌseɡrɪˈɡeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: seg-ri-GAY-shuhn
French: ségrégation

Definition: The separation of groups in housing, schools or social life.

Example: Critics of multiculturalism sometimes warn of segregation.

British values

IPA: /ˌbrɪtɪʃ ˈvæljuːz/
Pronunciation: BRIT-ish VAL-yooz
French: valeurs britanniques

Definition: Values officially promoted in schools, such as democracy, tolerance and the rule of law.

Example: British values are often mentioned in integration debates.

citizenship test

IPA: /ˈsɪtɪzənʃɪp test/
Pronunciation: SIT-i-zuhn-ship test
French: test de citoyenneté

Definition: A test on language, culture and institutions required for citizenship.

Example: The citizenship test has been criticised for obscure questions.

Life in the UK test

IPA: /ˌlaɪf ɪn ðə ˌjuː ˈkeɪ test/
Pronunciation: life in thuh yoo-kay test
French: test Life in the UK

Definition: The test required for British citizenship or settlement.

Example: The Life in the UK test assesses knowledge of British life.

English language requirement

IPA: /ˌɪŋɡlɪʃ ˈlæŋɡwɪdʒ rɪˈkwaɪəmənt/
Pronunciation: ING-glish LANG-gwij ri-KWY-uh-muhnt
French: condition de maîtrise de l’anglais

Definition: A rule requiring migrants to prove English ability.

Example: The English language requirement is part of citizenship policy.

naturalisation

IPA: /ˌnætʃərəlaɪˈzeɪʃən/

French: naturalisation

Definition: The process of becoming a citizen of another country.

Example: Naturalisation can be expensive and bureaucratic.

British citizenship

dual nationality

belonging

IPA: /bɪˈlɒŋɪŋ/
Pronunciation: bi-LONG-ing
French: appartenance

Example: Belonging is not only legal but also emotional.

identity

Pronunciation: eye-DEN-tuh-tee
French: identité

hyphenated identity

French: identité composée ; identité à trait d’union

diaspora

IPA: /daɪˈæspərə/
Pronunciation: dy-AS-puh-ruh
French: diaspora

Definition: A community living outside its ancestral homeland.

Example: Diaspora communities keep links with countries of origin.

remittances

IPA: /rɪˈmɪtənsɪz/
Pronunciation: ri-MIT-uhn-siz
French: transferts d’argent ; remises migratoires

Definition: Money sent by migrants to family or communities in their country of origin.

Example: Remittances support families abroad.

labour shortage

IPA: /ˈleɪbə ˈʃɔːtɪdʒ/
Pronunciation: LAY-buh SHOR-tij
French: pénurie de main-d’œuvre

Definition: A lack of workers for available jobs.

Example: Immigration can help address labour shortages.

NHS workforce

IPA: /ˌen eɪtʃ ˈes ˈwɜːkfɔːs/
Pronunciation: en-aych-ess WURK-force
French: personnel du NHS

Definition: The workers employed by the National Health Service.

Example: The NHS workforce depends heavily on migrant workers.

care sector

IPA: /ˈkeə ˌsektə/
Pronunciation: care SEK-tuh
French: secteur du soin et de l’aide à la personne

Definition: The sector providing care for elderly, disabled or vulnerable people.

Example: The care sector has recruited many overseas workers.

seasonal worker

IPA: /ˌsiːzənəl ˈwɜːkə/
Pronunciation: SEE-zuh-nuhl WUR-kuh
French: travailleur saisonnier

Definition: A worker employed for a limited season, often in agriculture.

Example: Seasonal workers are important in British farming.

low-paid work

IPA: /ˌləʊ peɪd ˈwɜːk/
Pronunciation: loh payd work
French: travail mal payé

Definition: Work with low wages.

Example: Migrants are often concentrated in low-paid work.

exploitation

IPA: /ˌeksplɔɪˈteɪʃən/
Pronunciation: ek-sploy-TAY-shuhn
French: exploitation

Definition: Unfair treatment of workers for profit or advantage.

Example: Visa dependency can increase the risk of exploitation.

brain drain

IPA: /ˈbreɪn dreɪn/
Pronunciation: brayn drayn
French: fuite des cerveaux

Definition: The emigration of skilled workers from one country to another.

Example: Recruiting health workers abroad can create brain drain elsewhere.

public services

IPA: /ˌpʌblɪk ˈsɜːvɪsɪz/
Pronunciation: PUB-lik SUR-vis-iz
French: services publics

Definition: Services provided by the state, such as healthcare, education and housing.

Example: Immigration debates often focus on pressure on public services.

E. Politics, rhetoric and high-value verbs

IPA: /ˈpreʃər ɒn ˈhaʊzɪŋ/
Pronunciation: PRESH-uh on HOW-zing
French: pression sur le logement

Definition: Strain on housing supply or affordability.

Example: Some politicians link immigration to pressure on housing.

tax contribution

Pronunciation: taks kon-tri-BYOO-shuhn
French: contribution fiscale

Definition: Money paid in taxes by individuals or groups.

Example: Migrants make an important tax contribution.

economic contribution

Pronunciation: ee-kuh-NOM-ik kon-tri-BYOO-shuhn
French: contribution économique

Definition: The value added to the economy through work, taxes or business.

Example: The economic contribution of migrants is often overlooked.

xenophobia

French: xénophobie

racism

IPA: /ˈreɪsɪzəm/
Pronunciation: RAY-siz-uhm
French: racisme

Definition: Discrimination or prejudice based on race or ethnicity.

Example: Racism affects many migrant and minority communities.

French: rhétorique anti-immigrés

Pronunciation: SKAYP-goht-ing
French: désignation d’un bouc émissaire

moral panic

French: panique morale

tabloid press

IPA: /ˈtæblɔɪd pres/

culture war
