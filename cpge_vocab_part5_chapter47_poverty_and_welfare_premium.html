Chapter objective

This premium chapter gives CPGE students the essential vocabulary needed to analyse poverty and welfare: absolute and relative poverty, deprivation, low-income households, in-work poverty, food insecurity, homelessness, the welfare state, benefits, universal basic income, redistribution, welfare reform, stigma, social mobility and anti-poverty policy.

How to use this chapter

For press articlesFocus on terms such as relative poverty, in-work poverty, material deprivation, food insecurity, social safety net, means-tested benefit, benefit cap, non-take-up, poverty trap and social investment.
For oral examsUse this vocabulary to explain how poverty is caused by low wages, unemployment, housing costs, health inequalities, education gaps and weak social protection.
For essaysMemorise the collocations: to reduce poverty, to lift people out of poverty, to make ends meet, to expand the safety net, to break the cycle of poverty.

A. Core poverty vocabulary and measurement

poverty

IPA: /ˈpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: POV-uh-tee
French: pauvreté

Definition: The state of lacking enough money or resources to meet basic needs.

Example: Poverty affects health, education, housing and life chances.

absolute poverty

IPA: /ˌæbsəluːt ˈpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: AB-suh-loot POV-uh-tee
French: pauvreté absolue

Definition: Poverty measured against a fixed minimum standard needed for survival.

Example: Absolute poverty is often linked to lack of food, shelter or clean water.

relative poverty

IPA: /ˌrelətɪv ˈpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: REL-uh-tiv POV-uh-tee
French: pauvreté relative

Definition: Poverty measured in relation to the living standards of the society around you.

Example: Relative poverty can exist even in wealthy countries.

extreme poverty

IPA: /ɪkˌstriːm ˈpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: ik-STREEM POV-uh-tee
French: extrême pauvreté

Definition: Very severe poverty, usually involving inability to meet basic survival needs.

Example: Extreme poverty has fallen globally but remains widespread in some regions.

deep poverty

IPA: /ˌdiːp ˈpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: deep POV-uh-tee
French: grande pauvreté

Definition: Severe poverty far below the poverty line.

Example: Deep poverty often affects people with multiple disadvantages.

persistent poverty

IPA: /pəˌsɪstənt ˈpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: puh-SIS-tuhnt POV-uh-tee
French: pauvreté persistante

Definition: Poverty that lasts over a long period.

Example: Persistent poverty can trap families across generations.

intergenerational poverty

IPA: /ˌɪntədʒenəˈreɪʃənəl ˈpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: IN-tuh-jen-uh-RAY-shuh-nuhl POV-uh-tee
French: pauvreté intergénérationnelle

Definition: Poverty passed from one generation to the next.

Example: Intergenerational poverty is linked to unequal education and housing.

child poverty

IPA: /ˈtʃaɪld ˌpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: CHYLD POV-uh-tee
French: pauvreté infantile

Definition: Poverty affecting children.

Example: Child poverty has long-term effects on health and achievement.

working poverty

IPA: /ˈwɜːkɪŋ ˌpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: WUR-king POV-uh-tee
French: pauvreté laborieuse

Definition: Poverty affecting people who are employed.

Example: Working poverty shows that having a job is not always enough.

in-work poverty

IPA: /ˌɪn wɜːk ˈpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: in-work POV-uh-tee
French: pauvreté au travail

Definition: Poverty experienced by people who work but earn too little.

Example: In-work poverty is linked to low wages and insecure hours.

rural poverty

IPA: /ˌrʊərəl ˈpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: ROOR-uhl POV-uh-tee
French: pauvreté rurale

Definition: Poverty in countryside or non-urban areas.

Example: Rural poverty is often linked to poor transport and limited services.

urban poverty

IPA: /ˌɜːbən ˈpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: UR-buhn POV-uh-tee
French: pauvreté urbaine

Definition: Poverty in towns and cities.

Example: Urban poverty can be hidden in wealthy cities.

fuel poverty

IPA: /ˈfjuːəl ˌpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: FYOO-uhl POV-uh-tee
French: précarité énergétique

Definition: Inability to afford enough energy or heating.

Example: Fuel poverty worsened during the energy price shock.

energy poverty

IPA: /ˈenədʒi ˌpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: EN-uh-jee POV-uh-tee
French: précarité énergétique

Definition: Lack of access to affordable, reliable energy.

Example: Energy poverty affects both developing and wealthy countries.

food poverty

IPA: /ˈfuːd ˌpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: food POV-uh-tee
French: précarité alimentaire

Definition: Inability to afford adequate food.

Example: Food poverty has increased demand for food banks.

period poverty

IPA: /ˈpɪəriəd ˌpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: PEER-ee-uhd POV-uh-tee
French: précarité menstruelle

Definition: Lack of access to menstrual products because of poverty.

Example: Period poverty can cause girls to miss school.

digital poverty

IPA: /ˈdɪdʒɪtəl ˌpɒvəti/
Pronunciation: DIJ-i-tuhl POV-uh-tee
French: précarité numérique

Definition: Lack of access to digital tools, internet or skills.

Example: Digital poverty limits access to education and jobs.

poverty line

IPA: /ˈpɒvəti laɪn/
Pronunciation: POV-uh-tee line
French: seuil de pauvreté

Definition: The income level below which someone is considered poor.

Example: The poverty line is used to measure poverty rates.

poverty threshold

IPA: /ˈpɒvəti ˌθreʃhəʊld/
Pronunciation: POV-uh-tee THRESH-hohld
French: seuil de pauvreté

Definition: The minimum income level used to define poverty.

Example: Poverty thresholds vary across countries.

poverty rate

IPA: /ˈpɒvəti reɪt/
Pronunciation: POV-uh-tee rayt
French: taux de pauvreté

Definition: The share of a population living in poverty.

Example: The poverty rate rose after welfare cuts.

material deprivation

IPA: /məˌtɪəriəl ˌdeprɪˈveɪʃən/
Pronunciation: muh-TEER-ee-uhl dep-ri-VAY-shuhn
French: privation matérielle

Definition: Lack of goods or conditions considered necessary for a decent life.

Example: Material deprivation includes inability to heat a home or replace clothes.

severe material deprivation

IPA: /sɪˌvɪə məˌtɪəriəl ˌdeprɪˈveɪʃən/
Pronunciation: si-VEER muh-TEER-ee-uhl dep-ri-VAY-shuhn
French: privation matérielle sévère

Definition: Serious lack of basic goods or living conditions.

Example: Severe material deprivation is a key social indicator.

deprivation

IPA: /ˌdeprɪˈveɪʃən/
Pronunciation: dep-ri-VAY-shuhn
French: privation

Definition: Lack of things necessary for a decent life.

Example: Deprivation can affect housing, food, education and health.

social deprivation

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl ˌdeprɪˈveɪʃən/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl dep-ri-VAY-shuhn
French: privation sociale

Definition: Lack of social opportunities, resources or participation.

Example: Social deprivation can isolate communities.

financial hardship

IPA: /faɪˌnænʃəl ˈhɑːdʃɪp/
Pronunciation: fy-NAN-shuhl HARD-ship
French: difficultés financières

Definition: Serious difficulty caused by lack of money.

Example: Financial hardship can lead to debt and homelessness.

economic hardship

IPA: /ˌiːkəˈnɒmɪk ˈhɑːdʃɪp/
Pronunciation: ee-kuh-NOM-ik HARD-ship
French: difficultés économiques

Definition: Difficulty caused by unemployment, low income or high prices.

Example: Economic hardship often increases during recessions.

destitution

IPA: /ˌdestɪˈtjuːʃən/
Pronunciation: des-ti-TYOO-shuhn
French: dénuement ; misère

Definition: Extreme poverty involving lack of basic necessities.

Example: Destitution can mean having no food, shelter or income.

hardship

IPA: /ˈhɑːdʃɪp/
Pronunciation: HARD-ship
French: difficulté ; épreuve

Definition: Severe suffering or difficulty, especially financial.

Example: Low-income households face hardship when prices rise.

social exclusion

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl ɪkˈskluːʒən/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl ik-SKLOO-zhuhn
French: exclusion sociale

Definition: Being cut off from opportunities, services or participation in society.

Example: Poverty often leads to social exclusion.

marginalisation

IPA: /ˌmɑːdʒɪnəlaɪˈzeɪʃən/
Pronunciation: mar-ji-nuh-ly-ZAY-shuhn
French: marginalisation

Definition: The process of pushing people or groups to the edge of society.

Example: Marginalisation makes it harder to escape poverty.

vulnerability

IPA: /ˌvʌlnərəˈbɪləti/
Pronunciation: vul-nuh-ruh-BIL-uh-tee
French: vulnérabilité

Definition: Exposure to risk or harm because of weak protection or resources.

Example: Poverty increases vulnerability to crises.

vulnerable households

IPA: /ˈvʌlnərəbəl ˈhaʊshəʊldz/
Pronunciation: VUL-nuh-ruh-buhl HOWSS-hohldz
French: ménages vulnérables

Definition: Households more likely to suffer hardship or crisis.

Example: Vulnerable households need targeted support.

B. Income, hardship, housing and food insecurity

low-income households

IPA: /ˌləʊ ˈɪnkʌm ˈhaʊshəʊldz/
Pronunciation: loh IN-kum HOWSS-hohldz
French: ménages à faible revenu

Definition: Households with limited income.

Example: Low-income households spend more of their budget on essentials.

low-income families

IPA: /ˌləʊ ˈɪnkʌm ˈfæməliz/
Pronunciation: loh IN-kum FAM-uh-leez
French: familles à faible revenu

Definition: Families with low earnings or benefits.

Example: Low-income families are vulnerable to food and fuel poverty.

the poor

IPA: /ðə ˈpʊə/
Pronunciation: thuh poor
French: les pauvres

Definition: People living in poverty; use carefully as it can sound reductive.

Example: Policies should help poor people without stigmatising them.

working class

IPA: /ˌwɜːkɪŋ ˈklɑːs/
Pronunciation: WUR-king class
French: classe ouvrière ; classes populaires

Definition: People in manual, service or lower-paid jobs, often with less economic power.

Example: The working class has been affected by deindustrialisation.

underclass

IPA: /ˈʌndəklɑːs/
Pronunciation: UN-duh-class
French: sous-classe sociale

Definition: A controversial term for a group seen as permanently poor and excluded.

Example: The term underclass can be stigmatising.

disadvantaged

IPA: /ˌdɪsədˈvɑːntɪdʒd/
Pronunciation: dis-ad-VAN-tijd
French: défavorisé

Definition: Having fewer opportunities or resources than others.

Example: Disadvantaged pupils often need extra support.

disadvantaged communities

IPA: /ˌdɪsədˈvɑːntɪdʒd kəˈmjuːnətiz/
Pronunciation: dis-ad-VAN-tijd kuh-MYOO-nuh-teez
French: communautés défavorisées

Definition: Communities with fewer resources, services and opportunities.

Example: Disadvantaged communities may suffer from poor housing and schools.

left-behind communities

IPA: /ˌleft bɪˈhaɪnd kəˈmjuːnətiz/
Pronunciation: left-bi-HYND kuh-MYOO-nuh-teez
French: communautés laissées pour compte

Definition: Communities that have not benefited from economic growth or globalisation.

Example: Left-behind communities often feel ignored by political elites.

income

IPA: /ˈɪnkʌm/
Pronunciation: IN-kum
French: revenu

Definition: Money received from work, benefits, investment or other sources.

Example: Income determines access to housing and consumption.

household income

IPA: /ˌhaʊshəʊld ˈɪnkʌm/
Pronunciation: HOWSS-hohld IN-kum
French: revenu du ménage

Definition: Total income received by all members of a household.

Example: Household income is used to measure poverty.

disposable income

IPA: /dɪˌspəʊzəbəl ˈɪnkʌm/
Pronunciation: di-SPOH-zuh-buhl IN-kum
French: revenu disponible

Definition: Income left after taxes and transfers.

Example: Disposable income affects living standards.

real income

IPA: /ˌrɪəl ˈɪnkʌm/
Pronunciation: REE-uhl IN-kum
French: revenu réel

Definition: Income adjusted for inflation.

Example: Real income falls when prices rise faster than wages.

wealth

IPA: /welθ/
Pronunciation: welth
French: patrimoine ; richesse

Definition: Assets owned by a person or household, such as property or savings.

Example: Wealth inequality is often greater than income inequality.

assets

IPA: /ˈæsets/
Pronunciation: AS-ets
French: actifs ; biens

Definition: Things owned that have value.

Example: Housing is a major asset for many families.

savings

IPA: /ˈseɪvɪŋz/
Pronunciation: SAY-vingz
French: épargne

Definition: Money kept rather than spent.

Example: People without savings are vulnerable to shocks.

debt

IPA: /det/
Pronunciation: det
French: dette

Definition: Money owed to another person, bank or institution.

Example: Household debt can worsen poverty.

household debt

IPA: /ˌhaʊshəʊld ˈdet/
Pronunciation: HOWSS-hohld det
French: endettement des ménages

Definition: Debt owed by households.

Example: Household debt rises when wages cannot cover living costs.

personal debt

IPA: /ˌpɜːsənəl ˈdet/
Pronunciation: PUR-suh-nuhl det
French: dette personnelle

Definition: Debt owed by an individual.

Example: Personal debt can spiral through interest charges.

debt trap

IPA: /ˈdet træp/
Pronunciation: det trap
French: piège de l’endettement

Definition: A situation where debt becomes difficult to escape.

Example: Payday loans can create a debt trap.

payday loan

IPA: /ˈpeɪdeɪ ləʊn/
Pronunciation: PAY-day lohn
French: prêt sur salaire

Definition: A short-term high-interest loan, often used by people in financial difficulty.

Example: Payday loans can worsen financial hardship.

loan shark

IPA: /ˈləʊn ʃɑːk/
Pronunciation: lohn shark
French: usurier ; prêteur illégal

Definition: An illegal lender charging very high interest.

cost of living

IPA: /ˌkɒst əv ˈlɪvɪŋ/
Pronunciation: kost ov LIV-ing
French: coût de la vie

Definition: The money needed to pay for basic goods and services.

Example: The cost of living rose faster than wages.

French: crise du coût de la vie

inflation

Pronunciation: in-FLAY-shuhn
French: inflation

purchasing power

essentials

free school meals

IPA: /ˌfriː skuːl ˈmiːlz/
Pronunciation: free school meelz
French: repas scolaires gratuits

Definition: Meals provided free to children from low-income families.

Example: Free school meals can reduce child hunger.

welfare

IPA: /ˈwelfeə/
Pronunciation: WEL-fair
French: aide sociale ; protection sociale

Definition: State support for people in need, often through benefits and services.

Example: Welfare protects citizens from poverty and hardship.

welfare state

IPA: /ˈwelfeə steɪt/
Pronunciation: WEL-fair state
French: État-providence

Definition: A system in which the state provides social protection, healthcare, pensions and benefits.

social welfare

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl ˈwelfeə/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl WEL-fair

Definition: Public support designed to improve well-being and protect people in need.

social protection

IPA: /ˌsəʊʃəl prəˈtekʃən/
Pronunciation: SOH-shuhl pruh-TEK-shuhn

Example: Social protection is crucial during economic crises.

safety net

IPA: /ˈseɪfti net/

social safety net

benefits

welfare benefits

social benefits
