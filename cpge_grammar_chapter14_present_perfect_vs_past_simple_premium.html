Chapter 14 — Present Perfect vs Past Simple
B1 grammar for article summaries, recent news, historical context and data commentary.

Chapter objective

This premium grammar chapter helps CPGE students choose correctly between the Present Perfect and the Past Simple. It focuses on current relevance, finished time, article summaries, news reporting, data commentary and the classic French-speaker trap caused by the French passé composé.

The golden rule

Present Perfect = past connected to now
Past Simple = finished past time
The crisis has changed politics. / The crisis began in 2008.
Main useUse Present Perfect when the present result or unfinished period matters. Use Past Simple when the time is finished or specific.
CPGE habitIn article summaries: Present Perfect for current relevance, Past Simple for chronology, Present Simple for the author’s argument.
Key trapFrench passé composé is not always Present Perfect: J’ai lu l’article hier = I read the article yesterday.

A. Lesson — Why compare Present Perfect and Past Simple?

Core contrast

French: contraste principal

Rule / explanation: The Present Perfect connects past and present; the Past Simple places an action in a finished past time.

Example / answer: The crisis has changed politics. / The crisis began in 2008.

Present Perfect

French: present perfect

Rule / explanation: Use it when the time is unfinished, unknown, or relevant now.

Example / answer: The scandal has damaged public trust.

Past Simple

French: prétérit

Rule / explanation: Use it when the time is finished, specified or clearly historical.

Example / answer: The scandal broke last month.

Current relevance

French: pertinence actuelle

Rule / explanation: Present Perfect often highlights the present result of a past action.

Example / answer: The reform has failed, so the government is under pressure.

Finished time

French: temps terminé

Rule / explanation: Past Simple is required with yesterday, last year, in 2016, ago, when, during a finished period.

Example / answer: The referendum took place in 2016.

News reporting

French: actualité

Rule / explanation: News often introduces a recent event with Present Perfect, then gives details in Past Simple.

Example / answer: The government has announced a reform. It presented the bill yesterday.

Experience vs specific event

French: expérience vs événement précis

Rule / explanation: Use Present Perfect for experience; Past Simple for a specific occasion.

Example / answer: The country has faced crises before. / It faced a major crisis in 2008.

CPGE use

French: usage CPGE

Rule / explanation: This contrast is essential for article summaries, historical background and data commentary.

Example / answer: The article has sparked debate, although it was published only yesterday.

B. Present Perfect — when the past matters now

Present result

French: résultat présent

Rule / explanation: Use Present Perfect when the result is visible or relevant now.

Example / answer: The scandal has weakened the minister.

Unfinished period

French: période non terminée

Rule / explanation: Use it with today, this week, this year, recently, lately, so far.

Example / answer: This year, inflation has affected millions of households.

Recent news

French: actualité récente

Rule / explanation: Use it to announce recent developments.

Example / answer: The opposition has called for an inquiry.

Life or historical experience

French: expérience

Rule / explanation: Use it when exact time is not stated.

Example / answer: Democracy has survived many crises.

Change over time

French: évolution jusqu’au présent

Rule / explanation: Use it for developments from past to present.

Example / answer: Public trust has declined over the past decade.

For + duration

French: for + durée

Rule / explanation: Use for to express how long something has continued.

Example / answer: The debate has lasted for several years.

Since + starting point

French: since + point de départ

Rule / explanation: Use since to mark when something began.

Example / answer: The issue has become more visible since 2016.

Repeated actions up to now

French: répétition jusqu’au présent

Rule / explanation: Use it for repeated events in a period continuing to the present.

Example / answer: The topic has appeared in several articles this month.

C. Past Simple — when the past is finished

Specific past time

French: date précise passée

Rule / explanation: Use Past Simple with a completed time expression.

Example / answer: The government introduced the law in 2019.

Finished period

French: période terminée

Rule / explanation: Use it with last week, last year, yesterday, during the pandemic if treated as finished.

Example / answer: The report was published last year.

Historical facts

French: faits historiques

Rule / explanation: Use Past Simple for historical events.

Example / answer: The welfare state expanded after 1945.

Narrative sequence

French: récit au passé

Rule / explanation: Use it to tell past events in order.

Example / answer: The scandal broke, the minister resigned, and the party lost support.

Specific experience

French: expérience précise

Rule / explanation: Use Past Simple when the exact occasion is known.

Example / answer: The Prime Minister visited Brussels yesterday.

Past cause

French: cause passée

Rule / explanation: Use it for causes in a completed past context.

Example / answer: The financial crash triggered a recession.

Graph commentary with finished period

French: graphique période terminée

Rule / explanation: Use it when the data period is finished.

Example / answer: Unemployment fell between 2015 and 2019.

Article publication details

French: publication

Rule / explanation: Use it for when the article appeared or what happened at a specific time.

Example / answer: The article was published on Monday.

D. Time markers and signal words

Already

French: déjà

Rule / explanation: Usually Present Perfect when meaning before now.

Example / answer: The government has already announced measures.

Yet

French: pas encore / déjà

Rule / explanation: Usually Present Perfect in negatives and questions.

Example / answer: Has the report been published yet?

Just

French: venir de

Rule / explanation: Usually Present Perfect for very recent events.

Example / answer: The minister has just resigned.

Recently

French: récemment

Rule / explanation: Often Present Perfect when relevance remains current.

Example / answer: Several studies have recently challenged the claim.

So far

French: jusqu’à présent

Rule / explanation: Present Perfect because the period continues.

Example / answer: So far, the policy has failed.

Ever / never

French: déjà / jamais

Rule / explanation: Often Present Perfect for life or historical experience.

Example / answer: Has democracy ever faced such pressure?

Yesterday / last year / in 2016

French: temps terminé

Rule / explanation: Past Simple because the time is finished.

Example / answer: The vote took place in 2016.

Ago

French: il y a

Rule / explanation: Past Simple because it refers to a completed point in the past.

Example / answer: The crisis began ten years ago.

E. Present Perfect and Past Simple in article summaries

Opening with Present Perfect

French: ouverture

Rule / explanation: Use Present Perfect to introduce a recent article’s impact or relevance.

Example / answer: The article has raised important questions about inequality.

Details with Past Simple

French: détails

Rule / explanation: Use Past Simple for specific details from the article or publication context.

Example / answer: It was published after a new government report.

Author’s argument

French: argument de l’auteur

Rule / explanation: Use Present Simple to summarise the author’s argument, not necessarily Present Perfect.

Example / answer: The author argues that the reform is insufficient.

Recent development

French: développement récent

Rule / explanation: Use Present Perfect if the situation is still developing.

Example / answer: The controversy has intensified in recent days.

Historical background

French: arrière-plan historique

Rule / explanation: Use Past Simple for background events.

Example / answer: The crisis began after the 2008 financial crash.

Mixed paragraph

French: paragraphe mixte

Rule / explanation: Combine tenses according to meaning.

Example / answer: The issue has become urgent, but politicians ignored it for years.

Avoid tense monotony

French: variété des temps

Rule / explanation: Do not use the same tense mechanically throughout a summary.

Example / answer: The article was published yesterday and has already sparked debate.

CPGE summary pattern

French: modèle CPGE

Rule / explanation: Present Perfect for relevance, Past Simple for chronology, Present Simple for argument.

Example / answer: The article has sparked debate. It was published on Monday. The author argues that regulation is necessary.

F. Present Perfect and Past Simple in data commentary

Unfinished data period

French: période non terminée

Rule / explanation: Use Present Perfect for periods continuing to now.

Example / answer: Since 2020, rents have increased sharply.

Finished data period

French: période terminée

Rule / explanation: Use Past Simple for completed periods.

Example / answer: Between 2010 and 2019, rents increased steadily.

Over the past decade

French: au cours de la dernière décennie

Rule / explanation: Often Present Perfect because it reaches the present.

Example / answer: Over the past decade, inequality has widened.

In the 1990s

French: années 1990

Rule / explanation: Past Simple because the period is finished.

Example / answer: In the 1990s, unemployment fell.

This year

French: cette année

Rule / explanation: Present Perfect if the year is not finished.

Example / answer: This year, the unemployment rate has fallen.

Last year

French: l’année dernière

Rule / explanation: Past Simple because last year is finished.

Example / answer: Last year, the unemployment rate fell.

Trend still relevant

French: tendance actuelle

Rule / explanation: Use Present Perfect to stress present relevance.

Example / answer: Public trust has fallen, which explains current frustration.

Graph warning

French: attention graphique

Rule / explanation: Choose tense according to the time period, not the chart itself.

Example / answer: The graph shows that prices rose between 2010 and 2015.

G. Typical mistakes by French speakers

Present Perfect with finished time

French: date terminée

Rule / explanation: Do not use Present Perfect with a finished date.

Example / answer: Wrong: The crisis has begun in 2008. Correct: The crisis began in 2008.

Past Simple with since

French: since

Rule / explanation: Use Present Perfect with since when the situation continues.

Example / answer: Wrong: Since 2016, trust declined. Correct: Since 2016, trust has declined.

Using Past Simple with so far

French: so far

Rule / explanation: So far requires Present Perfect.

Example / answer: Wrong: So far, the policy failed. Correct: So far, the policy has failed.

Confusing recently and last year

French: recently / last year

Rule / explanation: Recently may take Present Perfect; last year takes Past Simple.

Example / answer: The minister has recently resigned. / The minister resigned last year.

Overusing Present Perfect in history

French: histoire

Rule / explanation: Historical events usually take Past Simple.

Example / answer: Wrong: The welfare state has expanded after 1945. Correct: The welfare state expanded after 1945.

Forgetting the past participle

French: participe passé

Rule / explanation: Present Perfect needs the past participle.

Example / answer: Wrong: has wrote. Correct: has written.

Using did with Present Perfect

French: question incorrecte

Rule / explanation: Present Perfect questions use have/has.

Example / answer: Wrong: Did the policy has worked? Correct: Has the policy worked?

Translating French passé composé mechanically

French: passé composé français

Rule / explanation: French passé composé can correspond to Present Perfect or Past Simple depending on context.

Example / answer: J’ai lu l’article hier = I read the article yesterday. J’ai déjà lu cet article = I have already read this article.
