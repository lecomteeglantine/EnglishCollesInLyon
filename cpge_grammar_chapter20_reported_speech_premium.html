Chapter 20 — Reported Speech
B1 → B2 grammar for article summaries, interviews, viewpoints, questions and formal reporting.

Chapter objective

This premium grammar chapter helps CPGE students master reported speech. It covers tense changes, reporting verbs, reported statements, reported questions, reported commands, time and place changes, article-summary structures and common mistakes made by French speakers.

The golden rule

Reporting verb + that + reported clause
The minister said that the reform would help families.
The journalist asked why voters were angry.
Main useUse reported speech to summarise what someone said, asked, claimed, admitted, warned or suggested.
CPGE habitUse precise reporting verbs: argues, claims, explains, warns, acknowledges, suggests.
Key trapReported questions use statement word order: She asked why voters were angry, not why were voters angry.

A. Lesson — What is reported speech?

Reported speech

French: discours rapporté

Rule / explanation: Reported speech is used to report what someone said without quoting the exact words.

Example / answer: The minister said that the reform would help families.

Direct speech

French: discours direct

Rule / explanation: Direct speech repeats the exact words spoken.

Example / answer: The minister said: “The reform will help families.”

Reporting verb

French: verbe introducteur

Rule / explanation: A reporting verb introduces the reported statement: say, tell, argue, claim, explain, admit, warn.

Example / answer: The journalist argues that the crisis is structural.

Backshift

French: concordance des temps

Rule / explanation: When the reporting verb is in the past, verb tenses often move one step back.

Example / answer: “I support the reform” → He said he supported the reform.

No quotation marks

French: pas de guillemets

Rule / explanation: Reported speech usually does not use quotation marks.

Example / answer: The author claims that social media polarise debate.

That

French: that

Rule / explanation: That is often used after reporting verbs, but it can sometimes be omitted.

Example / answer: The report says that inequality has increased.

CPGE use

French: usage CPGE

Rule / explanation: Reported speech is essential for summarising articles, reporting arguments and presenting viewpoints.

Example / answer: The article suggests that the policy has failed.

Meaning over mechanics

French: nuance

Rule / explanation: The aim is not mechanical transformation only: choose reporting verbs that show nuance.

Example / answer: The author warns that the situation may deteriorate.

B. Tense changes in reported speech

Present Simple → Past Simple

French: présent simple → prétérit

Rule / explanation: When reporting in the past, Present Simple often becomes Past Simple.

Example / answer: “The reform works” → She said the reform worked.

Present Continuous → Past Continuous

French: présent continu → prétérit continu

Rule / explanation: Present Continuous often becomes Past Continuous.

Example / answer: “The economy is recovering” → He said the economy was recovering.

Past Simple → Past Perfect

French: prétérit → past perfect

Rule / explanation: Past Simple often becomes Past Perfect.

Example / answer: “The crisis began in 2008” → She said the crisis had begun in 2008.

Present Perfect → Past Perfect

French: present perfect → past perfect

Rule / explanation: Present Perfect often becomes Past Perfect.

Example / answer: “The policy has failed” → He said the policy had failed.

Will → would

French: will → would

Rule / explanation: Will becomes would.

Example / answer: “The bill will pass” → She said the bill would pass.

Can → could

French: can → could

Rule / explanation: Can becomes could.

Example / answer: “Citizens can challenge the law” → He said citizens could challenge the law.

May → might

French: may → might

Rule / explanation: May becomes might.

Example / answer: “The reform may fail” → She said the reform might fail.

Must → had to / must

French: must

Rule / explanation: Must often becomes had to for obligation, but can remain must for strong present necessity.

Example / answer: “We must act” → He said they had to act.

C. Reporting statements

Say that

French: dire que

Rule / explanation: Use say that to report a statement; no object is required.

Example / answer: The minister said that the policy was necessary.

Tell someone that

French: dire à quelqu’un que

Rule / explanation: Tell needs an object: tell someone something.

Example / answer: The expert told journalists that the data were unreliable.

Argue that

French: soutenir que

Rule / explanation: Use argue that for a reasoned position.

Example / answer: The author argues that regulation is necessary.

Claim that

French: affirmer / prétendre que

Rule / explanation: Use claim that when the statement may be disputed.

Example / answer: Critics claim that the reform is unfair.

Explain that

French: expliquer que

Rule / explanation: Use explain that to report clarification or reasoning.

Example / answer: The article explains that inequality has several causes.

Admit that

French: admettre que

Rule / explanation: Use admit that when someone accepts something negative or uncomfortable.

Example / answer: The minister admitted that mistakes had been made.

Warn that

French: avertir que

Rule / explanation: Use warn that for danger or negative consequences.

Example / answer: Experts warned that the crisis could worsen.

Suggest that

French: suggérer que

Rule / explanation: Use suggest that for cautious interpretation.

Example / answer: The figures suggest that public trust is declining.

D. Reporting questions

Reported yes/no questions

French: questions fermées rapportées

Rule / explanation: Use if or whether, and statement word order.

Example / answer: “Is the reform fair?” → She asked whether the reform was fair.

Reported Wh- questions

French: questions en wh- rapportées

Rule / explanation: Keep the question word, but use statement word order.

Example / answer: “Why are voters angry?” → He asked why voters were angry.

No inversion

French: pas d’inversion

Rule / explanation: Reported questions do not use question word order.

Example / answer: Wrong: He asked why were voters angry. Correct: He asked why voters were angry.

No question mark

French: pas de point d’interrogation

Rule / explanation: Reported questions are usually part of a statement and do not need a question mark.

Example / answer: She asked why the policy had failed.

Ask someone if/whether

French: demander si

Rule / explanation: Use ask someone if/whether for yes/no questions.

Example / answer: The journalist asked the minister whether he would resign.

Ask why/how/what

French: demander pourquoi/comment/quoi

Rule / explanation: Use ask + question word for information questions.

Example / answer: The examiner asked how the argument could be justified.

Do/does/did disappear

French: disparition de do

Rule / explanation: In reported questions, do/does/did usually disappear.

Example / answer: “Why did voters abstain?” → He asked why voters had abstained.

CPGE interview use

French: usage entretien

Rule / explanation: Reported questions help students summarise debates and interviews.

Example / answer: The interviewer asked whether democracy was under threat.

E. Reporting commands, advice and requests

Tell someone to

French: dire de

Rule / explanation: Use tell + object + to + verb for commands or instructions.

Example / answer: The teacher told students to justify their answers.

Ask someone to

French: demander de

Rule / explanation: Use ask + object + to + verb for requests.

Example / answer: The journalist asked the minister to clarify his position.

Advise someone to

French: conseiller de

Rule / explanation: Use advise + object + to + verb for advice.

Example / answer: The expert advised the government to act quickly.

Warn someone not to

French: avertir de ne pas

Rule / explanation: Use warn + object + not to + verb for warnings.

Example / answer: Experts warned citizens not to ignore misinformation.

Order someone to

French: ordonner de

Rule / explanation: Use order + object + to + verb for authority or command.

Example / answer: The judge ordered the company to release the documents.

Urge someone to

French: exhorter à

Rule / explanation: Use urge + object + to + verb for strong recommendation.

Example / answer: Activists urged the government to reduce emissions.

Encourage someone to

French: encourager à

Rule / explanation: Use encourage + object + to + verb for positive advice.

Example / answer: The article encourages readers to question simple narratives.

Tell someone not to

French: dire de ne pas

Rule / explanation: Use tell + object + not to + verb for negative instructions.

Example / answer: The examiner told students not to overgeneralise.

F. Time and place changes

Now → then

French: maintenant → alors

Rule / explanation: Now often becomes then.

Example / answer: “The crisis is serious now” → He said the crisis was serious then.

Today → that day

French: aujourd’hui → ce jour-là

Rule / explanation: Today often becomes that day.

Example / answer: She said the vote was taking place that day.

Yesterday → the day before

French: hier → la veille

Rule / explanation: Yesterday becomes the day before or the previous day.

Example / answer: He said the report had been published the day before.

Tomorrow → the next day

French: demain → le lendemain

Rule / explanation: Tomorrow becomes the next day or the following day.

Example / answer: She said the committee would meet the next day.

This week → that week

French: cette semaine → cette semaine-là

Rule / explanation: This week becomes that week.

Example / answer: He said the bill would be debated that week.

Last year → the year before

French: l’année précédente

Rule / explanation: Last year becomes the year before or the previous year.

Example / answer: The report said poverty had risen the year before.

Here → there

French: ici → là-bas

Rule / explanation: Here often becomes there.

Example / answer: She said the protest had started there.

This → that

French: ceci → cela

Rule / explanation: This often becomes that when the context changes.

Example / answer: He said that argument was misleading.

G. Reported speech in article summaries

The article argues that

French: l’article soutient que

Rule / explanation: Use this to present the author’s main argument.

Example / answer: The article argues that the housing crisis is political.

The journalist points out that

French: le journaliste souligne que

Rule / explanation: Use this for an important observation.

Example / answer: The journalist points out that young voters are increasingly disillusioned.

The report reveals that

French: le rapport révèle que

Rule / explanation: Use this for findings or data.

Example / answer: The report reveals that inequality has widened.

The author acknowledges that

French: l’auteur reconnaît que

Rule / explanation: Use this when the author accepts a limitation or opposing point.

Example / answer: The author acknowledges that regulation may threaten freedom of speech.

The article warns that

French: l’article avertit que

Rule / explanation: Use this for risks and future consequences.

Example / answer: The article warns that distrust could undermine democracy.

The columnist criticises

French: le chroniqueur critique

Rule / explanation: Use this to report criticism directly.

Example / answer: The columnist criticises the government’s lack of transparency.

The study suggests that

French: l’étude suggère que

Rule / explanation: Use this for cautious findings.

Example / answer: The study suggests that social media may increase polarisation.

The author concludes that

French: l’auteur conclut que

Rule / explanation: Use this to report the final point.

Example / answer: The author concludes that reform is urgently needed.

H. Typical mistakes by French speakers

Using question order in reported questions

French: ordre des questions rapportées

Rule / explanation: Reported questions use statement order.

Example / answer: Wrong: He asked why were voters angry. Correct: He asked why voters were angry.

Forgetting the object after tell

French: tell + objet

Rule / explanation: Tell needs an object.

Example / answer: Wrong: He told that the reform was unfair. Correct: He said that... / He told journalists that...

Using say to someone incorrectly

French: say to

Rule / explanation: Use said to someone, not said someone.

Example / answer: Wrong: He said me. Correct: He told me / He said to me.

Forgetting backshift

French: concordance des temps

Rule / explanation: When reporting in the past, tense changes are often needed.

Example / answer: Wrong: He said the policy is unfair. Correct: He said the policy was unfair.

Backshifting unnecessarily

French: backshift inutile

Rule / explanation: If the statement is still true or the reporting verb is present, backshift may not be needed.

Example / answer: The article says that democracy is fragile.

Using ask to for questions

French: ask

Rule / explanation: Ask to means request to do something; ask if/why reports questions.

Example / answer: Wrong: He asked to the reform was fair. Correct: He asked whether the reform was fair.

Mixing direct and reported speech

French: mélange direct/indirect

Rule / explanation: Avoid quotation marks with reported speech.

Example / answer: Wrong: The author argues that “the policy is unfair”. Better: The author argues that the policy is unfair.

Weak reporting verbs

French: verbes pauvres

Rule / explanation: Avoid repeating says all the time; choose precise verbs.

Example / answer: Better: argues, claims, warns, admits, suggests, highlights.
